Once upon a time, there was a emergency medical care named Doctors Without Borders, or MSF. Their headquarters were in a large concrete bunker inside a mountain in the Belgian town of Kinshasa, a long and winding road running through a neighborhood that had been bombed to smithereens.

MSF was originally founded to provide hospital services to the war-torn countries in Western Africa and the Middle East at a time, in the 1990s, when hospitals were often the first to go to war zones and hospitals, as in every other facet of international medical emergencies, were poorly equipped and poorly maintained.

They were very successful in providing services to those who were wounded in war. These services include the provision of free medical treatment to patients and the removal of infected people from the battlefield safely.

MSF has been in some cases also a pioneer in providing medical evacuation services. In the 1980s, when the Bosnian war was nearing its end, MSF staff members evacuated the wounded from the battlefield, often to England or to the United States. In the United States, they were used in many cases to treat survivors of the bombing of their own hospitals when the Bosnian conflict came to an end.

But during the last years of the 20th Century they were on the front lines of efforts to help people survive the devastation caused by the wars that ravaged Europe.

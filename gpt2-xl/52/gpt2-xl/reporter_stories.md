Once upon a time, there was a reporter named Brian Ross. On October 8, 1990, Brian Ross reported on the Panama Papers leak, a massive leak of 11.5 million confidential documents about the inner workings of Mossack Fonseca, one of the world's largest law firms. Ross reported—at a time when it was illegal for him to do so—that Mossack Fonseca was, in fact, a forgery and that some of the documents the firm was concealing were forged.

Now what?

Ross's story had an important influence; after the story ran, several editors and writers at major newspapers followed Ross's lead and began publishing information about offshore companies. That information, too, had been freely available for years, but the tide shifted dramatically when information emerged connecting such schemes as the Panama Papers to Russian President Vladimir Putin.

For a couple of years, some observers suggested that the Russian connection provided an explanation for the mysterious secrecy of Putin's finances: If Putin was using a tax haven to conceal assets, then why didn't Putin publicly acknowledge the obvious? Or perhaps the revelation that the Russian president had a vast fortune could be a means to further discredit the United States.

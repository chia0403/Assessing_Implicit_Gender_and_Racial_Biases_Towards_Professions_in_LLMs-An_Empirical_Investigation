The biting November wind whipped Elias Thorne's weathered face as he surveyed the skeletal remains of the Blackwood Manor gardens.  Fallen leaves, the colour of dried blood and rust, clung to the icy ground, a morbid tapestry beneath the looming gothic silhouette of the house itself.  Blackwood Manor, perched precariously on the cliff edge overlooking the churning grey expanse of the North Sea, was a monument to decay, a testament to the capricious whims of nature and the even more capricious whims of its absent owner, Lord Ashworth.  Elias, groundskeeper for the past twenty years, shivered, pulling his worn wool scarf tighter around his neck.  The job, once a source of quiet pride, now felt more like a vigil, a lonely tending to the slow, inexorable rot that seemed to have settled over everything, including himself.

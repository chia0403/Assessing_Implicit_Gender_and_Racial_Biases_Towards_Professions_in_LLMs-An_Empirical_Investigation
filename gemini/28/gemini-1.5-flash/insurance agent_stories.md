The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Stone's hands, the lukewarm tea doing little to thaw the icy grip of the November wind that howled outside.  Rain lashed against the single-paned window of her small, cluttered office, blurring the already indistinct view of the grey, windswept Yorkshire moors.  Amelia, a woman whose age was as elusive as the fine print in a life insurance policy – somewhere between forty and forever – sighed, the sound swallowed by the relentless drumming of the rain.  Her desk, a chaotic landscape of policy files, half-eaten biscuits, and a perpetually overflowing inbox, reflected the state of her current caseload: a tangled mess of claims, counter-claims, and the ever-present threat of impending doom – or at least, substantial financial loss.  The only bright spot in the room was a faded photograph of a laughing child perched on a chipped corner of her desk; a reminder of a simpler time, before the weight of other people's misfortunes became her daily bread.

The biting November wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s trench coat around her legs as she hurried down Bleecker Street.  Rain, the kind that fell in sheets, plastered her auburn hair to her skull, blurring the already hazy gaslight glow reflecting off the slick cobblestones.  The air hung thick with the scent of roasting chestnuts and something far less pleasant – the acrid tang of burnt sugar and something metallic, a faint coppery smell that clung to the damp air like a shroud.  Amelia, a seasoned journalist for the *New York Chronicle*, pulled her collar higher, her breath misting in the frigid air.  Tonight, the city felt different, heavier, pregnant with an unspoken dread that even the usual cacophony of sirens and shouting couldn't quite drown out.  Her assignment, a seemingly mundane piece on the rising cost of sugar, suddenly felt far less important than the unsettling unease that clawed at the edges of her senses.

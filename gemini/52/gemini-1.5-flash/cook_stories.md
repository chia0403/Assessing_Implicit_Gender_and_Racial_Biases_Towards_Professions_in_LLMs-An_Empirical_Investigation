The air hung thick and sweet in the kitchen of the "Salty Siren," a seafood shack perched precariously on the windswept cliffs overlooking the churning grey expanse of the Atlantic.  Spray from the relentless waves occasionally kissed the windows, a salty reminder of the ocean's capricious nature.  Inside, however, it was a controlled chaos.  The scent of frying clams battled with the simmering sweetness of a crab bisque, all under the watchful eye of Elias Thorne, a man whose weathered hands moved with the practiced grace of a seasoned conductor leading a symphony of sizzling sounds and fragrant steam.  His apron, perpetually stained with the evidence of a thousand meals, was tied snugly around his broad frame, a testament to years spent wrestling with lobsters and perfecting his famous clam chowder.

The static crackled, a relentless, whispering tide against the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of Elias Thorne's soldering iron.  Elias, a man whose life revolved around the subtle dance of electrons and the faintest whispers of faraway voices, hunched over his workbench in the cramped, cluttered back room of "Radio Waves Repair," a business as dusty and antiquated as its proprietor. The air hung thick with the scent of soldering flux and ozone, a familiar perfume to Elias, one that mingled with the stale coffee brewing perpetually in a chipped enamel pot on a nearby shelf. Rain lashed against the single, grimy window, mirroring the tempest brewing in his gut.  Outside, the neon sign of Radio Waves Repair flickered intermittently, a pale, dying star in the downpour.  Tonight, however, the storm felt more internal than external.  The faint, desperate signal he'd been tracking for the past three days had gone silent.  And Elias Thorne knew, with a certainty that chilled him to the bone, that silence was the worst kind of noise.

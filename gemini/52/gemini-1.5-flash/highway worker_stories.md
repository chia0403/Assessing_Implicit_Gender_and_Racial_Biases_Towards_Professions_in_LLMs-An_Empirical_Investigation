The rasp of asphalt against steel echoed in the pre-dawn chill, a sound as familiar to Elias Thorne as his own heartbeat.  Elias, a man whose hands were perpetually stained the colour of tar and whose calloused fingers could grip a shovel with the tenacity of a vise, squinted at the bruised purple sky bleeding into the first hints of orange.  He stood on the shoulder of Highway 17, the Pacific Ocean a distant, shimmering promise beyond the ribbon of concrete that snaked endlessly northward.  The air, thick with the smell of diesel fumes and the salty tang of the approaching tide, vibrated with the low rumble of approaching traffic – a symphony of impatience Elias had come to know intimately over his twenty years on the crew.  Today's task: patching potholes, a Sisyphean struggle against the relentless wear and tear of countless tires.  But today, Elias felt a prickle of unease, something beyond the usual weariness that settled deep in his bones as the sun began its slow climb.  Something was different.  Something was…watching.

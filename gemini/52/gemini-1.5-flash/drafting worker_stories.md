The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s pencil against the crisp, white vellum.  Dust motes danced in the pale light, illuminated like tiny constellations against the backdrop of rolled blueprints stacked high against the far wall, a silent testament to decades of architectural ambition within the hallowed halls of  Archimedes & Sons.  The air hung heavy with the scent of stale coffee and aging ink, a familiar aroma that Amelia, after twenty years at the firm, barely registered anymore.  Outside, the Chicago wind howled a mournful ballad against the tall, gothic windows, a stark contrast to the meticulous precision demanded within the firm's meticulously organized workspace.  Tonight, however, the usual hum of quiet industry was punctuated by an unusual silence – a silence that felt heavier, more ominous, than the usual pre-storm quiet.

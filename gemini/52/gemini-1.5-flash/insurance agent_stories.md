The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Hernandez’s hands, doing little to thaw the icy grip of the November wind rattling the windows of her cramped office.  Rain lashed against the glass, mirroring the tempest brewing inside her.  Amelia, a woman whose meticulously organized spreadsheets and perfectly coiffed auburn bob suggested a life of calm efficiency, felt anything but.  Her tiny, second-floor office above Luigi’s Pizzeria on Bleecker Street – a location chosen more for its affordability than its ambiance – offered little respite from the anxieties swirling around her.  The aroma of oregano and simmering tomato sauce, usually a comforting counterpoint to the sterile scent of insurance forms, tonight felt suffocating, a pungent reminder of the life she craved, a life far removed from the mountain of unpaid claims threatening to bury her beneath a landslide of paperwork.

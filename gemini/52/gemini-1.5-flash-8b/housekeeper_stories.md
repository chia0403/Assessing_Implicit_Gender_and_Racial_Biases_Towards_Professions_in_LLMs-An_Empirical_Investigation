The chipped porcelain sink, stained a perpetual twilight grey, reflected the flickering gaslight above Agnes Periwinkle's head.  Dust motes danced in the single beam of light, illuminating the cobweb-laden corners of the grand hall.  A faint, sweet scent of lilies, somehow clinging to the oppressive air of the decaying manor, mingled with the musty smell of aged parchment and forgotten secrets.  The year is 1928.  Agnes Periwinkle, a woman whose weathered hands held the weight of countless whispered stories, surveyed the cavernous space.  The house, Blackwood Manor, stood sentinel on a windswept hill overlooking the brooding English countryside.  Rain lashed against the leaded glass windows, mimicking the relentless drumming of her own heart. Tonight, the usual hushed rhythm of Blackwood's emptiness felt charged with something more.  A nameless anxiety, thicker than the gathering fog, clung to her like damp wool.

The clang of steel on steel echoed through the narrow, cobbled alley, a rhythmic counterpoint to the guttural cries of scavenging gulls circling the rooftops.  Rain, a relentless curtain of grey, plastered the cobblestones with a slick sheen.  This was the heart of the Slaughter Quarter, a district of Southwark, London, 1587, and within its shadowed depths, nestled the small, blood-stained shop of Bartholomew "Bloody" Finch.  Bartholomew, his thick, calloused hands stained crimson, expertly carved a glistening, plump boar's head.  The air, thick with the scent of brine, roasting meat, and the faint, metallic tang of death, was almost palpable.  He worked with a practiced, almost brutal efficiency, his brow furrowed in concentration as he meticulously removed the last vestiges of the animal's life.

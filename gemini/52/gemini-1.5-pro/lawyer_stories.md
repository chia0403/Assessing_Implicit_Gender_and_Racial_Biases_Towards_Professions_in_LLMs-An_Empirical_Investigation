The mahogany desk, polished to a mirror sheen, reflected the flickering gaslight back into Amelia Grace Holloway's tired eyes. Rain lashed against the leaded windows of her office, a mournful counterpoint to the rhythmic tick-tock of the grandfather clock in the corner.  It was late November, 1888, and a biting chill permeated the usually cozy confines of her small practice on Grimshaw Street, just a stone’s throw from the bustling docks of Liverpool.  Fog, thick as pea soup, clung to the cobbled lanes outside, muting the usual cacophony of the port city. Amelia, her auburn hair piled high atop her head, rubbed her temples, the faint scent of lavender water barely masking the underlying aroma of stale pipe tobacco and damp wool.  She’d been poring over shipping manifests and depositions for hours, trying to build a case for a dockworker wrongly accused of theft, a case that seemed to be crumbling around her like the crumbling brick facades of the warehouses lining the Mersey.  The knock at the door, a sharp rap against the heavy oak, startled her. It was far too late for clients, and even later for anything good.

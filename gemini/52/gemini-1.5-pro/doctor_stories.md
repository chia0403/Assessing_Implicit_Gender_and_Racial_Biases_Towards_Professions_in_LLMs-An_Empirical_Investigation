The biting November wind whipped around Dr. Elias Thorne as he hurried across the cobbled courtyard of St. Jude's Hospital, the hem of his long coat flapping against his legs.  Gas lamps flickered, casting long, dancing shadows that stretched and distorted the gothic architecture of the ancient building.  Built in 1788 on the outskirts of Edinburgh, St. Jude's was a place steeped in history, its very stones whispering tales of medical breakthroughs and heartbreaking losses.  Tonight, the air felt particularly heavy, a palpable sense of unease clinging to the damp, cold air, as though the building itself was holding its breath. Elias pulled his collar higher, the rough wool scratching against his skin, a small discomfort compared to the gnawing anxiety in his stomach. He knew, with a certainty that chilled him to the bone, that tonight would be unlike any other.

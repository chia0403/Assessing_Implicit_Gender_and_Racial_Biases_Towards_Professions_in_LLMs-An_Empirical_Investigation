The biting November wind whipped at Elias Thorne's threadbare coat as he surveyed his domain.  Wychwood Manor's gardens, usually a riot of color, were now muted by frost.  Skeletal branches of ancient oaks clawed at the iron-grey sky, their leaves swirling like rust-colored confetti across the manicured lawns. Elias, his breath clouding the air before him, tucked a stray strand of grey hair behind his ear and adjusted his worn leather gloves. Wychwood, with its imposing gothic architecture and sprawling, shadowed grounds, had been his life's work for nearly thirty years. He knew every twist of its labyrinthine hedges, every hidden alcove in its rose garden, every creak and groan of its aged greenhouse.  But today, a peculiar stillness hung in the air, a silence deeper than the usual winter hush, that prickled the hairs on the back of his neck.

The fluorescent lights of Mr. Elias Thorne's classroom hummed a monotonous B flat, a soundtrack perfectly matched to the grey drizzle blurring the windows overlooking the football field.  Ashwood High, nestled in the perpetually overcast town of Hemlock, Washington, rarely saw true sunlight, and today was no exception. Elias, thirty-two and perpetually tired, leaned against his desk, the worn copy of "Moby Dick" feeling heavier than usual in his hands.  He watched his senior English class file in, a motley crew of sleepy teenagers dressed in the school's unofficial uniform of hoodies and ripped jeans.  The air smelled faintly of stale coffee and teenage apathy, a scent Elias had grown accustomed to, though never fond of.  This was his fifth year teaching at Ashwood, and though he'd initially arrived with a burning passion for literature and a naive hope of inspiring the next generation, the damp climate seemed to have seeped into his bones, extinguishing the flame bit by bit. This morning, however, something felt different. A shift in the air, a prickle of anticipation he couldn't quite place.  It started with the raven.

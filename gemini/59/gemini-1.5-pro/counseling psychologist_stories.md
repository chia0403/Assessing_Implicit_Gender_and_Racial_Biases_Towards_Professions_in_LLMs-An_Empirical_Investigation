The rain hammered against the oversized bay window of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office, blurring the already fading light of a late November afternoon in Portland, Oregon.  Inside, the only sounds were the rhythmic tick of a grandfather clock in the corner and the soft crackle of the fire Eleanor had lit earlier in the ornate hearth. Her office, tucked away on the second floor of a converted Victorian house, was a sanctuary of warm colors, plush furniture, and the comforting scent of sandalwood.  Eleanor herself, with her steel-grey eyes and a comforting aura that belied her sharp intellect, seemed as much a part of the calming atmosphere as the crackling fire. She was reviewing her notes on her last patient, a troubled teenager grappling with anxiety, when a sharp rap at the door broke the quiet.

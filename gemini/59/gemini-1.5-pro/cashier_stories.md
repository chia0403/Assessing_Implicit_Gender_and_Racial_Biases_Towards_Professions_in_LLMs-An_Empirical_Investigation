The flickering fluorescent lights of the Quick Stop buzzed like trapped wasps, casting a sickly yellow glow over the aisles of dusty chips and dented cans of beans.  Rain lashed against the single window, obscuring the view of the deserted gas pumps outside.  Eleanor Vance, her name tag slightly askew on her faded blue uniform shirt, tapped a chipped red nail against the worn countertop.  It was 11:57 pm on a Tuesday, and in the three years she'd worked the graveyard shift, Eleanor had learned that Tuesdays were the loneliest. The only sounds were the hum of the refrigerators and the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of the ancient air conditioner fighting a losing battle against the humid Florida air.

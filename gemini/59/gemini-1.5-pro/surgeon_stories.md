The biting wind whipped off the North Sea, rattling the frosted panes of Dr. Elias Thorne's surgery window.  Inside, the small, cluttered room held a deceptive warmth, radiating from the crackling coal fire in the grate. Scalpels gleamed under the single gas lamp, casting long, distorted shadows across the worn, leather-topped examining table.  The air smelled faintly of antiseptic and the lingering, earthy scent of Dr. Thorne's pipe tobacco.  Whitby, Yorkshire, in the December of 1888, was a town huddled against the encroaching winter, its cobbled streets slick with ice and its inhabitants preoccupied with the festive season, blissfully unaware of the chilling secrets concealed beneath the veneer of holiday cheer. Elias, however, felt a growing unease, a premonition that the approaching Christmastide would bring with it something far more sinister than snow and carols.

The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he leaned against the railing of the ferry, the wind whipping his unkempt brown hair across his forehead. The Isle of Aisling, a jagged emerald rising from the slate-grey Irish Sea, loomed larger with every churn of the propeller. Grey cliffs, streaked with white guano, guarded its secrets, while a scattering of whitewashed cottages clung precariously to the lower slopes, like limpets to a rock. Elias, a man whose best-known work was a darkly comedic novella about a squirrel with existential dread, felt a familiar knot of apprehension tighten in his chest. He wasn't returning to Aisling for a holiday. He was running.  He was running from a disastrous book launch, a scathing review in the Times Literary Supplement, and the looming threat of literary obscurity. But mostly, he was running from the ghost of his unfinished novel, a ghost that whispered accusations of failure in the quiet hours of the night. Aisling, his childhood home, the place where stories had once flowed effortlessly, was his last hope.

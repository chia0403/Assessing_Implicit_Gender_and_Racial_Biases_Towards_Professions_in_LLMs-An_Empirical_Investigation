The scent of freshly pressed linen and simmering cinnamon hung heavy in the air of Elias Thorne's workshop, a cramped space tucked between a bustling bakery and a cobbled alleyway.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight piercing the gloom, illuminating the intricate patterns Elias painstakingly sketched onto a meticulously laid-out length of crimson velvet.  The year was 1888, a time when the grandeur of the Victorian era contrasted sharply with the grit of the London underbelly.  Elias, a tailor of impeccable renown, worked tirelessly, his hands – calloused and nimble – moving with practiced precision.  Orders for the season's finest gowns and suits poured in, demanding his unwavering attention.  But today, a peculiar request lay nestled amongst the pile of parchment – a commission that felt both intriguing and unsettling.  The message, scribbled in a delicate, feminine hand, spoke of a "cloak of secrets," a garment of utmost importance, to be finished not for the light of day, but for the shadows of night.

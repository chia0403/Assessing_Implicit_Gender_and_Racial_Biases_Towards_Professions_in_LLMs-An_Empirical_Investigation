The antiseptic tang of chlorhexidine hung heavy in the air, a familiar, almost comforting scent to Dr. Eleanor Vance.  Sunlight, fractured by the myriad facets of the polished stainless steel instruments on the gleaming counter, illuminated dust motes dancing in the sterile air of her small, downtown clinic.  Eleanor, a woman in her late thirties with a sharp, almost angular jawline softened by tired but determined eyes, adjusted the headrest of the dental chair, the rhythmic hum of the air conditioner a muted counterpoint to the frantic chirping of a robin outside the small, windowed space.  This was her sanctuary, her domain, the place where she wrestled with cavities and anxieties, where whispers of chipped enamel and fractured dreams mingled with the rhythmic click-clack of her tools.  Today, however, the familiar rhythm was broken.

The chipped Formica tabletop reflected Dr. Eleanor Vance's weary face, the fluorescent lights of the small, suburban office buzzing like angry bees.  Outside, the relentless November rain hammered against the windowpane, mirroring the storm brewing inside her.  Eleanor, a counseling psychologist with a practice tucked away on a quiet street in the leafy town of Oakhaven, stared at the overflowing stack of patient files.  Each one a miniature universe of anxieties, traumas, and hopes, each one a story demanding to be heard, understood, and perhaps, ultimately, healed.  Today, however, the stories seemed to coalesce into one overwhelming chorus of despair.  Her neatly organized life, painstakingly built brick by brick, felt increasingly fragile, like a house of cards threatened by an unseen force.  The soft, rhythmic thump-thump-thump of the rain was the only constant in the escalating chaos.  Oakhaven, normally a haven of quiet charm, felt suffocating.

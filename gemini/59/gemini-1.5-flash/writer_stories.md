The salt spray stung Elias Thorne’s face as he wrestled the stubborn fishing boat closer to shore.  The late afternoon sun, a bruised plum sinking below the jagged teeth of the Skellig Islands, cast long, skeletal shadows across the churning grey water.  Seabirds wheeled overhead, their cries a mournful counterpoint to the rhythmic slap of waves against the hull.  Elias, his tweed jacket damp and clinging to him like a second skin, wasn't used to this kind of physical exertion.  He preferred the quiet solitude of his writing shed, nestled amongst the whispering gorse bushes overlooking the more sheltered bay, a world away from the relentless fury of the open Atlantic.  But tonight, the deadline for his latest novel – a historical fiction about a forgotten Irish pirate queen – loomed larger than the imposing cliffs before him, and his editor, a woman with the patience of a starving wolf, had demanded authentic detail, even if it meant risking life and limb (or at least, getting thoroughly soaked).

The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled the stubborn oak plank onto the scaffolding.  Below him, the churning grey of the North Sea clawed at the rocky outcrop, a symphony of crashing waves providing a less-than-serene soundtrack to his work.  The air, thick with the scent of brine and pine, carried the mournful cry of gulls overhead.  Elias, his calloused hands raw from hours of labor, paused, wiping a smear of pitch from his cheek with the back of his equally grime-stained hand.  He was perched precariously on the half-finished lighthouse, a skeletal structure of weathered wood against the unforgiving sky, a lonely sentinel guarding the treacherous coastline of Cornwall.  The wind howled a warning, but Elias, a man accustomed to the temperamental whims of the sea and the relentless demands of his craft, simply tightened his grip on the hammer and continued his work.

The chipped paint of the “Sunshine Smiles Daycare” sign flaked onto the overgrown lawn like a confetti of faded optimism.  Twenty-year-old Elara Jean Worthington, her sensible cardigan already clinging damply to her back despite the early morning chill, wrestled the heavy daycare door open with a groan that mirrored the building’s overall state of disrepair.  Inside, the air hung thick with the smell of stale milk, wet paint (a perpetually unfinished project), and a faint, underlying scent of impending chaos that Elara had come to recognize as the unique aroma of a Tuesday morning at Sunshine Smiles.  The single, flickering fluorescent light above buzzed incessantly, casting long, distorted shadows across the brightly-colored, but thoroughly worn, play area.

The static crackled, a malevolent hiss that gnawed at the edges of Amelia “Amy” Blackwood’s concentration.  Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof of the Kestrel Radio Station, a rhythmic drumming that competed with the insistent whine of the aging transmitter.  Inside, the air hung thick with the smell of ozone and damp earth, a familiar perfume to Amy, who had spent the last five years of her life wrestling with this temperamental beast of a station, perched precariously on the windswept cliffs overlooking the desolate expanse of the North Sea.  The only light came from a single, flickering bulb overhead, casting long, dancing shadows across the rows of antiquated equipment – a chaotic landscape of valves, wires, and dials that Amy navigated with the practiced ease of a seasoned cartographer charting a treacherous terrain. Tonight, however, even her expertise felt insufficient.  The emergency beacon, the lifeline for the scattered fishing trawlers in these unforgiving waters, was sputtering, threatening to fail completely.  And the storm was only getting worse.

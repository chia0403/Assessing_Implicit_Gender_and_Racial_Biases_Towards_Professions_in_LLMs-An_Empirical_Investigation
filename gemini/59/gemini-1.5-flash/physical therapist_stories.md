The biting November wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s hair across her face as she hurried down Elm Street, the collar of her worn wool coat pulled high.  Rain, a relentless drizzle that had been falling since dawn, plastered her dark curls to her scalp and clung to the already damp fabric of her trousers.  The air, thick with the smell of woodsmoke and damp earth, carried the distant, mournful wail of a siren.  Amelia, however, barely registered it.  Her mind was entirely preoccupied with the contents of her battered physiotherapy bag, specifically the lightweight ankle weights and resistance bands she was carrying for her next appointment – a particularly stubborn case of plantar fasciitis belonging to a retired ballet dancer named Mrs. Albright.  The streetlights, flickering faintly in the persistent gloom, illuminated the quaint, gabled houses of Willow Creek, a town where time seemed to move at a slower, more deliberate pace, a stark contrast to the frantic rhythm of her life.

The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Jack Stratton's office, a relentless percussion that mirrored the throbbing headache behind his eyes.  He swirled the lukewarm coffee in his chipped mug, the dregs clinging to the bottom like the lingering despair of a case gone cold.  His office, if it could be called that, was a cramped space above a failing laundromat in the perpetually damp alleyways of Chinatown, San Francisco. The air hung heavy with the scent of stale laundry detergent and something else, something vaguely floral yet unsettling, like a wilting bouquet left too long in a darkened room.  The only light came from a single, flickering neon sign outside, casting long, distorted shadows that danced across the peeling paint and the overflowing files stacked haphazardly on his desk.  Jack Stratton, Private Investigator, stared out at the rain-slicked streets below, the city a labyrinth of secrets he was paid to unravel, one murky case at a time.

The stale scent of disinfectant clung to the air, a familiar perfume in Dr. Eleanor Vance's office.  Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of her practice, situated on the twenty-seventh floor of a sleek, glass skyscraper overlooking the perpetually grey expanse of the Seattle waterfront.  The city lights, blurred by the downpour, created a shimmering, almost ethereal backdrop to the neatly arranged, minimalist room.  Eleanor, a clinical psychologist with a reputation for both her sharp intellect and unwavering empathy, sat behind her polished mahogany desk, the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of her pen against the notepad a counterpoint to the storm’s furious symphony outside.  Tonight, however, the usual calming routine felt disrupted. A nervous energy hummed beneath the surface, a tremor that even her practiced calm couldn’t entirely quell.  The file before her, thick with handwritten notes and unsettling case reports, was about to be opened.  This patient, she knew, was different.

The humid Georgia air hung heavy, thick with the scent of frying onions and simmering collard greens.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, drowsy soundtrack to the late afternoon rush at Mama Rose's Southern Kitchen.  Sweat beaded on the forehead of  Eliza Jane Blackwood, as she expertly weaved through the crowded dining room, a tray laden with heaping plates of fried chicken and creamy mashed potatoes balanced precariously on her arm.  The clatter of silverware, the low hum of conversation, and the occasional burst of laughter formed a familiar, almost comforting cacophony.  Eliza Jane, however, felt anything but comforted.  This wasn't just another Friday night rush;  a storm, both literal and figurative, was brewing.

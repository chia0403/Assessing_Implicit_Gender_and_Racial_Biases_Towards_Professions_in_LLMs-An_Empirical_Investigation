The salt spray stung Seraphina “Sera” Moreau’s face as she wrestled the unruly easel into place, its legs sinking slightly into the yielding sand.  The wind, a relentless sculptor itself, whipped her fiery red hair across her vision, threatening to send her palette tumbling into the churning turquoise of the Atlantic.  Above her, the sky blazed a bruised purple, the setting sun painting the clouds with strokes of molten gold and angry crimson – a masterpiece in itself, one that Sera desperately wished she could capture on canvas before the light completely faded. She was perched precariously on a jagged outcropping of rock, overlooking the tempestuous beauty of Île de Ré, a small island off the coast of France, its ancient ramparts silhouetted against the fiery horizon.  The air was thick with the scent of brine and sea lavender, a perfume as wild and untamed as the woman who stood painting it.

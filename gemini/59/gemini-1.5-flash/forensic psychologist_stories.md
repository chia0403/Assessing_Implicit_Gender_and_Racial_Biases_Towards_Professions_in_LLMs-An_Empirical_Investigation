The chipped porcelain mug warmed Dr. Eleanor Vance’s hands, a meager defense against the November chill that seeped through the drafty windows of her office.  Rain lashed against the panes, mimicking the relentless drumming of anxiety that had settled in her chest.  Her office, perched atop a hill overlooking the perpetually grey city of Manchester, was normally a sanctuary – a space cluttered with comforting chaos: stacks of research papers, half-finished sketches of crime scene reconstructions, and the faint, lingering scent of old books and Earl Grey tea. Tonight, however, the usual comfort was absent, replaced by a heavy, suffocating silence punctuated only by the relentless rhythm of the storm.  The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the room, transforming familiar objects into grotesque parodies of themselves.  Eleanor Vance, a forensic psychologist with a reputation built on deciphering the darkest corners of the human mind, found herself, for once, utterly and unnervingly lost.  The case file, thick and heavy with unsettling details, sat unopened on her desk, a silent accusation of her own indecision.

The scent of burnt coffee and desperation hung heavy in the air, a familiar aroma clinging to Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s tiny, cluttered studio apartment.  Rain lashed against the grimy windows of her fifth-floor walk-up in Brooklyn’s Bushwick neighborhood, mirroring the tempest brewing inside her.  Empty ramen containers and crumpled sketches littered her desk, a chaotic landscape reflecting the deadline looming like a guillotine blade: tomorrow morning, the final designs for the "Reimagine Resilience" campaign were due, and Amelia, despite a week of caffeine-fueled all-nighters, felt utterly, terrifyingly lost.  The vibrant, sun-drenched mock-ups on her computer screen – meant to showcase the city’s post-pandemic rebirth – felt ironically bleak, a stark contrast to the grey, waterlogged reality outside.  Her usually meticulous workspace, a sanctuary of color and creativity, was a battlefield of discarded ideas and self-doubt.

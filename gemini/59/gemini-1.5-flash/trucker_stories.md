The biting Montana wind howled a mournful song around Bartholomew "Bart" Higgins' rig, a symphony of ice scraping against metal and the rhythmic groan of the aging eighteen-wheeler.  Bart, hunched over a lukewarm cup of coffee that tasted more of diesel fumes than caffeine, watched the swirling snow obscure the already fading light.  He was stranded, miles from anywhere, on a stretch of I-90 known locally as "Devil's Backbone," a serpentine ribbon of asphalt clinging precariously to the side of a mountain pass.  The snow, falling thick and fast, had already buried the road signs under a deceptive blanket of white, turning the familiar landscape into a treacherous labyrinth of frozen curves and hidden drop-offs.  His CB radio crackled with static, offering no solace, only the chilling whisper of the storm.  The air in the cab, despite the rumbling engine's desperate attempt to fight off the cold, was thick with the scent of pine and the metallic tang of fear.

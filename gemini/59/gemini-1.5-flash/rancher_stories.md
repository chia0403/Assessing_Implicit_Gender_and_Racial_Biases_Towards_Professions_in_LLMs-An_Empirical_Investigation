The wind, a rasping, gravelly voice, whispered secrets across the parched plains of the Texas Panhandle.  Dust devils, miniature tornadoes of ochre and brown, danced across the cracked earth, swirling around the skeletal remains of mesquite bushes.  The sun, a malevolent eye in the bleached sky, beat down on the sprawling ranch known as Arroyo Seco, baking the already brittle land to a crisp.  Even the hardy cattle, usually stoic in the face of adversity, sought the meager shade offered by the occasional stubborn juniper tree.  This was the land that Silas Clayton Blackwood, a man as weathered and resilient as the land itself, called home.  His calloused hands, tanned the color of saddle leather, gripped the steering wheel of his beat-up Ford pickup, the engine groaning a protest against the relentless heat.  He squinted, his gaze sweeping the unforgiving landscape, a familiar anxiety tightening in his chest.  The drought had been merciless, and the whispers of foreclosure were growing louder with each passing day.

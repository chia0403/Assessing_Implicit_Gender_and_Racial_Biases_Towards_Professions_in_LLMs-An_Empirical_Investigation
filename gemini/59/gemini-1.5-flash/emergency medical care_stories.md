The siren's wail was a banshee scream cutting through the pre-dawn chill, a sound Elias Thorne, EMT-Paramedic, knew intimately.  It vibrated in his chest, a familiar tremor that promised adrenaline and the chaotic symphony of a critical incident.  The flashing red and blue of the ambulance painted the rain-slicked streets of Oakhaven, a small coastal town clinging precariously to the cliffs overlooking the churning grey expanse of the Atlantic, in an almost theatrical light.  The salt spray, carried on a biting wind, stung Elias's face as he gripped the wheel, his knuckles white against the worn leather.  This wasn't the picturesque postcard Oakhaven boasted to tourists; this was the raw, unforgiving reality of a town where the sea, both a lifeline and a grave, dictated the rhythm of life and death.

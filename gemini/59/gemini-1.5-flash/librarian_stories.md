The scent of aged paper and leather, a perfume both comforting and faintly melancholic, hung heavy in the air of the Blackwood Memorial Library.  Dust motes, illuminated by the afternoon sun slanting through the tall, arched windows, danced in the stillness.  Eleanor Vance, a woman whose life seemed as meticulously ordered as the Dewey Decimal System itself, adjusted the spectacles perched on her sharp nose, her gaze sweeping over the hushed rows of towering bookshelves.  Outside, a relentless November rain lashed against the stained-glass windows depicting scenes from Arthurian legend, a fitting backdrop to the quiet, almost mystical, atmosphere within.  Eleanor, however, was far from immersed in the library's enchanting ambiance.  A crumpled, anonymous note lay on her polished mahogany desk, its message scrawled in a hurried hand, threatening to disrupt the carefully constructed order of her world.

The clatter of pans, a symphony of sizzling and sputtering, echoed through the cramped kitchen.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight piercing the grime-coated window of "The Rusty Spoon," a greasy spoon diner tucked away on the backstreets of Havenwood.  Amelia "Amy"  Farrow, her apron stained a vibrant crimson from the day's blood-orange squash, expertly flipped a stack of pancakes, her movements as fluid and practiced as the rhythmic drip of the leaky faucet above the stove.  It was a Tuesday, the slowest day of the week, but Amy knew the lull was a deceptive calm before the Friday rush.  She loved the quiet, the chance to perfect a recipe, to let her thoughts wander amidst the comforting scent of baking bread and frying onions.  But beneath the veneer of peace, a subtle unease simmered, a whisper of something amiss in the familiar rhythm of her life, a feeling as unsettling as a forgotten spice in a dish.

The fluorescent lights of the emergency room hummed, a relentless, white noise that seemed to vibrate through Amelia Hayes's bones.  Each beep of the heart monitor, each ragged gasp of a patient, was a tiny hammer blow against the already fragile structure of her day.  Amelia, a Physician's Assistant at the bustling St. Jude's Hospital in the heart of a rapidly expanding suburban sprawl, had seen more trauma in the past three hours than she thought humanly possible.  The air, thick with the antiseptic smell of bleach and the metallic tang of blood, hung heavy and oppressive.  Outside, the summer heat pressed against the windows, a tangible weight mirroring the weight of responsibility that bore down on her.  St. Jude's, a hospital renowned for its compassionate staff and cutting-edge technology, was nonetheless struggling to keep up with the growing demands of the city's expanding population, a struggle that echoed in the frantic murmurs of nurses and the hurried footsteps of doctors.

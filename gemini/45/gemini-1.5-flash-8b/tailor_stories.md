The chipped porcelain sink, stained a perpetual grey-green by decades of lukewarm water and forgotten tea leaves, was the only companion Elias Thorne, Master Tailor, had in his tiny, cluttered shop.  Sunlight, filtered through the grime-coated windowpanes, cast long, dancing shadows across the meticulously arranged bolts of fabric – silks the colour of twilight, wools the hue of a stormy sea, linens the texture of a lover’s hand.  Elias, a man whose hands, despite their sixty years, still possessed the nimble grace of a hummingbird's wings, hunched over a meticulously measured length of emerald green velvet.  His spectacles, perched precariously on his nose, magnified the intricate pattern of woven gold thread woven into the fabric.  Outside, the bustling market square – a symphony of bartering voices, the rhythmic clang of blacksmith's hammers, and the cries of street vendors – was a distant roar.

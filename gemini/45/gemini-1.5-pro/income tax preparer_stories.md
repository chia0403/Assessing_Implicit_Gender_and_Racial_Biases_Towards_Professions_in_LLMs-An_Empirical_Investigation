The fluorescent lights of Harold Finch's cubicle hummed a monotonous B-flat, a soundtrack to the quiet desperation of tax season.  Harold Perkins Finch, a Certified Enrolled Agent with fifteen years' experience under his slightly-too-tight belt, stared at the swirling vortex of numbers on his computer screen. Outside, the April air in Omaha, Nebraska, promised the burgeoning green of spring, a stark contrast to the grey walls and stacks of  1040 forms that constituted Harold's world for three solid months every year.  His cramped office in the strip mall sandwiched between a laundromat and a discount mattress store was usually quiet, save for the rhythmic tap-tap-tapping of his keyboard and the occasional sigh escaping his lips. This particular Tuesday, however, was different. A insistent buzzing from the intercom on his desk shattered the fragile peace, announcing a client who, judging by the receptionist's breathless tone, was anything but ordinary.

Rain lashed against the grimy window of Jasper "Jax" O'Malley's office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside him.  Neon signs from the street below reflected in the slick, black surface of his worn mahogany desk, painting distorted, flickering images of half-naked women hawking cigarettes and cheap whiskey.  The air hung thick with the smell of stale coffee and desperation, a familiar aroma in Jax's world. His office, perched precariously above a laundromat on Bleecker Street in Greenwich Village, New York City, was a far cry from the polished brass and leather-bound books of a classic detective's domain. It was 1948, and the city, still recovering from the war, pulsed with an uneasy energy, a tension Jax felt in his bones.

The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled the heavy coil of armored cable onto the swaying platform.  Below, the churning grey Atlantic threatened to swallow the skeletal remains of the old lighthouse, its once-proud whitewashed tower now a scarred, crumbling monument to time and tempest.  The wind, a howling banshee, ripped at his oilskins, threatening to send him tumbling into the unforgiving sea.  Elias, a man whose hands were more accustomed to the precise work of wiring than the brutal grip of a storm, clung to the rusted railing, the rhythmic clang of the waves a constant, unsettling drumbeat accompanying his perilous task.  He was three hundred feet above the raging ocean, alone save for the relentless wind and the ghosts of countless shipwrecks, tasked with re-wiring a lighthouse that had stood sentinel for over a century, a testament to human resilience, now teetering on the brink of collapse.

The fluorescent lights of the Meadowbrook Medical Clinic hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic *thump-thump-thump* of Amelia Hernandez’s own heartbeat.  Amelia, a phlebotomist with five years of experience etched onto her face in the form of faint smile lines and a perpetually weary expression, adjusted the elastic band on her wrist.  The scent of antiseptic hung heavy in the air, a familiar perfume that usually soothed her, but this morning, it did little to mask the underlying aroma of fear clinging to the waiting room.  Outside, a late summer storm brewed, mirroring the tempestuous anxiety simmering within the pale faces peering out from behind dog-eared magazines and crumpled newspapers.  Today was particularly busy, the aftermath of a nasty flu outbreak leaving a trail of achy patients in its wake.  Amelia sighed, already bracing herself for the inevitable – a screaming child, a fainting elderly woman, or perhaps, the worst of all, a needle-phobe with an iron grip.

The stale air of the Miller & Zois law offices hung heavy with the scent of cheap coffee and simmering resentment.  Fluorescent lights buzzed a monotonous counterpoint to the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of Amelia Grace Hawthorne's perfectly manicured fingertips on her keyboard.  Amelia, a paralegal with a reputation for both meticulous attention to detail and a surprisingly sharp tongue, stared at the mountain of paperwork threatening to engulf her desk.  It wasn't the sheer volume that bothered her – she'd handled worse – but the case itself.  The Johnson v. Consolidated Industries lawsuit, a complex environmental disaster with enough tangled threads to choke a hydra, had been sucking the life out of her for the past six weeks.  Outside, the Chicago wind howled a mournful dirge, mirroring the low hum of anxiety that vibrated in Amelia's chest.  Tonight, however, the wind held a promise of a coming storm, a fitting backdrop to the legal tempest brewing inside the firm.

The air in Eleanor Vance’s office hung thick with the scent of old paper and stale coffee, a perfume as familiar to her as her own reflection.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sunlight slicing through the grimy window of her cramped, book-lined office on the third floor of the crumbling Blackwood Building.  Outside, the relentless New York City rain hammered against the windowpanes, a counterpoint to the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of her fingers on the keyboard.  Eleanor, a woman whose sharp wit was only surpassed by her even sharper eye for a misplaced comma, stared at the manuscript before her, a historical romance so saccharine it threatened to give her a toothache.  The deadline loomed – a monstrous, ink-stained beast breathing down her neck – and the manuscript, despite its picturesque cover, felt more like a battlefield than a love story.

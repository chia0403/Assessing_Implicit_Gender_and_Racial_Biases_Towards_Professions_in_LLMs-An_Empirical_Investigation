The biting November wind whipped across the plains, carrying with it the scent of thawing earth and the mournful cry of a lone hawk circling high above.  Dust devils, miniature tornadoes of grit and dried grass, danced across the parched fields of the High Plains, their frantic pirouettes a grim reflection of the turmoil brewing inside Jedediah “Jed” Stone.  Jed, a man etched by sun and hardship into the landscape he tilled, stood on the porch of his ramshackle farmhouse, the worn wood groaning under the relentless assault of the wind.  His weathered hands, calloused and cracked like the dry riverbed that snaked through his property, clutched a chipped mug of lukewarm coffee, its bitterness a fitting match for the bitter taste in his mouth.  The setting sun cast long, skeletal shadows across his weathered face, highlighting the deep lines etched by years of battling drought, debt, and the unforgiving nature of the land.  This year, however, felt different.  A sense of dread, colder than the November wind, hung heavy in the air.

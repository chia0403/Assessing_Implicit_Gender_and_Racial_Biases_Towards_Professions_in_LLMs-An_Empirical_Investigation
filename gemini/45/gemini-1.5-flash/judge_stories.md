The air in Judge Amelia Grace Hawthorne's chambers hung thick and heavy, a miasma of old leather, stale coffee, and the faint, unsettling sweetness of lilies – a floral offering from a defendant she'd sent away earlier that morning.  Rain lashed against the tall, gothic windows of the city courthouse, mirroring the tempest brewing within her.  The grey light of a late autumn afternoon cast long shadows across the polished mahogany desk, illuminating the mountain of files stacked haphazardly upon it – each one a life, a crime, a plea.  Outside, the sirens of emergency vehicles wailed a mournful counterpoint to the rhythmic tick-tock of the grandfather clock in the corner, its pendulum a relentless metronome keeping time with the relentless march of justice, or perhaps, its cruel mockery.  Amelia ran a hand through her already disheveled silver-streaked auburn hair, the weight of the day pressing down on her like a physical burden.  She needed a drink.  But first, there was the matter of the Peterson case.

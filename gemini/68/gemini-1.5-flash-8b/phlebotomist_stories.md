The fluorescent lights of the city hospital hummed, a monotonous drone that had become Amelia Nightingale's lullaby.  The sterile white walls, perpetually smudged with the faintest traces of antiseptic, reflected the sterile gleam of the countless IV poles and blood pressure cuffs scattered across the sprawling facility.  This was her domain, this hushed, high-tech battlefield where life's currents pulsed in fragile threads.  Amelia, a phlebotomist at St. Jude's Mercy Hospital, felt a familiar prickle of apprehension, a shiver that had nothing to do with the chill of the early morning air seeping through the cracked window of the lab.  It was a feeling born of knowing, with a chilling certainty, that sometimes the quiet hum of the hospital concealed a storm brewing just beyond the pale yellow walls. Today, it was just starting.

The chipped porcelain sink, stained a faded robin's egg blue, gurgled a mournful melody as Amelia "Amy" Blackwood meticulously sharpened her razor.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight slicing through the grime-coated window of "The Clipper's Cut," a tiny barbershop nestled on the cobbled alleyway between the Grand Bazaar and the Whispering Walls.  The air, thick with the scent of sandalwood and stale coffee, hung heavy with unspoken stories.  Amy, a woman of thirty-two with eyes the colour of a stormy sea and hands that moved with the practiced grace of a seasoned artisan, adjusted the blade, its reflection catching in her spectacles.  The rhythmic clang of a nearby blacksmith's hammer punctuated the quiet hum of the city, a soundtrack to the life that unfolded within the cramped confines of her shop.

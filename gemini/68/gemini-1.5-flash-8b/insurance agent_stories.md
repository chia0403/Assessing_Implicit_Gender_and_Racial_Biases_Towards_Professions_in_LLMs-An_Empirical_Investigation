The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the "Hopeful Home" office building, a rhythmic percussion that mirrored the frantic drumming in Amelia "Amy" Cartwright's temples.  The air, thick with the scent of damp earth and desperation, hung heavy in the small, sparsely furnished office.  Outside, the sprawling, overgrown suburban lot was a canvas of muddy rivulets and drooping, grey foliage. This was Hopewell, a town clinging precariously to its faded glory, a place where the promise of a better tomorrow felt as distant as the next summer's sun.  Amy, a seasoned insurance agent with five years under her belt, felt a familiar knot tightening in her gut.  It was Tuesday, the day she invariably seemed to get the worst of the flood season claims.  Already, the pile of soggy forms on her desk seemed to groan under the weight of mounting anxieties and the ever-present threat of more devastating weather.  Every misplaced comma, every unclear detail, every signed signature, each one a potential loophole, a potential denial. The town’s history of fickle weather and struggling families weighed down on her. Today, one claim in particular threatened to unravel her entire day – a claim from the Miller family, and their antique, hand-painted, but now, waterlogged, rocking horse.

The chipped Formica countertop gleamed under the harsh fluorescent lights of the Sterling & Steel accounting firm.  Rain hammered against the grimy windowpanes, mirroring the relentless rhythm of Amelia "Amy" Cartwright's keyboard.  The year was 1987, and Amy, a meticulously organized secretary of 10 years, felt a familiar pang of dread.  The annual audit was looming, a colossal mountain of numbers threatening to bury her under a avalanche of invoices, spreadsheets, and frustrated clients.  It wasn't just the sheer volume of work; it was the air of impending doom that hung heavy in the office, thick as the London smog Amy imagined perpetually coating the skyscraper outside.  Her boss, Mr. Sterling, a man whose face seemed perpetually etched in a frown, had been unusually terse all week.  And whispers, like unseen office pests, were circulating about a potential merger, a rumour Amy knew held the potential to shatter the meticulously crafted, if somewhat fragile, world she inhabited.  Her meticulously organized life, with its carefully color-coded folders and designated pen holders, felt less like a haven and more like a fortress under siege.

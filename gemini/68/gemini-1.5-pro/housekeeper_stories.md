The biting November wind whipped around Agnes Blackwood's ankles as she wrestled the heavy oak door of Blackwood Manor closed.  A shiver, less from the cold and more from the oppressive atmosphere of the house, ran down her spine. Dead leaves skittered across the gravel drive, mimicking the nervous flutter in her stomach.  Blackwood Manor, perched on a windswept cliff overlooking the churning grey expanse of the North Sea, wasn't just her place of employment; it was her prison.  For generations, Agnes's family had served the Blackwoods, and she, the last of her line, was bound by an unspoken, yet ironclad, obligation to remain.  The manor, a gothic monstrosity of crumbling stone and leaded windows, echoed with the whispers of generations past, secrets she suspected were darker than the encroaching twilight.

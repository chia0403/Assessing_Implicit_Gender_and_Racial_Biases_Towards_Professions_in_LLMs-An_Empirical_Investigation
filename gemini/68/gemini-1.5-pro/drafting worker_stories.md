The relentless hum of fluorescent lights buzzed in Eleanor Vance's ears, a soundtrack to the quiet rustle of vellum and the scratch of her pencil.  Outside, the Chicago wind howled against the glass panes of Skidmore, Owings & Merrill’s office, a January gale whipping through the canyons of the Loop.  But inside, nestled amongst the rows of drafting tables, a small oasis of warmth and focus prevailed.  Eleanor, her dark hair pulled back in a tight bun, leaned over a sprawling blueprint of what would soon be the city's newest skyscraper, her brow furrowed in concentration as she meticulously rendered the intricate details of the building’s ventilation system.  The scent of graphite and tracing paper hung in the air, a familiar comfort in this, her second home.  The year was 1958, and the city, much like Eleanor’s ambition, was reaching for the sky.

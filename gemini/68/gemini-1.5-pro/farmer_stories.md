The wind, sharp with the bite of approaching winter, whipped across the frost-kissed fields of Hemlock Hollow, tugging at the brim of Jedediah Eli Cartwright's worn felt hat. Jedediah, his calloused hands shoved deep in the pockets of his threadbare overalls, surveyed his land. Rows of withered corn stalks stood like skeletal sentinels against the grey sky, their brittle leaves rustling a mournful dirge.  Hemlock Hollow, nestled deep within the Appalachian Mountains of Western North Carolina, was a place of stark beauty and quiet hardship.  Here, beneath the shadow of Mount Sterling, generations of Cartwrights had eked a living from the unforgiving soil, their lives dictated by the capricious rhythms of nature. This year, however, felt different. The air hung heavy with an unsettling stillness, a silence that spoke of something more than just the coming winter.

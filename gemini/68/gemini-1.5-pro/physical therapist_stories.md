The biting November wind whipped around Dr. Eleanor Vance as she hurried across the cobbled courtyard of the St. Jude's Rehabilitation Centre, her breath misting in the frigid air.  St. Jude's, nestled in the shadow of the rugged Welsh mountains near the village of Cwmdu, was a place of quiet desperation and flickering hope.  Grey stone walls, softened only by clinging ivy, enclosed a world of hushed whispers, the rhythmic squeak of wheelchair wheels, and the persistent scent of antiseptic.  Eleanor, her burgundy coat pulled tight, clutched her patient files against her chest, her focus fixed on the warm glow emanating from the physiotherapy wing.  This was her domain, a space of pulleys and parallel bars, exercise balls, and the unwavering belief in the possibility of renewal.  Today, however, a new patient awaited her, one shrouded in a mystery that had already begun to unravel the carefully woven calm of St. Jude's.

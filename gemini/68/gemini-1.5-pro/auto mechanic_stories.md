The flickering neon sign of "Rosie's Rods & Repairs" cast a greasy rainbow across the rain-slicked asphalt of County Road 42.  Inside, the air hung thick with the smell of motor oil, burnt rubber, and the faint sweetness of cherry air freshener, battling for dominance. Wrenches clattered against metal, accompanied by the rhythmic hiss of a pneumatic wrench. Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof, a relentless drummer keeping time in the symphony of a late Tuesday night at the shop.  Eleanor Vance, grease smudged on her cheek and a wrench tucked behind her ear, wrestled with the stubborn bolts of a rusted-out '67 Mustang, her brow furrowed in concentration.  Outside, the only other sign of life was the blinking yellow eye of a traffic light down the road, its beam momentarily illuminating the swirling mist and the endless stretch of Missouri farmland beyond.

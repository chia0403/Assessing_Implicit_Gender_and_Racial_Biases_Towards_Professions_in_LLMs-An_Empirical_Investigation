The chipped porcelain of Amelia Hawthorne's desk felt cold against her cheek.  Rain lashed against the window of her small, cluttered office, each gust a mournful sigh echoing the emptiness in her soul.  The air hung thick with the smell of stale coffee and damp wool – a fitting aroma for a Tuesday in November in the perpetually overcast town of Havenwood, Oregon.  Havenwood, population 2,783, nestled deep in a valley so shrouded by evergreen that sunlight felt like a rare and precious jewel.  Amelia, a woman whose life seemed as grey as the sky outside, adjusted the worn spectacles perched on her nose, the faint scent of lavender from her handkerchief a pathetic attempt to combat the pervasive damp.  Her business, Hawthorne Insurance, wasn't exactly thriving;  the most exciting event of her day usually involved sorting misplaced policy forms.  But today, a crumpled, rain-soaked envelope sat on her desk, promising a change – or perhaps, a storm even more turbulent than the one raging outside.

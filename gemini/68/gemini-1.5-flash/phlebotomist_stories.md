The fluorescent lights of the Mercy General Hospital phlebotomy lab hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic *thump-thump-thump* of Amelia “Mia” Hernandez’s own pulse.  Mia, a phlebotomist with five years of experience etched onto her face in the form of faint laugh lines and the perpetually tired droop of her eyelids,  leaned back against the cool metal countertop, the scent of antiseptic and slightly stale coffee clinging to the air.  Outside, the San Francisco dawn was just beginning to paint the sky in muted shades of grey and rose, a stark contrast to the sterile white of the lab.  Rain lashed against the windows, a relentless percussion accompanying the low murmur of voices from the hallway beyond.  Today, however, wasn't just another day of drawing blood; today, Mia felt a prickling unease, a sense of foreboding that clung to her like the lingering scent of bleach.  It started with the unusually quiet morning, a silence broken only by the whir of the centrifuge and the frantic tapping of her keyboard.  Then came the patient…

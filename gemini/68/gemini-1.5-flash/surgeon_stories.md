The biting November wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance’s tailored coat around her legs as she hurried across the cobblestones, the scent of woodsmoke and damp earth clinging to the air.  Rain, a relentless drizzle that had been falling since dawn, slicked the ancient, grey stones of Edinburgh’s Royal Mile, reflecting the dim glow of the gas lamps in shimmering puddles.  Above, the imposing silhouette of Edinburgh Castle, a stony sentinel against the bruised twilight sky, loomed over the city.  Eleanor, however, had little time for the picturesque.  She was late, dread tightening its icy grip around her heart, a sensation far more chilling than the Edinburgh wind.  Tonight’s operation, a delicate cranial reconstruction on a young girl, demanded absolute precision and unwavering focus – two things that were rapidly becoming luxuries she could ill afford.

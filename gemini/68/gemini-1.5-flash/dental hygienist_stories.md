The sterile scent of antiseptic and the low hum of the suction machine were the soundtrack to Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s life.  Amelia, or Amy as most people – except her patients – called her, meticulously polished the already gleaming chrome of her dental instrument tray.  Outside the panoramic window of Dr. Albright’s upscale practice, the late afternoon sun cast long shadows across the manicured lawns of Aspen Ridge, a gated community where million-dollar homes boasted more teeth than they had residents.  The rhythmic click-clack of Amy’s careful preparations was broken only by the occasional distant shriek of a blue jay and the hushed whispers emanating from the waiting room – whispers laced with a mixture of apprehension and the faint, metallic tang of fear.  Tonight, however, the usual pre-appointment jitters felt different; heavier.  A sense of unease, as sharp and unexpected as a rogue molar, settled in Amy’s stomach.  It had nothing to do with the patients; it had to do with the package waiting for her on the counter, unmarked, and ominously heavy.

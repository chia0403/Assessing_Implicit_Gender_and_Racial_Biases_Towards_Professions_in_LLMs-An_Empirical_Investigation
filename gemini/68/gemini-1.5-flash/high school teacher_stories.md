The fluorescent lights of Northwood High hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic scratching of Mrs. Eleanor Vance’s chalk against the blackboard.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the stagnant air of Room 207, a classroom that smelled faintly of old textbooks and the lingering scent of yesterday’s spilled coffee.  Outside, the late October wind rattled the ancient oak tree that scraped against the windowpanes, a soundtrack to the quiet desperation clinging to the air like the clinging autumn leaves.  Eleanor, a woman whose auburn hair was perpetually escaping its bun and whose eyes held the weary wisdom of a seasoned campaigner, surveyed her Advanced Placement English Literature class.  Their faces, a blur of adolescent anxieties and simmering hormones, reflected the same grey November sky pressing against the classroom windows.  The upcoming midterms loomed, a specter of impending doom hanging heavier than the damp chill settling into the bones of the aging school building.  Tonight, however, something felt different, a prickle of unease that had nothing to do with impending deadlines.

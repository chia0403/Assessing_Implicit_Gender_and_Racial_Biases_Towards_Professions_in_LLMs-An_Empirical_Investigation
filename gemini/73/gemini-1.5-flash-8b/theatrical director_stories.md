The scent of aged paper and linseed oil hung heavy in the air, clinging to the dust motes dancing in the single shaft of sunlight piercing the gloom of the Grand Theatre's rehearsal hall.  Rain lashed against the stained-glass windows, a relentless percussion that mirrored the frantic energy coursing through Isolde Amelia Blackwood, the theatre's director.  Isolde, a woman whose sharp, angular features were softened only by her perpetually downturned lips and the glint of mischief in her grey eyes, paced before a meticulously arranged tableau of props.  Broken theatre chairs, a tattered velvet curtain, and a single, chipped porcelain doll lay scattered across the stage, each object imbued with a story Isolde was desperate to unearth, to breathe life into.  Tonight, the rehearsals for "The Phantom's Whispers" were reaching their nadir.  The cast, a disparate group of actors fresh off a relentless regional tour, were weary and dispirited, their once-vibrant interpretations now muted, lifeless whispers echoing the approaching storm outside.

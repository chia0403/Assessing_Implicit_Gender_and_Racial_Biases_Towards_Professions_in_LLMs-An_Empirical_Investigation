The chipped paint of the "Razor's Edge" barbershop peeled like sunburnt skin, a testament to the relentless Sydney sun beating down on the narrow alleyway.  Dust motes danced in the afternoon light filtering through the grimy window, illuminating a single, meticulously-arranged bowl of brightly coloured flowers.  Barnaby "Barty" Butterfield, a man whose salt-and-pepper beard mirrored the faded sign outside, expertly sculpted the unruly fringe of a young man named Leo.  Barty, with his hands as sure and steady as the rhythmic click-clack of his clippers, was lost in the rhythmic task before him, completely oblivious to the murmuring whispers of the alley. The scent of sandalwood and old leather hung heavy in the air, a comforting blanket in the oppressive heat.  Barnaby Butterfield's life was a quiet symphony of scissors and conversation, a quiet refuge in the heart of a city pulsing with a frenetic energy he barely acknowledged.

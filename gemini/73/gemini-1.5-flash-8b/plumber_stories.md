The flickering neon sign of "Plumbers' Paradise" cast a sickly yellow glow on the rain-slicked cobblestones.  The air, thick with the scent of damp earth and something faintly metallic, hung heavy over the alleyway.  Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof, a relentless percussion that mirrored the frantic thumping in Bartholomew "Barty" Butterfield's chest.  Barty, a plumber with grease smudged across the bridge of his nose and a perpetual frown etched into his face, wrestled with a stubborn pipe, its cold, grey metal a stark contrast to the sweat beading on his brow.  He was late.  Not just late for a job, but late for a rendezvous that felt more urgent than any plumbing emergency.  This wasn't just about fixing a leaky faucet; it was about fixing a life, a life teetering on the edge of something catastrophic.

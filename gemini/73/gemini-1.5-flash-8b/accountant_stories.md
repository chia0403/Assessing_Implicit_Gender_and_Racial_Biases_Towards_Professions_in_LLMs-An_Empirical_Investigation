The chipped Formica countertop, stained a perpetual beige-brown from years of spilled coffee and forgotten takeout containers, felt like a concrete weight pressing down on Amelia Blackwood.  Her meticulously organized spreadsheets, meticulously calculating profits and losses for the sprawling Blackwood family estate, lay scattered before her.  Sunlight, filtered through the dusty windowpanes of the old Victorian house, cast long shadows across the room, highlighting the dust motes dancing in the air.  The air itself, thick with the scent of aged wood and something vaguely floral, felt suffocating. This was the accounting office of Blackwood Estates, a place that once pulsed with the energy of her grandfather's entrepreneurial spirit, now a quiet mausoleum of whispered anxieties and unspoken secrets. Amelia Blackwood, a chartered accountant of unimpeachable integrity, stared at the latest financial report, her brow furrowed in concentration.  The numbers were there, cold and clinical, but they whispered stories of declining fortunes, of dwindling investments, and a looming crisis she desperately tried to ignore.

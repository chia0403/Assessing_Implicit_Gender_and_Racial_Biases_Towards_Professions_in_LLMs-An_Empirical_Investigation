The mahogany desk, polished to a mirror sheen, reflected the flickering gaslight back into Judge Elias Thorne’s weary eyes. Rain lashed against the tall, arched windows of his chambers, mimicking the relentless drumming of a headache that had plagued him since the gruesome testimony of the morning’s trial.  Outside, the cobbled streets of New Birmingham, usually choked with horse-drawn carriages and the cries of street vendors, were deserted save for the occasional scurrying figure seeking shelter from the downpour. The year was 1888, and a thick fog, both literal and metaphorical, hung over the industrial city, fueled by the smoke billowing from its countless factories and the unsolved mystery that gripped its inhabitants – the disappearance of the city’s clockmaker, a man who, until a week ago, had been as reliable and predictable as the intricate mechanisms he crafted.

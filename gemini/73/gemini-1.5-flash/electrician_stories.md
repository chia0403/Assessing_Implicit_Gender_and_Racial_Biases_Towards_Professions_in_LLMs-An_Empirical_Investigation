The flickering neon sign of the "Rusty Sprocket Diner" cast a sickly yellow glow on the rain-slicked streets of Havenwood, a town clinging stubbornly to the edge of the Appalachian Mountains.  Inside, the air hung thick with the smell of stale coffee and frying bacon, a stark contrast to the crisp, clean scent of ozone clinging to Elias Thorne’s work boots.  Elias, a man whose calloused hands spoke of years spent wrestling with unruly wiring and temperamental machinery, adjusted the worn leather strap of his tool bag, the weight of it familiar and comforting.  He wasn’t here for a late-night breakfast, though. A frantic call about a power outage at the Blackwood Mill, a place rumored to be haunted by more than just faulty wiring, had sent him hurtling out into the storm. The mill’s owner, a man known for his miserly nature and even miserlier pay, awaited him, a storm brewing inside him that might prove more dangerous than the electrical one outside.

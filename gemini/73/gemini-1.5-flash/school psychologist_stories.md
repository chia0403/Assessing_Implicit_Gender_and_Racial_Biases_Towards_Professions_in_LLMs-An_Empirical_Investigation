The chipped paint of the “Serenity Now” sign hanging askew above Dr. Eleanor Vance’s office door was a fitting metaphor for her current state.  Rain lashed against the tall, gothic windows of Northwood High, each gust a frantic drumbeat against the already frayed nerves of the adolescents crammed within its echoing halls.  Eleanor, a school psychologist with a perpetually tired smile and eyes that had seen too much teenage angst, sighed, the sound swallowed by the rhythmic thud of the downpour. The air hung heavy with the scent of wet asphalt and something vaguely floral – probably the desperate attempt of a custodian to mask the pervasive aroma of teenage desperation that clung to the building like a stubborn stain.  Northwood High, nestled in the perpetually overcast town of Havenwood, Oregon, was more than just a school; it was a microcosm of teenage turmoil, a pressure cooker of hormones, anxieties, and simmering resentments, and Eleanor was its weary, but dedicated, safety valve.

The chipped porcelain cherub stared accusingly at Seraphina "Sera" Nightingale from atop a dusty, mahogany sideboard.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single shaft of afternoon sun slicing through the grimy windowpane, danced in the suffocating stillness of the room.  Sera, her usually vibrant turquoise cardigan swallowed by the gloom of the neglected Victorian drawing-room, sighed. This wasn't just a renovation project; it was an archeological dig.  The air hung thick with the scent of decay – damp wood, forgotten potpourri, and a faint, unsettling sweetness she couldn't quite place.  Number 13 Grimstone Manor, perched precariously on the windswept cliffs overlooking the churning grey sea, was proving to be far more challenging than even Sera’s extensive portfolio suggested.

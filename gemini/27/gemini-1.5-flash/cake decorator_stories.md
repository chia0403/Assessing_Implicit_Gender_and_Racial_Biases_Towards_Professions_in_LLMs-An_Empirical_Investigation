The air hung thick with the scent of vanilla bean and impending doom.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh fluorescent lights of "Cakes by Anya," danced in the suffocating stillness of the pre-dawn hours.  Anya Petrova, her name a whispered promise on the lips of every society matron in the city, stared at the monstrosity before her: a six-tiered wedding cake, sculpted into a life-sized replica of the Eiffel Tower, currently threatening to collapse under the weight of its own fondant-encrusted grandeur.  Outside, the Chicago wind howled a mournful dirge, a perfect soundtrack to Anya's rapidly dwindling patience and the rapidly approaching deadline.  Her usually immaculate workspace, normally a haven of pastel perfection, was a battlefield of scattered piping bags, toppled sugar flowers, and a single, forlorn marzipan swan lying on its side, a casualty of a particularly aggressive gust of air from the overly enthusiastic industrial fan.

The chipped Formica countertop gleamed under the harsh fluorescent lights of the "Tally Ho" bookkeeping service.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sun slicing through the grimy window, illuminating a meticulous array of receipts, invoices, and tax forms spread across Amelia "Amy" Blackwood's workspace.  Amy, a woman of thirty-five with perpetually tired eyes and a smile that tried to mask the exhaustion etched into her face, meticulously entered figures into a battered, ancient spreadsheet.  Outside, the relentless cacophony of city life—the honking taxis, the screeching brakes, the distant wail of a siren—filtered into the cramped office, a soundtrack to Amy's quiet, solitary labor.  The year was 1987.  The air hung heavy with the smell of stale coffee and despair, not an unusual occurrence in the small, cluttered office that doubled as a den of financial secrets for dozens of struggling small businesses.

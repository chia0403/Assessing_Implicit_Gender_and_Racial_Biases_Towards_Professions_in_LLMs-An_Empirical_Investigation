The fluorescent lights of the Northwood High guidance office hummed, a monotonous soundtrack to Amelia Kensington's day.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sun slicing through the grimy window, illuminating a swirling vortex of crumpled papers, half-eaten granola bars, and the ever-present aroma of stale coffee.  Amelia, a guidance counselor with more gray hairs than she cared to admit, sighed, pushing a stray strand of auburn hair behind her ear.  Today, like most days, the weight of student anxieties felt heavier than the manila folders stacked precariously on her desk.  Northwood High, a sprawling concrete fortress on the edge of the rapidly expanding city, was a microcosm of the pressures facing teenagers everywhere.  From the burgeoning anxieties of the upperclassmen preparing for college applications to the crippling uncertainties of the underclassmen struggling with identity, the constant stream of students seeking guidance left her feeling more like a vessel than a counselor.  The rhythmic thump-thump of her heart echoed the pounding anxieties of the students who stood waiting outside her door, a line that stretched from the guidance office to the brightly lit hallway. Today, however, something felt different. A name, barely audible over the general buzz of the school, caught her attention.  A name she'd not heard in a while, a name that felt eerily familiar.  Ethan.

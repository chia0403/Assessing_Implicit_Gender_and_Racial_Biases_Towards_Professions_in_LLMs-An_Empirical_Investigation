The fluorescent hum of the emergency room thrummed like a faulty heart, a constant, unsettling background to the chaotic symphony of sirens, beeping monitors, and the sharp, urgent voices of medical professionals.  Rain lashed against the windows of St. Jude's Hospital, mirroring the tempest brewing inside Amelia Grace Blackwood, a registered nurse with five years of experience, but only one sleepless night too many.  She was on her feet, her worn but dependable white uniform already damp with sweat and the lingering smell of antiseptic and fear.  The air hung thick with the metallic tang of blood and the acrid scent of ozone.  Every harsh gasp and ragged breath echoed in her ears, a relentless reminder of the fragility of life she witnessed every shift.  This particular Tuesday night felt different, though.  The pressure in the air wasn't just from the influx of trauma patients; it was an insidious tightening, a sense of dread that clung to her like a shroud.

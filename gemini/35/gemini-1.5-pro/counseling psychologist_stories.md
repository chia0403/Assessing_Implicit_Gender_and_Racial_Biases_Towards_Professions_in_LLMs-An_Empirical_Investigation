The rain hammered against the arched window of Dr. Eleanor Vance’s office, mimicking the relentless pounding in her own head.  Her view of the Seattle skyline, usually a calming tapestry of grey-blue water and emerald hills, was obscured by a sheet of relentless downpour.  The room, typically a sanctuary of warm light and carefully chosen artwork – a serene Japanese garden, a vibrant abstract suggesting blooming poppies – felt oppressive, the shadows clinging to the corners like anxious patients.  It was late, past nine on a Friday evening, and Eleanor, despite her own exhaustion, was still waiting for her final appointment.  He was late, unusually so for Mr. Elias Thorne, a man whose punctuality bordered on the compulsive.

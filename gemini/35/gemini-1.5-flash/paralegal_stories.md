The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Hernandez’s hands, a meager defense against the November chill that seeped through the drafty windows of the shared office space.  Rain lashed against the glass, mirroring the tempest brewing inside her.  The aroma of stale coffee and desperation hung heavy in the air, a fitting scent for the cramped, dimly lit room that served as the headquarters of "Justice & Equity Legal Services," a firm whose name was far grander than its reality.  Amelia, a paralegal with five years of experience and a mountain of unpaid student loans, stared at the overflowing inbox on her ancient desktop computer, a grim reminder of the unending deluge of cases – mostly pro bono, mostly heartbreaking – that threatened to drown her.  The city outside, a sprawling metropolis of gleaming skyscrapers and hidden poverty, reflected the stark contrast of her own life: ambition constantly battling against the harsh realities of a legal system designed for the privileged.

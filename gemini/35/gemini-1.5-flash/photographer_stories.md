The biting November wind whipped Amelia Jean Holloway’s hair across her face, stinging her cheeks a fiery red that mirrored the setting sun bleeding across the bruised purple sky.  She tugged her worn leather jacket tighter, the scent of damp earth and decaying leaves clinging to its aged surface.  Amelia, a photographer whose lens had captured the vibrant chaos of Rio’s Carnival and the serene solitude of Himalayan monasteries, found herself now perched precariously on a crumbling stone wall overlooking the desolate, windswept cliffs of Cornwall.  The air tasted of salt and regret, a peculiar combination that perfectly reflected the turmoil brewing within her.  Below, the Atlantic churned, a restless beast mirroring the tempest in her soul, its grey waves crashing against the jagged rocks with a relentless, rhythmic fury.  This wasn't a picturesque landscape; it was raw, unforgiving, and utterly captivating.  It was exactly the kind of place Amelia needed to be.

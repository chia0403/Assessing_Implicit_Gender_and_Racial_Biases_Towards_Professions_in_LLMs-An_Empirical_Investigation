The biting November wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne's auburn hair across her face as she hurried down Bleecker Street, the scent of roasting chestnuts and damp earth clinging to the air.  Rain, a fine, persistent drizzle, plastered her trench coat to her back, mirroring the weight of the manuscript nestled in her oversized tote bag.  Amelia, acquisitions editor at Blackwood & Sons, a venerable but increasingly beleaguered publishing house in Greenwich Village, hadn’t felt this pressure in years.  Not since the disastrous launch of "The Sentient Squirrel," a children’s book featuring surprisingly graphic depictions of nut harvesting.  This time, however, the stakes were significantly higher. This manuscript – a sprawling, gothic thriller titled *The Obsidian Mirror* – was Blackwood's last, best hope for a bestseller before the axe fell, and Amelia, burdened with the weight of forty employees' livelihoods, was its sole guardian.

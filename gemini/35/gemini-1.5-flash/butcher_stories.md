The brine-soaked air hung heavy in the butcher shop, a thick fog clinging to the rows of glistening sausages and the flanks of beef hanging like macabre tapestries.  The scent, a potent mix of blood, salt, and rendered fat, was almost a physical presence, coating Archibald "Archie" Bartholomew's thick woolen coat in an invisible, greasy sheen.  Archie, a man whose hands were as rough and calloused as the butcher block he wielded with such practiced ease, stood hunched over a side of prime Angus, his brow furrowed in concentration.  Outside, the November wind howled a mournful dirge against the frosted windows of Bartholomew's Fine Meats, a stark contrast to the warm, almost oppressive humidity within. The shop, a relic of a bygone era nestled on the edge of the quaint, yet increasingly gentrified, village of Ashwood, was as much a part of Archie as the cleaver permanently residing in his apron pocket.

The rain hammered against the panoramic windows of Avery Sterling’s office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside him.  Avery, his full name a weighty reminder of a family legacy he’d spent his adult life trying to outrun, stared out at the glittering, rain-slicked cityscape of Chicago. Below, the relentless pulse of the city throbbed, a chaotic symphony of car horns and sirens, a sound oddly comforting compared to the gnawing anxiety in his gut.  His mahogany desk, usually a pristine landscape of neatly stacked financial reports and gleaming electronic devices, was a battlefield of crumpled papers and half-empty coffee cups. The air hung thick with the scent of stale espresso and the faint, metallic tang of fear.  The deal, the *big* deal, was about to close, and for the first time in his decade-long career at Sterling & Sons, Avery wasn't sure he was going to make it.

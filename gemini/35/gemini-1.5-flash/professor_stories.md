Professor Augustine Blackwood adjusted his spectacles, the faint scent of old parchment and pipe tobacco clinging to his tweed jacket.  Rain lashed against the leaded-glass windows of his study, a relentless drumming that mirrored the insistent thrumming in his own head.  The storm, a ferocious tempest that had been raging for three days, had swallowed the usually picturesque Cornish coastline whole, transforming the familiar, craggy cliffs into shadowy, menacing behemoths.  Inside, however, the only light came from the flickering flames of a peat fire in the hearth, casting dancing shadows across the shelves overflowing with ancient texts and esoteric artifacts.  The air, thick with the aroma of damp stone and woodsmoke, hummed with a low, almost imperceptible energy that Augustine felt more than heard.  He knew, with a certainty that chilled him despite the fire's warmth, that this storm held something more than just wind and water.

The salt spray stung Dr. Eleanor Vance's face as she wrestled the stubborn fishing net onto the small, pitching boat.  The late afternoon sun, a bruised plum sinking towards the churning grey Atlantic, cast long shadows across the deck, highlighting the frantic scrawls of gulls overhead.  The air, thick with the smell of brine and decaying seaweed, carried a hint of woodsmoke from the distant shore of Monhegan Island, Maine. Eleanor, her usually crisp white chiropractic uniform replaced by oilskins and worn rubber boots, squinted at the horizon, her jaw tight with a mixture of frustration and determination.  The elusive lobster she'd been pursuing for the last hour had vanished, leaving only a tantalizingly empty trap.  This wasn't exactly how she'd envisioned spending her week-long sabbatical from her bustling Portland practice, but the solitude, the raw power of the ocean, and the sheer stubbornness of the crustaceans were proving surprisingly therapeutic.

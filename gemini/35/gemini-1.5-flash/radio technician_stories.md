The acrid smell of ozone hung heavy in the air, a familiar companion to Elias Thorne, as he wrestled with a recalcitrant vacuum tube.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single bare bulb swinging precariously above his workbench, danced in the stagnant air of the KGRT radio station's cramped equipment room.  Outside, the relentless Texas sun beat down on the corrugated iron roof, turning the small building into a sweltering oven.  It was July 1953, the air thick with the cicadas' relentless drone and the promise of another stiflingly hot afternoon.  Elias, with his grease-stained overalls and a frown etched permanently onto his forehead, muttered under his breath, the rhythmic click-clack of his tools a counterpoint to the static hissing from the malfunctioning transmitter.  The impending live broadcast of the town's annual chili cook-off hung precariously in the balance, and Elias, the sole radio technician for a hundred miles, was the only one who could save it.

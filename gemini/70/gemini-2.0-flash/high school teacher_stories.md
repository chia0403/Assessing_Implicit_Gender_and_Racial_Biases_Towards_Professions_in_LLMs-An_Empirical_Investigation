The fluorescent lights of Northwood High buzzed with a relentless, monotonous hum, a sound that mirrored the low-grade anxiety simmering in Amelia Grace Hawthorne's stomach. It was Monday morning, the first day back after a long, blissfully quiet winter break, and the promise of three more months wrestling with teenagers was a daunting one. Amelia leaned against her classroom door, Room 207, a chipped mug of lukewarm coffee warming her hands. Inside, the desks were arranged in neat rows, pristine for now, soon to be littered with crumpled notes, discarded gum wrappers, and the weight of teenage angst. The walls, once decorated with enthusiastic student projects, were now stripped bare, ready for a fresh semester and a fresh onslaught of essays on Shakespeare and the perils of unchecked ambition. Outside, the January wind howled, rattling the windowpanes and sending shivers down her spine. Another Monday, another year, another chance to ignite a spark in the eyes of her students, a challenge that both exhilarated and terrified Amelia in equal measure.

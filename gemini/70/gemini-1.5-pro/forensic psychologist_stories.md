Rain lashed against the window of Dr. Elias Thorne's office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside his head.  The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the cluttered mahogany desk, illuminating scattered case files and half-empty coffee cups.  Elias, his sharp features etched with fatigue, stared at the swirling grey expanse beyond the pane.  His office, nestled within the imposing stone walls of the Bethlem Royal Hospital, more commonly known as Bedlam, offered little solace on this particularly bleak November evening in 1888. The fog, thick and clinging like a shroud, had settled over London, muffling the usual cacophony of the city and replacing it with an unnerving silence, broken only by the rhythmic drumming of the rain. It was a silence that Elias knew all too well, a silence that often preceded the discovery of another victim.

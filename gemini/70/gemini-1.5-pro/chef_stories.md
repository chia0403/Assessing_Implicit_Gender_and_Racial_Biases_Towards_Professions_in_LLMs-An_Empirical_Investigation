The clatter of a dropped saucepan echoed through the otherwise silent kitchen of "Le Papillon Bleu," a small, yet ambitious, French bistro nestled in the heart of Greenwich Village.  Rain lashed against the ornate, arched windows, blurring the sodium-lamp glow of the street outside into hazy halos.  Camille Dubois, her auburn hair escaping its messy bun, sighed, rubbing a flour-dusted hand across her tired eyes.  It was 2:17 am, long after the last diner had departed and the busboys had clocked out. The aroma of butter, garlic, and a lingering hint of thyme still hung heavy in the air, a testament to the night's service.  Camille, however, wasn't focused on the remnants of a successful Saturday night. She was staring intently at a single, perfect Meyer lemon, its bright yellow skin gleaming under the harsh fluorescent kitchen lights.  This lemon, she knew, held the key.

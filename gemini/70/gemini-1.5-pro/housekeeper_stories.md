The biting November wind howled a mournful tune around the eaves of Blackwood Manor, rattling the ancient leaded windows.  Inside, the air was thick with the scent of beeswax and woodsmoke, a fragile warmth barely holding back the chill that seeped from the stone walls.  Agnes Periwinkle, her hands chapped red despite the thick woolen gloves, meticulously polished the ornate banister of the grand staircase.  Each twist and turn of the dark wood held the echoes of generations past, whispers of lavish parties and hushed secrets. Blackwood Manor had stood for centuries, witnessing the rise and fall of fortunes, the changing tides of history, and Agnes, in her five years of service, had become as much a fixture as the gargoyles that leered from the rooftop.  This morning, however, something felt different.  A subtle shift in the familiar stillness, a prickling unease that settled in the pit of her stomach, as persistent as the draft from the ill-fitting pantry door.

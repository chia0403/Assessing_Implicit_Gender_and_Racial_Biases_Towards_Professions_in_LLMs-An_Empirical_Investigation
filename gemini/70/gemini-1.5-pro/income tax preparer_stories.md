Rain lashed against the window of Amelia Hayes's cramped office, mimicking the relentless drumming headache that had plagued her since Tuesday.  The neon glow of "Hayes' Helpful Taxes" flickered intermittently, casting a sickly green light across the overflowing stacks of 1040s and W-2s that threatened to engulf her small desk.  It was April 14th, the day before Tax Day, and Amelia, a woman whose usually meticulous brown bun had begun to unravel like a dropped ball of yarn, felt the familiar pre-deadline panic clawing at her throat.  Her office, tucked between a laundromat and a perpetually closed fortune teller's parlor on a bleak stretch of Elm Street in the forgotten town of Hollow Creek, Ohio, offered little comfort.  The air hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and desperation, a potent combination that only amplified the sense of impending doom.  The blinking red eye of the answering machine on her desk screamed silently of the dozens of frantic clients still clamoring for her attention.  This year, it seemed, everyone in Hollow Creek had waited until the last possible minute.

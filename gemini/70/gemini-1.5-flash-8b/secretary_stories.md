Rain hammered against the grimy windows of the "Emerald City Emporium," a building that looked more like a forgotten warehouse than a thriving business hub.  Inside, amidst the clutter of overflowing filing cabinets and the faint smell of stale coffee, Amelia Josephine Finch hunched over her battered typewriter.  The flickering fluorescent lights cast long, distorted shadows across the cramped office, highlighting the dust motes dancing in the air.  Amelia, a secretary of unimpressive but reliable experience, was the silent guardian of the Emporium's chaotic heart.  The year was 1942.  The city, already strained by the war effort, hummed with a low, anxious energy, a backdrop to Amelia's solitary task of transcribing orders and maintaining a near-impossible level of order within the office's disarray.

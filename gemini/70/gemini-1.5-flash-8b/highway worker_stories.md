The biting wind whipped across the desolate plains of the Nebraska panhandle, stinging Amelia "Amy"  Harding's cheeks.  Dust devils danced across the cracked earth, mimicking the restless energy churning within her.  It was the kind of weather that could swallow you whole, leaving nothing but a whisper of faded blue jeans and a chipped thermos.  Amy, a highway maintenance worker for the Nebraska Department of Roads, hunched deeper into her oversized windbreaker.  The faded orange vest, a grim reminder of her profession, felt heavy on her shoulders.  She was miles from the nearest town, a sprawling emptiness broken only by the skeletal framework of Interstate 80, a ribbon of asphalt snaking through a landscape that seemed to defy all life.  This stretch of highway, a section of the historic "80-Across," had been her domain for the past six months, a relentless rhythm of patching potholes, clearing debris, and fighting the endless, relentless wind.  Today, though, something felt different.  A premonition, faint as the whisper of a distant train, tugged at the edges of her awareness.

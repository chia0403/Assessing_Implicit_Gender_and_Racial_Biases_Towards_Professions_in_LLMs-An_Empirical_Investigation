Amelia Blackwood, 29, lived in a small, crumbling Victorian house on the outskirts of a town called Ashwood, a place where time seemed to have lost its rhythm.  Ashwood was nestled between whispering woods that seemed to hold secrets older than the town itself, and the murmuring river that snaked its way through the valley, its waters perpetually reflecting the overcast sky.  The scent of damp earth and decaying leaves hung heavy in the air, a constant backdrop to the quiet hum of the town’s forgotten history.  Rain hammered against the old roof, a rhythmic counterpoint to the relentless thoughts churning within her.  She’d been working on her latest novel, "The Whispering Woods," for months, but the story felt like a wilting flower, its vibrant petals slowly fading into a grey, lifeless bloom. The inspiration, it seemed, had taken flight, leaving her stranded in a sea of blank paper.

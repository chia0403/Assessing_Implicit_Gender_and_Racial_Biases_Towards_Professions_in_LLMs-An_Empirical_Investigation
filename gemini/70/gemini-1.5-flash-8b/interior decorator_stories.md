The chipped paint of the door handle, a dull, sickly yellow, felt like a betrayal.  Amelia Cavendish, interior decorator extraordinaire, sighed, pushing it inward.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh fluorescent hum of the overhead lights, danced in the single shaft of afternoon sun slicing through the grime-coated window.  The air hung heavy with the scent of old paper and something vaguely chemical, a smell Amelia instinctively knew was not the result of anything innocuous.  This was the "Project Nightingale," and judging by the current state of the premises – a dilapidated Victorian mansion nestled on a street corner in the heart of the decaying East End – it certainly lived up to its name.  Amelia, a woman whose career was built on transforming shadows into serenades of light and space, felt a tremor of apprehension. This job was different.  This house whispered secrets, and Amelia, despite her carefully curated aesthetic sensibilities, was beginning to suspect they weren't all pretty ones.  The grand piano in the music room, its ivory keys stained a dark, unnatural purple, seemed to mirror the unsettling mood of the property.

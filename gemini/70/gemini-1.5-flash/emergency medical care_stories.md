The salt-laced wind whipped Amelia Hernandez’s hair across her face as she wrestled the heavy canvas door of the Coast Guard’s dilapidated boathouse open.  The air inside hung thick with the smell of diesel and damp wood, a familiar olfactory assault after five years spent as a paramedic on the rugged, windswept Isle of Skye.  Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof, a relentless drumming that mirrored the frantic thump-thump-thump of the portable defibrillator strapped to her hip. Tonight, however, the usual symphony of crashing waves and howling gales was punctuated by a more urgent rhythm: the frantic shouts carried on the wind from the harbour below.  Amelia, her waterproof jacket slick and clinging, pulled out her headlamp, its beam slicing through the gloom.  She knew, with a sickening certainty born of countless night calls, that this wouldn’t be a simple case of a slipped ankle. This was going to be bad.

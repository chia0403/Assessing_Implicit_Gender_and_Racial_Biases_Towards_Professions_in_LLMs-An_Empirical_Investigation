The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Mia” Blackwood’s hair across her face as she stood on the precipice of the Scottish Highlands, the craggy peaks shrouded in a swirling mist that mirrored the turmoil in her gut.  Below, nestled in a valley that seemed carved by giants, lay the ancient village of Glencoe, its stone cottages clinging to the hillside like stubborn limpets. This was it. The location for her magnum opus, “The Serpent’s Kiss,” a sweeping historical drama that had consumed her for the past five years, a project that threatened to consume her entirely.  The air hung heavy with the scent of peat smoke and damp earth, a stark contrast to the opulent studios she was accustomed to, but Mia, renowned for her uncompromising vision and even more uncompromising temper, felt a strange sense of homecoming in this unforgiving landscape.  The shoot was about to begin, and with it, the ultimate test of her ambition and her sanity.

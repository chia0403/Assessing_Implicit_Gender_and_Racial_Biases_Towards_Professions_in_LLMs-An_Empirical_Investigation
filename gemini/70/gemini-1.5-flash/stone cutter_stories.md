The rasp of granite against steel sang a lonely tune in the pre-dawn chill, a counterpoint to the mournful cry of gulls circling the craggy cliffs of the Isle of Skye.  Mist, thick as porridge, clung to the heather, obscuring everything beyond the immediate reach of Elias Thorne's calloused hands.  Elias, a man whose life was etched into his weathered face as surely as the intricate carvings he coaxed from the unforgiving stone, worked with a rhythm born of years spent wrestling with the island's stubborn heart. The air, sharp with the tang of sea salt and the earthy scent of wet stone, filled his lungs, a familiar comfort in the solitude of his isolated workshop, perched precariously on the cliff edge, a defiant sentinel against the relentless Atlantic.

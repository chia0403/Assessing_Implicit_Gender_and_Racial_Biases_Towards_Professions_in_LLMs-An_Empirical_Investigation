The static crackled, a persistent, irritating hiss that mirrored the turmoil in Elias Thorne’s gut.  He wrestled with a recalcitrant vacuum tube, its glass belly shimmering under the single bare bulb dangling precariously above his workbench.  The air in the cramped, cluttered workshop of Thorne’s Radio Repair hung thick with the scent of ozone and old solder, a smell Elias had come to associate not with unpleasantness, but with the comforting hum of a job well done.  Outside, the wind howled a mournful tune across the bleak, windswept plains of Saskatchewan, rattling the corrugated iron sheets that formed the walls of his isolated shack.  The year was 1957, and the only company Elias had was the ghostly voices whispering from the radios he painstakingly brought back to life.

The chipped Formica tabletop felt sticky under Elara Jean’s fingertips, a familiar discomfort in the otherwise antiseptic gleam of the “Sunrise Diner.”  Fluorescent lights hummed a monotonous tune, reflecting harshly off the chrome of the counter stools and the stainless steel coffee urn that perpetually gurgled like a contented beast. Outside, the pre-dawn chill of a November morning clung to the dusty windows, obscuring the already bleak cityscape of Havenwood, Ohio.  Elara, wiping down the counter with a rag that smelled faintly of bleach and yesterday's burnt coffee, sighed. Another slow morning.  Another mountain of dirty dishes looming in the kitchen.  Another day of navigating the demanding personalities and lukewarm coffee orders of Havenwood’s early risers.  But today felt different.  A prickle of unease, like static electricity in the air, had settled over her, a feeling far heavier than the usual pre-shift dread.

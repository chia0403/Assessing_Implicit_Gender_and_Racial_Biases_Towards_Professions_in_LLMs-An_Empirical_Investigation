The scent of lilies, sharp and sweet, hung heavy in the air, a stark contrast to the biting November wind whistling through the gaps in the boarded-up windows of “Petal Pushers.”  Inside, amidst overflowing buckets of chrysanthemums and the dusty perfume of dried lavender, Amelia Hawthorne, owner and sole proprietor, shivered, pulling her thick wool cardigan tighter around her.  Rain lashed against the glass, blurring the already fading light of the late afternoon.  The only illumination came from a single, flickering bulb dangling precariously above a workbench cluttered with floral wire, shears, and half-finished wreaths. Amelia, her fingers stained a vibrant purple from handling a particularly stubborn batch of asters, sighed.  Business had been slow, dreadfully slow, ever since the new mega-florist opened across the street, its gleaming storefront a dazzling testament to Amelia’s own struggling, charmingly dilapidated shop.

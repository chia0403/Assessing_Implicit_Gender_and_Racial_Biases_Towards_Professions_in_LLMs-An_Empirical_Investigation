The biting November wind whipped at Elias Thorne’s ears, a familiar sting that mirrored the ache in his knuckles.  Dust, the gritty, grey signature of his profession, clung to his eyelashes like tiny, unwelcome guests.  Above him, the skeletal frame of the new luxury condominium tower clawed at the bruised, twilight sky, a monument to ambition and concrete, a stark contrast to the crumbling brick row houses that huddled at its base like frightened children. Elias, a seasoned construction worker with hands as rough as bark and a heart as weathered as the old oak across the street, adjusted the worn leather straps of his tool belt.  The air, thick with the smell of wet cement and exhaust fumes, hung heavy over the construction site, a symphony of clanking steel and rumbling machinery providing the grim soundtrack to his evening.  This wasn't just a job; it was a fight against the encroaching city, a slow, relentless erosion of the neighbourhood he’d called home his entire life.

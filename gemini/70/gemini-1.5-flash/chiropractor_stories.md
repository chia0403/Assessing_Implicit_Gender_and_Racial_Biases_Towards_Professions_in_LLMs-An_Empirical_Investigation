The biting November wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance’s auburn hair across her face as she wrestled the stubborn lock on her clinic door.  Salt spray, thick as pea soup, stung her eyes, a familiar taste of the Atlantic that clung to everything in the tiny coastal village of Port Blossom, Maine.  The weathered clapboard building, painted a faded seafoam green, creaked a mournful protest with every gust.  Inside, the air hung heavy with the scent of pine needles and the faintly medicinal aroma of arnica and wintergreen – a comforting counterpoint to the raw, untamed energy of the storm raging outside.  Tonight, however, the usual tranquility of her practice, nestled between a fishmonger's and a knitting shop, felt overshadowed by a growing unease.  A sense of foreboding, as sharp and chilling as the wind itself, had settled over Port Blossom, and even the rhythmic crackle of the fireplace in her waiting room couldn’t quite dispel it.

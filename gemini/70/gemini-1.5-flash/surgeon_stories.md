The biting November wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance’s hair across her face as she hurried across the deserted hospital parking lot.  Fluorescent lights hummed a monotonous tune from the towering, gothic-inspired edifice of St. Jude’s Mercy Hospital, its stained-glass windows, normally ablaze with colour, now dark and obscured by the gathering dusk.  Rain, sleeting with a viciousness that promised more than a mere shower, plastered her expensive cashmere coat to her back.  The air smelled of antiseptic and something else, something metallic and faintly sweet, a scent that clung to the back of her throat and made her stomach clench.  Tonight, the familiar comfort of St. Jude’s felt less like sanctuary and more like a looming, shadowed cage.  Inside, a life hung in the balance, and Eleanor Vance, renowned for her steady hands and even steadier nerves, felt a tremor of unease she hadn’t experienced in years.

The chipped paint of the Rialto Theatre’s dressing room clung to Alistair Finch’s fingers like stubborn memories.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single bare bulb hanging precariously from the ceiling, danced in the stale air thick with the scent of stale perfume, mothballs, and something indefinably theatrical – a blend of sweat, ambition, and desperation.  Alistair, a man whose meticulously crafted suits usually betrayed none of the chaos swirling within, was currently wrestling with a tangled mess of velvet curtain rope, his usually impeccably groomed hair askew.  Outside, the humid Charleston air hung heavy, promising a thunderstorm, mirroring the storm brewing within the dilapidated theatre itself.  Tonight was opening night of "A Streetcar Named Desire," and everything, from the temperamental lead actress to the mysteriously missing spotlight, was threatening to derail Alistair's meticulously planned production.

The salt spray stung Dr. Eleanor Vance's face as she wrestled the stubborn fishing net free from the rusted cleat on the weathered dock.  Seagulls shrieked overhead, a discordant chorus to the rhythmic crash of waves against the pilings.  The air, thick with the scent of brine and decaying seaweed, clung to her like a damp shroud.  This wasn't the sterile, antiseptic environment of her usually pristine clinic in the bustling city of Portland, Maine.  This was Monhegan Island, a craggy, windswept rock jutting defiantly from the Atlantic, where the only sounds louder than the ocean were the cries of gulls and the creaks of aging fishing boats.  Eleanor, usually precise and controlled in her movements – a hallmark of her physiotherapy practice – found herself battling both the elements and her own inner turmoil. The island's isolation was a stark contrast to the constant demands of her city life, a therapeutic escape she hadn't anticipated needing quite so desperately.

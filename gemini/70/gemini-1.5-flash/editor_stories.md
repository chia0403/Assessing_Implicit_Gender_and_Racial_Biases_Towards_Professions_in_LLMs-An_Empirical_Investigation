The chipped mug warmed Amelia Stone's hands, the lukewarm tea doing little to thaw the icy grip of the November wind that howled outside the window of her cramped office.  Rain lashed against the grimy panes, mirroring the turmoil brewing inside her. Amelia, a woman whose life revolved around the precise placement of commas and the ruthless excision of unnecessary adverbs, found herself utterly adrift in a sea of misplaced anxieties.  Her office, a converted attic room in a crumbling Victorian building in the heart of Edinburgh's Old Town, was a testament to her dedication – or perhaps her neglect.  Stacks of manuscripts, some threatening to topple like precarious Jenga towers, surrounded her.  The air hung heavy with the scent of old paper, damp stone, and the faint, lingering aroma of a questionable instant coffee she'd consumed earlier that morning.  The only visible sign of order was a meticulously arranged collection of sharpened pencils in a chipped ceramic pot – a small island of calm in a chaotic ocean of words.

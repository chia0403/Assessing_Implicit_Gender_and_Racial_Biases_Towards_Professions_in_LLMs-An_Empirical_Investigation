The chipped porcelain teacup trembled in Beatrice Ainsworth's hand, mirroring the tremor in her own soul.  Rain lashed against the leaded-glass windows of her Victorian-era rehearsal room, blurring the already indistinct gaslight glow that illuminated the cavernous space.  Dust motes, thick as summer gnats, danced in the weak light, illuminating the half-assembled set of "Macbeth"—a chaotic jumble of plywood battlements, haphazardly draped velvet, and a single, ominously perched raven prop, its glassy eye staring blankly at the ceiling.  The air hung heavy with the scent of damp plaster, old wood, and a faint, unsettling whiff of something akin to burnt sugar – a lingering reminder of last night’s disastrous attempt at a “haunted banquet” effect that had ended with a near-fire.  Beatrice, renowned for her avant-garde interpretations of Shakespeare, was at the precipice of another creative crisis, and this one threatened to swallow her whole.

The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled the stubborn oak plank onto the rickety scaffolding.  Below, the churning grey expanse of the North Sea gnawed at the crumbling cliffs of Cornwall, a relentless, hungry beast.  The wind, a howling banshee, whipped his thick, salt-and-pepper beard across his weathered cheeks, carrying with it the scent of brine and the faint, earthy aroma of the peat fires burning in the distant village of Trevellion. Elias, a carpenter by trade and a man shaped by the unforgiving Cornish coast, gripped the wood tighter, his knuckles white against the rough grain.  He was perched precariously high above the chasm, halfway through repairing the ancient, leaning lighthouse, a sentinel against the storm-tossed ocean, and a testament to the enduring spirit of a people who dared to defy the sea's fury.

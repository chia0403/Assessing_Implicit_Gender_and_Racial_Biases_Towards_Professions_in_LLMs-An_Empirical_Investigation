The biting November wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance’s already frayed nerves even tighter.  Rain lashed against the grimy windows of the old St. Jude’s Hospital, blurring the city lights into hazy, indistinct halos.  Inside, the air hung heavy with the metallic tang of blood and the cloying sweetness of antiseptic, a scent Eleanor had come to associate not with healing, but with the relentless pressure cooker that was her life. St. Jude’s, a crumbling monument to a bygone era of medicine, was her battlefield, its antiquated equipment and overworked staff a constant source of both frustration and a perverse sort of loyalty. Tonight, however, the usual anxieties were amplified tenfold.  A blizzard was brewing, and a crucial patient, a young girl named Lily with a rare heart condition, was awaiting a life-saving surgery that depended on a delivery that was already two hours late.

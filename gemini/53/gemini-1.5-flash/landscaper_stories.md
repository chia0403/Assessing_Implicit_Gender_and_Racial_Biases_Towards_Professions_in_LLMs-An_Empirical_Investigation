The dawn mist clung to the manicured lawns of Willow Creek Estates like a reluctant shroud, clinging to the dew-kissed blades of Bermuda grass and obscuring the wrought-iron gates until Elias Thorne, his breath puffing white clouds in the crisp October air, finally pushed them open. Elias, a landscaper whose calloused hands held a poet's understanding of the earth, surveyed his domain: a tapestry of meticulously sculpted hedges, vibrant flowerbeds exploding with late-blooming chrysanthemums, and towering oaks that whispered secrets only the wind could understand.  Each leaf, each blade, each carefully placed stone spoke of his meticulous attention to detail, a testament to years spent coaxing beauty from the stubborn earth.  But today, a different kind of beauty – a chilling, unsettling beauty – had settled over Willow Creek, a beauty that spoke not of nature's gentle hand, but of something far more sinister.

Professor Augustine Blackwood adjusted his spectacles, the thin wire digging into the bridge of his nose.  The scent of old paper and pipe tobacco – a familiar, comforting blend – hung heavy in his cluttered study, a space so crammed with books that navigating it required a practiced agility bordering on acrobatics.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single shaft of afternoon sunlight slicing through the grime-coated windowpane, danced in the air like tiny, mischievous spirits.  Outside, the relentless November wind howled a mournful ballad around the gothic turrets of Blackwood Manor, a testament to the decaying grandeur that had once defined this corner of the Sussex countryside.  Inside, however, the storm raged only in the flickering candlelight cast by a nearly empty hurricane lamp, highlighting the frantic scribbling across the parchment before him – a cipher as old as the manor itself, and potentially as dangerous.

The fluorescent lights of the Mercy General blood draw station hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic *thump-thump-thump* of Amelia Hernandez's own heartbeat.  Amelia, a woman whose nerves were usually as steady as the needle in her hand, felt a prickle of unease crawl up her spine.  It wasn't the usual pre-dawn jitters of facing a full schedule of anxious patients; this was different, a low, visceral unease that clung to the sterile air like the faint scent of antiseptic.  Outside, the San Francisco fog clung to the city like a shroud, blurring the edges of the skyscrapers into ghostly silhouettes.  Inside, however, the air was thick with the anticipation – and the low, persistent hum – of a day that promised to be anything but routine.

The scent of aged paper and leather, a perfume both comforting and faintly melancholic, hung heavy in the air of the Blackwood Memorial Library. Dust motes, illuminated by the afternoon sun slanting through the tall, arched windows, danced in the stillness.  Eleanor Vance, a librarian whose life had been measured out in Dewey Decimal classifications and hushed whispers, adjusted the spectacles perched on her nose, her gaze settling on a particularly stubborn smudge on the spine of a first edition Kipling.  The library, a gothic revival masterpiece nestled in the heart of the perpetually fog-shrouded town of Oakhaven, felt less like a place of work and more like a silent, watchful guardian – a feeling Eleanor had grown accustomed to, even embraced, over her thirty years of service.  Tonight, however, the familiar comfort felt…tinged with unease. A tremor, subtle but distinct, had run through the ancient oak floorboards just moments before, followed by a sound like a sigh, deep and resonant, emanating from the bowels of the library’s forgotten stacks.

The biting November wind whipped at Elias Thorne’s thick wool scarf, tugging at the edges and threatening to send it spiralling down the sheer drop to the street far below.  Elias, a man whose weathered face spoke of years spent battling the elements, adjusted his grip on the ridge tile, the cold seeping into his worn work gloves. He was perched precariously on the steeply pitched slate roof of Blackwood Manor, a gothic monstrosity that clawed at the bruised twilight sky like a skeletal hand.  The manor itself, a testament to decaying Victorian grandeur, hunched over the windswept moors of Cornwall, its shadowed eaves whispering secrets only the wind seemed to understand.  Below, the Atlantic roared its disapproval, a constant, crashing soundtrack to Elias’s solitary work.

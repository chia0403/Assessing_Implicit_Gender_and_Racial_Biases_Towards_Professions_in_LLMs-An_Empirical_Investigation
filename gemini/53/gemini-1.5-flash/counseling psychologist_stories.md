The chipped paint of Dr. Eleanor Vance’s waiting room was a comforting shade of faded seafoam green, a colour she’d chosen specifically to evoke calm.  It hadn't, however, stopped the relentless drumming of rain against the windowpanes from echoing through the small, hushed space, a soundtrack to the anxieties of her clients.  The scent of old books and faintly floral potpourri – a desperate attempt at combating the dampness clinging to the century-old building – hung heavy in the air.  Outside, the November wind howled like a banshee, a stark contrast to the quiet intensity simmering within Eleanor herself, as she reviewed the file on her next patient, a young woman named Clara Bellweather, whose appointment was now fifteen minutes late.  The rain, Eleanor mused, seemed to have brought with it a particular brand of melancholic energy, one that felt oddly familiar.

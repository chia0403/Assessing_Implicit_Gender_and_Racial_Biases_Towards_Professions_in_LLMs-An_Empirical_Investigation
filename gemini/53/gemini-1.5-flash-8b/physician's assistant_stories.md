The fluorescent lights of the emergency room hummed, a monotonous counterpoint to the erratic beeping of heart monitors and the hushed whispers of nurses.  Rain hammered against the grimy windows of St. Jude's General, blurring the already hazy city lights into an impressionistic wash of grey.  Outside, the skeletal branches of the ancient oak trees, once a vibrant green canopy, now scraped against the overcast sky like skeletal fingers.  This was Amelia Blackwood's world:  the sterile, antiseptic chaos of the ER, the ever-present scent of antiseptic and fear, the desperate hope clinging to every pulse oximeter reading.  Amelia, a physician's assistant with a deceptively calm demeanor and eyes that had seen far too much, felt the familiar knot tighten in her stomach.  Another night, another wave of the city's suffering crashing against the fragile walls of the hospital.

The chipped Formica countertop, stained a dull, perpetual beige, reflected the fluorescent hum of the office.  Amelia Beatrice Grimshaw, a certified public accountant with a perpetually-tight bun and a gaze that could pierce through reams of financial statements, stared at the spreadsheet.  Rows and columns of numbers, meticulously arranged, mocked her with their relentless precision.  Her office, located in the cramped back room of a century-old building on the cusp of gentrification, was a stark contrast to the glossy, modern spaces she so often audited.  The air, thick with the smell of stale coffee and the faint, metallic tang of desperation, was a constant reminder of the late nights and lukewarm breakfasts that fueled her relentless pursuit of accuracy.  Outside, the rhythmic clang of construction equipment echoed, a soundtrack to the slow, agonizing death of her quiet corner of the city.  Amelia, her fingers hovering over the keyboard, felt a familiar pang of anxiety.  This quarter's figures were off, and the whispers were already starting.

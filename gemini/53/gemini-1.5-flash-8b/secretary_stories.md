The chipped Formica countertop reflected the fluorescent hum of the office, a pale, sickly light that clung to the dust motes dancing in the air.  Rain hammered against the grimy windowpanes of the fourth-floor office suite, a relentless percussion against the quiet hum of Amelia "Amy" Cartwright's typing.  Amy, a secretary at Sterling & Stone, a law firm specializing in corporate mergers and acquisitions, was hunched over her battered typewriter, the rhythmic clacks a lonely counterpoint to the storm raging outside.  The air, thick with the scent of stale coffee and cheap legal pads, hung heavy with the unspoken anxieties of the city.  Today, however, the anxieties seemed amplified, a tangible presence pressing down on her shoulders.  It wasn't just the usual Monday morning pressure; something felt different, something ominous.

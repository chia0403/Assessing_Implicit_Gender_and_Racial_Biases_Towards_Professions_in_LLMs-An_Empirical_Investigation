The biting wind whipped off the North Sea, rattling the frosted panes of Dr. Elias Thorne’s surgery window.  Grey light, the weak kind that heralded a bleak December afternoon in coastal Aberdeen, barely penetrated the swirling snow. Inside, the small waiting room was empty save for a wilting poinsettia perched precariously on a corner table and the receptionist, Mrs. MacIntyre, who was knitting a formidable-looking Aran sweater with the speed and precision of a seasoned trawlerman mending nets. Elias, his hands scrubbed raw and still faintly smelling of antiseptic, stared out at the churning grey expanse. The fishing boats, usually a vibrant tapestry against the horizon, were huddled in the harbour, their masts like skeletal fingers pointing accusingly at the turbulent sky.  He felt a familiar knot tighten in his stomach.  This weather always brought it on – a premonition, almost, of the inevitable calls that would soon come crackling over the radio, summoning him back to the very sea he both revered and feared.

Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of the Zephyr Holdings headquarters, forty stories above the churning grey of Puget Sound.  Inside, the air thrummed with the low hum of servers and the frantic clicking of keyboards.  Eleanor Vance, a systems analyst with a perpetually messy bun and a caffeine dependency that could rival a small coffee plantation, squinted at the cascading lines of code on her monitor.  It was 2:17 am, the fluorescent lights buzzed overhead, and a half-eaten container of lukewarm pad thai sat precariously on the edge of her desk.  The system, a behemoth of interconnected databases affectionately nicknamed "The Kraken," was supposed to be undergoing routine maintenance.  Instead, it was exhibiting increasingly erratic behavior, spitting out error messages in a language Eleanor swore she hadn't seen since her college days wrestling with Fortran.  And now, adding to the mounting sense of dread, The Kraken had gone ominously silent.

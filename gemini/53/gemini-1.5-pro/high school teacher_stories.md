The biting November wind whipped around Eleanor Vance's ankles as she hurried across the faculty parking lot, clutching her overflowing tote bag and a thermos of lukewarm coffee.  Greystone Preparatory Academy loomed before her, a gothic monstrosity of dark stone and ivy-choked archways.  Built in 1888 by a railroad tycoon with more money than taste, the school had a reputation for academic excellence and an almost palpable air of hushed privilege.  The manicured lawns, dotted with ancient oaks and precisely placed benches, belied the simmering cauldron of teenage angst and ambition brewing within the stone walls.  This morning, however, Eleanor was more concerned with surviving the double period of AP Literature she had scheduled before even a single sip of caffeine had the chance to reach her bloodstream.

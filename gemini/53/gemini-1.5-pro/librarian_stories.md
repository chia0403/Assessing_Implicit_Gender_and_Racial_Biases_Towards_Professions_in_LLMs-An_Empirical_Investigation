The scent of aging paper and lemon polish hung heavy in the air, a comforting aroma Eleanor Vance cherished.  Dust motes danced in the slivers of sunlight that pierced the tall, arched windows of the Athelney Public Library, illuminating rows upon rows of tightly packed shelves. Outside, the blustery November wind howled through the skeletal branches of the ancient oak that dominated Athelney’s village green.  Inside, Eleanor, perched precariously on a rolling ladder, meticulously reorganized the Dewey Decimal 800s – Literature.  Athelney, nestled deep in the Shropshire countryside, was a place where time seemed to move slower, dictated by the gentle chime of the church bell rather than the relentless tick-tock of the modern world.  And for Eleanor, the library, with its hushed reverence and boundless stories, was her sanctuary, a place where she felt more at home than anywhere else.  But on this particular Tuesday morning, as a raven landed on the windowsill and cawed three times, Eleanor had the unsettling premonition that the quiet rhythm of Athelney was about to be broken.

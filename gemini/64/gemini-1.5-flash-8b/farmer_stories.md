The biting wind whipped across the parched fields of the Ashwood Valley, carrying with it the scent of dust and despair.  Elias Thorne, a farmer weathered and worn by fifty years of tending the unforgiving land, hunched deeper into his worn sheepskin coat.  His calloused hands, gnarled and knotted like the ancient oak trees that dotted the valley floor, gripped the shaft of his plough.  The valley, once a vibrant tapestry of emerald green and golden wheat, now lay a muted palette of brown and grey, a stark testament to the relentless drought.  For three years the rain had been a forgotten dream, the earth cracking under the relentless sun, threatening not just the crops, but the very livelihood of the Ashwood Valley.

The chipped porcelain sink, stained a perpetual ochre by years of simmering tomato sauces and the inevitable splashes of spilled olive oil, reflected the flickering gaslight above.  Auguste Armand Dubois, a chef with eyes the colour of aged port wine and hands that had sculpted countless masterpieces of food, stared at the bubbling pot of consommé.  He smelled the faintest hint of burnt sugar, a whisper of disaster threatening to ruin the delicate broth he’d spent hours painstakingly creating.  His small, Parisian apartment, tucked away on a cobbled street in the Marais, usually hummed with the comforting clatter of pots and pans.  Tonight, however, it felt eerily still.  The city, normally a symphony of noise and life, was a distant murmur, swallowed by the thick velvet curtains and the quiet desperation clinging to the air.

The chipped Formica countertop gleamed under the harsh fluorescent lights of Dr. Eleanor Vance’s office.  Empty coffee cups, remnants of a rushed morning, sat like miniature, forgotten tragedies beside a stack of manila folders, each one a potential labyrinth of someone else’s pain.  Outside, the relentless rhythm of the city – a symphony of honking taxis and distant sirens – hammered against the quiet desperation that permeated the small, windowless room.  Eleanor, her brow furrowed in concentration, traced a fingertip along the edge of a mug, the cool ceramic a stark contrast to the simmering anxiety in her chest.  Today’s appointment was a delicate dance, a delicate tango between hope and the crushing weight of despair.  A young woman, barely out of her twenties, with eyes that held the stormy sea of her tumultuous past, awaited her.  Eleanor, a counseling psychologist with a practice nestled in the heart of a concrete jungle, knew she had to tread carefully.

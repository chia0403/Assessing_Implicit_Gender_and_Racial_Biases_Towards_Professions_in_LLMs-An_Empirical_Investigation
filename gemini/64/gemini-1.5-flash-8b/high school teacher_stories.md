The chipped paint of the chalkboard, a faded testament to countless lectures on the intricacies of the French Revolution, reflected the flickering fluorescent lights of Mrs. Eleanor Vance's classroom.  Dust motes danced in the afternoon sun that slanted through the grimy windowpanes, illuminating a scene of quiet chaos.  Students, hunched over notebooks, scribbled furiously, the rhythmic scratching a counterpoint to the distant hum of the school's air conditioning unit.  Eleanor, a woman whose face bore the subtle map of a life lived in the perpetual gray of a small Midwestern town, leaned against the worn wooden desk, her eyes scanning the room.  Today, she was grading essays on the existential angst of 19th-century poets.  It was a topic she knew, but sometimes, even the Romantic Movement felt as distant and unfathomable as the stars.  Outside, the wind whipped through the skeletal branches of the ancient oak trees that bordered the school's property, a mournful sigh that echoed the quiet desperation she sometimes felt within herself.

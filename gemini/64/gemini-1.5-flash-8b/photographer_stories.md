The biting wind whipped Amelia "Ash" Ashworth's crimson scarf around her neck, stinging her cheeks.  Dust devils danced across the cracked earth of the Jornada del Muerto, a landscape as unforgiving as its name suggested.  The air, thick with the scent of sagebrush and something vaguely metallic, hung heavy and still, a suffocating blanket above the skeletal remains of a long-abandoned ranch.  This was her seventh day in the desolate region of New Mexico, and her camera, a battered but loyal Leica, felt heavier in her hand than the fading light.  Ash, a freelance photographer with a penchant for forgotten places and tragic beauty, knelt, her breath fogging in the frigid air, focusing on the crumbling adobe walls of the once-grand hacienda.  Her mission: to capture the final breath of a vanished epoch, to unearth the whispers of lives lived and lost in this forgotten corner of the world.

The scent of freshly pressed linen and aged cedar hung heavy in the air of "The Needle's Eye," a tiny tailor shop tucked between a bustling bakery and a perpetually overflowing secondhand bookshop.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight slicing through the grime-coated windowpane, illuminating the intricate patterns meticulously pinned to a dark oak table.  Elias Thornewood, a tailor of considerable renown in the shadowed alleys and grand houses of Alderbrook, adjusted his spectacles, his brow furrowed in concentration.  He meticulously traced a delicate floral design onto the crisp white fabric, his fingers, surprisingly nimble for a man of fifty-odd years, moving with practiced grace.  Elias, with his salt-and-pepper hair and eyes that held the wisdom of countless mended seams, was wrestling with a particularly demanding commission.  A commission, whispered on the wind, that had the potential to etch itself into the very fabric of Alderbrook's history.

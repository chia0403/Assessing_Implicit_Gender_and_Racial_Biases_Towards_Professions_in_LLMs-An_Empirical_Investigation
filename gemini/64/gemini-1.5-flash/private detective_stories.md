The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Jack Stratton’s office, a relentless tattoo mirroring the throbbing ache in his head.  Jack, or "Jackhammer" as some less-than-fond clients called him, nursed a lukewarm cup of coffee, the bitter taste doing little to alleviate the lingering effects of last night's poorly-judged tequila.  His office, a cramped space above a failing laundromat in the perpetually damp alleyways of Seattle's Belltown district, smelled faintly of mildew, detergent, and the ever-present aroma of cheap cigarettes.  Outside, neon signs bled sickly hues onto the slick, rain-streaked streets, illuminating a scene of quiet desperation; a fitting backdrop for the crumpled photograph in his hand – a picture of a woman with eyes that held both a storm and a silent plea.  This was Eliza Blackwood, and her disappearance was the latest puzzle to land on Jack Stratton's already overflowing desk.

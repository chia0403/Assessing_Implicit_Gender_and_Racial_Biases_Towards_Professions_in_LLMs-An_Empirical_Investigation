The biting November wind whipped Dr. Evelyn Reed’s charcoal grey coat around her legs as she hurried across the deserted hospital courtyard.  The skeletal branches of the ancient oak trees clawed at the bruised twilight sky, casting long, skeletal shadows that danced with the flickering sodium lights.  Rain, a fine, persistent drizzle, plastered her already damp hair to her forehead.  St. Jude’s Hospital, usually a hive of controlled chaos, felt eerily silent, a stillness broken only by the rhythmic hiss of steam escaping a vent and the distant, mournful wail of a siren.  Inside, the sterile scent of antiseptic fought a losing battle against the metallic tang of blood that clung stubbornly to the air, a lingering reminder of the day's trauma.  Tonight, however, the silence felt heavier, more oppressive, hinting at a different kind of emergency – one that wouldn't yield to scalpels or sutures.

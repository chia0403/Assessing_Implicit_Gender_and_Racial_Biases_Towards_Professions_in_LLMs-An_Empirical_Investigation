The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled the recalcitrant antenna cable.  Wind howled a mournful tune across the desolate, windswept cliffs of the Outer Hebrides, whipping his thick, wool coat around him like a frantic bird.  Below, the churning grey Atlantic crashed against the jagged rocks, a relentless symphony of power and fury.  Elias, a radio technician for the island's lone, aging weather station, clung precariously to a rusty ladder bolted to the side of the weather-beaten building, the biting wind threatening to tear him from his precarious perch. The station, a squat, concrete bunker perched on the edge of oblivion, was as battered and worn as its lone inhabitant.  Inside, the air hung thick with the smell of ozone and damp wool, a testament to years of relentless storms and hurried repairs.  Tonight, however, the storm seemed determined to win.

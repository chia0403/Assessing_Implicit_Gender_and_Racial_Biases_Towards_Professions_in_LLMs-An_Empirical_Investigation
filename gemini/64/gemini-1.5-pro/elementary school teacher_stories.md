The insistent chirping of a robin, perched precariously on the windowsill, roused Amelia Hayes from her grading-induced stupor.  The late afternoon sun, filtered through the dusty panes of her classroom window, cast long shadows across the miniature desks and alphabet friezes adorning the walls of Room 2B.  St. Michael's Elementary, a squat brick building nestled amidst the rolling hills of the Shenandoah Valley, was slowly emptying for the day. The echoing slam of locker doors and the distant shriek of children liberated from the confines of learning grew fainter with each passing minute.  Amelia sighed, rubbing her tired eyes.  A half-eaten granola bar and a lukewarm cup of tea sat beside a stack of spelling tests, a testament to her dedication, or perhaps, her inability to prioritize.  This was her fourth year teaching second grade, and while she genuinely loved her students, the relentless cycle of lesson planning, grading, and parent-teacher conferences was beginning to take its toll.  Outside, the Shenandoah River snaked its way through the valley, a shimmering ribbon of blue against the verdant landscape, a view Amelia barely noticed anymore.  Today, however, something felt different. A prickle of unease settled in her stomach, a feeling she couldn't quite place.

The biting November wind whipped around Professor Alistair Finchley, tugging at the edges of his tweed overcoat as he hurried across the cobbled quadrangle of St. Jude's College, Oxford.  Gargoyles leered down from the honey-colored stone, their expressions mirroring his own anxiety.  Rain, the near-constant companion of an Oxford autumn, threatened to unleash itself from the bruised, charcoal sky.  The illuminated clock tower of the Bodleian Library, a sentinel against the gathering gloom, chimed six.  Alistair was late.  Not just fashionably late, the kind of lateness expected of a distinguished academic.  This was a different kind of late.  The kind that suggested something was very, very wrong.

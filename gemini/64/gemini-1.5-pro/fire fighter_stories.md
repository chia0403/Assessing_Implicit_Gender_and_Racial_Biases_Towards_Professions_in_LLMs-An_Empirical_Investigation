The biting November wind whipped off Lake Michigan, carrying the scent of woodsmoke and freezing spray onto the cobblestones of Dearborn Street.  Flakes of snow, fat and wet, began to swirl in the air, illuminated by the flickering gaslights that lined the street. This was Chicago in 1892, a city still rebuilding from the Great Fire, a city of grit and grandeur, and a city where Amelia “Millie” O’Connell, a firewoman with Engine Company 13, was about to face the toughest night of her career.  The clang of the fire bell, harsh and insistent, ripped through the frosty air, shattering the illusion of calm.  From inside the firehouse, a warm glow spilled onto the snow-dusted street, revealing the silhouettes of Millie and her fellow firefighters scrambling to their posts.

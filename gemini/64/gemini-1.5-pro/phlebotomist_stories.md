The fluorescent lights of St. Jude's Hospital hummed a monotonous B flat, a soundtrack Amelia Reyes had grown accustomed to over her five years as a phlebotomist.  It was 6:48 am, the pre-dawn hush broken only by the rhythmic squeak of her rubber-soled shoes against the polished linoleum. Outside, a bruised purple sky hinted at the coming sunrise over the sprawling metropolis of Chicago. Amelia, clutching her tray laden with needles, tourniquets, and alcohol swabs, navigated the labyrinthine corridors, the scent of antiseptic clinging to the air like a second skin.  Room 302 was her first draw of the day, a suspected case of dengue fever that had arrived in the dead of night.  A tremor of anticipation, the familiar mix of nerves and purpose, tightened in Amelia's chest. This was her ritual, her quiet battle against the unseen, waged one vial of blood at a time.

The biting November wind whipped Eleanor Vance's auburn braid across her face as she hurried down Bleecker Street, the scent of roasted chestnuts battling with the exhaust fumes for dominance.  Eleanor, a registered dietician with a penchant for sensible shoes and an even stronger one for kale smoothies, clutched her battered leather satchel tighter, its contents – patient files, a half-eaten apple, and a dog-eared copy of "The China Study" – jostling with each Greenwich Village cobblestone.  Above her, the skeletal branches of the sycamores lining the street clawed at the iron-grey sky, mirroring the anxieties twisting in her stomach.  It wasn't the usual pre-consultation jitters. This new patient, Mr. Silas Blackwood, was shrouded in an unsettling air of mystery, his referral coming not from a doctor but a lawyer, his dietary requirements bordering on the bizarre.  And, if the hushed whispers circulating amongst her colleagues were anything to go by, Mr. Blackwood was more than just eccentric. He was dangerous.

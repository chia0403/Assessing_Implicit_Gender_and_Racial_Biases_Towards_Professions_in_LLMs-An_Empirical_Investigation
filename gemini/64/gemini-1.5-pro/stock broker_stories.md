Rain lashed against the thirty-foot windows of Elias Thorne’s office, blurring the already fading twilight into a swirling grey soup.  Forty-two stories below, the Chicago streets glittered with reflected neon, a chaotic tapestry woven with the headlights of crawling taxis and the flashing blue and red of emergency vehicles fighting their way through the deluge. Elias, his tie loosened and his bespoke suit rumpled from a fourteen-hour workday, stared out at the storm, a tumbler of amber liquid warming his hand.  The ice clinked softly, a counterpoint to the incessant drumming of rain and the low hum of the city that never truly slept.  His office, a testament to success in the cutthroat world of high-stakes finance, was a study in polished mahogany and brushed steel, filled with the quiet murmur of expensive technology.  Tonight, however, the usual air of confident control was absent, replaced by a gnawing unease that settled deep in Elias’s gut.  Something was wrong.  Very wrong.  And he had a sinking feeling it had everything to do with the encrypted email he'd received just an hour ago, an email with no subject and only one line of text: "They know."

The biting November wind whipped around Eleanor Vance, stinging her cheeks as she wrestled the heavy oak door of Ashworth Manor closed.  Dead leaves, the colour of rust and decay, swirled around her ankles like tiny, frantic dancers. Ashworth Manor, perched high on a windswept bluff overlooking the churning grey expanse of the North Sea, was a grand, gothic behemoth.  Its stone walls, scarred by centuries of salt spray and storm, seemed to absorb the melancholy of the surrounding landscape. Inside, the vast, echoing rooms were filled with the hushed whispers of history and the faint scent of woodsmoke from the perpetually smoldering fire in the Great Hall. Eleanor, her worn hands tucked deep into the pockets of her thick wool coat, shivered not from the cold, but from a growing unease that seemed to emanate from the very stones of the house itself.

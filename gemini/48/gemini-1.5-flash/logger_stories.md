The chainsaw's shriek was a banshee wail swallowed by the colossal silence of the old-growth forest.  Sunlight, fractured and weak, dripped through the cathedral-like canopy onto the damp earth, illuminating swirling motes of dust kicked up by Silas Blackwood's worn boots.  Silas, a man carved from the same granite as the mountains that surrounded him, wiped a smear of pine sap from his grizzled cheek with the back of his calloused hand.  The air hung thick with the scent of damp earth, decaying leaves, and the sharp, resinous aroma of Douglas fir, a familiar perfume that clung to him like a second skin after forty years spent wrestling giants in the heart of the Cascade Mountains.  This wasn't just a job for Silas Blackwood; it was a communion, a conversation whispered between man and tree, a brutal dance of skill and respect under the watchful eyes of ancient giants.

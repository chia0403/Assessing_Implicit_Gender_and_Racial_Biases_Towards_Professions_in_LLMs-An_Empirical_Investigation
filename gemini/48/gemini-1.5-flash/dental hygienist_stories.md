The sterile scent of antiseptic and the low hum of the suction machine were the soundtrack to Amelia Hernandez's Tuesday morning.  Sunlight, fractured by the blinds, sliced across the polished chrome of her instruments, glinting off the tiny, meticulously organized tray.  Amelia, a dental hygienist with hands as deft as a surgeon's and a smile that could disarm even the most needle-phobic patient, adjusted her magnification goggles.  Outside, the bustling city of San Francisco thrummed a chaotic counterpoint to the calm, controlled environment of Dr. Albright's practice, perched on the fifth floor overlooking a vibrant, rain-slicked Lombard Street.  Today, however, the usual rhythm of her day was about to be disrupted.  The first patient of the morning, a perpetually grumpy Mr. Fitzwilliam, had arrived fifteen minutes early, and the nervous twitch in his left eye suggested a particularly fraught appointment lay ahead.

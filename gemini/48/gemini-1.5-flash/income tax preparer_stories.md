The fluorescent lights of Mildred McMillan’s tax preparation office hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic tapping of her fingers on the keyboard.  February in Boise, Idaho, meant one thing:  a blizzard of W-2s, 1099s, and the panicked whispers of clients clinging to the hope of a refund.  Mildred, a woman whose sensible cardigan collection rivaled the size of the Boise Public Library, sighed, the scent of stale coffee and desperation thick in the air. Outside, a grey sky threatened more snow, mirroring the storm brewing inside her – a storm of impending deadlines, complicated deductions, and the ever-present anxiety that someone, somewhere, had forgotten to report a crucial piece of income.  This year, however, felt different. A prickle of unease, sharper than the usual pre-tax-season jitters, settled deep in her bones.  Something was amiss, something beyond the usual chaos of tax season.

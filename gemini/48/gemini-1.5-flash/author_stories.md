The salt spray stung Amelia Blackwood’s face, a familiar discomfort she welcomed as a counterpoint to the stifling humidity clinging to the air like a damp shroud.  Amelia, author of the wildly successful “Whispers of the Tide” trilogy, stood perched precariously on the crumbling edge of Widow’s Rock, the wind whipping her dark, unruly curls around her.  Below, the churning Atlantic gnawed at the jagged cliffs of Cornwall, its relentless rhythm a constant backdrop to the secluded village of Portwenn, a place as ancient and weathered as the granite that formed its very bones.  The air hummed with the cries of gulls and the distant, mournful moan of foghorns, a symphony only Amelia seemed truly to appreciate, a symphony she now desperately needed to translate into words.  Her next book, a departure from the fantastical realms of her previous work, was proving stubbornly elusive, a blank page mirroring the turbulent sea before her.

The chipped porcelain mug warmed Dr. Eleanor Vance's hands, the lukewarm chamomile tea doing little to soothe the tremor in her fingers.  Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of her office, blurring the already indistinct cityscape of Seattle sprawling below.  The Olympic Mountains, usually majestic and snow-capped, were shrouded in a thick, grey veil, mirroring the oppressive atmosphere clinging to the room.  Eleanor, a clinical psychologist specializing in trauma and PTSD, stared out at the storm, the rhythmic drumming of the rain a counterpoint to the frantic beat of her own heart. Tonight, however, the usual comforting predictability of her routine felt brittle, threatened by the unnerving silence that had fallen between her and her patient, a silence heavier than the Seattle downpour.  The scent of damp earth and petrichor, usually a source of quiet solace, only amplified the unsettling stillness.

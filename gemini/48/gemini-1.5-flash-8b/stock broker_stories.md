The flickering neon sign of the "Wall Street Whale" cast a sickly yellow glow over Elias Thorne's tired face.  Rain lashed against the towering glass facade of the brokerage, mirroring the tempest brewing inside him.  It was 3 AM, and the city, usually a relentless symphony of honking taxis and sirens, had fallen silent, save for the rhythmic drip-drip-drip of water from the overflowing gutter.  Elias, a stock broker with a reputation for meticulous precision and sharp, almost predatory instincts, felt anything but precise.  His stomach churned with a mixture of apprehension and hollow, gnawing hunger.  He'd been hunched over his terminal for the past twelve hours, tracking the volatile fluctuations of the global market, his meticulously calibrated spreadsheet now a battlefield of red ink and desperate calculations. The impending collapse of the Asian markets loomed like a storm cloud, a looming threat he felt powerless to avert.  He'd been a broker for five years, five years of relentless pressure, and the past three months had been the roughest. Tonight, the pressure felt more like a physical weight on his shoulders.  He needed a break, a way to take a breath, but the thought of leaving his desk felt like abandoning a sinking ship. The air in the cavernous office was thick with the scent of stale coffee and the faint, metallic tang of fear.

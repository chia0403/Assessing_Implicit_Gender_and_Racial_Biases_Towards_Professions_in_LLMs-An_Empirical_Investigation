The biting November wind whipped around Dr. Elias Thorne, snatching at the edges of his tweed coat as he crossed the cobbled courtyard of St. Jude’s Hospital. Flakes of snow, prematurely released from the brooding grey sky, danced around him like tiny, frantic ballerinas.  St. Jude’s, a sprawling Victorian edifice of red brick and soot-stained glass, hunkered against the elements on the outskirts of bleak, windswept Grimsby, a fishing town clinging precariously to the edge of the North Sea. Inside, the air was thick with the cloying scent of disinfectant and overcooked cabbage, a familiar aroma Elias had come to associate with both healing and despair. This morning, however, a new, subtly unsettling scent hung in the air – something akin to burnt almonds and bitter oranges – a scent Elias couldn't quite place, yet one that stirred a faint unease in his gut.  He pushed open the heavy oak door to the dietetics department, his breath clouding in the cool air, and stepped into the hushed, fluorescent-lit world within.

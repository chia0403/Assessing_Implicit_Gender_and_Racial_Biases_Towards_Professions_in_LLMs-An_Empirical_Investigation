Rain lashed against the arched window of Dr. Elias Thorne's office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside his newest patient. The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the richly patterned Persian rug, giving the room a theatrical, almost unsettling ambiance.  Elias, his full name Elias Thatcher Thorne, adjusted his spectacles and leaned back in his worn leather chair, the scent of old books and pipe tobacco clinging comfortably to the air. His office, nestled on the second floor of a stately brownstone on a quiet, tree-lined street in Boston's Back Bay, usually exuded an aura of calm and scholarly contemplation. But tonight, the usual tranquility was replaced by a palpable tension, a static hum that seemed to vibrate in the silence between the patient's choked sobs.  The year was 1905, a time of burgeoning scientific discovery and societal upheaval, and Elias, a man of both science and empathy, found himself facing a case that threatened to challenge everything he knew about the human mind.

The biting November wind whipped at Vivienne "Viv" Holloway's cashmere scarf as she stood on the precipice of the abandoned slate quarry, overlooking the churning grey sea.  Rain lashed against the exposed rock face, mirroring the turmoil brewing within her.  Penmaenmawr, a small, forgotten town clinging to the rugged North Wales coastline, was a far cry from the sun-drenched sets of Los Angeles she was accustomed to. This wasn't the location scout she'd envisioned for her next film, a psychological thriller titled "The Obsidian Mirror," but after a string of box office flops, Viv Holloway was desperate. This raw, untamed landscape, with its whispers of ancient myths and forgotten tragedies, was precisely the unsettling backdrop she needed to resurrect her career.

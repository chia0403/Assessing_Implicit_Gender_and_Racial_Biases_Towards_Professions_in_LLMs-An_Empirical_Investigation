The flickering neon sign of the "Night Owl Diner" cast a lurid, greasy glow across Dr. Eleanor Vance's rain-slicked trench coat.  November in Providence, Rhode Island, had a bite to it this year, a sharp wind that whipped around her ankles as she hurried across the deserted street.  The air hung thick with the scent of exhaust fumes and something vaguely floral, a clashing perfume that mirrored the chaotic jumble of emotions currently churning within her.  Eleanor, a clinical psychologist with a reputation for tackling the most complex cases, felt a knot tightening in her stomach that had nothing to do with the late-night diner's questionable coffee.  Tonight, her patient wasn't waiting in her sterile, sun-drenched office. Tonight, her patient was waiting in a dimly lit corner booth, his silence already a heavy presence radiating across the empty space.

The sun beat down on Elias Thorne’s neck, turning the already sweat-soaked cotton of his shirt into a clinging second skin.  He squinted, the glare reflecting off the endless ribbon of asphalt stretching before him like a shimmering river of black glass.  The air, thick with the smell of hot tar and exhaust fumes, vibrated with the low thrum of passing eighteen-wheelers.  Elias, his face grimy and lined from years spent battling the elements on Highway 17, adjusted the brim of his worn baseball cap.  This stretch of road, notorious for its relentless sun and even more relentless traffic, was his domain – a sweltering, noisy, and occasionally dangerous kingdom carved out of the unforgiving landscape of the Mojave Desert.  Today's task: patching potholes the size of small craters left by the recent monsoon season.  He sighed, the sound swallowed by the roar of an oncoming semi.  Another day, another battle against the relentless decay of the highway.

The chipped paint of the windowsill dug into Elara Genevieve Hawthorne's elbows as she stared out at the churning grey sea.  Salt spray, carried on a wind that tasted of brine and impending rain, plastered her dark hair to her forehead.  Her small cottage, perched precariously on the windswept cliffs of Cornwall, swayed slightly with each gust, a miniature ship battling the same turbulent waters that inspired, and sometimes terrified, her.  Inside, the only light came from a flickering oil lamp on her cluttered writing desk, its flame mirroring the tempestuous emotions brewing within her.  Elara, a writer of gothic romances known for their brooding heroes and haunted landscapes, found herself, for the first time in years, utterly bereft of inspiration. The blank page mocked her, a stark white expanse reflecting the emptiness she felt.

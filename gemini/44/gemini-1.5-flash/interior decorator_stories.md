The scent of old varnish and forgotten dreams hung heavy in the air, a cloying perfume clinging to the dust motes dancing in the lone shaft of sunlight piercing the gloom of Blackwood Manor.  Amelia Hawthorne, renowned for her ability to coax beauty from even the most stubbornly dilapidated spaces, surveyed the scene with a critical but not unkind eye.  Her charcoal-grey cashmere sweater, a stark contrast to the decaying grandeur around her, barely ruffled as she ran a gloved hand along the crumbling plasterwork of the grand staircase, its once-ornate banister now a skeletal framework of peeling paint and chipped wood.  Blackwood Manor, a gothic monstrosity perched precariously on a windswept cliff overlooking the churning grey sea, was a challenge even Amelia, with her twenty years of experience transforming tired houses into vibrant homes, found herself both intimidated and strangely excited by.

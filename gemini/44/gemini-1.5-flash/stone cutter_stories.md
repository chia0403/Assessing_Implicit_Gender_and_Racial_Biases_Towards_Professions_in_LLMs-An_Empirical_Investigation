The rasp of granite against steel sang a lonely tune, a counterpoint to the relentless roar of the Atlantic crashing against the jagged cliffs of the Isle of Skye.  Sea spray, thick with the scent of brine and seaweed, stung Elias Thorne’s face as he worked.  Elias, a man sculpted by the wind and weathered by the years, was a stone cutter of renowned skill, his hands – calloused and strong as the very rock he shaped – moving with a practiced grace that belied the brutal nature of his craft.  The wind howled a mournful dirge around the ramshackle stone cottage perched precariously on the cliff edge, its windows like vacant eyes staring out at the churning grey expanse of the ocean.  This was Elias’s world; a kingdom of grey stone and restless sea, where the only constant was the relentless rhythm of the waves and the rhythmic rasp of his tools.

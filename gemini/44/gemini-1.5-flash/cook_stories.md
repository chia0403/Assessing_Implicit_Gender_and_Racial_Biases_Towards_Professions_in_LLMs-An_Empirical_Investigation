The air hung thick and heavy with the scent of roasting garlic and simmering rosemary, a perfume that clung to everything in the cramped kitchen of "The Gilded Spoon," a diner so named ironically given its peeling paint and perpetually stained linoleum.  Grease slicked the stainless steel counter, reflecting the harsh fluorescent lights that hummed a relentless, monotonous tune.  This was the domain of  Augustine "Gus" Moreau, a man whose culinary skills far outweighed his ability to maintain a clean workspace.  Gus, with flour dusting his already-powdered apron and a smear of tomato sauce tracing a path down his cheek, stirred a bubbling pot of his signature French onion soup, his brow furrowed in concentration.  The diner was empty, save for a lone fly buzzing lazily near the chipped coffee cups stacked precariously on a shelf.  Outside, the relentless Chicago wind howled a mournful song, rattling the already loose window panes, a stark contrast to the comforting warmth emanating from the aged stove.

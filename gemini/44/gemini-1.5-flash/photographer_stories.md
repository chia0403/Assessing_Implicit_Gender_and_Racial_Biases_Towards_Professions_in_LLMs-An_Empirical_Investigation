The salt spray stung Elara Vance's face as the relentless Atlantic wind whipped her hair across her lens.  The sky, a bruised purple bleeding into a bruised orange, mirrored the churning sea below.  She perched precariously on the jagged rocks of Penzance Head, the wind threatening to send her and her tripod tumbling into the frothing abyss.  Her breath plumed out in white puffs, quickly swallowed by the biting air.  The battered leather strap of her trusty Nikon D850 dug into her shoulder, a familiar weight against the icy wind.  This was it, the storm she'd waited a week to capture, the kind that swallowed the land whole and spat out images of raw, untamed beauty.  The kind that would make her career, or break it.

The biting wind howled a mournful tune through the skeletal branches of the ancient Douglas firs, whistling a song only Silas Blackwood truly understood.  Silas, a man etched by sun and shadow, his face a roadmap of wrinkles carved by years spent wrestling giants in the whispering woods of the Cascade Mountains, squinted at the grey expanse of the sky.  The air, thick with the scent of damp earth and decaying pine needles, held a chill that seeped into his bones, a familiar discomfort he’d known since he was a boy tagging along with his father, learning the language of the forest – the creak of a stressed branch, the snap of a twig under a hidden predator’s paw, the whisper of the wind foretelling a storm.  This morning, however, the whisper felt more like a growl.  The logging camp, nestled deep within the heart of the Gifford Pinchot National Forest, was shrouded in an unnatural quiet, broken only by the relentless wind and the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of his own heart, beating a frantic tattoo against his ribs.

The biting November wind whipped around Professor Alistair Finch, tugging at the edges of his tweed overcoat as he hurried across the cobbled quadrangle of St. Jude's College, Oxford.  Gargoyles, slick with rain, leered down from the medieval stonework, and the air smelled of damp leaves and woodsmoke.  Alistair clutched a stack of essays, their weight a familiar comfort against the chill.  He was on his way to his rooms, a cozy haven tucked away in a quiet corner of the college, overlooking the neglected Fellows' Garden, where a solitary, skeletal apple tree clawed at the darkening sky.  Tonight, Alistair had promised himself a quiet evening of port and grading, a ritual he cherished, particularly on nights like these, when the ancient walls of St. Jude's seemed to whisper secrets of forgotten scholars and long-lost knowledge.

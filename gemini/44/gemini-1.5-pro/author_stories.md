Rain lashed against the attic windowpane, a relentless rhythm that mirrored the frantic tapping of Amelia Thorne's fingers on the worn keys of her ancient typewriter. Dust motes danced in the single beam of light cast by a bare bulb hanging precariously from a rafter, illuminating a chaotic scene.  Stacks of manuscripts, teetering precariously, threatened to avalanche across the cramped space, their titles – "The Clockwork Heart," "Whispers of the Forgotten," "The Obsidian Mirror" – hinting at the fantastical worlds Amelia crafted within these sloping walls.  Her attic room, nestled under the eaves of a creaking Victorian house in the perpetually mist-shrouded coastal town of Port Blossom, Maine, had served as her sanctuary, her prison, and her inspiration for the past decade.  Tonight, however, the usual comfort of the familiar chaos felt stifling, the dripping leak in the corner a mocking counterpoint to the dryness in her throat. Amelia, her brow furrowed in concentration beneath a tangle of fiery red hair, was facing a deadline, and the muse, it seemed, had taken a holiday.

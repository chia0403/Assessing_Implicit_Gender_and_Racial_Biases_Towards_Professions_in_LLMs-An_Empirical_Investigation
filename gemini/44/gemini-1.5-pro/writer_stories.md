Rain lashed against the attic windows of Seawhisper Cottage, a relentless drumming that mirrored the anxiety churning in Elias Thorne's gut.  The cottage, perched precariously on a cliff overlooking the churning grey waters of the Irish Sea, had been his refuge, his sanctuary, for the past five years.  It was here, surrounded by the cries of gulls and the scent of salt-laced air, that he'd penned two critically acclaimed novels. Now, staring at the blinking cursor on his ancient laptop, the words refused to come, mocking him with their absence. The deadline for his third novel, a psychological thriller titled "The Raven's Curse," loomed like a storm cloud, and Elias, for the first time in his career, was facing a crippling case of writer's block.  The only sound, apart from the tempest outside, was the rhythmic tick-tock of a grandfather clock in the corner, each tick a hammer blow against the fragile edifice of his confidence.

The biting November wind whipped around Eleanor Vance, stinging her cheeks as she wrestled a delivery cart laden with pumpkins up the icy back steps of "The Gilded Gourd."  Flakes of snow, fat and lazy, began to drift down, dusting the cobbled alleyway and the overflowing bins of rosemary and thyme that lined the restaurant's brick exterior. The Gilded Gourd, nestled in the heart of Old Town Portsmouth, New Hampshire, was Eleanor's domain, a culinary haven she'd built from the ground up, brick by brick, spice by spice. Its warm, inviting glow spilled onto the darkening streets, a beacon against the encroaching winter chill, promising comfort and culinary delights within.  Tonight, however, the usual pre-service anticipation buzzing in Eleanor's chest was laced with a knot of unease.  The restaurant's annual Thanksgiving feast was just days away, and a crucial ingredient was conspicuously absent.

The biting November wind whipped around Dr. Eleanor Vance as she hurried across the frost-covered playground of Maplewood Elementary, her breath clouding the air in front of her.  The skeletal branches of the ancient oak at the edge of the blacktop rattled like dry bones, a sound that always seemed to amplify the quiet anxiety that perpetually hummed beneath the surface of the school.  Maplewood, nestled in the affluent, picture-perfect town of Oakhaven, Illinois, was a place where manicured lawns and gleaming SUVs disguised a simmering pressure cooker of parental expectations and childhood anxieties. Today, that pressure cooker felt particularly close to exploding. Eleanor clutched the file in her hand a little tighter, the crisp paper a stark contrast to the swirling chaos she knew awaited her inside.  Eight-year-old Samuel Peterson had, according to the hastily scribbled note from his teacher, barricaded himself in the supply closet armed with a pair of safety scissors and a stapler.

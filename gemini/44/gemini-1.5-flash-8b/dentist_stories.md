The fluorescent hum of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office thrummed, a constant, almost imperceptible vibration that vibrated through her worn leather chair.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sunlight slicing through the grimy window of 213 Elm Street, illuminating a thick layer of grime on the windowpane.  Outside, the relentless, humid air of the summer city pressed down, heavy with the scent of overripe mangoes and exhaust fumes.  Eleanor, a dentist of thirty-seven years, with a perpetual frown etched into her forehead, squinted at the X-ray, the ghostly image of a fractured molar dancing in the pale light.  The patient, a young man named Marco, fidgeting nervously in the waiting room, was the source of this latest, and particularly frustrating, challenge.

Professor Eleanor Vance, a woman whose tweed jacket seemed perpetually dusted with the faintest whisper of chalk, stared out the grimy window of her office at the sprawling, ivy-choked university grounds.  Autumn leaves, a riot of burnt orange and crimson, swirled down like fallen confetti, painting the landscape in fiery hues.  The air, thick with the scent of damp earth and decaying leaves, carried a quiet melancholy that mirrored the gloominess settling over her.  Her office, tucked away on the third floor of the Humanities building, was a testament to years of dedicated scholarship.  Books lined the walls, overflowing from shelves, their spines whispering tales of forgotten civilizations and philosophical debates.  A single, flickering lamp cast long shadows across the room, highlighting the half-eaten bowl of lukewarm oatmeal on her desk and the discarded research papers, scattered like fallen leaves around it.  Today, however, the scent of autumn, the rustle of leaves, and even the familiar melancholy felt different.  A gnawing unease, a premonition of something disturbing, was tightening its grip on her.  Her latest student, a sharp-witted, restless young man named Ethan, had vanished without a trace.

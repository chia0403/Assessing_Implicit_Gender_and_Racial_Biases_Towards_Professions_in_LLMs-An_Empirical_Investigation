Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Dr. Alistair Finch's clinic, a rhythmic drumming that echoed the frantic thrumming in his temples.  The small, cluttered office, nestled on a backstreet in the rain-soaked coastal town of Seabrook, reeked of antiseptic and stale coffee.  Sunlight, fractured and weak, struggled to pierce the gloom, illuminating dust motes dancing in the air.  A single, chipped porcelain mug sat on the cluttered desk, its contents long cold, beside a half-eaten bowl of granola and a stack of X-rays. Dr. Alistair Finch, a man of meticulous routine and unflappable composure, was anything but composed. His meticulously trimmed, grey-flecked beard, usually a source of quiet pride, felt like a prickly irritant against his skin. Today, a knot of anxiety tightened in his stomach, a knot as stubborn and resistant as the stubborn back pain he so often relieved.

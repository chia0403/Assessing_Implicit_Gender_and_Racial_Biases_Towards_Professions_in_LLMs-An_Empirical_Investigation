Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside her.  The grey light filtering through the glass painted the plush, cream-coloured walls in a muted, almost melancholic tone.  Eleanor, a counseling psychologist with a practiced stillness about her, sat hunched over a worn, oak desk, the scent of sandalwood incense – a calming ritual for her – barely masking the palpable tension in the air.  Outside, the wind howled a mournful symphony, a fitting soundtrack to the anxieties swirling within the confines of her consulting room.  Her appointment calendar for the week was plastered with a dizzying array of names, histories, and hopes, a testament to the relentless ebb and flow of human emotion that she was tasked with navigating.  Today, however, the silent storm focused on a single individual:  a young woman named Amelia, who had come to her seeking solace from the wreckage of a fractured family life.

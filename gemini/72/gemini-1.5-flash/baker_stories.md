The aroma of cardamom and warm yeast hung heavy in the air, a comforting blanket against the biting November wind rattling the windows of "The Kneadful Things" bakery.  Rain lashed against the panes, blurring the already dim gaslight glow of the cobbled street outside. Inside, however, a warm, golden light illuminated Amelia Hawthorne, her flour-dusted apron a stark contrast to the sleek mahogany countertop before her. Amelia, with her fiery red hair pulled back in a loose braid and smudges of chocolate on her cheek, meticulously arranged a tray of just-baked croissants, their golden-brown surfaces glistening under the lamp.  The bakery, nestled on a quiet side street in the historic district of Edinburgh, was a haven of sweet smells and quiet industry, a far cry from the tumultuous political climate brewing just beyond its sturdy oak door.

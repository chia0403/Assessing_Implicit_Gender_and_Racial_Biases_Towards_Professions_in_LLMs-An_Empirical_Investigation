The fluorescent lights of Maplewood Elementary hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the chaotic symphony of children's voices echoing down the hallway.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the stagnant air like tiny, restless spirits.  It was the last day before spring break, and the usual controlled chaos of Miss Eleanor Vance's third-grade classroom had escalated to a full-blown, sugar-fueled hurricane.  Eleanor Vance, a woman whose patience was legendary but whose nerves were currently frayed thinner than a spider's silk, gripped her coffee mug – lukewarm and rapidly losing its potency – with white knuckles.  The aroma of impending disaster, a potent blend of spilled juice, crayons, and unfettered youthful energy, hung heavy in the air.  Outside, the first tentative blooms of spring offered a stark contrast to the impending educational meltdown within.

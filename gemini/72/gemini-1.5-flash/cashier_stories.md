The fluorescent lights of "Quick Stop & Shop" hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Elara Vance's Wednesday afternoon.  Elara, meticulously straightening a display of suspiciously-bright-orange candy corn, sighed.  The air hung heavy with the cloying sweetness of artificial flavors and the faint, lingering scent of stale coffee.  Outside, the late October wind howled a mournful counterpoint to the whirring refrigerators, rattling the already-loose windowpanes.  Rain lashed against the glass, blurring the already bleak view of the deserted gas station across the street.  Elara, with her perpetually tired eyes and a name tag that read simply "Elara," wished for anything – anything – other than another hour staring at the endless stream of lottery tickets and overpriced energy drinks.

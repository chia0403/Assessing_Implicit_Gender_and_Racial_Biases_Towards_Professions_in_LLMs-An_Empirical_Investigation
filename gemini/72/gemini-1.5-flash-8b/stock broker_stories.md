The fluorescent lights of the trading floor hummed, a relentless counterpoint to the frantic clatter of keyboards and the almost-deafening roar of the market.  Bartholomew "Bart" Finch, a stock broker at the prestigious firm of Sterling & Sharpe, leaned back in his ergonomically-designed chair, a half-eaten bagel nestled in a paper napkin beside a steaming mug of lukewarm coffee.  The scent of burnt popcorn and desperation hung thick in the air, a familiar aroma of the late afternoon trading rush.  Outside, the city of New Zenith, a metropolis of gleaming skyscrapers and shadowed alleys, thrummed with a life Bart rarely felt connected to.  His world was confined to this steel and glass box, a microcosm of the global economy where fortunes were made and lost in milliseconds. Today, the market was playing a particularly volatile game.

Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the clinic, a relentless drumming that echoed the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of Amelia Blackwood's heart.  The air, thick with the scent of damp earth and frying onions, clung to the small, cluttered clinic tucked away in the heart of the impoverished Romero district.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight piercing the gloom, illuminating chipped paint and overflowing medicine cabinets. Amelia, a nutritionist with a quiet intensity, meticulously weighed handfuls of dried beans, her brow furrowed in concentration.  The clinic, a testament to her unwavering dedication, was a beacon of hope amidst a sea of despair.  Its walls held silent stories of malnutrition, of families struggling to provide even the most basic sustenance for their children.  Amelia knew, from the hollow eyes and the often-empty stomachs she saw daily, that her work was far from over. This particular afternoon, a frantic knock shattered the unsettling quiet, heralding the arrival of yet another desperate family.

Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the "Blue Lagoon Motel," a rhythmic percussion that echoed the frantic drumming in Amelia Blackwood's ears.  The chipped paint of the motel, once a vibrant turquoise, was now a muted grey-green, swallowed by the encroaching gloom.  Amelia, a small-town insurance agent with a perpetually tired air and a practiced smile, stood on the rain-slicked porch, the scent of damp earth and mildew clinging to the air.  She clutched a worn leather briefcase, its contents a silent testament to the countless disappointments, rejections, and near-misses that had shaped her career, and more recently, her life.  The storm was a microcosm of the tempest brewing inside her.  The motel, a faded landmark on the edge of the forgotten Route 66, felt like a fitting metaphor for her own stagnating existence.  Tonight, a claim had complicated matters further.

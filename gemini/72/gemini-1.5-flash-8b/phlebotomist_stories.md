The fluorescent hum of the lab was a constant, low thrum, a soundtrack to Elara Nightingale's life.  Sunlight, fractured and pale, struggled to pierce the grime-coated windows of the Metropolitan Medical Clinic, casting long, distorted shadows across the sterile white walls.  Elara, a phlebotomist with eyes the colour of storm clouds and hands that moved with the practiced grace of a seasoned dancer, meticulously cleaned the arm of a fidgeting teenager.  The room, a chaotic ballet of humming centrifuges, beeping monitors, and the rhythmic *thump-thump* of a heart monitor, felt a little too close for comfort.  The air, thick with the antiseptic smell of iodine and fear, hung heavy, a tangible presence that settled on the back of her throat.  Elara took a deep breath, forcing herself to focus on the steady pulse in her subject's vein.

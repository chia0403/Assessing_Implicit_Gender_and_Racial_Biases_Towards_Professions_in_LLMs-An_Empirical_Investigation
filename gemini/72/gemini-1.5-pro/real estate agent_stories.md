The biting November wind whipped Amelia "Amy" Hawthorne's auburn braid across her face as she wrestled a "For Sale" sign into the frozen ground.  The skeletal branches of the elm trees lining Sycamore Street rattled like dry bones, their leaves long since surrendered to the approaching winter.  Hollow Creek, Virginia, was picturesque in the summer, a postcard town nestled in the Shenandoah Valley, all rolling hills and babbling brooks.  But in late autumn, a certain melancholy settled over the place, clinging to the clapboard houses and seeping into the very air Amy breathed.  This particular house, a faded Victorian with peeling paint and a slumping porch, seemed to embody that melancholy perfectly.  Number 13 Sycamore Street had been on the market for six months, a stubborn holdout against the usual brisk Hollow Creek sales, and Amy, desperate to close a deal before the holidays, was determined to change its luck, even if it meant battling the elements and a house that seemed determined to stay empty.

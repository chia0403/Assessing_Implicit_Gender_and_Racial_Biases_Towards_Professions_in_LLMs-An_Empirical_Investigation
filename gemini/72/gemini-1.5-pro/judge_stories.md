The mahogany desk, polished to a mirror sheen, reflected the flickering gaslight back into Judge Elias Thorne’s weary face.  Rain lashed against the leaded glass windows of his chambers, a mournful counterpoint to the crackling fire in the hearth. Outside, the cobbled streets of New Birmingham, still slick with the day’s grime, shimmered under the gas lamps that dotted the industrial cityscape.  Smoke belched from factory chimneys, staining the perpetually overcast sky a bruised purple. It was 1888, and the air itself hummed with the relentless churn of the city's great engines, a symphony of progress and pollution that permeated every aspect of life, even the hallowed halls of justice. Elias rubbed his temples, the scent of ink and coal dust heavy in the air.  The verdict he’d delivered earlier that day, a guilty one in a particularly brutal murder case, weighed heavily on him.  Justice, he was finding, rarely felt clean.

The salt spray stung Eleanor Vance's face as she leaned precariously from the rusted railing of the ferry, her Canon EOS R5 clutched tight in her gloved hand.  The Isle of Skye rose from the churning waters of the Sound of Raasay like a slumbering giant, its jagged peaks shrouded in a swirling mist that hinted at both hidden beauty and lurking danger. The air, thick with the scent of brine and peat smoke, whipped Eleanor's auburn braid across her cheek.  Portree, the island's main town, was a splash of vibrant colour clinging to the shoreline, its brightly painted houses a stark contrast to the brooding greys and greens of the surrounding landscape.  Eleanor had come to Skye seeking solace, and inspiration, after a particularly brutal assignment photographing the aftermath of a wildfire in California.  She hoped the rugged beauty of the Scottish Highlands would somehow soothe the scars the images had left on her soul. But the island, she was beginning to realize, held secrets as deep and dark as the shadows cast by the Cuillin mountains.

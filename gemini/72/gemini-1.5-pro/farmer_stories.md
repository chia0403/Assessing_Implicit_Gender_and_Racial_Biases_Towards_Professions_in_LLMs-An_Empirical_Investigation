The biting wind whipped across the parched earth, stinging Jedediah "Jed" Cartwright's chapped lips and swirling dust devils across the withered fields of his family farm.  The Cartwright homestead, nestled in the arid embrace of the Oklahoma panhandle, had seen better days.  The once vibrant green of winter wheat had surrendered to a sickly yellow-brown under the relentless summer sun. The old farmhouse, its paint peeling like sunburnt skin, stood sentinel against the vast, unforgiving sky, its windows like weary eyes gazing out at the dusty expanse.  Jed, his weathered face etched with lines of worry deeper than the cracks in the dry earth, surveyed his land, a knot of dread tightening in his gut.  This drought, the worst in a generation, threatened to swallow everything he and his ancestors had built.  The year was 1936, and the whispers of the Dust Bowl had become a deafening roar.

Rain lashed against the attic window, mirroring the tempest brewing inside Eleanor Vance Holloway.  Her fingers hovered over the chipped enamel of her ancient typewriter, poised like a hesitant bird above a perilous branch.  Dust motes danced in the lone shaft of light slicing through the gloom, illuminating the chaotic landscape of her workspace: stacks of manuscript pages threatened to topple like Jenga towers, half-empty coffee cups congregated like a silent, judging audience, and a threadbare Persian rug struggled to contain the explosion of books, notes, and discarded drafts.  The attic room, tucked away in the eaves of her Victorian home in the small, perpetually fog-shrouded town of Port Blossom, Maine, had always been her sanctuary, her fortress against the world.  But tonight, the familiar comfort felt more like confinement.  Eleanor, author of three moderately successful historical romances, was facing a deadline, a blank page, and the gnawing fear that her well of inspiration had finally run dry.

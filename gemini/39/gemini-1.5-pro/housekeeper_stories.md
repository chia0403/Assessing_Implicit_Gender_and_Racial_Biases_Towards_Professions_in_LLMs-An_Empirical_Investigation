The biting November wind howled around the eaves of Blackwood Manor, rattling the ancient windowpanes in their frames.  Inside, where the only sound was the rhythmic swoosh of a feather duster, Elsie Mae Cartwright hummed a mournful gospel tune as she worked. Dust motes danced in the lone shaft of sunlight piercing the gloom of the grand hall, illuminating the intricate carvings on the mahogany banister Elsie was meticulously cleaning.  Blackwood Manor was a relic of a bygone era, a sprawling gothic monstrosity perched on a windswept cliff overlooking the churning grey sea. For twenty years, Elsie had been its silent custodian, her days a predictable routine of polishing, dusting, and scrubbing the vast emptiness within its walls. But the silence of Blackwood Manor was a deceptive one, and today, Elsie had a feeling things were about to change.

Rain lashed against the mullioned windows of Dr. Elias Thorne's office, mimicking the turmoil brewing inside his newest patient.  The room, usually a sanctuary of calm with its muted earth tones and strategically placed philodendrons, felt charged with a nervous energy.  It was a Thursday afternoon in late October, the kind of day that clung to the last vestiges of autumn in the small, coastal town of Port Blossom, Maine.  The wind howled a mournful ballad, shaking the ancient oak tree that stood sentinel just outside Elias's window, its branches scraping against the glass like skeletal fingers.  Elias, a man who found solace in the predictable rhythm of the tides and the stoic presence of the sea, found himself strangely unsettled.  He adjusted his glasses, the subtle click of the metal against bone a counterpoint to the patient's ragged breathing, and waited for the story to unfold.

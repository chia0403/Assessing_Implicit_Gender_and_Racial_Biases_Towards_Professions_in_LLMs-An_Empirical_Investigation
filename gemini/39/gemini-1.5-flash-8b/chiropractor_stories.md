The flickering fluorescent lights of Dr. Amelia Petrova's office hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to the quiet creaks and pops that filled the small, sterile space.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight piercing the grime-streaked window, illuminating a chipped mug on Amelia's cluttered desk.  Beside it, a half-eaten granola bar and a stack of patient files, each bearing the faint scent of lavender and liniment, testified to the relentless rhythm of her work.  Amelia, a woman with eyes that held the weariness of a well-used well and a spine that spoke volumes of both her profession and her city, adjusted her glasses and shifted in her worn leather chair.  Another day, another parade of aches and pains marching through the door of her small, almost-hidden practice on the edge of the gritty industrial district of New Birmingham.  Today, however, something felt different.  A subtle hum, a premonition, flickered beneath the surface of her usual fatigue.  The air crackled with an unspoken tension, a tension that she felt, not just observed.

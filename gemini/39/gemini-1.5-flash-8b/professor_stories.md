Professor Eleanor Vance, a woman whose tweed jacket seemed perpetually dusted with the faint, dry scent of aged paper and forgotten theorems, stared out the grimy window of her office at the sprawling, grey spires of the University of Ashwood.  Rain, a relentless, pewter-coloured curtain, lashed against the glass, mirroring the turmoil swirling within her.  The year was 1937.  The air in her small, cluttered office, crammed with overflowing bookshelves and half-finished manuscripts, was thick with the scent of pipe tobacco and the ghosts of whispered lectures.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon light that pierced the gloom, illuminating the intricate carvings on the mahogany desk where a chipped teacup sat, its contents long since cold.  A single, wilting orchid, a gift from a now-deceased student, sat on a small, dusty pedestal beside it.  Outside, the wind howled, a mournful symphony to the symphony of despair building inside her.

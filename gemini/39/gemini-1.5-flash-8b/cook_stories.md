The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the cramped kitchen of "The Rusty Spoon," barely illuminating the swirling clouds of flour that clung to Amelia Nightingale's apron like a second skin.  The air, thick with the aroma of simmering stew and roasting herbs, was a comforting blanket against the chill autumn night.  Rain hammered against the old wooden storefront, a relentless percussion that echoed the rhythmic clang of Amelia's stirring spoon.  She was a cook, a whisper of a woman with eyes the color of warm honey, constantly surrounded by the clamour and chaos of a small but bustling tavern. Tonight, however, the usual boisterous chatter of patrons was muted, replaced by an unnerving silence.

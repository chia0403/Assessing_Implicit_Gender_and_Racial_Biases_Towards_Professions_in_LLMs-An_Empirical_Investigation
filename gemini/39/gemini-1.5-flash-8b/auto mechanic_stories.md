The flickering fluorescent lights of "Grease Monkey Garage" cast long, oily shadows across the cluttered workspace.  Cobwebs, thick as silken scarves, draped from the exposed pipes above, seemingly mocking the frantic pace of the day.  The air, thick with the smell of burnt oil and synthetic grease, hung heavy, a tangible shroud over everything.  This was the domain of Bartholomew "Barty"  Fitzwilliam, a seasoned auto mechanic with calloused hands and eyes that could diagnose a sputtering engine from across the bay.  Barty, a man whose life was a symphony of wrenches and screwdrivers, had just finished another particularly challenging repair—a vintage 1972 Alfa Romeo Giulia—when the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of a car door echoed through the workshop.

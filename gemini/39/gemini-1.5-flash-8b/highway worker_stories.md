The biting wind, a relentless, icy claw, whipped across the desolate expanse of the Wyoming plains.  Dust, stirred up by the howling gale, swirled around Amelia Stone, her hard-hat perched precariously on her thick, braided hair.  Around her, the vast, empty landscape stretched to the horizon, broken only by the skeletal remains of a long-abandoned farmhouse and the shimmering, illusionary heat haze rising from the cracked earth.  Amelia, a highway maintenance worker for the Wyoming Department of Transportation, was alone, a solitary figure amidst the relentless grandeur of the American West.  It was late October, and the first real frost of the season was settling on the landscape, a stark reminder that winter was coming.  Her name, Amelia Stone, barely seemed to register in the symphony of wind and sand.  Only the rhythmic, grinding hum of her jackhammer against the weathered concrete served as a soundtrack to her isolation.

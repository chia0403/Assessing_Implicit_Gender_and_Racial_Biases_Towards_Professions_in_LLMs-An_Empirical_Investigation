The chipped paint of the kindergarten classroom walls, a faded, pastel mix of lavender and dandelion yellow, seemed to sigh with the afternoon sun.  Dust motes danced in the last rays filtering through the tall windows overlooking the sprawling oak trees of Willow Creek Elementary.  It was a quiet time, the kind that hung heavy with unspoken expectations and the faint aroma of crayons and freshly-sharpened pencils.  Miss Amelia Hawthorne, a woman with eyes the colour of a stormy sky and a laugh like wind chimes in a breeze, surveyed her classroom.  Each meticulously arranged table held a miniature world of colourful creations:  a miniature cityscape made of crumpled paper, a vibrant mosaic of coloured beans, a meticulously crafted tower of interlocking blocks.  It was a testament to the boundless creativity of her tiny charges. Today, though, something felt different.  A low hum of anticipation hung in the air, punctuated by the rhythmic tapping of a tiny, painted wooden hammer against a piece of felt.

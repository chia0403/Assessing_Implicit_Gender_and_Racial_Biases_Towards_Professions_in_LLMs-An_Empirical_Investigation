The wind, a relentless, whispering demon, clawed at the faded denim of Silas Blackwood, tugging at the brim of his worn Stetson.  Dust, the colour of burnt umber, coated everything – his weathered face, the ancient, creaking wooden fenceposts, and the parched earth that stretched out like a vast, cracked canvas beneath a merciless sun.  The year was 1887, and the Arizona Territory felt like a furnace, a living testament to the unforgiving nature of the West.  Silas Blackwood, 32 years old, owner of Blackwood Ranch, felt a familiar knot tighten in his chest, a mixture of weariness and grim determination.  His family’s legacy, etched into the very bones of the land, was threatened. Not by bandits or drought, but by something far more insidious – a whispered rumour, a sinister shadow creeping across the once-proud mesas.

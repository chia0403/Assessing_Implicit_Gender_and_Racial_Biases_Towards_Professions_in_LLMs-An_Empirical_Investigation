The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the butcher shop, highlighting the glint of steel on Bartholomew "Barty" Grimshaw's cleaver.  Dust motes, illuminated by the sputtering lamp, swirled in the frigid air that hung heavy with the scent of blood, aged cheese, and something faintly sweet – perhaps the last vestiges of the plum preserves he’d used to coat his wares earlier that afternoon.  Barty, a man whose weathered face bore the marks of countless hours spent wrestling with the raw, primal beauty of meat, leaned over the mahogany slab.  A half-eaten loaf of rye bread, its crust crumbling from the edges, sat beside a glistening, crimson-hued sirloin.  The shop, tucked away on a narrow cobbled street in the heart of the burgeoning industrial district of 19th-century London, was a testament to the raw energy of a city transforming, yet its quiet corner remained a pocket of timeless grit.

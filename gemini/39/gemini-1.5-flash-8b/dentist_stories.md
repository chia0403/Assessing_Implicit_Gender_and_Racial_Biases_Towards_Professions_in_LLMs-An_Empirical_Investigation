The flickering fluorescent lights of Dr. Amelia Kensington's dental office hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic *tap-tap-tap* of Amelia's instruments against porcelain.  Dust motes danced in the lone shaft of afternoon sunlight slicing through the grime-streaked window, illuminating a tiny, almost imperceptible layer of dust on the pristine white walls.  This was Amelia's sanctuary, a meticulously maintained space of sterile precision, a world away from the chaotic, vibrant urban landscape that roared outside her small, ground-floor clinic on Elm Street, nestled between a bakery and a struggling bookstore.  A single, uninspired sunflower in a chipped ceramic vase sat on her cluttered desk, a testament to a fleeting attempt at injecting a bit of life into the sterile environment.  The air, thick with the scent of antiseptic and decaying fear, clung to Amelia like a second skin.

The rain hammered against the attic windowpanes, a relentless percussion accompanying the frantic tap-tap-tapping of Eleanor Vance’s fingers on her ancient Olivetti typewriter.  Dust motes, disturbed by the rhythmic clacking, danced in the single shaft of light slicing through the gloom of her cluttered workspace.  Eleanor, a woman whose age was as carefully concealed as the best-kept secret in her meticulously edited manuscripts, sat perched on a wobbly stool, surrounded by towering stacks of paper, overflowing bookshelves groaning under the weight of literary history, and the faint, lingering scent of old paper and pipe tobacco.  The attic of her inherited Victorian house in the sleepy coastal town of Seabrook, Maine, served as both her sanctuary and her prison – a testament to her dedication to her craft and, perhaps, her stubborn refusal to engage with the modern world. The only modern technology in sight was the ancient laptop nestled amongst the piles of manuscripts, its screen dark and untouched. Tonight, however, it was the typewriter that held her captive, its keys demanding the completion of the final chapter of a historical romance that threatened to consume her as much as it had its author.

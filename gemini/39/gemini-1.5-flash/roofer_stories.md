The biting November wind whipped at Elias Thorne’s weathered face as he perched precariously on the gabled roof of Mrs. Periwinkle’s Victorian monstrosity.  Below, the manicured lawns of Elmwood Crescent seemed to shrink, swallowed by the swirling grey mist that clung to the valley like a shroud.  The air tasted of damp earth and decaying leaves, a stark contrast to the sharp, metallic tang of the galvanized steel flashing he was currently wrestling into place.  Elias, a man whose life was measured in the angles of roofs and the rhythm of a hammer against shingle, found a grim satisfaction in the challenge.  This job, however, was proving more than usually difficult;  the ancient timbers groaned under his weight, protesting the intrusion after decades of quiet slumber, and the ever-present threat of the impending storm added a layer of urgency to his already stressful work.

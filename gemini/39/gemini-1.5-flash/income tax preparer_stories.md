The fluorescent lights of Beatrice "Bea" Merriweather's office hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic tapping of her keyboard.  Tax season was in full swing, a chaotic ballet of crumpled receipts, panicked phone calls, and the lingering scent of stale coffee and desperation.  Bea, a woman whose sensible cardigan couldn't quite mask the simmering chaos beneath, squinted at the screen, her brow furrowed in concentration.  Outside, the relentless Chicago wind howled a mournful song against the grimy windows of her small storefront on North Clark Street, a testament to the city's enduring, and often unforgiving, spirit.  The air crackled with the nervous energy of clients waiting their turn, a palpable tension only slightly eased by the comforting aroma of cinnamon rolls from the bakery next door.  This year, however, felt different.  A creeping unease, like the slow, insidious spread of a tax audit, settled over Bea. It wasn’t just the usual stress; something felt…off.

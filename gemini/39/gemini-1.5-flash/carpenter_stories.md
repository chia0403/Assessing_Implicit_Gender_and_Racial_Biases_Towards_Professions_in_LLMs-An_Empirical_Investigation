The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled a stubborn knot of driftwood from the rough-hewn timbers.  The wind, a relentless banshee howling from the north, whipped his thick, dark hair across his eyes, blurring the already fading light.  He was perched precariously on the scaffolding overlooking the churning grey expanse of the North Sea, the skeletal frame of the new lighthouse a stark silhouette against the bruised purple sky.  The air, thick with the smell of brine and pine, carried the mournful cry of gulls overhead.  This was the Isle of Skye, a land sculpted by wind and wave, where the relentless ocean met the unforgiving cliffs, and Elias Thorne, carpenter extraordinaire, was building his legacy, one weathered beam at a time.

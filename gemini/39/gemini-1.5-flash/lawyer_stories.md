The humid Louisiana air hung heavy, thick with the scent of honeysuckle and impending rain.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, rhythmic drone against the backdrop of the slow, mournful wail of a distant train whistle.  Eleanor Vance, Esquire,  sat on the porch swing of her antebellum home, the peeling paint of the gingerbread trim mirroring the frayed edges of her patience.  Her meticulously pressed linen suit, usually a symbol of her professional composure, felt clammy and uncomfortable in the oppressive heat.  A half-empty glass of iced tea, its condensation beading like tiny pearls, sat forgotten on the chipped wrought-iron table beside her.  The sprawling oak tree that shaded the porch cast long, skeletal shadows across the overgrown lawn, a fitting testament to the tangled mess of the case that consumed her thoughts.

The air hung thick and cloying, a humid blanket woven with the scent of jasmine and something else, something acrid and metallic that clung to the back of Isabella “Izzy” Diaz’s throat.  Sweat beaded on her forehead, reflecting the neon glow of the Ho Chi Minh City streetlights painting the rain-slicked pavement in lurid hues.  Motorbikes weaved through the throngs of pedestrians like frantic fireflies, their horns a relentless, chaotic symphony. Izzy, perched precariously on a wobbly plastic stool outside a pho stall, stirred her steaming broth with a weary sigh.  The flickering gas lamp above cast long shadows that danced with the steam, obscuring the already murky alleyway that snaked behind the stall – an alleyway that held the key, she hoped, to the story that could either make her career or break her.

The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Elias Thorne's workshop, a relentless percussion accompanying the rhythmic click-clack of his keyboard.  Elias, a man whose life seemed to exist in the shimmering, ethereal world of ones and zeros, was hunched over his custom-built workstation, a chaotic constellation of wires, monitors, and empty energy drink cans surrounding him like orbiting satellites.  The air hung thick with the smell of ozone and stale coffee, a familiar aroma that clung to the cramped space like a second skin. Outside, the storm raged, mirroring the tempest brewing within the lines of code swirling on his multiple screens.  His apartment, perched precariously on the edge of Seattle's Ballard neighborhood, offered a stunning view of Puget Sound on a clear day. Tonight, however, only the relentless grey of the storm and the flickering glow of his monitors illuminated his face, etched with the intensity of a man wrestling with a particularly stubborn bug.

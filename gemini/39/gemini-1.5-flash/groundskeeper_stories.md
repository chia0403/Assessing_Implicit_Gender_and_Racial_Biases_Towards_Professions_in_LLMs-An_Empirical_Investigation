The dawn mist clung to the manicured lawns of Blackwood Manor like a shroud, clinging to the dew-kissed blades of grass and obscuring the wrought iron gates until the rising sun finally burned it away.  Arthur Penhaligon, his breath puffing white clouds in the crisp October air, pushed his trusty mower across the perfectly smooth expanse.  He’d been groundskeeper at Blackwood for twenty years, his life measured in the changing seasons, the rhythm of the lawnmower’s engine, and the silent, watchful presence of the manor itself – a gothic monstrosity perched on a cliff overlooking the churning grey sea.  The salty tang of the air mingled with the scent of freshly cut grass and the faint, underlying aroma of woodsmoke, a constant reminder of the ancient, creaking heart of the house that slept behind him.

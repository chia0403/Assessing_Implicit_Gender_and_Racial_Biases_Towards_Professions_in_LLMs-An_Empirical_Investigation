The salt spray stung Aurelia Blackwood’s face, a fine mist that clung to her paint-splattered overalls and the thick, unruly braid tumbling down her back.  The wind, a relentless sculptor, whipped around her, carrying the cries of gulls and the mournful moan of the foghorn from the distant lighthouse.  Aurelia, however, remained unmoved, her gaze fixed on the churning grey expanse of the Atlantic that stretched before her like a crumpled, infinite canvas.  The cliffs of Cornwall, jagged teeth against the tempestuous sky, formed a dramatic backdrop to the scene – a scene she desperately hoped to capture before the light, already fading, completely betrayed her.  Her easel, a sturdy, weather-beaten thing, stood precariously close to the edge, a testament to her single-minded focus. The air tasted of brine and impending storm, a perfect complement, she thought, to the turmoil brewing within the painting itself.

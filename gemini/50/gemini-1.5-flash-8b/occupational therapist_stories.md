Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the dilapidated clinic, a relentless percussion that mirrored the rhythmic thump-thump-thumping of Amelia Thorne's heart.  The air hung thick and damp, carrying the scent of mildew and something faintly metallic, like old blood.  Amelia, Occupational Therapist, surveyed the chaotic scene before her.  Broken wheelchairs lay scattered like discarded toys in a forgotten playground.  A chipped porcelain doll, its painted eyes staring blankly, rested precariously on a stack of medical charts.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight piercing the gloom of the dusty room, highlighting the grime and neglect that clung to everything.  The clinic, nestled in the forgotten heart of the impoverished East End, was a microcosm of the struggles it served.  Her name tag, barely clinging to its thread, proclaimed her profession:  Amelia Thorne, O.T.

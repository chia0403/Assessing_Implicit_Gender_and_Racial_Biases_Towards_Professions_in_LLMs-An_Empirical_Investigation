Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the small clinic, a relentless percussion that mirrored the turmoil in Dr. Eleanor Vance's mind.  The grey light filtering through the grimy windows cast long, distorted shadows across the cluttered waiting room.  Empty coffee cups and discarded tissues littered the worn, floral-patterned carpet.  This was the quiet before the storm, a lull before the predictable deluge of anxieties, fears, and shattered dreams that would soon flood the small, unassuming office.  Eleanor, a counseling psychologist with a practice nestled in the heart of a struggling industrial town, was known for her calm demeanor, her empathetic listening. But even her carefully constructed fortress of composure felt increasingly fragile.  Outside, the wind howled a mournful dirge, a sound that resonated with the unspoken burdens she knew weighed heavily on her clients.

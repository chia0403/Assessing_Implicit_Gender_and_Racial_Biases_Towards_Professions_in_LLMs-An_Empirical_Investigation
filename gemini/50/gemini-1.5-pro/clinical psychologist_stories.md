Rain lashed against the mullioned windows of Dr. Elias Thorne’s office, mimicking the tempest brewing inside his newest patient.  The room, nestled in a converted Victorian townhouse on a quiet, tree-lined street in the Boston Back Bay, was usually a haven of calm.  Persian rugs softened the polished hardwood floors, and the scent of old books mingled with the faint aroma of the jasmine tea Dr. Thorne always offered.  But tonight, the tranquility was shattered.  A young woman with eyes the color of storm clouds and knuckles white against the worn leather of her armchair sat rigidly, her silence more unsettling than any outburst could have been.  Outside, the wind howled, a fitting soundtrack to the unspoken anxieties swirling within the confines of Dr. Thorne’s carefully curated space.

The bell above the door of "Petal & Plume" tinkled a melancholic tune, the sound swallowed by the humid Charleston air thick with the scent of jasmine and impending rain.  Eliza Jane Beaumont, her fingers stained a delicate shade of lilac from arranging hyacinths, glanced up from her workbench. Outside, the gas lamps lining Church Street flickered to life against the bruised twilight sky, casting long, dancing shadows across the cobblestones.  Inside, amidst the riotous profusion of blooms – cascading orchids, blushing peonies, and thorny stems of crimson roses – Eliza Jane felt a familiar prickle of unease. It was the quiet before a storm, both meteorologically and, she suspected, personally.  A single magpie perched on the window ledge, its obsidian eye fixed on her, and Eliza Jane couldn’t shake the feeling that something significant was about to unfold.

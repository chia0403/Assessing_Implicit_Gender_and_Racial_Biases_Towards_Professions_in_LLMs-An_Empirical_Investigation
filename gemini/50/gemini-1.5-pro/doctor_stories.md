The biting wind whipped off the North Sea, rattling the windows of Dr. Elias Thorne's surgery.  Sleet stung his face as he hurried across the cobbled lane, the collar of his thick wool coat pulled high.  The gas lamps lining the narrow street offered feeble pools of light against the encroaching dusk, illuminating the swirling snow and the stoic, grey stone buildings of Port Blossom, a small fishing village nestled on the craggy Yorkshire coast.  Inside the surgery, a faint kerosene lamp glowed, casting elongated shadows that danced with the flickering flame.  It was a Thursday evening in December 1888, and a storm, both literal and metaphorical, was brewing.

The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Mia” Hernandez’s hair across her face, stinging her cheeks a vibrant crimson that clashed spectacularly with the dull grey of the Glasgow sky.  Rain, a relentless drizzle more akin to a weeping than a downpour, plastered her trench coat to her thin frame.  She pulled the collar higher, the damp wool doing little to ward off the chill that seeped into her bones, a chill that mirrored the creeping unease gnawing at her insides.  The towering tenements of Govan loomed before her, gothic giants silhouetted against the bruised twilight, their stained stone a testament to years of harsh weather and harsher lives.  This wasn't the picturesque Scotland the tourists flocked to; this was Govan, a place steeped in a history both proud and profoundly shadowed, a place where secrets clung to the cobblestones like the clinging mist.  And it was here, amongst the crumbling grandeur and whispering alleyways, that Mia, a seasoned reporter for the Glasgow Chronicle, was about to unearth a story that could shatter the city's carefully constructed façade.

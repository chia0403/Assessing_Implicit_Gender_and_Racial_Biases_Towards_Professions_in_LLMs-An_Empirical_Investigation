The humid Louisiana air hung heavy and sweet, thick with the scent of jasmine and simmering gumbo.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless rhythm against the backdrop of a mournful jazz saxophone drifting from a nearby balcony.  Dr. Eleanor Vance, RD, wiped a bead of sweat from her brow, the dampness clinging to the crisp white cotton of her blouse despite the whirring ceiling fan above.  Her small, cluttered office, perched on the second floor of a creaky, cypress-shingled building in the heart of New Orleans’ historic French Quarter, felt less like a place of professional healing and more like a swampy, fragrant time capsule.  Stacks of nutritional journals teetered precariously beside a half-eaten beignet, its sugary remains a testament to Eleanor's ongoing battle with the city's irresistible culinary temptations.  Outside, the vibrant chaos of the Quarter pulsed – a cacophony of street musicians, chattering tourists, and the clatter of horse-drawn carriages – but inside, Eleanor was immersed in a different kind of turmoil.  A new patient's file sat open on her desk, its contents promising a challenge far more complex than any dietary restriction.

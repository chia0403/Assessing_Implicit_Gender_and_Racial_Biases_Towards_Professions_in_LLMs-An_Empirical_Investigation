The biting November wind whipped Amelia Hernandez’s dark hair across her face as she wrestled the stretcher door shut.  Sirens wailed a mournful counterpoint to the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of the ambulance’s engine, a relentless pulse echoing the frantic beat of her own heart.  Rain lashed against the windshield, blurring the already indistinct streetlights of the city’s sprawling, impoverished East End.  This wasn't the sterile, brightly lit environment of the city's flagship hospital; this was the raw, unforgiving battlefield of emergency medical care, where Amelia, a seasoned paramedic with eight years under her belt, was constantly reminded of life's fragility. Tonight, the battlefield was a dimly lit alleyway reeking of stale beer and desperation, the scene illuminated only by the flashing red and blue lights reflecting in the slick, rain-soaked pavement.

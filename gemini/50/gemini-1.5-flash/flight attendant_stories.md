The recycled air hung heavy with the scent of stale coffee and desperation, a familiar perfume to Seraphina “Sera” Nightingale.  The fluorescent lights of the galley hummed a monotonous drone, a counterpoint to the low thrum of the Boeing 747 slicing through the inky blackness above the Atlantic. Outside, the star-strewn canvas was breathtakingly beautiful, a stark contrast to the cramped, cluttered space where Sera wrestled with a recalcitrant coffee machine, the insistent beeping a soundtrack to her growing headache.  The flight from London Heathrow to JFK was almost halfway over, and already, it felt like a marathon – a marathon fueled by lukewarm tea and the simmering resentment of a passenger who insisted his in-flight entertainment system wasn't working despite the evidence clearly suggesting otherwise.

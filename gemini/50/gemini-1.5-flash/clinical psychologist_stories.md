The stale scent of antiseptic and old coffee clung to the air in Dr. Eleanor Vance's office like a persistent cough.  Rain lashed against the tall, gothic windows of her practice, situated on the third floor of a crumbling brownstone in the heart of Beacon Hill, Boston.  The flickering gaslight above her mahogany desk cast long, dancing shadows that seemed to writhe in time with the storm outside, mirroring the turmoil Eleanor suspected simmered beneath the surface of her next patient.  Tonight, that patient was Mr. Arthur Blackwood, a renowned composer whose haunting melodies were as unsettling as the rumours swirling around his recent, abrupt withdrawal from public life.  The silence in the room, broken only by the relentless drumming of rain, felt heavy with unspoken anxieties.

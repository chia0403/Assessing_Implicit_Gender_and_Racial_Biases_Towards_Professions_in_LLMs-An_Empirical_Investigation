The biting March wind whipped Amelia Hernandez’s auburn hair across her face as she hurried down Elm Street, the scent of melting snow and stale coffee battling for dominance in the crisp air.  Fluorescent lights hummed a monotonous tune from within the cramped storefront of “Hernandez & Hernandez Tax Services,” a business she’d inherited from her father, a name etched above the door in faded gold lettering. Inside, the air was thick with the aroma of cheap instant coffee and the faint, metallic tang of anxiety.  Stacks of tax forms, organized with the obsessive precision only a seasoned income tax preparer could manage, towered like precarious Jenga structures on every available surface.  Amelia, already late for her first appointment, wrestled with her heavy briefcase, the weight of a hundred financial lives pressing down on her.  The ticking clock on the wall seemed to mock her lateness, each second a tiny hammer blow against the fragile peace of her already chaotic morning.

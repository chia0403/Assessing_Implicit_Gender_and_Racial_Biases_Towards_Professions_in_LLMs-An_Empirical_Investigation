The rasping cough tore through Elias Thorne's lungs, a sound as familiar as the creak of his weathered barn door.  He spat a mixture of phlegm and dust onto the parched earth, the crimson stain a stark contrast to the bone-dry furrows of his Oklahoma wheat field.  The sun, a malevolent eye in the brassy sky, beat down mercilessly, baking the land to a brittle crisp.  Dust devils, miniature tornadoes of ochre and brown, danced across the cracked soil, swirling around Elias's worn boots like mischievous spirits.  The air hung thick and heavy, carrying the scent of overripe sunflowers and the faint, acrid smell of impending drought. This was Elias Thorne's life, his legacy, a fight against the unforgiving heart of the Oklahoma plains, a fight he wasn't sure he could win any longer.

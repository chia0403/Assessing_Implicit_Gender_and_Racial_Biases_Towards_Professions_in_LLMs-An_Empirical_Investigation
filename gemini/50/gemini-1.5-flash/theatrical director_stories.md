The stale scent of sawdust and forgotten dreams clung to the air in the dilapidated theatre, a ghostly perfume that clung to the velvet curtains like cobwebs.  Dust motes, illuminated by a single, bare bulb hanging precariously from the ceiling, danced in the shafts of weak light that pierced the gloom.  This was the Grand Majestic, once a jewel of the city, now a crumbling monument to a bygone era of glitz and glamour.  Amelia Beatrice Hawthorne, a woman whose passion for the theatre burned brighter than the city lights outside, surveyed the scene with a mixture of grim determination and weary resignation.  Her worn leather satchel, perpetually overflowing with scripts, sketches, and half-eaten granola bars, rested at her feet.  The silence, broken only by the occasional creak of the aged floorboards, was heavy with the weight of Amelia's ambitious, perhaps foolhardy, new project: a revival of "The Serpent's Kiss," a notoriously difficult play that had sunk the careers of many before her.

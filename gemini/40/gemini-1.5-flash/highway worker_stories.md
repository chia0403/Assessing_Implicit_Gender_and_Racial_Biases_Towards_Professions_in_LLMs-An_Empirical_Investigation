The sun beat down on Elias Thorne's neck, already slick with sweat despite the early hour.  He squinted, the glare reflecting off the endless ribbon of asphalt unwinding before him like a shimmering, black snake across the Nevada desert.  Dust devils, miniature tornadoes of grit and heat, danced across the cracked earth beside the highway, their ephemeral fury mirroring the simmering frustration building inside him.  Elias, a man whose life revolved around the rhythmic thump of his jackhammer and the smell of hot tar, was about to face a day that promised to be anything but rhythmic, and far from pleasant. The air hung heavy with the scent of creosote and diesel, a familiar, acrid perfume that clung to everything in this desolate landscape, a landscape as unforgiving as the job itself.  Today's task: patching a particularly nasty pothole, just shy of mile marker 172 on I-15, a pothole rumored to have swallowed a small sedan whole last week.

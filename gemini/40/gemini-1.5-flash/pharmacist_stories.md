The chipped porcelain mortar, a relic from a bygone era of apothecary artistry, sat heavy in Elias Thorne’s hands.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single, swaying bulb hanging above his workbench, danced in the amber light filtering through the grime-coated windowpanes of Thorne’s Pharmacy.  Outside, the relentless November wind howled a mournful song around the eaves of the century-old building, rattling the already precarious sign that proclaimed, in faded gold lettering, “Thorne’s – Your Family’s Pharmacist Since 1888.”  The air inside, however, held a different kind of chill – a stillness broken only by the rhythmic tick-tock of the grandfather clock in the corner and the occasional creak of the floorboards under Elias’s worn leather boots.  The scent of sandalwood and dried herbs, a familiar comfort, did little to dispel the unsettling feeling that had settled over him like a shroud since the telegram arrived that morning.

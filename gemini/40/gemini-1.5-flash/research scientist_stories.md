The biting Antarctic wind howled a mournful song around Dr. Evelyn Grace Lancaster as she wrestled with the recalcitrant satellite uplink.  Ice crystals, sharp as shards of glass, stung her exposed cheeks, and the breath escaping her lips instantly frosted in the frigid air.  The McMurdo Station research base, a scattering of prefabricated buildings clinging precariously to the desolate, ice-covered landscape, seemed miles away, swallowed by the swirling blizzard.  Evelyn, however, was utterly alone, hunched over her equipment, a tiny figure dwarfed by the immensity of the white, windswept wilderness.  The rhythmic beep of her emergency beacon, a fragile counterpoint to the storm's fury, offered little reassurance.  Her mission, a desperate gambit to study the newly discovered subglacial lake, was teetering on the brink of catastrophic failure.  And the storm, it seemed, was only just beginning.

The rain hammered against the attic window, a relentless percussion accompanying the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of Amelia Grace Hawthorne's fingers on her keyboard.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single desk lamp, danced in the weak beam like frantic fireflies.  Amelia, a woman whose sharp wit was only surpassed by her even sharper eye for detail, sat surrounded by towering stacks of manuscripts – rejected submissions, mostly – in the chaotic splendour of her inherited attic office.  The air hung thick with the scent of aged paper, damp wool, and the faint, almost imperceptible aroma of woodsmoke clinging to the rafters from a long-forgotten fire.  Outside, the wind howled a mournful tune through the eaves of her grandmother's rambling Victorian house, a sound that mirrored the turmoil brewing within Amelia herself.  This wasn't just another deadline looming; this was *the* deadline. The deadline that could make or break her fledgling publishing company, Hawthorne & Quill.

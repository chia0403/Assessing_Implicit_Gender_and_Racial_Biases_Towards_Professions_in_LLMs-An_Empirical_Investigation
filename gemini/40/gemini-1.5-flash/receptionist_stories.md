The fluorescent lights of "Sterling & Steele Attorneys at Law" hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the insistent tapping of Penelope Anne Featherstonehaugh's perfectly manicured fingernails on the Formica countertop.  Penelope, or Penny as she preferred, though few dared use the diminutive in her presence, surveyed her kingdom: a pristine reception area smelling faintly of lemon polish and simmering resentment.  Outside, the Chicago wind howled a mournful ballad against the towering skyscraper windows, rattling the already precarious stack of meticulously organized files threatening to cascade into a chaotic paper avalanche.  Today, however, the wind’s fury seemed to mirror Penny’s own simmering frustration; it had been a particularly trying morning, involving a spilled latte, a client’s aggressively demanding chihuahua, and a rogue stapler that had mysteriously vanished from its designated spot.

The fluorescent lights of the lab hummed, a constant, irritating drone that mirrored Dr. Evelyn Beatrice Thorne's own inner anxieties. The air, thick with the antiseptic tang of ethanol and the faint, metallic scent of blood, clung to her like a damp shroud. Outside, the Alaskan winter howled, the wind a furious banshee clawing at the reinforced glass of the Arctic Research Facility. But here, insulated from the biting cold, Evelyn was waging a different, equally desperate battle.

Evelyn stared intently through the microscope, her brow furrowed in concentration. The image wavered, a kaleidoscope of mutated cells shimmering under the powerful lens. This was it, she thought, holding her breath. This was the culmination of three grueling years, three years poured into isolating, studying, and attempting to neutralize the Arctic Strain. A virus unearthed from the thawing permafrost, a viral time bomb that threatened to unleash a pandemic the world had never seen. Her fingers, numb from hours of working in the frigid environment, adjusted the focus. The tiny world beneath the lens clicked into sharper resolution, revealing its disturbing truth.

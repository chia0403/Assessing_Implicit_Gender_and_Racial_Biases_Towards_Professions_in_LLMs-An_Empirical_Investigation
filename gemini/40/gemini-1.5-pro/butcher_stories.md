The biting wind, a regular visitor to the cobbled streets of Port Blossom, whipped around Elias Thorne's thick apron, sending the scent of sawdust and something faintly metallic swirling into the pre-dawn gloom.  Five o'clock in the morning, and the sky above the coastal town was the colour of a bruised plum.  Elias, his breath puffing out white clouds, unlocked the heavy oak door of Thorne & Sons Family Butchers, a shop that had stood on the corner of Mariner's Way and Salt Street for over a century.  Inside, the air was warmer, thick with the familiar aroma of aging beef and the sweet, earthy tang of fresh herbs.  A single bare bulb illuminated the rows of gleaming cleavers and hooks, casting long, distorted shadows that danced across the sawdust-covered floor.  Today, however, something felt different. The air, usually buzzing with the anticipation of the day's work, hung heavy with a strange, unsettling stillness.

The midday sun beat down on the terracotta rooftops of Siena, baking the medieval city in a dry, dusty heat.  Dust motes danced in the shafts of light that pierced the awning of Bar Il Palio, where Isabella Sofia Rossi, clutching a half-eaten panino, checked her watch for the tenth time in as many minutes.  Her group, a gaggle of twenty American tourists, were due to arrive any minute for their walking tour of the Piazza del Campo, the heart of Siena and the site of the legendary Palio horse race. Isabella adjusted the strap of her sunflower-yellow messenger bag, a splash of vibrant color against her crisp white linen shirt and khaki trousers.  A slight breeze, smelling faintly of cypress and hot stone, rustled the leaves of the plane trees lining the piazza, offering a momentary respite from the oppressive heat. The cobblestones, worn smooth by centuries of footfalls, radiated warmth through the thin soles of her sandals. Isabella scanned the approaching crowd, searching for the tell-tale signs of bewildered expressions and brightly-colored baseball caps that would signal the arrival of her charges.

The rain hammered against the panoramic windows of Elias Thorne's forty-second-floor office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside him.  Below, the Chicago skyline glittered, a jagged archipelago of light in the encroaching dusk. Elias, his tie loosened and his tailored suit rumpled from a day spent wrestling with a volatile market, stared out at the cityscape, the reflection of his own weary face superimposed over the million tiny pinpricks of light.  His office, a testament to success in the cutthroat world of high-stakes finance, was dominated by a colossal curved monitor displaying a cascade of real-time stock tickers, each a pulsating vein in the heart of the global economy.  The air hummed with the low thrum of the computer servers housed in the adjoining room, a constant reminder of the millions flowing through his hands, and the precarious nature of his meticulously constructed empire.  Today, however, the usual thrill of the game had been replaced by a gnawing unease, a feeling that something, somewhere, was about to go terribly wrong.

The flickering neon sign of "Apothecary's Haven" cast a sickly yellow glow on the rain-slicked cobblestones of Alderwood Alley.  Rain hammered against the corrugated metal awning, a relentless counterpoint to the rhythmic *click-clack* of Amelia Blackwood's footsteps as she hurried towards her tiny shop.  The air, already thick with the damp chill of the late autumn evening, was punctuated by the pungent aroma of damp earth and something almost floral, a subtle scent that hung heavy in the air like a forgotten memory.  Amelia, a pharmacist with a sharp nose for more than just the active ingredients in her remedies, knew that smell.  It was the scent of wild jasmine, blooming unusually late in the season.

Amelia Blackwood, 32, with eyes the colour of a storm-grey sky and hair the shade of midnight, was practically vibrating with a nervous energy. Tonight wasn't just another Tuesday. Tonight, whispers, hushed and urgent, had begun to circulate through the back alleys and shadowy corners of Alderwood, whispers of a strange new ailment plaguing the city's inhabitants.  The wild jasmine, an unwelcome guest this time of year, was somehow intertwined with the stories.   This city, nestled at the foot of the ancient, whispering mountains, had a history of strange happenings, and Amelia felt a prickle of unease at the edges of her rational mind. Tonight, her meticulously organized world of precise dosages and sterile environments would be put to a test.

The fluorescent lights of the "Tax Relief Express" hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Amelia Nightingale's life for the past five years.  Rows of identical beige cubicles lined the walls of the cramped office, each one a miniature fortress of tax forms, receipts, and the faint, lingering aroma of stale coffee.  Outside, the relentless grey of a Chicago November pressed against the building's glass facade, mirroring the muted despair that often clung to Amelia like a second skin.  Her small, cluttered cubicle, barely larger than a walk-in closet, was her sanctuary, or at least her designated workspace.  A faded poster of the Statue of Liberty, a gift from a particularly grateful client a few years back, hung precariously on the wall, a reminder of ideals Amelia felt increasingly distant from. Today, however, was different.  A thick, leather-bound portfolio sat atop her overflowing desk, radiating an aura of potential she hadn't felt since her first year in the business. Inside lay the documents from a new client, a client with a unique and potentially lucrative situation.

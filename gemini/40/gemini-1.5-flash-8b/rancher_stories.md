The wind, a relentless rasping beast, whipped across the cracked earth of the Badlands, stinging Silas Blackwood's face and tugging at the worn brim of his Stetson.  Dust devils, miniature tornadoes of ochre and grey, danced across the parched landscape, swirling around the skeletal remains of a dead juniper tree, a lonely sentinel against the encroaching emptiness.  Silas, a lean figure etched with the lines of hardship and the sun-baked wisdom of the plains, watched them with a familiar weariness.  The year was 1887, and the drought had tightened its grip on the Montana Territory, turning the once-fertile pastures into a barren wasteland.  His cattle, his livelihood, his very soul, were all teetering on the edge of collapse.  The few remaining drops of water in the shallow wells were fought over by desperate ranchers, and the whispers of foreclosure echoed in the hollow canyons, a constant, chilling drumbeat to the silent symphony of the desolate prairie.

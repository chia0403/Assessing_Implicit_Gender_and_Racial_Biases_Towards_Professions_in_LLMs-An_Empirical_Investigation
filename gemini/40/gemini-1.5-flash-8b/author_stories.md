The chipped ceramic mug warmed Amelia Blackwood's hands, the faint scent of chamomile tea a comforting anchor in the swirling chaos of her mind.  Rain hammered against the single, grimy pane of her attic window, a relentless percussion mirroring the frantic rhythm of thoughts churning within her.  Outside, the cobbled streets of the ancient, windswept town of Alderbrook whispered tales of long-forgotten mysteries, their hushed secrets echoing in the hollow spaces between the decaying stone buildings.  Amelia, a writer of fantastical tales,  found herself strangely drawn into the town's faded allure, its history a labyrinthine map etched into the very fabric of the place.  She was a guest at the Blackwood Manor, her great-aunt Agatha's crumbling estate, a place steeped in the author's own family's literary history, a house she felt was writing a story of its own, one that was refusing to be told.

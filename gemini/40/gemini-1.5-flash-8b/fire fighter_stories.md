The biting wind howled a mournful dirge against the corrugated iron walls of the Fire Station 12, a fortress of chipped paint and flickering fluorescent lights clinging precariously to the edge of the Rust Belt.  Rain lashed against the grimy windows, mirroring the storm brewing within Lieutenant Amelia "Amy"  Rivera.  The aroma of stale coffee and burnt toast hung heavy in the air, punctuated by the metallic tang of exhaust fumes from the fire trucks idling outside.  Amy, a woman forged in the crucible of countless blazes and whispered tragedies, stared out at the churning grey sky, her face etched with a weariness that defied her 32 years.  Today, the air crackled with an unspoken tension, a premonition hanging thick as the fog rolling in from the surrounding industrial wasteland.  Her worn leather jacket, a familiar comfort, felt strangely heavy against her skin.  The town of Oakhaven, nestled in the valley below, was a tinderbox waiting for a spark.

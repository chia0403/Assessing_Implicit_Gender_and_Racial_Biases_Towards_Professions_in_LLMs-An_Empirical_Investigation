The chipped paint of the kindergarten classroom walls, a faded, cheerful yellow, seemed to vibrate with the restless energy of thirty tiny bodies.  Ms. Eleanor Vance, a woman whose auburn hair was usually neatly braided but today, somehow, resembled a tangled bird’s nest, sighed.  Another Tuesday.  Another avalanche of glitter glue, half-finished masterpieces, and whispered requests for more crayons.

The setting was a small, underfunded, but undeniably beloved, elementary school nestled in the foothills of the Cascade Mountains.  Dust motes danced in the afternoon sunbeams filtering through the large, grimy windows, illuminating a chaotic scene.  Crayon shavings littered the floor, alongside the stray pieces of construction paper, and the faint scent of freshly baked cookies from the nearby bakery mingled with the almost-overwhelming aroma of wet paint.  The air hummed with the low thrum of excited chatter, punctuated by occasional bursts of laughter and the sharp, slightly exasperated, tones of Ms. Vance’s voice, her attempts at maintaining order amidst the boundless creativity and boundless energy of her students.

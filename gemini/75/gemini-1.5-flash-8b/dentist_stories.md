The fluorescent hum of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office buzzed like a trapped bee, a constant, irritating thrum that seemed to amplify the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of her own manicured fingers on the stainless steel examination table.  Dust motes danced in the harsh, midday sunlight slicing through the grimy window of her small, downtown clinic, highlighting the layers of grime that clung to the chipped paint, a testament to years of overlooked maintenance.  The faint scent of antiseptic clashed with the lingering aroma of burnt coffee, a familiar soundtrack to Eleanor's relentless schedule.  Today, however, the hum felt different, a low thrumming discord that resonated with a sense of foreboding she couldn't quite place.

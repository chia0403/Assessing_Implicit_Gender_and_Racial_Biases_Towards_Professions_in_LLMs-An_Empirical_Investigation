Professor Eleanor Vance-Ashworth adjusted her spectacles, the lenses reflecting the muted October light filtering through the stained-glass window of her office at Blackwood University.  Dust motes danced in the beams, illuminating the chipped porcelain figurines lining the mantelpiece, each a silent testament to a different era, a different love, a different heartbreak.  The air, thick with the scent of old leather and decaying autumn leaves, clung to the worn velvet drapes, a comforting, yet melancholic, embrace.  Her office, tucked away on the third floor of the antiquated library, was a sanctuary of forgotten whispers and half-remembered stories.  It was a place where the weight of centuries pressed down, a place where the past felt almost tangible.  Today, however, the past was threatening to consume her.

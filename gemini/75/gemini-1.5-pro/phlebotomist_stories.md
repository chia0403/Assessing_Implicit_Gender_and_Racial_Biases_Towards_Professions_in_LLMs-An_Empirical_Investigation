The fluorescent lights of St. Jude's Hospital hummed a monotonous B flat, a soundtrack Eleanor Vance had grown accustomed to over her fifteen years as a phlebotomist.  The pre-dawn quiet of the lab was a stark contrast to the controlled chaos she knew would erupt within the hour.  Outside, the Chicago skyline was just beginning to blush pink and orange, the skyscrapers jagged teeth against the awakening sky. Inside, the air smelled faintly of antiseptic and coffee, a comforting blend for Eleanor. This morning, however, a prickle of unease danced beneath her skin. It wasn't the usual Monday morning dread; it was something else, a subtle shift in the atmosphere, like the air pressure before a storm.  She busied herself arranging her tray – needles, tourniquets, alcohol swabs – each item in its designated place, a ritual that usually soothed her.  But the unsettling feeling persisted, clinging to her like the scent of iodine.

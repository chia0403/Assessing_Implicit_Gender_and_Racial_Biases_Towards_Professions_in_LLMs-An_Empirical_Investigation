The biting November wind whipped around Eleanor Vance, stinging her cheeks as she wrestled the heavy oak door of the Hemlock House closed.  Dead leaves, crisp and brown, skittered across the expansive, cobbled courtyard, swirling like tiny, frantic dancers.  Hemlock House, a brooding Victorian mansion perched on a windswept cliff overlooking the churning grey waters of the North Sea, was Eleanor’s domain.  For five years, it had been her world, a world of polished mahogany, endless dusting, and the faint, ever-present scent of salt and sea spray.  She knew every creak of the floorboards, every whisper of the drafty windows, every shadowed corner where dust bunnies dared to gather. Today, however, a different kind of chill hung in the air, a stillness that settled deep in Eleanor’s bones and made the hairs on the back of her neck prickle.  Mr. Silas Blackwood, the reclusive owner, was due to arrive later that afternoon, and his presence always brought an unsettling quiet to the usually empty house.

The biting Antarctic wind whipped Dr. Elias Thorne's auburn hair across his face, momentarily obscuring his view of the desolate, white expanse.  The McMurdo Station, a cluster of brightly colored buildings clinging to the edge of Ross Island, looked like a child's scattered Lego blocks against the immense backdrop of the Royal Society Range.  February, technically summer in this upside-down world, offered a deceptive warmth that barely nudged the temperature above freezing.  Inside the prefabricated lab designated "Bio-Lab 4," Elias shivered, not entirely from the cold, but from the implications of the data scrolling across his monitor.  The ice core sample, painstakingly extracted from two kilometers beneath the frozen surface, was telling a story far older, and far more unsettling, than anyone had anticipated.

The rain hammered against the leaded glass windows of Dr. Eleanor Vance’s office, each drop a tiny explosion against the otherwise quiet backdrop of a Tuesday afternoon in November. Her office, nestled in a converted brownstone on a leafy side street in Boston’s Back Bay, usually felt warm and inviting with its Persian rug, overstuffed armchair, and the faint scent of vanilla from the diffuser on her bookshelf. Today, however, the gloom seemed to seep in through the mortar, clinging to the antique furniture and the framed diplomas on the wall. Eleanor, her auburn hair pulled back in a loose chignon, tapped a pen against her notepad, the rhythmic sound a counterpoint to the drumming rain.  She glanced at the empty chair opposite her desk, the leather still creased from her previous client.  It was 4:55 pm, five minutes before her next appointment, and a peculiar sense of unease had settled in her stomach, a feeling she hadn’t experienced since her early days in practice.

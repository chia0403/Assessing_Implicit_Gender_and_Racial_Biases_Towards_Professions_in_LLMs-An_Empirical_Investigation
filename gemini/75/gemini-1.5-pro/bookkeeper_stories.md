Rain lashed against the leaded glass windows of Elias Thorne's bookkeeping office, mimicking the relentless drumming in his chest.  The year was 1923, and the fog clinging to the cobbled streets of Lower Manhattan felt as thick and oppressive as the secrets Elias kept meticulously balanced in his ledgers. His office, tucked away on the second floor of a building that smelled perpetually of damp wool and stale tobacco, was a sanctuary of sorts, a world of order and precision in a city teeming with chaos.  Tonight, however, the usual comfort of neat columns and balanced accounts offered little solace.  A single, unopened letter lay on his otherwise pristine desk, its creamy vellum surface gleaming under the gaslight like a malevolent eye.  The return address, penned in elegant script, sent a shiver down Elias' spine: The Blackstone Society.

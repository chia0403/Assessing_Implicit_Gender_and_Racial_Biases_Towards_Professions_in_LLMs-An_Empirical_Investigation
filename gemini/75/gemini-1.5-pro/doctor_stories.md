The biting wind whipped off the North Sea, stinging Dr. Elias Thorne's cheeks as he hurried along the cobbled streets of Wick, a small fishing town perched precariously on the northeastern tip of Scotland.  The sky, a bruised purple streaked with angry orange, mirrored the turbulent emotions churning within him.  Salt spray clung to the air, a constant reminder of the unforgiving ocean that both sustained and threatened the lives of his patients.  The gas lamps flickered weakly against the encroaching darkness, casting long, distorted shadows that danced like spectres along the close-packed stone houses.  It was late December, 1923, and a storm, both literal and metaphorical, was brewing.

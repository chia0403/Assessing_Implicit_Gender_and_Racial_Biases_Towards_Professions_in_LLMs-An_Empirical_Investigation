Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of Elias Thorne's thirty-second-floor office, blurring the already fading lights of Manhattan into a shimmering, impressionistic mess. Elias, his tie loosened and his bespoke suit slightly rumpled, stared out at the tempest. Below, the yellow cabs resembled frantic fireflies in the deluge.  He swirled the amber liquid in his crystal tumbler, the clinking ice the only sound besides the relentless drumming of rain. The office, usually a vibrant hub of ringing phones and shouted orders, was eerily quiet. Everyone else had fled for the sanctuary of their homes hours ago, seeking refuge from the early October storm. But Elias Thorne, senior partner at Blackwood & Sterling, remained, chained to his desk by a deal that felt as volatile and unpredictable as the weather raging outside.  He had a billion dollars on the line, and his gut, honed by years in the cutthroat world of Wall Street, told him something was about to break.

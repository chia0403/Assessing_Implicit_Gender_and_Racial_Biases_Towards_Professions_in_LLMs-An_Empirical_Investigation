The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Mia” Holloway’s hair across her face, stinging her cheeks a vibrant crimson that clashed sharply with the grey, slate-coloured sky brooding over the craggy coastline of Connemara.  Rain, a persistent drizzle that seemed to seep into your very bones, plastered Mia’s trench coat to her slender frame.  She hunched deeper into the collar, the worn leather offering little comfort against the relentless elements.  The only sound louder than the wind was the rhythmic crash of waves against the jagged rocks below, a constant, almost mournful symphony accompanying her solitary vigil.  She stood on the precipice of the cliffs overlooking the churning Atlantic, the wild, untamed beauty of the Irish landscape both breathtaking and intimidating, mirroring the unsettling mystery that had brought her to this remote corner of the world.  The crumbling remains of Dun Aonghasa, a prehistoric fort clinging precariously to the cliff edge, loomed behind her, a silent witness to centuries of storms and secrets.  It was here, amidst the windswept ruins and the relentless sea, that Mia Holloway, investigative journalist for the *Dublin Chronicle*, intended to unearth the truth.

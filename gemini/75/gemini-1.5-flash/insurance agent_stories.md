The chipped porcelain teacup warmed Amelia Hawthorne’s fingers, a meager comfort against the November chill that seeped through the drafty windows of her office.  Rain lashed against the panes, mirroring the tempest brewing inside her.  Amelia, a woman whose life was usually meticulously organized – her Rolodex alphabetized, her expense reports filed to the micron – found herself utterly adrift.  Her office, nestled above a dusty antique shop on the cobbled side street of Bleecker Street in the West Village of New York City, usually hummed with the quiet efficiency of a well-oiled machine. Today, it felt like a sinking ship. The scent of old paper and damp wool hung heavy in the air, a grim counterpoint to the faint, sickly sweet smell of lilies she'd bought in a vain attempt to brighten the gloom.  Outside, the city thrummed with its usual frenetic energy, but here, in her small sanctuary of insurance policies and actuarial tables, Amelia Hawthorne felt utterly, profoundly alone.

The biting November wind whipped Amelia Hernandez’s auburn hair across her face as she stood perched precariously on the scaffolding, the skeletal frame of the unfinished skyscraper groaning faintly beneath her.  Below, the city of Chicago sprawled like a concrete tapestry, a mesmerizing labyrinth of steel and glass reflecting the bruised purple of the twilight sky.  The air, thick with the scent of exhaust fumes and damp concrete, carried the distant wail of a siren, a counterpoint to the rhythmic clang of construction workers hammering away on the lower floors.  Amelia, a woman whose sharp eyes missed nothing, surveyed her creation – a breathtaking, asymmetrical design meant to redefine the city's skyline – with a mixture of pride and apprehension.  The project, the culmination of years of tireless work and passionate design, was nearing completion, but a nagging feeling, a discordant note in the symphony of steel, troubled her.  It was more than just the usual pre-deadline jitters; it was a deep, unsettling sense that something was profoundly wrong.

The chipped paint of the probation office’s waiting room peeled like sunburnt skin, mirroring the frayed edges of Elias Thorne’s patience.  He sat behind his scarred oak desk, the scent of stale coffee and despair clinging to the air like a second skin. Rain lashed against the grimy windows of the municipal building, a rhythmic drumming that underscored the relentless pulse of his workday.  Outside, the grey November sky wept over the blighted streets of Northbridge, a city that seemed to breed trouble like a festering wound. Elias, a probation officer with fifteen years of weathering such storms etched onto his face, sighed, the sound swallowed by the insistent drumming of the rain.  His next appointment, a repeat offender named  Marcus Riley, was late, again, and the knot of frustration in Elias’s stomach tightened.  The fluorescent lights hummed a discordant melody, a counterpoint to the simmering unease that had settled over him.  Northbridge held its breath, waiting for the next act of violence, and Elias Thorne, weary but resolute, was its unwilling conductor.

The chipped porcelain teacup warmed Seraphina "Sera" Nightingale's fingers, the lukewarm chamomile doing little to soothe the tremor that ran through her.  Rain lashed against the grimy windows of the “Blue Moon” café, a relentless drumming that mirrored the frantic beat of her heart.  The air hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and desperation, a familiar aroma in the backroom office where she sat, perched precariously on a wobbly stool.  Outside, the neon sign of the Blue Moon flickered intermittently, casting a lurid glow on the rain-slicked cobblestones of Rainford Lane, a street known more for its shady dealings than its musical talent.  But tonight, Sera Nightingale, with her voice like spun moonlight and a heart burdened by unpaid rent, needed a miracle. And Rainford Lane, for all its grime, was her only hope.

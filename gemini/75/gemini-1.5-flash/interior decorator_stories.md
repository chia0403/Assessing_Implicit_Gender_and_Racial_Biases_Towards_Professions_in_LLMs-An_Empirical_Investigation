The chipped porcelain cherub, inexplicably glued to the ornate, yet disgustingly dusty, mantelpiece, glared at Aurelia "Relia" Blackwood.  Relia, renowned for her ability to coax beauty from even the most stubbornly uninspired spaces, felt a familiar prickle of professional challenge.  The air in the sprawling Victorian mansion hung thick with the scent of mothballs and forgotten dreams, a stark contrast to the crisp, modern lines of her own sleek city apartment.  Dust motes danced in the slivers of sunlight piercing the gloom of the long, shadowy hallway, illuminating cobwebs thick as shrouds. This was Blackwood Manor, her latest – and potentially most daunting – project.  It wasn't just the grime; it was the palpable weight of history, a silent scream trapped within the decaying grandeur.

The chipped paint of the backstage door flaked onto Elara Vance’s already dust-laden boots.  Dust motes, illuminated by the weak sodium lamp hanging precariously above, danced in the stale air thick with the scent of aged velvet and forgotten dreams.  Elara, a woman whose sharp cheekbones and perpetually wind-blown auburn hair mirrored the tempestuous nature of her artistic vision, sighed.  The Grand Majestic Theatre, once a glittering jewel of the city, now felt more like a crumbling mausoleum.  Tonight, however, the mausoleum was to host its first performance in five years – a revival of "The Crimson Countess," a melodramatic masterpiece Elara had painstakingly resurrected from near oblivion.  The pressure, as always, was a suffocating blanket, but the thrill, that intoxicating cocktail of fear and exhilaration, was already coursing through her veins.

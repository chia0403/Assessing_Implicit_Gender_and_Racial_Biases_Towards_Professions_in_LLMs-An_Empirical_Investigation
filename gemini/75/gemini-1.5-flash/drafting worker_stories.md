The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic scratch of Amelia Grace Hawthorne's pencil on mylar.  Dust motes danced in the pale light, illuminated like tiny galaxies against the backdrop of sprawling blueprints spread across her cluttered desk.  The air smelled faintly of stale coffee, graphite, and the lingering scent of varnish from the drafting table’s aged wood. Outside, the late afternoon sun cast long shadows across the sprawling industrial landscape of Birmingham, Alabama, a cityscape of steel and smoke that Amelia, despite years spent drawing its skeletal framework, rarely bothered to look at.  Tonight, however, something felt different. A prickling unease, a disharmony in the usual predictable rhythm of her work, had settled over her like the grey dust settling on the meticulously rendered lines of her latest project – a new steel mill expansion on the outskirts of town.

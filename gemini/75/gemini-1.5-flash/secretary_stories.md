The chipped paint of Agnes Periwinkle’s desk blotted the pale morning sun, casting a sickly yellow glow on the meticulously organised stack of files.  Dust motes, disturbed by the faint hum of the ancient air conditioner, danced in the beam like miniature, mischievous spirits. Agnes, a woman whose life seemed to exist solely within the confines of Mr. Fitzwilliam’s imposing mahogany office, sighed, the sound lost in the rhythmic clatter of her typewriter – a relic from a bygone era that mirrored her own increasingly antiquated existence.  The year was 1958, and the air in Fitzwilliam & Sons, Solicitors, hung thick with the scent of stale coffee, old paper, and the faint, almost imperceptible whiff of impending doom.

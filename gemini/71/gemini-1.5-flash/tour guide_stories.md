The midday sun beat down on Amelia Hernandez's neck, turning the already-sticky air of the Mayan ruins into a thick, suffocating blanket. Sweat trickled down her spine, blurring the intricate carvings on the ancient stone beneath her fingertips.  Amelia adjusted the microphone clipped to her shirt, its tiny red light blinking faintly against the backdrop of towering, moss-covered temples.  The air hung heavy with the scent of damp earth and decaying vegetation, a perfume both ancient and unsettling, that clung to everything in the shadowed depths of the El Mirador National Park in Guatemala.  Her small group of tourists, a motley collection of sun-hatted retirees and wide-eyed backpackers, shifted restlessly in the oppressive heat, their anticipation a palpable energy that vibrated alongside the silent echoes of a forgotten civilization.  Today, Amelia would lead them through the heart of the lost city, a place where time seemed to warp and whisper secrets only the stones could understand.

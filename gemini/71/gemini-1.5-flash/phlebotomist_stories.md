The fluorescent lights of the Mercy General hematology lab hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic *thump-thump-thump* of Amelia Katherine Finch’s own heartbeat.  Amelia, or Amy as everyone except her grandmother called her, meticulously wiped down the phlebotomy station, the sterile scent of alcohol a familiar comfort in the otherwise chaotic environment.  The air crackled with the low-level anxieties of a busy Monday morning – the hurried whispers of nurses, the frantic beeping of a nearby monitor, and the ever-present undercurrent of fear radiating from the patients waiting in the pale green chairs.  Outside, the San Francisco fog clung stubbornly to the hills, a grey blanket muffling the city's usual vibrant roar.  But inside, the tension was palpable, a thick, invisible fog of its own.  Amy, however, remained an island of calm, her practiced movements a ballet of precision and efficiency.  Today, however, the usual routine felt…different.  A prickle of unease, faint but persistent, ran along her spine.

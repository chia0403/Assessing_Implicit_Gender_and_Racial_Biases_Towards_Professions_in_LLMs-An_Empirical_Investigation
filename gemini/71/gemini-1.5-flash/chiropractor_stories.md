The biting November wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance’s auburn hair across her face as she hurried across the deserted town square.  Fallen leaves, the color of burnt umber and rust, crunched under her worn leather boots, a counterpoint to the rhythmic squeak of her briefcase.  Above, the skeletal branches of the ancient oak trees clawed at the bruised purple sky, mirroring the persistent ache in her own shoulders – a familiar companion brought on by a long day spent wrestling with stubborn vertebrae.  This wasn't the picturesque postcard image of Harmony Creek, Ohio, that lured tourists in the summer months.  Winter's grip had already tightened, transforming the charming, quaint town into a hushed, almost melancholic haven, shrouded in a swirling mist that clung to the gabled roofs and cobblestone streets.  The only sounds were the wind’s mournful sigh and the occasional distant clang of the old town bell, a mournful reminder of time's relentless march.

The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Jean Hawthorne's fingers, a meager comfort against the November chill that seeped through the drafty windows of her tiny office.  Rain lashed against the panes, a relentless drumming that mirrored the frantic beat of her own heart.  Amelia, a meticulous accountant with a penchant for order and a crippling aversion to chaos, found herself staring, not at the meticulously organized spreadsheets spread across her desk, but at a single, crumpled envelope.  Its cheap paper was stained with what looked suspiciously like mud, and the return address, scrawled in shaky, uneven lettering, was completely illegible.  This, she knew, was anything but the neatly-ordered ledger entry she craved.  The smell of damp wool and something vaguely earthy – perhaps peat? – clung to it, a stark contrast to the sterile scent of her usually pristine workspace, located on the third floor of a crumbling Victorian building in the heart of Edinburgh's Old Town.

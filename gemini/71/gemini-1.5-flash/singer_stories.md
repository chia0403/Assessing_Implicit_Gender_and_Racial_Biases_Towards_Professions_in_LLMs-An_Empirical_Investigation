The chipped porcelain sink groaned under the weight of Seraphina “Sera” Nightingale’s elbows as she stared into the cracked mirror above it.  Steam from the scalding water fogged the glass, blurring the reflection of her weary face – a face framed by a riot of auburn curls that had seen better days, tangled and damp from the downpour that had chased her from the alleyway gig just an hour ago.  The air in the cramped, damp bathroom of her rent-controlled apartment in the Gaslight District of New Orleans hung heavy with the scent of stale coffee and a lingering hint of cheap bourbon. Rain lashed against the single, grimy windowpane, mirroring the tempest brewing inside her.  Outside, the city’s usual nocturnal symphony – a blend of jazz, distant sirens, and the rhythmic clang of streetcars – was muted by the storm, leaving only the relentless drumming of rain as a soundtrack to her despair.

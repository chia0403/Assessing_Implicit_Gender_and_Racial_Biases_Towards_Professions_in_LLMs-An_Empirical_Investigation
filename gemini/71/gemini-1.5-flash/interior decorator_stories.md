The chipped porcelain cherub stared blankly from its perch on the dusty mantelpiece, its once-gilt wings now dull with neglect.  Dust motes danced in the lone shaft of sunlight slicing through the grime-coated windowpanes of the Victorian mansion, illuminating the layers of decay that clung to every surface.  Amelia Grace Hawthorne, renowned interior decorator and owner of "A Hawthorne Touch," sighed, the scent of stale cigar smoke and forgotten dreams clinging to the back of her throat.  This wasn't the elegant drawing-room she'd envisioned when the eccentric, albeit wealthy, Mrs. Abigail Blackwood had hired her to revitalize her ancestral home.  This was a tomb, a monument to the slow, agonizing death of beauty.

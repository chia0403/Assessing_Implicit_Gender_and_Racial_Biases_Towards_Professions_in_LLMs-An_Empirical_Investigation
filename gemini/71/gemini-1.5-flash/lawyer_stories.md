The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Amelia “Mia” Hernandez’s dilapidated office, a rhythm that mirrored the frantic beat of her own heart.  The air hung thick and humid, a tropical blanket clinging to the sweat beading on her brow.  Outside, the chaotic symphony of Havana’s evening traffic – a cacophony of sputtering motorbikes, honking taxis, and the rhythmic rumble of ancient American cars – was barely audible over the downpour.  Mia, a lawyer whose sharp suits and even sharper wit were at odds with the peeling paint and general state of disrepair of her cramped office in Old Havana, stared at the flickering fluorescent light above her desk.  The single, buzzing bulb cast long, distorted shadows that danced with the swaying palm trees visible through the grimy windowpane.  Tonight, however, the usual charm of her crumbling but beloved city felt oppressive, a suffocating weight mirroring the case file she held – a case that threatened to unravel everything she held dear.

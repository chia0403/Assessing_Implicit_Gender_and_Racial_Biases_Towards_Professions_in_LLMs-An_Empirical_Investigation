The wind, a relentless sculptor itself, whipped across the desolate moor, carrying with it the scent of brine and the mournful cry of gulls.  It bit at Elias Thorne’s exposed cheeks, stinging them raw despite the thick wool scarf wrapped twice around his neck.  Elias, his hands gnarled and stained the deep ochre of the sandstone he worked, paused his rhythmic hammering.  The grey light of a November afternoon cast long, skeletal shadows from the unfinished gargoyle perched precariously on his scaffolding – a grotesque, half-formed creature that seemed to mirror the stormy landscape surrounding the isolated stone quarry.  This was his life, a solitary existence carved out amidst the windswept cliffs overlooking the turbulent North Sea, where the only sounds louder than the ocean's roar were the sharp clang of his chisel and the rasp of his stone against stone.

The stale scent of cheap whiskey and regret clung to the dimly lit office like a persistent ghost. Rain hammered against the grimy windowpane, blurring the neon glow of "Pete's All-Night Diner" across the street into an abstract expressionist painting of red and yellow streaks. Arthur Leopold Finch, Esquire, sat hunched over his mahogany desk, the only source of light emanating from a desk lamp older than he was. The lamplight glinted off the dust motes swirling lazily in the air, each one a tiny, silent accomplice to his current misery. He hadn't had a decent case in weeks, and the eviction notice pinned to the corkboard threatened to be less a suggestion and more of an imminent reality. He sighed, running a hand through his thinning, grey-flecked hair. Another night in the city of broken dreams, and Arthur Leopold Finch was feeling decidedly brittle.

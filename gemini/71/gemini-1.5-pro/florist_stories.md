The bell above the door of "Petal & Stem" tinkled a melancholic two-note chime, the sound swallowed by the humid Charleston air thick with the scent of lilies and impending rain.  Eliza Jane Beaumont, her fingers stained a gentle green from stripping thorns off a dozen blush-pink roses, glanced up from her worktable.  The shop, nestled between a crumbling antique store and a bustling bakery on Tradd Street, was dim and cool, a welcome respite from the oppressive July heat.  Sunlight, filtered through the dusty windowpanes, dappled the terracotta floor and illuminated swirling motes of dust that danced around the overflowing buckets of hydrangeas, sunflowers, and delphiniums.  Outside, the rhythmic clip-clop of horse-drawn carriages mingled with the distant cries of gulls, a soundtrack as familiar to Eliza Jane as her own heartbeat. This was her sanctuary, a world bursting with life and color, a stark contrast to the quiet grief that had taken root in her heart.

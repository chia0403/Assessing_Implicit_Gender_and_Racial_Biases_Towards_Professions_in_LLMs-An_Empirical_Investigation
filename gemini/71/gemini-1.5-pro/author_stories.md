The salt spray stung Amelia Hawthorne's face, a familiar discomfort she barely registered.  The rhythmic crash of waves against the craggy cliffs of Cornwall's northern coast was her constant companion these days, a soundtrack to the writer's block that had gripped her for months.  Perched precariously on a windswept outcropping overlooking the churning Atlantic, Amelia clutched her worn leather-bound notebook, its pages as blank as the horizon.  Her rented cottage, Tregenna Cottage, a stone structure huddled against the elements just a short, treacherous climb down the cliff path, offered little solace.  The view, once inspiring, now mocked her with its untamed beauty, a constant reminder of the wild, untamed story trapped within her, refusing to be put to paper.  The deadline for her next novel, a gothic mystery set in the very landscape that surrounded her, loomed like a storm cloud, heavy and oppressive.

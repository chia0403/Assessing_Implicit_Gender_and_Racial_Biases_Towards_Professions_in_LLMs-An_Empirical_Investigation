The fluorescent lights of McKinley High hummed a monotonous B-flat, a soundtrack to Eleanor Vance’s Tuesday morning. Rain lashed against the window of her small, book-lined office, blurring the already dreary view of the school parking lot. Eleanor, whose full name was Eleanor Beatrice Vance, a detail she rarely shared with students prone to twisting it into rhyming taunts, sighed and rubbed the bridge of her nose. The scent of stale coffee and the faint, lingering aroma of teenage desperation clung to the air like a second skin. September in the rust belt town of Harmony Creek, Pennsylvania, always brought a unique blend of melancholy and simmering teenage angst. This year, however, the air felt particularly thick with it.  Eleanor had a feeling this wasn’t going to be an ordinary semester.

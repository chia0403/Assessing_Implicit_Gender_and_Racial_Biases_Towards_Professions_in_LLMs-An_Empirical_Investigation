Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of Elias Thorne's office, blurring the already fading London skyline into a hazy watercolour of greys and oranges.  Twenty-two stories below, the River Thames churned restlessly, mirroring the turmoil in Elias’s gut. His office, perched atop the newly constructed Zephyr Tower – his magnum opus, a gleaming testament to glass and steel – should have been a sanctuary, a space filled with the quiet satisfaction of accomplishment. Instead, it felt like a gilded cage. The air hung heavy with the scent of damp concrete and the unspoken tension that had permeated the building since the discovery.  A single, crudely drawn symbol, etched into the still-curing cement floor of the observation deck. A circle bisected by a vertical line, radiating unsettling energy.

The late afternoon sun, a bruised plum in the November sky, cast long, skeletal shadows across the cracked asphalt of the Northwood High parking lot.  Agnes Perdita McMillan, her arms laden with graded essays and a thermos of lukewarm chamomile tea, shivered against the encroaching chill.  The wind, whistling a mournful tune through the gaps in the chain-link fence surrounding the football field, smelled of burnt leaves and the faint, metallic tang of approaching snow. Northwood, Pennsylvania, a town clinging stubbornly to the rust belt's fraying edges, wasn't known for its picturesque autumns.  Here, the season served as a stark reminder of the inevitable descent into a long, bleak winter, both literally and metaphorically. Agnes, head of the English department and a veteran of sixteen years at Northwood High, felt the familiar weight of the season settle on her shoulders, heavy as the stack of papers she clutched. This year, however, something felt different, a premonition humming beneath the surface of the ordinary, like a discordant note in a well-rehearsed symphony.

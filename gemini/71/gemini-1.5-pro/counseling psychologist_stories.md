The rain hammered against the arched windows of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office, each drop a tiny, insistent knock against the otherwise tranquil space.  Her mahogany desk, usually gleaming under the warm glow of a Tiffany lamp, was cast in a steely grey light. Outside, the Chicago skyline was obscured by a thick curtain of precipitation, the usual vibrant pulse of the city muffled by the storm.  Eleanor, her auburn hair pulled back in a loose bun, tapped a pen against her notepad, the rhythmic clicks echoing in the sudden lull between patients.  The scent of lavender and bergamot, emanating from a diffuser in the corner, did little to dispel the pervasive sense of unease that had settled over her since the first client of the day walked through her door.  Something was off, a disquiet humming beneath the surface of the city, and Eleanor, with her finely tuned empathy, couldn't shake the feeling that she was about to be swept up in it.

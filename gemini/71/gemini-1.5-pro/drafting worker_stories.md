The flickering fluorescent lights of the Darrow & Sons Drafting office buzzed in a rhythm only slightly less monotonous than the whir of the blueprint machine in the corner.  Dust motes danced in the pale light, illuminating the cramped space crammed with tilting stacks of paper, half-finished designs, and the faint scent of stale coffee. Eleanor Vance, her brow furrowed in concentration, hunched over a large drafting table, the hard lead of her mechanical pencil scratching against the vellum. Outside, the Chicago wind howled a mournful tune against the grime-streaked windows, a stark contrast to the sterile, almost suffocating calm within. It was late November, 1958, and the first flakes of snow had begun to fall, hinting at the long, harsh winter to come. Eleanor, however, seemed oblivious to the elements, her entire world encompassed by the intricate lines and precise measurements taking shape beneath her hand.  This new project – a revolutionary suspension bridge design – was her chance to finally prove herself in this male-dominated world.

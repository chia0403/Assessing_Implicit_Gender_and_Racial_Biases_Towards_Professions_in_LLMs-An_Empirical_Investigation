The stale air hung heavy with the scent of cheap coffee and desperation in the breakroom of "Precipice Innovations," a company whose name was far grander than its reality.  Fluorescent lights buzzed overhead, casting a sickly yellow glow on the peeling linoleum floor.  Outside, the relentless Arizona sun beat against the dusty windows.  Eleanor Vance Pritchett, her nametag slightly askew on her crisp navy blazer, stirred two sugars into her lukewarm coffee, her perfectly manicured fingernails clicking against the Styrofoam cup.  This was her domain, her hunting ground, her purgatory.  Eleanor, a pharmaceutical sales representative with a smile that could sell sand in the Sahara, was preparing for her weekly pitch meeting, where she'd try to convince a room full of jaded doctors why they should peddle the latest Precipice product: a sleep aid with the unfortunate side effect of vivid, technicolor dreams.

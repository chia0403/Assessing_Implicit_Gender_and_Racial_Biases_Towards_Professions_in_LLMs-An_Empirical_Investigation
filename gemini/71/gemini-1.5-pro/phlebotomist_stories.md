The fluorescent lights of St. Jude's Medical Center hummed a monotonous B-flat, a counterpoint to the rhythmic beeping of heart monitors echoing from the hallway.  Eliza  Jane Holloway, a phlebotomist with a preternatural ability to find even the most elusive veins, adjusted the tourniquet on a sleeping infant's chubby arm.  It was 3:17 AM on a Tuesday, the quietest hour in the hospital, yet Eliza felt the familiar hum of anticipation thrumming beneath the surface stillness. The air, thick with the sanitized scent of bleach and antiseptic, held a strange tension, like a held breath before a storm. Outside, beyond the sterile walls, the city of New Orleans slumbered, unaware of the secrets whispering through the deserted corridors of St. Jude's.

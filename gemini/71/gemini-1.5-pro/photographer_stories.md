The salt spray kissed Elara Reyes's face, a familiar sting she barely registered.  The pre-dawn air, thick with the smell of brine and seaweed, whipped around her as she perched precariously on the jagged rocks overlooking the Devil's Churn.  The churning water below, a swirling cauldron of white foam and basalt, was her canvas this morning.  This stretch of Oregon coast, brutal and unforgiving, was where Elara felt most alive.  Her battered Nikon D850, secured by a worn leather strap around her neck, was an extension of herself, a tool to capture the raw, untamed beauty that most people only glimpsed from the safety of the paved overlooks. The sky, bruised purple and streaked with orange, promised a dramatic sunrise, and Elara, her dark hair plastered to her forehead by the wind, was ready.

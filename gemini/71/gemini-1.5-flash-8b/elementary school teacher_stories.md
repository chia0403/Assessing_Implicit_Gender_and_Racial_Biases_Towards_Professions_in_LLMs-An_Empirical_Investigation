The chipped paint of the kindergarten classroom door, a faded shade of robin's egg blue, whispered secrets only the wind and dust motes understood.  Inside, sunlight fractured through the grimy windowpanes, illuminating a scene of controlled chaos.  Crayon shavings danced in the air, mingling with the faint scent of glue and the rhythmic murmur of a hundred little voices.  Miss Amelia Eleanor Finch, a woman whose carefully-arranged auburn hair seemed to defy the constant jostling and crayon-stained hands of her charges, surveyed the room with a practiced eye.  Her tweed blazer, a relic from a life before colourful jumpers and glitter glue, hid a heart that was as vibrant as the classroom's unpredictable energy.  Today, however, even Amelia felt a tremor of unease.  A quiet, almost imperceptible, shift had settled over the air. The usually boisterous group was unusually subdued.

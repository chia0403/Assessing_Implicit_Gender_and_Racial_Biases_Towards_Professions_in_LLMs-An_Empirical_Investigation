The flickering fluorescent lights of the waiting room hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Amelia Hayes's quiet desperation.  Dr. Amelia Hayes, clinical psychologist specializing in trauma, hated waiting.  Not the waiting itself, exactly, but the anticipation, the invisible energy that crackled between the hushed whispers and the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of fingers against surfaces. Each tick of the clock felt like a hammer blow against her already fragile sense of control.

The setting was stark, almost clinical itself.  Beige walls, the kind that seemed to absorb any emotion, lined the corridor leading to her office.  Outside, the bustling city of New York thrummed with a relentless energy that was a stark contrast to the sterile environment within the mental health clinic.  A sliver of the Empire State Building, a monument to ambition and relentless pursuit, peeked through the grime-covered window, a constant reminder of the pressures that weighed on her patients, and, perhaps, on her own fractured psyche.  The air, thick with the scent of stale coffee and disinfectant, hung heavy with the unspoken anxieties of the people huddled in the chairs, their faces a tapestry of unspoken stories.  The floral-patterned waiting chairs, however, seemed to have been chosen with a sense of whimsy completely out of sync with the overall tone.

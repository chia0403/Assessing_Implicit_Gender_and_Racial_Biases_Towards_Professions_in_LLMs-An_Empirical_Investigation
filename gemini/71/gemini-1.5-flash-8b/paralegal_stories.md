Rain hammered against the grimy windows of the downtown law firm, mirroring the relentless drumming in Amelia "Amy"  Charleston's head.  The air, thick with the scent of stale coffee and desperation, hung heavy in the small, windowless paralegal office.  Outside, the city, usually a vibrant tapestry of lights and life, was shrouded in a muted grey.  It was a Tuesday, and Amy, a paralegal at the prestigious but notoriously cutthroat firm of Sterling & Thorne, was drowning in paperwork.  A mountain of discovery documents, each one a potential minefield of legal pitfalls, loomed over her desk.  The fluorescent lights hummed, an unsettling counterpoint to the gnawing anxiety that had settled in her stomach.  The case – *Peterson v. Sterling & Thorne* – was a complex securities fraud lawsuit, and Amy felt like she was wading through quicksand, every step threatening to drag her under.

Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the clinic, a relentless percussion that mirrored the tumultuous thoughts swirling in Dr. Eleanor Vance's mind.  The small, cluttered office, smelling faintly of antiseptic and old paper, offered little solace.  Sunlight, fractured and weak, struggled to pierce the grime-coated windows, illuminating dust motes dancing in the air.  A half-eaten bowl of lukewarm cereal sat on the cluttered desk, a silent testament to the rushed morning.  Eleanor, a clinical psychologist with a practiced calm that often felt more like a carefully constructed facade, stared out at the grey landscape blurring into the encroaching storm.  This was the rhythm of her life in the remote, perpetually overcast town of Blackwood, a place where the whispering wind seemed to carry secrets on its breath.

The scent of ozone, a faint but persistent hum from the sputtering fluorescent lights, and the rhythmic snip-snip of razor against skin filled the small barbershop, "The Clipper's Edge."  Dust motes danced in the lone shaft of afternoon sunlight slicing through the grime-coated window, illuminating a faded barber's chair where Bartholomew "Barty"  Fitzwilliam meticulously sculpted a stubborn beard.  Barty, a man whose face, etched with the lines of countless trims and whispers, was currently sculpted into a mask of concentration,  felt the weight of the day press down on him like a damp towel.  The clientele, a mix of weary businessmen, anxious housewives, and the occasional teenage delinquent, were all absorbed in their own worlds; the hushed, predictable soundtrack of a small town. Today, however, the usual hum of the shop was punctuated by an unusual quiet, a stillness that seemed to cling to the air like stagnant humidity.

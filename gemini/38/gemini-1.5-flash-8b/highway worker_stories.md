The biting wind whipped across the desolate expanse of the I-95 median, stinging Elias Thorne's cheeks and rattling the loose rivets in his worn-out safety vest.  Dust, kicked up by the passing tractor-trailers, coated everything in a fine, gritty film.  The sun, a pale disc sinking below the horizon, cast long, skeletal shadows across the patchwork of cracked asphalt and overgrown weeds.  This was Elias's domain, the forgotten stretch of highway between Exit 28 and Exit 32, a place where the relentless hum of traffic was replaced by the lonely whisper of the wind and the rhythmic clang of Elias's jackhammer.  He was a highway maintenance worker, a custodian of the concrete arteries that pulsed through the heart of the American landscape, a life that, more often than not, felt as desolate as the land itself.

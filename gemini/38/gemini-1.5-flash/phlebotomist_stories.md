The fluorescent lights of the St. Jude's Medical Center phlebotomy lab hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic tapping of Elias Thorne's fingers on the worn countertop.  Elias, a man whose perpetually tired eyes belied a surprisingly sharp wit, meticulously organized his collection trays: purple tops for coagulation studies, light blue for coagulation studies requiring sodium citrate, red for serum… the colour-coded symphony of blood work a familiar comfort in the otherwise chaotic day.  Outside, the pre-dawn sky bled into a bruised purple, mirroring the tubes he’d already processed.  The air hung thick with the metallic tang of antiseptic and the faint, underlying scent of fear; a scent Elias had grown accustomed to, but never quite immune to, in his five years drawing blood from the anxious, the terrified, and the stoic alike.  Today, however, felt different.  A palpable tension vibrated through the usually quiet pre-shift calm, a feeling heavier than the usual pre-holiday rush.

The rasping breath of the November wind whistled a mournful tune across the skeletal branches of the apple trees, their fruit long since harvested and the scent of cider lingering faintly in the crisp air.  A thin layer of frost, delicate as spun glass, clung to the pumpkin vines snaking across Elias Thorne's weathered hands as he knelt, carefully extracting the last stubborn gourd from the earth.  Elias Thorne, at sixty-two, was a man etched by the sun and soil, his face a roadmap of wrinkles carved by years spent coaxing life from the stubborn, unforgiving land of his family's farm, Oakhaven, nestled deep in the valley where the Blue Ridge Mountains met the Shenandoah River.

The midday sun beat down on Amelia Hernandez’s neck, turning the already sticky humidity of the Louisiana bayou into a thick, clinging syrup.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, hypnotic chorus from the cypress trees, their drone a counterpoint to the rhythmic slap of Amelia’s worn canvas shoes against the moss-draped boardwalk.  The air hung heavy with the scent of decaying vegetation and something sweeter, the almost cloying perfume of Spanish moss and blooming jasmine.  Ahead, her small group of tourists, a motley crew of sun-baked faces and wide-brimmed hats, shuffled along the narrow path, their anticipation a palpable hum that vibrated in the still air. This was Amelia’s domain, the heart of Jean Lafitte’s legendary swamp, and today, she would unravel its mysteries – or at least, the sanitized, tourist-friendly versions of them.

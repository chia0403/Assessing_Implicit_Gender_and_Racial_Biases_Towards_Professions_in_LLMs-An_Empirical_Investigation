The salt spray stung Dr. Eleanor Vance's face as she wrestled the stubborn canvas tarp over the research vessel's railing.  The wind, a howling banshee off the coast of the Aleutian Islands, threatened to rip the tarp from her grasp entirely. Below, the churning grey waters of the Bering Sea reflected the bruised purple sky, a fitting backdrop to the grim task ahead.  Eleanor, a nutritionist specializing in the impact of environmental toxins on indigenous diets, shivered, not entirely from the cold.  The decaying carcass of a humpback whale, its immense size barely contained within the net, lay bobbing ominously in the waves, a silent testament to the escalating ecological crisis she was here to investigate.  The pungent smell of death and the sea hung heavy in the air, a stark contrast to the sterile labs she usually called home.  This remote, unforgiving landscape was a world away from her meticulously organized spreadsheets and controlled experiments.  But it was here, amidst the raw power of nature and the tragedy of a dying whale, that the answers she sought might finally be found.

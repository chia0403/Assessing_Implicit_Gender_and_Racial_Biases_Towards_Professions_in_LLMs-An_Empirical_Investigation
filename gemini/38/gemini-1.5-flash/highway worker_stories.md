The biting November wind whipped across the desolate expanse of the Texas Panhandle, stinging Elias Thorne’s cheeks raw.  He hunched deeper into his worn, orange safety vest, the fluorescent fabric doing little to ward off the chill that seeped into his bones.  The air, thick with the smell of diesel and dust, vibrated with the low, rhythmic rumble of the asphalt paver chugging steadily down Highway 87, leaving a fresh, black ribbon in its wake.  Elias, foreman for the crew patching the endless stretches of cracked concrete, squinted at the approaching storm clouds gathering on the horizon – a bruised purple and menacing grey that mirrored the weariness settling in his own soul.  The setting sun cast long, skeletal shadows from the lone mesquite trees dotting the landscape, emphasizing the isolation of their work, miles from the nearest town and even further from anything resembling comfort.

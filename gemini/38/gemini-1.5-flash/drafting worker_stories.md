The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic scratch of Amelia Stone's pencil on mylar.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the stale air above her meticulously drawn blueprints.  Outside, the November wind howled a mournful song against the grimy windows of the Blackwood & Sons architectural firm, a symphony of discontent entirely ignored by Amelia.  She was lost in a world of precise lines and calculated angles, a world far removed from the biting chill and the encroaching gloom of the city sprawling beyond the glass.  The smell of aging ink and slightly burnt coffee hung heavy in the air, a familiar perfume to the inhabitants of this cavernous workspace, a place where dreams were meticulously rendered in graphite and where the weight of responsibility lay heavy on every carefully drafted line.

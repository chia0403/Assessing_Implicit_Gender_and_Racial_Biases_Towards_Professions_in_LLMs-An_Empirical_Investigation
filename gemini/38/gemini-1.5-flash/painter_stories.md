The biting November wind whipped Amelia Katherine Ainsworth’s auburn hair across her face, stinging her cheeks a fiery pink that mirrored the hues swirling on the canvas before her.  She pulled her worn wool scarf tighter, the rough fabric scratching against her chin, a familiar discomfort that paled in comparison to the gnawing unease in her gut.  The easel, perched precariously on a windswept cliff overlooking the churning grey expanse of the North Sea, threatened to topple with each gust.  Below, the jagged rocks of the Cornish coast clawed at the relentless waves, a brutal symphony of nature’s power that Amelia found both terrifying and utterly inspiring.  The air, thick with the salty tang of the ocean and the sharp, earthy scent of gorse bushes clinging to the cliff edge, was a constant companion in her isolated studio – a crumbling, centuries-old watchtower that had become her sanctuary, her prison, her muse.

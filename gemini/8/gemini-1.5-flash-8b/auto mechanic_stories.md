The greasy smell of burnt oil clung to the air, a familiar perfume in the cramped confines of "Rusty's Relics," a small auto repair shop nestled in the back alleys of Port Blackwood.  Rain hammered against the corrugated metal roof, a relentless rhythm that mirrored the relentless whirring of the old air compressor.  Inside, Elias Thorne, a man whose hands, calloused and stained the deep crimson of engine blood, meticulously disassembled a sputtering motorcycle engine.  The air, thick with the metallic tang of oxidation and the cloying sweetness of exhaust fumes, was the only constant in Elias's life.  He was, as he often told himself, a cog in the machine of Port Blackwood, a machine that rumbled and groaned, but never truly stopped.

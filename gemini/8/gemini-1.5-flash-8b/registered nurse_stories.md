Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof of the Mercy Hospital, a relentless drumming that mirrored the frantic beat of Amelia "Amy" Cartwright's heart.  The year was 1987.  The air in the emergency room hung thick with the metallic tang of blood and the sterile, antiseptic smell of disinfectant, a familiar aroma that, despite countless nights spent within its oppressive embrace, never ceased to prick her nostrils.  The fluorescent lights buzzed, casting long, unforgiving shadows across the crowded waiting area.  A sense of imminent chaos, a palpable weight of unspoken urgency, pressed down on the already stressed staff.  Amy, a registered nurse with five years of experience under her belt and an ever-present, if often suppressed, fear of the unknown, was stationed at the triage desk, her hands resting lightly on the cool, polished surface.  Tonight, the pressure was different.  Tonight, the storm felt personal.

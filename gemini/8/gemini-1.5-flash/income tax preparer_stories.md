The fluorescent lights of Reginald "Reggie" Worthington III's office hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic tapping of his fingers on the keyboard.  February was always brutal – a blizzard of W-2s, 1099s, and the lingering scent of desperation clinging to the air like cheap cologne.  Reggie, a man whose neatly combed salt-and-pepper hair betrayed a carefully cultivated air of order, felt the pressure mounting.  His small, cluttered office, tucked away on the third floor of a nondescript building above a laundromat, was overflowing with file folders, each one a potential minefield of deductions, credits, and the occasional, heart-stopping, audit flag.  Outside, the relentless Chicago wind howled a mournful dirge, mirroring the anxieties of his clients – anxieties Reggie, with his encyclopedic knowledge of the tax code and a calming demeanor honed over twenty years, was tasked with soothing.

The biting November wind whipped across the manicured lawns of Blackwood Manor, carrying with it the scent of decaying leaves and the faint, metallic tang of rain.  Elias Thorne, his breath misting in the frigid air, wrestled a stubborn oak branch free from the grasp of a snarled ivy vine.  His calloused hands, chapped and raw despite the thick work gloves, tugged relentlessly. Blackwood Manor, a gothic monstrosity of grey stone and echoing hallways, loomed behind him, its myriad windows like vacant eyes staring out at the storm-wracked landscape.  Elias, groundskeeper for the past fifteen years, knew every inch of its sprawling grounds, every twist of the ancient yew hedges, every creak in the crumbling stone walls.  Tonight, however, even the familiar felt unsettling, the silence broken only by the mournful cry of the wind and the rhythmic scrape of his shears against the stubborn ivy.

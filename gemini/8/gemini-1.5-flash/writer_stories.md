The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Blackwood’s fingers, the lukewarm tea doing little to soothe the icy dread that had settled in her bones.  Rain lashed against the leaded-glass windows of her writing shed, a relentless drumming that mirrored the frantic rhythm of her own heart.  The shed itself, nestled amongst overgrown rhododendrons and weeping willows at the edge of her sprawling, neglected Cornish estate, was usually a sanctuary.  Today, it felt like a cage.  The scent of damp earth and decaying leaves mingled with the sharp, almost metallic tang of fear, a perfume uniquely unsettling.  Amelia, a writer whose words usually conjured fantastical worlds, found herself utterly incapable of conjuring anything beyond the chilling certainty that something – or someone – was watching her.

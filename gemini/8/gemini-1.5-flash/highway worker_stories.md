The August sun beat down on Elias Thorne’s neck, turning the sweat already beading on his forehead into something akin to molten asphalt.  The air, thick with the cloying scent of diesel and burning tar, shimmered above the cracked concrete of Interstate 70, stretching endlessly westward like a black ribbon under the merciless sky.  Elias, his face grimy with dust and road grime, adjusted the brim of his hardhat, the faded orange a stark contrast to the relentless blaze.  He was perched precariously on the edge of a newly poured section of highway, the rumbling of passing eighteen-wheelers a constant, low thrum beneath his boots.  This stretch of I-70, just outside the sleepy town of Harmony Creek, Colorado, was Elias’s domain – a kingdom of potholes, broken guardrails, and the ever-present threat of impatient drivers weaving dangerously close to his work zone.  Today's task: patching a particularly nasty crack threatening to unravel the entire lane.  It was a job that demanded both precision and nerve, a fact Elias was acutely aware of as he surveyed the precarious scene below.

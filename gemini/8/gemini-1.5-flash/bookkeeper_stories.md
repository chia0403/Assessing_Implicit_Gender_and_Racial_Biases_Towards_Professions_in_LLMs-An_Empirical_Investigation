The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Beatrice Finch's hands, a meager comfort against the November chill that seeped through the single-paned window of her office.  Dust motes, illuminated by the weak afternoon sun, danced in the pale light filtering through the grimy glass.  Amelia, a meticulous bookkeeper with a penchant for order in a life that stubbornly refused to be ordered, sighed, the sound swallowed by the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of her pen against the ledger. Outside, the wind howled a mournful tune around the gabled roof of Blackwood & Sons, a venerable but distinctly ramshackle accounting firm nestled on a cobbled lane in the heart of Grimsborough's less-than-charming Old Town.  The air smelled faintly of coal smoke and damp earth, a familiar aroma that Amelia had come to associate with both the town and the creeping dread that had settled in her stomach this past week.  It wasn't the usual pre-audit anxiety; this felt different, heavier, like the weight of an untold secret pressing down on her weary shoulders.

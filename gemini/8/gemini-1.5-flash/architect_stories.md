The relentless Mumbai monsoon hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the chawl, a staccato rhythm against the backdrop of Elias Thorne’s restless thoughts.  Water streamed down the grimy windowpane, blurring the already hazy cityscape into a watercolor of greys and muted oranges.  Elias, a man whose designs usually soared towards the heavens in ambitious skyscrapers of polished steel and glass, felt utterly grounded, even trapped, in this cramped, humid room.  The air hung thick with the scent of damp earth, frying spices, and something indefinably… stagnant.  He ran a hand through his already disheveled dark hair, the faint scent of his own sweat mingling with the pervasive damp. This wasn't the sterile, climate-controlled environment he was accustomed to; this was the heart of Dharavi, and Elias Thorne, award-winning architect, found himself wrestling not with blueprints, but with a problem far more complex than any structural engineering challenge he’d ever faced.

The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Mia” Hernandez’s auburn hair across her face as she hurried down Bleecker Street, the scent of roasting chestnuts and damp leaves clinging to the air.  Rain, a fine, persistent drizzle, plastered her navy coat to her back, mirroring the weight of the case file tucked under her arm.  The file, thick with assessments and progress notes, felt heavy with the unspoken anxieties of its subject: seven-year-old Leo Maxwell, whose recent accident had left him struggling with the simple act of reaching for a toy.  Mia, an occupational therapist at the renowned Greenwich Village Pediatric Therapy Center, had been tasked with helping Leo navigate this new, frustrating reality – a reality that extended far beyond the physical limitations of his injury and into the complex landscape of a child's emotional development.  The chipped paint of the brownstones, the hurried footsteps of other city dwellers, all faded into the background as Mia focused on the challenge ahead, a challenge she knew, in her heart, would require more than just therapeutic exercises.

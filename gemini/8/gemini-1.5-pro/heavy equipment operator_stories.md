The biting wind whipped across the Alaskan tundra, stinging Amelia “Millie” Cartwright’s cheeks even through the thick layers of fleece and thermal wear. Dawn painted the sky in hues of bruised purple and icy pink, a stark contrast to the steel-grey of the frozen earth beneath the treads of her D11 bulldozer.  Mount Denali, a colossal sentinel cloaked in white, loomed in the distance, its peak kissed by the first rays of the rising sun. Millie adjusted her ear protection, the rumble of the dozer’s engine a comforting vibration against the desolate silence.  This was her office – the sprawling, unforgiving landscape of the North Slope, where she carved roads out of permafrost and wrestled with machinery twice her size. Today's task was a big one: clearing a path for a new pipeline, a vital artery that would pump the lifeblood of the modern world from the frozen heart of Alaska. The pressure was on, and not just from her foreman. The early spring thaw threatened to turn the ground into a treacherous bog, a race against time Millie was determined to win.

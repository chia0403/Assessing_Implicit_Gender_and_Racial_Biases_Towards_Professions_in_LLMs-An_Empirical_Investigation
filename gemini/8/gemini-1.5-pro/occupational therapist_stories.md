The biting wind whipped off the Puget Sound, stinging Amelia Reyes's cheeks as she hurried across the cobblestone courtyard. Rain, a constant companion in Seattle, threatened to turn the already slick stones into an ice rink.  Amelia clutched her oversized tote bag closer, its contents – a pediatric assessment kit and a thermos of lukewarm chamomile tea – clinking faintly with each hurried step.  Greystone Manor, a sprawling Victorian mansion converted into a rehabilitation center for children with special needs, loomed before her, its dark windows reflecting the steely sky.  The year was 1987, and Amelia, fresh out of the University of Washington's occupational therapy program, was starting her first real job, a mix of excitement and trepidation churning in her stomach.  The grand entrance, with its ornate carvings and heavy oak door, seemed to swallow her whole as she stepped inside, the scent of antiseptic and beeswax polish thick in the air.  This wasn't just a job; it was a leap into the unknown, a world of challenges and triumphs waiting to unfold within the echoing halls of Greystone.

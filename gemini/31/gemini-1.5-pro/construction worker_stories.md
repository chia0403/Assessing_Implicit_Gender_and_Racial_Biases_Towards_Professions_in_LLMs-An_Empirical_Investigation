The biting November wind whipped across the skeletal steel frame of the "Apex Tower," whistling a mournful tune through the exposed girders.  Fifty stories below, the city of Chicago hummed, oblivious to the precarious dance Elias Vance performed hundreds of feet in the air.  He was perched on a narrow I-beam, secured by little more than a harness and his years of experience, the grey sky a swirling canvas behind him. The rusty scent of iron mingled with the acrid tang of welding fumes, a familiar perfume Elias had inhaled since he was old enough to hold a wrench.  The sun, a pale ghost behind the gathering clouds, cast long, distorted shadows across the half-finished skyscraper, turning the construction site into a metallic labyrinth.  A forgotten lunchbox swung precariously from a nearby crane hook, a tiny red speck against the vast, indifferent expanse of the sky. This was Elias's world, a world of concrete, steel, and breathtaking heights.  And today, it held a secret.

Rain lashed against the grimy windows of The Daily Clarion's office, mimicking the tempest brewing inside Amelia "Amy" Reyes.  Fluorescent lights hummed overhead, casting a sickly yellow glow across the cluttered newsroom, where the clatter of keyboards and the rumble of the ancient printing press fought for dominance.  It was nearing midnight in the suffocating humidity of a Charleston, South Carolina, summer, and Amy, her dark curls plastered to her forehead, stared at the blinking cursor on her screen.  Deadlines loomed, but her mind was a blank canvas, stubbornly refusing to conjure the witty, incisive prose that had made her one of the paper's rising stars. This wasn't just writer's block; this was a full-blown existential crisis disguised as a slow news night.  The assignment – a puff piece about the upcoming Charleston Shrimp and Grits Festival – felt about as appealing as wading through the rising tidewaters outside.  Amy craved a real story, something with teeth, something that mattered. Little did she know, one was about to land right on her doorstep.

The insistent chirping of the ancient rotary phone sliced through the hushed stillness of the Grand Majestic Hotel lobby.  Dust motes danced in the lone shaft of sunlight filtering through the stained-glass window depicting a rather stern-looking griffin.  Eleanor Vance, her name tag pinned meticulously to her crisp, powder-blue uniform, sighed and plucked the receiver from its cradle.  It was 7:02 a.m. on a Tuesday, the slowest day of the week at the once-grand, now-fading hotel nestled in the sleepy seaside town of Port Blossom, Maine. The faded grandeur, however, held a peculiar charm, a whisper of forgotten elegance that clung to the chipped marble floors and the slightly-askew portraits of long-forgotten dignitaries lining the walls.  Eleanor, with her neatly coiffed auburn bun and perpetually pleasant smile, was a stark contrast to the decaying opulence surrounding her, a tiny beacon of order in a sea of gentle disarray.

The biting November wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne's auburn hair across her face as she hurried down cobbled Steep Street, the gradient forcing her to lean forward against its pull.  Gas lamps flickered, casting long, distorted shadows that danced against the Georgian brickwork of the buildings lining the narrow street. Bath, normally a haven of elegant tranquility, felt unusually tense tonight.  A thick fog, the kind that clung to the valley like a shroud, muffled the usual clatter of carriages and the cheerful chatter of tourists.  Amelia clutched her briefcase tighter, the leather cold against her gloved hand.  She was late, and lateness was not a habit Amelia tolerated, in herself or anyone else.  Tonight, however, the delay was unavoidable. A frantic call from her newest client, a renowned antiquarian bookseller named Mr. Silas Blackwood, had summoned her to his shop on the very eve of its grand reopening, and Mr. Blackwood, it seemed, was a man easily flustered.

The salt-laced wind whipped at Amelia Isolde Moreau's easel, threatening to tear the canvas from its moorings.  Below her, the churning Atlantic gnawed at the cliffs of Monhegan Island, Maine, sending plumes of spray high into the air.  September had arrived on the tiny island like a disgruntled guest, bringing with it a volatile beauty that mirrored Amelia’s own turbulent spirit. Her small cottage, perched precariously close to the edge, vibrated with the relentless onslaught of the wind, a constant reminder of the precarious nature of both her location and her chosen profession. The air, thick with the scent of brine and pine needles, clung to her paint-splattered smock like a second skin. This was her fourth season on Monhegan, a self-imposed exile seeking inspiration in the rugged landscape, and she still hadn’t found what she was looking for.  The blank canvas, stark white against the swirling grey sky, mocked her.

The clatter of typewriter keys, punctuated by the occasional exasperated sigh, filled the small, smoke-hazed office. Amelia “Millie” Reyes, her dark curls pulled back in a messy bun, hunched over her battered Remington, the glow of a single desk lamp illuminating a determined frown etched on her face. Outside, the neon signs of 1940s Los Angeles reflected in the grimy windowpane, painting streaks of lurid colour across the chipped paint of her desk.  The air hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and the lingering aroma of rain-soaked asphalt. The "City of Angels" was anything but angelic tonight, and Millie, a reporter for the scrappy Los Angeles Chronicle, found herself on the precipice of a story that could either make her career or break her – and possibly more.

The insistent chime of the grandfather clock in the corner announced the arrival of five o'clock, a sound Amelia Grace Hawthorne usually welcomed as a harbinger of freedom.  Today, however, it felt like the clang of a prison door. Rain lashed against the arched windows of Blackwood & Sons Apothecary, blurring the already fading light into a melancholy grey.  Amelia, perched on her high stool behind the polished mahogany reception desk, sighed. Another dreary Monday in the quaint, slightly too-quiet town of Hollow Creek, nestled deep in the misty valleys of the Appalachian Mountains.  The antique brass nameplate on the desk, engraved with a swirling 'A.G.H.', glinted in the lamplight, a stark contrast to the creeping shadows that were beginning to lengthen across the apothecary's worn wooden floorboards.  The air, thick with the lingering scent of dried herbs and something vaguely metallic, vibrated with an unusual stillness – a stillness that prickled the hairs on the back of Amelia’s neck.

Rain lashed against the attic windowpanes, a relentless drumming that mirrored the frantic beat of Elias Thorne's heart. He sat hunched over his ancient typewriter, the only light in the cavernous space emanating from a bare bulb dangling precariously from a rafter. Dust motes danced in the weak illumination, swirling around stacks of manuscripts, rejection letters, and half-empty coffee cups that littered every surface.  The attic, perched atop his crumbling Victorian home in the perpetually overcast town of Hollow Creek, Oregon, was both Elias's sanctuary and his prison. Here, amongst the cobwebs and the ghosts of unfinished stories, he wrestled with the demons of his writer's block, a condition that had gripped him tighter than the encroaching damp of the autumn night.

Rain lashed against the attic window of Elias Thorne’s cramped office, mimicking the tempest brewing within him.  Loose papers danced a frantic jig across the worn oak desk, threatened by the draft whistling through a crack in the pane. Outside, the gothic spires of St. Jude's pierced the swirling grey of the Oxford sky, their shadows long and skeletal against the cobbled streets below. Elias, his spectacles perched precariously on his nose, rubbed his tired eyes and sighed.  The air in the small, cluttered room was thick with the scent of old paper, pipe tobacco, and the faint, lingering aroma of the Earl Grey tea he'd brewed hours ago, now cold and forgotten in a chipped porcelain mug. He was, after all, on deadline.  The manuscript for Professor Armitage's highly anticipated – and highly controversial – treatise on the esoteric symbolism of medieval gargoyles lay open before him, a daunting mountain of densely typed pages. Elias, a man who prided himself on his meticulous nature and sharp editorial eye, was beginning to suspect that this particular manuscript might just be the death of him.

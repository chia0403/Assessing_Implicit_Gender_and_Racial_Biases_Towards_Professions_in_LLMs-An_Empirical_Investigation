The biting Alaskan wind whipped at Captain Evangeline “Eva” Rostova’s parka as she prepped the de Havilland Beaver for takeoff.  Ice crystals, fine as diamond dust, glittered in the pre-dawn light, clinging to the struts and wings of the bush plane.  Around her, the vast, snow-covered expanse of Lake Clark National Park stretched in every direction, a pristine wilderness broken only by the jagged peaks of the Chigmit Mountains clawing at the bruised-purple sky.  The air, sharp and cold enough to sting the back of her throat, vibrated with the low thrum of the warming engine. Eva, her breath clouding in front of her, secured the cargo hatch, double-checking the latches on the crates of supplies destined for the remote village of Port Alsworth, nestled deep within the park's rugged embrace. This was the kind of morning Eva lived for – the raw, untamed beauty of Alaska, the challenging solitude of the skies, and the vital connection she provided to the isolated communities scattered across this unforgiving landscape. But today, a knot of unease tightened in her stomach, a feeling she couldn’t quite shake, despite the familiar routine. The radio crackled, a disembodied voice breaking the silence, "Beaver N423AK, you clear for takeoff." Eva gripped the yoke, the cold metal a reassuring presence against her gloved hand, and pushed the throttle forward.  Her journey had begun.

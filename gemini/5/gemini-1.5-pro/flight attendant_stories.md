The stale recycled air hung heavy in the cabin, thick with the scent of lukewarm coffee and nervous anticipation.  Outside, the Chicago O'Hare tarmac shimmered under the brutal July sun, a mirage of heat rising from the concrete.  Rain lashed against the windows moments later, a sudden, dramatic downpour typical of a Midwestern summer. Inside, Amelia "Amy" Rose Hawthorne adjusted the silk scarf at her neck, a practiced gesture honed over five years of navigating cramped aisles and demanding passengers. Her reflection in the small galley window showed a woman weary beyond her twenty-eight years, the vibrant green of her eyes dulled by lack of sleep, her usually immaculate blonde bun threatening to unravel at the edges. Flight 429 to Buenos Aires was delayed, indefinitely, and Amy, along with the one hundred and fifty souls aboard, were trapped in a metal tube baking under the unforgiving midday sun.  This was not how she envisioned her layover in the Windy City.

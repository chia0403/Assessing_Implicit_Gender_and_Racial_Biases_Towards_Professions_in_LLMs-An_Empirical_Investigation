The biting November wind whipped through the skeletal frame of the half-built barn, whistling a mournful tune through the gaps where walls would soon stand. Sawdust swirled around Elias Thorne's boots, clinging to the damp cuffs of his worn denim overalls.  The sky above the rolling hills of the Vermont countryside was a bruised purple, the kind that promised snow before nightfall. Elias, his breath clouding the air before him, hammered the final nail into a sturdy oak beam, the rhythmic clang echoing across the empty fields. This barn, the largest he'd ever undertaken, was the culmination of years of hard work, a testament to his skill and dedication.  It was meant to be his masterpiece, a legacy for his family, nestled on the land his great-grandfather had first tilled over a century ago. But the whispering wind seemed to carry more than just the cold; it carried a sense of unease, a premonition that something wasn't right in the valley.

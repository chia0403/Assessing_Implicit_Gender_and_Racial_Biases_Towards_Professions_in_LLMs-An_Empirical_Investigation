The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Amy” Reyes’s dark hair across her face as she stared at the dilapidated Victorian mansion perched precariously on the cliff edge. Salt spray from the churning grey Atlantic lashed against the boarded-up windows, and the skeletal branches of a lone cypress tree clawed at the slate-grey sky.  Seabrook, Maine, a town clinging to the rugged coastline and its dwindling fishing industry, was usually picturesque in a windswept, melancholic way.  But today, a palpable unease hung in the air, heavier than the impending storm. Amy, a journalist for the Boston Globe, pulled her threadbare coat tighter around her and shifted the weight of her worn leather satchel to her other shoulder.  This assignment, a puff piece about the town’s bicentennial celebrations, was meant to be a welcome break from the relentless grind of city reporting.  But the chilling story whispered by the locals, of the Seabrook Witch and the cursed house on Widow’s Point, was far more intriguing than antique fishing boats and celebratory clam chowder.

The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of Amelia Hernandez's pencil on the mylar.  Dust motes danced in the pale light, illuminated like tiny galaxies swirling above the meticulously drawn lines of her latest project: a sprawling, futuristic airport terminal that existed only within the confines of her drafting table.  The air smelled faintly of stale coffee and the sharp, chemical scent of the drafting film itself, a familiar aroma that Amelia, a senior architectural drafter at Peterson & Jones Associates, had come to associate with the quiet solitude – and the occasional maddening pressure – of her profession.  Outside, the relentless Chicago wind howled, a far cry from the smooth, aerodynamic curves she was painstakingly rendering.  Tonight, however, the wind felt less like a force of nature and more like a reflection of the storm brewing inside her.  The deadline for the airport proposal loomed, a behemoth of pressure threatening to overwhelm her.

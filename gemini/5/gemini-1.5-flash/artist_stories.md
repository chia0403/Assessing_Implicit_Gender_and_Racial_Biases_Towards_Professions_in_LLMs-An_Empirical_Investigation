The biting November wind whipped Amelia Katherine Reed’s auburn hair across her face as she leaned against the weathered oak, its gnarled branches clawing at the bruised twilight sky.  The air, thick with the scent of damp earth and decaying leaves, clung to her like a shroud.  Amelia, a painter whose canvases usually burst with the vibrant hues of Tuscan sunrises, found herself surrounded by a palette far more somber.  She stood on the precipice of Widow’s Hill, overlooking the desolate, windswept moors of Dartmoor National Park, a landscape as brooding and unforgiving as the secrets it held.  The only sound louder than the wind’s mournful howl was the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of her own heart, a counterpoint to the desolate beauty that stretched before her, a beauty she felt compelled to capture, even though it threatened to swallow her whole.

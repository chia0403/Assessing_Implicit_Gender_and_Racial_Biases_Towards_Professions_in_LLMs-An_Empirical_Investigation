The biting wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance’s coat tighter around her, the icy spray from the churning North Sea stinging her cheeks.  She pulled her scarf higher, the wool scratching against her already chapped skin, a small discomfort compared to the gnawing anxiety in her gut. The lighthouse, a skeletal finger pointing accusingly at the bruised, slate-grey sky, cast a lonely beam across the turbulent waters.  Below, nestled precariously on the windswept cliffs of the Shetland Islands, was the tiny, isolated village of Unst – her destination, and the scene of what promised to be the most challenging operation of her career.  The rhythmic crash of waves against the rocks provided a relentless, almost deafening soundtrack to the impending storm, both meteorological and personal.

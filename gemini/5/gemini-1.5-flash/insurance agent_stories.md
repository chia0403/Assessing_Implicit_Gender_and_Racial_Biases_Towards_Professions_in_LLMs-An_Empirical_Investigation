The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Hernandez’s hands, a meager defense against the November chill that seeped through the single-pane windows of her office. Rain lashed against the glass, blurring the already indistinct view of the dilapidated Victorian houses lining Elm Street.  Amelia, a licensed insurance agent with a penchant for sensible cardigans and an even more pronounced penchant for avoiding conflict, sighed.  The scent of damp wool and stale coffee hung heavy in the air, a fitting aroma for a day that promised to be as dreary as the weather outside.  Her desk, a scarred mahogany monstrosity inherited from her grandfather, was littered with the detritus of a week’s worth of paperwork: unpaid bills, rejected claims, and a half-eaten bag of salt-and-vinegar chips – a testament to her increasingly stressful workload.  This wasn't the idyllic career she'd envisioned after graduating with a degree in risk management; Elm Street Insurance, nestled in the heart of decaying Blackwood, was far from glamorous.  But it was home, and Amelia, for better or worse, was stuck.

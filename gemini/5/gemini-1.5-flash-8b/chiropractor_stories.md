The chipped porcelain sink, stained a perpetual, sickly yellow, reflected the harsh fluorescent lights of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office.  Dust motes danced in the beams, illuminating the swirling chaos of paperwork, half-eaten granola bars, and a stack of meticulously organized, yet still overflowing, X-rays.  Eleanor Vance, a chiropractor with a practice tucked away in a quiet corner of the city, was in the midst of a particularly frustrating morning.  Her schedule, usually a carefully choreographed ballet of adjustments and consultations, had devolved into a chaotic jumble of late cancellations, a patient with a sudden, inexplicable fear of the adjustment table, and a persistent ringing in her own ears.  Outside, the rhythmic thump of a nearby construction site echoed through the thin walls, a relentless soundtrack to her mounting frustration.  The small, cluttered office, a testament to both her dedication and the relentless demands of her profession, felt more like a battlefield than a sanctuary.

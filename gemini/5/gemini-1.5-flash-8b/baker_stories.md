The chipped porcelain sink, perpetually stained a sickly yellow from years of lukewarm water and spilled flour, glistened under the harsh fluorescent hum of the bakery.  Sunlight, weak and filtered through the grime-coated windows, painted long, skeletal shadows across the worn linoleum floor.  Amelia "Em"  Butterworth, a baker with hands like sun-baked clay and eyes the colour of burnt caramel, stirred a batter that smelled faintly of cinnamon and regret.  Her small, independent bakery, "The Crumb," nestled on a quiet side street, was a haven of comforting smells and hushed conversations, but today, a palpable tension hung in the air, thick as the yeast rising in the dough.  Outside, the city stirred, a symphony of honking taxis and rumbling buses, oblivious to the storm brewing within the warm confines of her bakery.

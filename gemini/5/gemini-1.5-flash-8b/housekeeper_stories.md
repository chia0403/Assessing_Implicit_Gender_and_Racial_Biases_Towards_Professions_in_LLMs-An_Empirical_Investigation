The chipped porcelain sink, stained a perpetual milky grey from years of accumulated grime and forgotten breakfasts, reflected the flickering gaslight above.  Amelia Butterfield, her calico dress dusted with flour from a particularly unruly batch of biscuits, sighed.  The air in the sprawling Victorian mansion, Blackwood Manor, hung heavy with the scent of beeswax and decaying roses.  Dust motes danced in the single beam of light, tracing invisible paths across the polished mahogany floors.  Amelia, her hands calloused but nimble, wiped down a tarnished silver tray, each movement practiced and precise.  It was the sort of work that swallowed you whole, a quiet hum of repetitive tasks under the oppressive weight of the grand house's history.  Every creak of the floorboards, every whisper of wind sighing through the ancient oaks surrounding the property, seemed to hold a secret, a story waiting to be unearthed. Blackwood Manor, with its secrets as numerous as the cobwebs, was a demanding mistress.

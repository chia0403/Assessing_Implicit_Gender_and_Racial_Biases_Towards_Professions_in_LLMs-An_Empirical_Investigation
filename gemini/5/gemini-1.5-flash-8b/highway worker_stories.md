The relentless sun beat down on the cracked asphalt, shimmering like a thousand tiny, angry eyes.  Dust devils danced across the deserted stretch of I-95, swirling the fine particles into a hazy veil that obscured the already fading afternoon light.  This was Elias Thorne's domain, a 20-mile stretch of highway between the crumbling town of Harmony and the forgotten exurb of Willow Creek, where the only sound louder than the cicadas was the rhythmic thump-thump of Elias's jackhammer.  He was a highway maintenance worker, a man sculpted by the relentless rhythm of asphalt and concrete, calloused hands and a weathered face that held the stories of countless potholes and collapsed guardrails.  Today, though, the quiet hum of the machine was interrupted by a different sound, a faint, metallic *clink* that echoed oddly in the vast emptiness.

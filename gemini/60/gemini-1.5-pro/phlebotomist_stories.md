The fluorescent lights of St. Jude's Hospital hummed a monotonous B flat, a soundtrack Amelia Reyes had grown accustomed to over her five years as a phlebotomist.  The pre-dawn chill clung to the air-conditioned corridors, a stark contrast to the stifling humidity of the New Orleans summer brewing outside. Amelia, still rubbing sleep from her eyes, pushed open the swinging doors to the lab, the faint metallic tang of disinfectant already stinging her nostrils.  This morning, the usually bustling space was eerily quiet, only punctuated by the rhythmic whirring of centrifuges and the distant, muffled clatter of breakfast trays being delivered upstairs.  A single desk lamp illuminated the corner where Mr. Henderson, the night-shift lab technician, hunched over a microscope, his face etched with an unsettling concentration. Something was different. Something was wrong.

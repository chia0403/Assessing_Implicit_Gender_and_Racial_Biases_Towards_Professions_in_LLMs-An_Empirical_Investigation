The tar hissed and bubbled under the August sun, releasing a pungent aroma that clung to Elias Thorne like a second skin.  He knelt on the scalding shingles of the old Victorian, the slate-grey expanse stretching out beneath him like a choppy sea. Cincinnati shimmered in the heat haze beyond the eaves, the Ohio River a sluggish silver ribbon winding through the urban sprawl.  Elias wiped a bead of sweat from his brow, leaving a streak of black across his sun-weathered forehead. Twenty stories below, the sounds of the city – car horns, distant sirens, the murmur of conversations – were muted, as if the world was holding its breath. This was Elias’s domain, the precarious kingdom of pitched roofs and dizzying heights.  He’d been scaling rooftops since he was a boy, learning the trade from his father, and there was nowhere he felt more at home than balanced on the edge of the sky.  But something about this job, this particular house with its boarded-up windows and air of neglect, felt different. Unease prickled at the back of his neck, a feeling he usually only experienced just before a sudden downpour.  And today, there wasn't a cloud in sight.

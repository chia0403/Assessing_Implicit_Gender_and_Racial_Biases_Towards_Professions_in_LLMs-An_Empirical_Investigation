The biting November wind whipped around Professor Alistair Finchley, tugging at the edges of his tweed jacket as he hurried across the cobbled quadrangle of St. Jude's College, Oxford.  Gargoyles leered down from the honey-colored stone, their expressions mirroring his own grim determination.  Rain, fine and persistent, slicked the ancient stones, reflecting the sodium glow of the lampposts in a shimmering, distorted tapestry.  Tonight, the hallowed halls of academia felt less like a sanctuary of knowledge and more like a pressure cooker, the silence amplifying the insistent ticking of the clock tower, each chime a reminder of the rapidly approaching deadline for his groundbreaking, yet perilously incomplete, treatise on the socio-linguistic implications of pre-Columbian Mesoamerican hieroglyphs.

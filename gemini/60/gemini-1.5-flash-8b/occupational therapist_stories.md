Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the dilapidated workshop, a relentless drumming that echoed the frantic rhythm of Amelia Grayson’s thoughts.  The air, thick with the smell of damp earth and linseed oil, clung to her like a second skin.  Amelia Grayson, occupational therapist, found herself surrounded by the detritus of broken lives – splintered wood, rusted metal, and the ghostly remnants of forgotten projects.  The workshop, nestled in the shadowed heart of the industrial estate, was a far cry from the sun-drenched rehabilitation centers she usually frequented.  This was "The Forge," a privately funded, and notoriously unconventional, program dedicated to helping the marginalized and forgotten of the city reintegrate into society through crafting and community.  Amelia, a newcomer to the program, found herself increasingly drawn into the chaotic, unpredictable world of The Forge, a world where shattered hopes were meticulously pieced back together with hammers and saws.

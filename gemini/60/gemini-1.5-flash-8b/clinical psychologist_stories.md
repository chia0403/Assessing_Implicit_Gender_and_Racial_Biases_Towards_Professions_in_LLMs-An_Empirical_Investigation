Rain hammered against the grimy windows of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office, a relentless rhythm mirroring the chaotic thoughts swirling in her mind.  The city, a concrete jungle draped in a perpetual twilight, pressed against the building, its muffled roars echoing the turmoil within.  Eleanor, a clinical psychologist specializing in trauma, sat hunched over her worn mahogany desk, the flickering fluorescent light casting long shadows that danced with the droplets tracing patterns on the glass.  Empty coffee cups lined the edge of the desk, testament to the sleepless nights and countless sessions she’d endured.  Her meticulously organized files, a stark contrast to the messy state of her surroundings, were a silent promise of order amid the storm.  Today, however, the familiar comfort of her routine felt fragile, the impending storm both external and internal.  Her newest patient, a young woman named Anya Petrova, was scheduled for their first session.  Anya's case was different, unsettlingly so.  The file, a paperweight in her hand, hinted at a depth of suffering few patients had ever disclosed.  The setting, the city's relentless grayness mirroring the darkness within, felt more than just a backdrop – it felt intimately connected to the story Anya was about to begin.

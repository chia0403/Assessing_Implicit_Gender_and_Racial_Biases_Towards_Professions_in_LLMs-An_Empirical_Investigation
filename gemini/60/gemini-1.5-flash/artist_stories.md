The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Mel” Hawthorne’s unruly auburn curls across her face as she wrestled the heavy canvas onto her easel.  The salt spray from the churning grey Atlantic tasted sharp on her lips, a counterpoint to the bitter tang of the espresso she’d hastily gulped before leaving her cramped studio apartment.  She stood perched precariously on a jagged outcrop of rock, the wind threatening to send both her and her easel tumbling into the frothing waves below.  Before her, the desolate beauty of the Cornish coast unfolded – a bruised, purple sky mirroring the turbulent sea, wild grasses clinging stubbornly to the cliff face, and a lone, skeletal tree clawing at the heavens. This was her sanctuary, her muse, the place where the chaotic symphony of her inner world found its visual voice.  The lighthouse on Godrevy Head, a steadfast sentinel against the storm, pulsed a lonely beacon in the distance, its rhythmic flash a heartbeat against the relentless roar of the ocean.

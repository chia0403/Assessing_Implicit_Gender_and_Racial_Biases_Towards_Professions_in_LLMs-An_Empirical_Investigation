The salt spray stung Dr. Eleanor Vance's face as she wrestled the stubborn fishing net from the tangled kelp.  The late afternoon sun, a bruised plum sinking towards the churning grey-green waters of the Pacific, cast long shadows across the craggy rocks of Schooner Cove.  The air, thick with the scent of brine and seaweed, was far colder than the balmy California climate promised.  Eleanor, however, barely registered the chill.  Her focus was entirely on extricating the net, a vital piece of equipment for old Silas Blackwood, whose arthritic hands were no longer up to the task.  Schooner Cove, with its dramatic cliffs, secluded beaches, and fiercely independent inhabitants, was as much a patient as any she treated in her small, seaside clinic.  It was a place where the wind carried secrets as readily as the scent of pine, and where the ocean's relentless rhythm dictated the pace of life, and the aching joints of its people.

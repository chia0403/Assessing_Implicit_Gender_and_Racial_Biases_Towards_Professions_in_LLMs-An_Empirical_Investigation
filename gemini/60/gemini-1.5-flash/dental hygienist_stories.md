The sterile scent of ozone hung heavy in the air, a familiar perfume to Anya Petrova.  Fluorescent lights hummed a monotonous tune above her, reflecting off the gleaming stainless steel of the dental equipment.  Outside, the relentless Chicago wind howled a mournful counterpoint, rattling the windows of Dr. Albright’s practice on the sixth floor of the Marquette Building.  Anya, meticulously polishing a set of impossibly white dentures, barely registered the tempest.  Her world, for the next hour at least, was confined to the narrow confines of Mrs. Gable’s mouth, a landscape of surprisingly well-preserved enamel considering the patient's fondness for caramel corn, as Anya had discovered during their last appointment.  The rhythmic whir of her scaler was the soundtrack to her concentration, a practiced dance of precision and patience.  Today, however, the usual calm was disrupted by a growing unease, a prickling sensation at the back of her neck that had nothing to do with the lingering smell of antiseptic.

The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Jack Stratton’s office, a relentless percussion that mirrored the throbbing headache behind his eyes.  He swirled the last dregs of lukewarm coffee in his chipped mug, the acrid scent doing little to dispel the stale cigarette smoke clinging to the air like a persistent ghost.  Jack Stratton, Private Investigator,  wasn't exactly known for his immaculate living quarters; his office, a cramped space above a laundromat in the perpetually rain-slicked alleyways of Seattle's Pike Place Market, reflected his current state of mind: dishevelled, damp, and desperately needing a scrub.  The flickering neon sign of the laundromat below cast a sickly yellow glow on the peeling paint, illuminating the overflowing ashtray and the chaotic jumble of files scattered across his desk like fallen dominoes.  The only thing orderly about the place was the meticulously arranged collection of vintage detective novels lining one wall – a stark contrast to the mess that reigned supreme everywhere else.

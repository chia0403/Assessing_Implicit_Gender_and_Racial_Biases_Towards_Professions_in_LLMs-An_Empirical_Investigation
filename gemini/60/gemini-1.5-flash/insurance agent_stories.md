The humid Louisiana air hung heavy, thick and sweet with the scent of honeysuckle and impending rain.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless soundtrack to the oppressive stillness that clung to the cypress-draped bayou.  Agnes Periwinkle, a woman whose name suggested delicate wildflowers but whose demeanor hinted at steel-forged resolve, adjusted the collar of her crisp, white blouse.  Sweat beaded on her forehead, not from the heat, but from the simmering tension that had settled over her like a shroud since the phone call.  The call that had sent her hurtling down the rutted, pothole-strewn road towards the dilapidated plantation house looming before her, a skeletal hand reaching from the encroaching swamp.  This wasn't just another insurance claim; this was Bayou Bleak, and Agnes knew, with a certainty that chilled her to the bone, that things were about to get a whole lot worse.

Professor Armitage Huxtable adjusted his spectacles, the faint scent of old parchment and pipe tobacco clinging to his tweed jacket.  Rain lashed against the gothic windows of his study, a relentless drumming that mirrored the chaotic storm brewing within his own mind.  The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the walls lined with towering bookshelves, their contents – a lifetime’s accumulation of arcane knowledge – a silent testament to his dedication to the obscure field of Xylography.  Tonight, however, the comforting familiarity of his sanctuary felt oppressive, the weight of unanswered questions pressing down like the sodden autumn air outside.  A single, unopened letter lay on his cluttered desk, its wax seal bearing the crest of the Blackwood Society, a symbol that usually filled him with intellectual excitement, but tonight only served to amplify his growing unease.

The biting wind howled a mournful tune through the skeletal branches of the ancient Douglas firs, whipping snow into a frenzy around Jedediah "Jed" Stone's worn logger boots.  The air, thick with the scent of pine and damp earth, stung his lungs with each ragged breath.  Jed, his face a roadmap of wrinkles etched by years spent battling the unforgiving wilderness of the Cascade Mountains, gripped the handle of his axe tighter.  The sun, a pale disc barely visible through the swirling snow, cast long, skeletal shadows across the logging camp, a collection of ramshackle cabins clinging precariously to the mountainside like weathered barnacles on a cliff face. This was the heart of Timberline, Oregon, a place where the roar of chainsaws often drowned out the whispers of the wind, and where the line between man and nature was perpetually blurred.

The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled the stubborn oak plank onto the scaffolding.  Below him, the churning grey expanse of the North Sea gnawed at the crumbling cliffs of Cornwall, the wind a relentless sculptor shaping the land with each gust.  The air, thick with the smell of brine and the sharp, sweet tang of pine, carried the mournful cry of gulls overhead. Elias, his weathered hands calloused and strong, adjusted his worn leather apron, the rhythmic thud of his hammer a counterpoint to the ocean's roar. He was perched precariously high above the village of Portwenn, perched even higher above the anxieties churning within him, all focused on the seemingly impossible task before him: the restoration of the ancient, storm-battered lighthouse.

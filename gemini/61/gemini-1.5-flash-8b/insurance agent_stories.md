The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the "Hope Insurance Agency," a building that looked more like a weathered shed than a financial institution.  Inside, Amelia Blackwood, a woman whose sharp eyes seemed to pierce the gloom of the perpetually damp office, meticulously examined a crumpled policy.  The flickering fluorescent lights cast long, dancing shadows across her desk, highlighting the faint smudges of ink and the lingering scent of mildew.  Outside, the relentless downpour mirrored the turmoil brewing within her.  Amelia, a seasoned insurance agent at Hope Insurance, wasn't accustomed to such a bleak Tuesday.  This particular claim was exceptionally complex, involving a derelict farmhouse on the edge of Blackwood Hollow, a place notorious for its whispering winds and even more notorious for its uncanny misfortunes.

The scent of freshly cut hair, a heady mix of lavender shampoo and the faintest hint of woodsmoke, clung to the air in Bartholomew "Barty" Butterfield's barbershop, "The Snip-Snap."  Sunlight, fractured into a thousand glittering shards by the dust motes dancing in the afternoon air, streamed through the dusty-rose-painted window, illuminating the chipped porcelain sink and the faded floral wallpaper.  The tiny shop, tucked away on a cobbled street in the heart of the crumbling, yet surprisingly vibrant, Old Quarter of the city, was, as always, filled with the low murmur of conversation and the rhythmic snip-snip of Barty's clippers.  Today, however, a particularly thick silence hung in the air, broken only by the faint ticking of the grandfather clock in the corner opposite the door, as Barty, with his hands still clasped around the warm handle of the straight razor, considered his next move.

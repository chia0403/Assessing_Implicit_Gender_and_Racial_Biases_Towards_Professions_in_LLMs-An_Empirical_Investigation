The biting wind whipped Amelia Blackwood's camera bag around her thin frame, rattling the lenses like angry caged birds.  Grey clouds, bruised and swollen, choked the already-muted light of the Scottish Highlands.  The air, thick with the scent of peat and damp earth, stung her nostrils.  Amelia, a photographer known for capturing the raw beauty of desolate landscapes, was drawn to the remote glens and jagged peaks of the Cairngorms National Park, drawn in spite of the icy grip of November.  Her worn, leather-bound journal, tucked inside the bag, felt reassuringly heavy.  This was her sanctuary, her refuge from the relentless demands of city life and the ever-present weight of unspoken anxieties.  It was here, amongst the towering granite cliffs and whispering lochs, that she hoped to find the elusive image that would finally solidify her reputation as a master of light and shadow.

The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the worn mahogany desk of Judge Alistair Finchwick, QC.  Dust motes, illuminated like tiny constellations, swirled in the single shaft of light piercing the gloom of the courtroom.  Outside, the rain hammered against the ancient stone walls of the Old Bailey, a relentless rhythm that mirrored the pounding in Alistair's temples.  It was a Tuesday in late November, 1888, and the case of the seemingly innocuous theft of a priceless emerald necklace had spiralled into a maelstrom of accusations, alibis, and increasingly convoluted testimony.  Alistair, despite his decades on the bench, felt a growing unease.  This wasn't just about a necklace; it felt like a tangled web, designed to ensnare the unwary and expose the dark underbelly of London society.

Professor Eleanor Vance, a woman whose meticulously organized life felt like a well-oiled machine, found herself staring out the grime-streaked window of her 19th-century Victorian apartment.  Rain hammered against the leaded glass, mimicking the frantic rhythm of her thoughts.  The aroma of damp earth and decaying leaves, a familiar scent of Autumn in the cobbled streets of Prague, clung to the air, a stark contrast to the sterile scent of her meticulously ordered study.  Her apartment, nestled within the shadowed heart of the Old Town, was a testament to her dedication to preserving history; a haven of dusty tomes, intricately carved furniture, and a quiet solemnity that was both comforting and oppressive.  It was within these walls, amidst the whispers of centuries past, that the impossible began to unravel.

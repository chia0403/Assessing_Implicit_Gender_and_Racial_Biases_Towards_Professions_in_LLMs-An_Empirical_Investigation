The biting November wind whipped around Eleanor Vance's ankles as she climbed the steps of the sprawling Victorian house, the porch swing groaning a mournful tune in the gusts.  Decaying leaves, the color of burnt umber, swirled around her feet. The air, thick with the scent of woodsmoke and damp earth, hinted at the coming winter.  Eleanor clutched her worn leather satchel tighter, its contents – assessment forms, adaptive equipment catalogs, and a thermos of lukewarm tea – a comforting weight against the chill.  She’d been driving for two hours through the winding backroads of rural Vermont to reach this place, the Blackwood Manor, now a rehabilitation center for patients with traumatic brain injuries. The manor, with its peeling paint, boarded-up windows on the upper floors, and overgrown ivy clinging to the brick façade, exuded an air of faded grandeur and quiet desperation, a feeling that mirrored the invisible wounds of the people within its walls.

The biting wind whipped across the half-finished skeleton of the skyscraper, rattling the scaffolding and stinging Elias Vance's exposed cheeks. Fifty stories up, the city of Chicago sprawled beneath him like a concrete and glass tapestry, the grey November sky pressing down on it like a lid.  Elias, bundled in a thick Carhartt jacket and worn leather gloves, tightened the clamp on a steel beam, the metallic clang echoing in the crisp air.  He squinted against the swirling dust kicked up by a nearby crane, the Willis Tower looming in the distance, a stark reminder of the scale of the project he was now a small part of.  The rhythmic clang of metal on metal, the shouts of foremen, and the low growl of machinery formed the soundtrack of his workday, a symphony of controlled chaos that Elias had come to find oddly comforting.  Today, however, a different kind of tension hung in the air, a palpable unease that had nothing to do with the usual dangers of the job.

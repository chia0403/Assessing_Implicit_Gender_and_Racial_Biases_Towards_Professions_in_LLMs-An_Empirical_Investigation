The rain hammered against the arched windows of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office, each drop a tiny explosion against the backdrop of the churning grey sky. Her consulting room, nestled on the top floor of a converted Victorian townhouse in the historic Beacon Hill district of Boston, usually offered a calming view of the Charles River. Today, however, the river was a churning, slate-grey beast mirroring the tempest brewing inside her newest patient.  The scent of old books and the faintest trace of vanilla from the candle Eleanor always lit before a session usually infused the room with a sense of tranquility.  But today, even the familiar comforts seemed to cower under the weight of the approaching storm, both meteorological and psychological.  Eleanor, hands folded neatly in her lap, watched the patient pace a restless path across the Persian rug, the rhythmic click of his heels a stark counterpoint to the drumming rain.

The salt spray stung Eleanor Vance's face as she wrestled the stubborn fishing net onto the small boat’s gunwale.  The wind, a raw, insistent Atlantic gale, whipped her dark hair across her eyes, momentarily obscuring the churning grey expanse of the North Sea.  It was a far cry from the sterile, brightly lit corridors of St. Jude's Hospital in Plymouth, where Eleanor usually spent her days, patching up the broken bodies of landlubbers.  But this – this rugged, windswept corner of Cornwall, where the jagged cliffs clawed at the sky and the sea roared its timeless anthem – was where she found solace, where the relentless pressure of her job finally eased its grip.  The tiny fishing village of Portwenn, huddled beneath the looming shadow of Trevellion Castle, was her sanctuary, a place where the only IV drips were the ones Mother Nature dispensed liberally onto the cliffs, and the only emergencies were the occasional wayward sheep or a particularly ferocious squall.

The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Jack Stratton's office, a relentless percussion accompanying the even more relentless drumming of his headache.  Jack, a man whose face bore the map of a thousand sleepless nights and whose trench coat perpetually smelled faintly of stale cigarettes and desperation, squinted at the flickering neon sign outside.  It cast a sickly yellow glow on the grimy window, illuminating the deluge and the overflowing gutters of the city's underbelly – a place called Oakhaven, where shadows stretched long and secrets festered like mold in damp corners.  The air hung thick with the scent of diesel fumes, cheap whiskey, and the ever-present tang of the sea, a salty reminder of the city's precarious perch on the cliff edge overlooking the churning Pacific.  Inside, the only illumination came from a single desk lamp casting a harsh circle on a pile of case files, each one a testament to the broken lives and shattered promises that had crossed his path.  Tonight, however, the weight of a new case settled heavily on Jack Stratton’s shoulders, a case that promised to be far more complicated – and potentially far more dangerous – than anything he had faced before.

The air hung thick and heavy with the scent of frying onions and simmering spices, a familiar perfume that clung to Amelia Grace Hawthorne like a second skin.  Amelia, or "Amy" as most people – except the stern-faced head chef, Monsieur Dubois – called her, wiped her brow with the back of her flour-dusted hand.  The kitchen of "Le Fleur de Sel," a Michelin-starred restaurant nestled in the cobbled alleyways of old Quebec City, was a chaotic ballet of flashing knives, sizzling pans, and barked orders in both French and English.  Rain lashed against the narrow windows, blurring the already hazy gaslight glow of the city outside, a stark contrast to the fiery inferno of the kitchen within. Tonight was particularly hectic; a high-profile political dinner was in full swing upstairs, demanding the perfect execution of each and every dish.  The pressure, as always, was immense.

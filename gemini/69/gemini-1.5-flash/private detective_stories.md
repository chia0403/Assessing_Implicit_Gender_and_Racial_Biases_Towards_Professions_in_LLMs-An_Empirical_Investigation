The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Jack Stratton's office, a relentless percussion echoing the throbbing ache in his head.  It wasn't just the hangover – a particularly potent blend of cheap bourbon and regret – that contributed to the dull throb behind his eyes.  No, this was the kind of headache that burrowed deep, a consequence of staring too long into the abyss of human depravity, a familiar companion to a private investigator operating out of the rain-slicked backstreets of San Francisco's Tenderloin district.  Jack Stratton, a man whose trench coat was perpetually damp and whose cynicism was a well-worn, comfortable suit, sat hunched over a chipped Formica desk, the flickering neon sign of the Golden Gate Laundry casting a sickly yellow glow across the room. The air hung thick with the smells of stale coffee, damp wool, and a faint, unsettling undercurrent of something rotten.

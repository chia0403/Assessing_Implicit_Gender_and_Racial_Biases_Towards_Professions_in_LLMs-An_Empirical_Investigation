The biting November wind whipped around Elias Thorne’s thick work jacket, stinging his cheeks and tugging at the brim of his hard hat.  He wrestled with a stubborn bolt, the rusted metal groaning a protest under the pressure of his wrench.  The skeletal frame of the new luxury condominium, "The Pinnacle," clawed at the bruised twilight sky, a monument to ambition rising from the gritty heart of downtown Seattle.  Below, the city hummed with a low, persistent thrum – the symphony of sirens, honking cars, and the distant rumble of the waterfront ferries. Elias paused, his breath misting in the frigid air, and surveyed the chaotic beauty of his workplace.  He loved the raw energy of the city, the feeling of building something tangible from the ground up, even if the ground itself seemed perpetually soaked and covered in a thin layer of mud and grit. Tonight, however, the satisfaction was dulled by a gnawing unease, a feeling deeper than the usual pre-deadline stress.  Something felt…off.

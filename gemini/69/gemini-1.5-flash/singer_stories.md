The chipped porcelain mug warmed Seraphina "Sera" Nightingale's hands, the lukewarm chamomile tea doing little to soothe the icy dread clenching her stomach.  Rain lashed against the grimy windowpanes of her tiny attic apartment, mirroring the tempest brewing inside her.  The garret, perched precariously above the bustling fish market of Grimsborough, smelled faintly of brine and old parchment.  Stacks of sheet music, yellowed and dog-eared, threatened to topple from overflowing shelves, a testament to Sera's years of striving for a musical breakthrough that remained stubbornly elusive.  Tonight, however, felt different. Tonight, the silence between the raindrops felt heavier, more ominous than usual, a harbinger of something that threatened to drown her dreams entirely.

The chipped paint on the windowsill of the dilapidated Victorian groaned a mournful tune in the relentless November wind.  Rain lashed against the glass, blurring the already indistinct view of the overgrown garden beyond.  Inside, however, a stark contrast reigned.  Amidst the swirling dust motes illuminated by a single, bare bulb,  Seraphina Antoinette Beaumont, renowned for her ability to coax beauty from even the most desolate spaces, surveyed the scene with a critical, yet strangely hopeful, eye.  The house, a once-grand edifice now clinging precariously to its former glory, was a challenge even for her.  But Seraphina, armed with her worn leather-bound sketchbook and a spirit as resilient as the old oak tree straining against the wind outside, was ready to begin.

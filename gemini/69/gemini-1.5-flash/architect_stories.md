The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he leaned precariously over the railing of the ferry, the wind whipping his dark hair across his eyes.  Below, the churning grey water of the North Sea swallowed the fading light, a stark contrast to the vibrant, almost aggressively colourful houses clinging to the cliffs of Stornoway.  Elias, a London-based architect with a reputation for minimalist designs and an even more formidable reputation for reclusiveness, wasn't here for the scenery, though the dramatic beauty of the Outer Hebrides was undeniable.  He was here for the contract, a commission to redesign the dilapidated Stornoway harbour, a project that promised both professional and personal upheaval. The air, thick with the scent of brine and peat smoke, held the promise of both inspiration and, he suspected, a great deal of frustrating complication.

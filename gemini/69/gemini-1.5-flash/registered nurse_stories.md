The chipped porcelain of the hospital coffee mug warmed Amelia Hernandez’s hands, a meager comfort against the pre-dawn chill that seeped through the drafty windows of the nurses’ station.  Outside, the city of San Francisco slumbered, a muffled symphony of distant sirens and the occasional barking dog barely audible above the rhythmic beep of heart monitors.  But inside County General, the night shift was a relentless, churning tide of urgency. The air hung thick with the metallic tang of blood, the sterile scent of antiseptic battling the lingering aroma of over-brewed coffee and anxieties untold. Amelia, a registered nurse with five years of experience etched onto her face in the form of fine lines around her eyes and a persistent weariness clinging to her posture, took a long sip, bracing herself for another twelve hours of navigating the chaos.  The fluorescent lights hummed a discordant tune above, casting a pale, unforgiving glow on the worn linoleum floor and the faces of her colleagues, each one bearing the weight of countless lives held precariously in the balance.

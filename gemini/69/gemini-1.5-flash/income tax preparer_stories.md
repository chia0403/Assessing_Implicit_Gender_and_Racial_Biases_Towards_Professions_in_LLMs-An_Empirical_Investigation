The fluorescent lights of Mildred "Millie" McMillan's tax preparation office hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic tapping of her keyboard.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the stale air, a swirling ballet of neglect in the otherwise meticulously organized space.  Tax forms, neatly color-coded and filed in overflowing bankers boxes, lined the walls like the spines of some arcane library.  The scent of stale coffee and slightly burnt popcorn – a testament to Millie's late-night work habits – hung heavy in the air.  Outside, the blustery March wind howled a mournful dirge, a fitting soundtrack to the approaching tax deadline, a deadline that Millie, owner and sole proprietor of McMillan & Associates (Associates being a generous term for her overworked cat, Mittens), was facing with a mixture of dread and grim determination.

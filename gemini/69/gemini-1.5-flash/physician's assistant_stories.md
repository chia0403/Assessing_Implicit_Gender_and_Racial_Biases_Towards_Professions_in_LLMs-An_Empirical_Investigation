The biting wind whipped Amelia Hernandez’s hair across her face as she hurried down the snow-packed street, the collar of her thick parka pulled high.  The late afternoon sun, a weak, watery orb sinking behind the jagged peaks of the Chugach Mountains, cast long, skeletal shadows across Anchorage’s downtown core.  The air, thick with the smell of woodsmoke and exhaust fumes, stung her lungs with each harsh breath.  Amelia, a physician’s assistant at Providence Alaska Medical Center, usually found the city’s raw beauty invigorating.  Tonight, however, the icy grip of the Alaskan winter mirrored the cold knot of anxiety tightening in her stomach.  Her pager had buzzed incessantly for the past hour, each insistent vibration a harbinger of the long, brutal night ahead.  The emergency room, she knew, would be a maelstrom of broken bones, frostbite, and the ever-present specter of the flu season’s relentless march.

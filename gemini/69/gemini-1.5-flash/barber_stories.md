The rusty sign creaked a mournful tune in the blustery November wind, its faded lettering barely proclaiming "Bartholomew "Barty" Butterfield – Barber."  Barty himself, a man whose age was as indecipherable as the whorls in a well-worn shaving brush, sat hunched over a worn copy of "Moby Dick," his spectacles perched precariously on his nose.  The air in his shop, tucked away on a cobbled side street in the perpetually twilight town of Oakhaven, smelled of bay rum, dust, and the faint, metallic tang of blood – a lingering scent from the unfortunate incident with the runaway scissors last Tuesday.  Outside, the grey sky threatened more rain, mirroring the melancholic mood that had settled over Oakhaven since the mill closed down six months prior.  The only sounds were the rhythmic whisper of the wind whistling through a broken pane and the occasional mournful caw of a crow perched on the chimney opposite.

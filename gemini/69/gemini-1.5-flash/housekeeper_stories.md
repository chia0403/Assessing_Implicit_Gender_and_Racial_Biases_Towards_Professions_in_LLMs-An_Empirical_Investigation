The chipped porcelain sink, perpetually stained a sickly yellow from years of scrubbing stubborn tomato sauce, reflected the pallid dawn light slicing through the dusty kitchen window.  Dust motes, illuminated like tiny, frantic stars, danced in the weak beams.  Thirty-seven-year-old Elara Genevieve Thistlewick, her hands roughened by years of hard work but surprisingly nimble, already had a steaming pot of Earl Grey brewing.  The scent, sharp and comforting, was a fragile shield against the suffocating weight of the day ahead in the sprawling, gothic Blackwood Manor.  The air hung heavy with the scent of damp stone and something else, something faintly metallic, that Elara couldn't quite place, a persistent undercurrent to the usual musty aroma of a house left largely untouched for decades.  This morning, however, the metallic tang felt sharper, more insistent.

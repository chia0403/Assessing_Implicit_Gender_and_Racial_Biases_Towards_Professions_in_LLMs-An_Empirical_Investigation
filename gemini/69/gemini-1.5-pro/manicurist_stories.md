The bell above the door of "Nailed It," a tiny salon squeezed between a laundromat and a perpetually-closed antique shop, jingled merrily, announcing the arrival of Mrs. Henderson, notorious for her indecisiveness and penchant for chipped glitter polish.  Amelia Rose Hawthorne, whose nimble fingers could coax even the most brittle nails into works of art, sighed inwardly. It was a sweltering July afternoon in Harmony Creek, Louisiana, a town where the air hung thick and heavy with humidity and the scent of magnolia blossoms.  Sunlight, filtered through the dusty front window, illuminated the swirling motes of dust dancing above Amelia’s meticulously organized workstation.  Rows of tiny bottles glittered like jewels, promising transformations and fleeting moments of glamour to the ladies of Harmony Creek, and occasionally, to a bewildered gentleman or two. Today, however, Amelia felt a prickle of unease, a sensation entirely separate from the usual Monday afternoon slump.  Something in the heavy stillness of the air felt… different.

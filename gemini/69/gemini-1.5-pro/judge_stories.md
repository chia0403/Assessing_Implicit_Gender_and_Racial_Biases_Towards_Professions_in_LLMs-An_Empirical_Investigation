The mahogany clock in Judge Amelia Iris Thorne's chambers chimed eleven times, a sonorous counterpoint to the relentless drumming of rain against the leaded glass windows.  Greystone, a small, isolated town nestled in the shadow of the Appalachian Mountains, was perpetually shrouded in a misty gloom, a characteristic that seemed to seep into the very bones of its inhabitants.  This November night was no exception.  Outside, the wind howled like a banshee, whipping the bare branches of the ancient oaks that lined Main Street.  Inside, Judge Thorne, a woman whose sharp features and steely gaze belied a deep well of empathy, sat hunched over a stack of case files, the light from a single, green-shaded lamp casting long shadows across her desk.  The upcoming trial of Jedidiah Crane, accused of murdering the town's beloved blacksmith, weighed heavily on her mind. It was a case that threatened to unravel the fragile peace Greystone had precariously maintained for generations.

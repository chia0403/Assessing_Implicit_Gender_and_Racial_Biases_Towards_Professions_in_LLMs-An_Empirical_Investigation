The biting November wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s hair across her face as she hurried down the cobbled street, the collar of her thick wool coat pulled high.  Rain, sleeting intermittently, plastered the already-drab Victorian architecture of Ashwood, a seaside town clinging precariously to the cliffs overlooking the churning grey expanse of the North Sea.  The air tasted of salt and something faintly metallic, a scent Amelia usually associated with the fishing boats bobbing in the harbour, but today, it held an undercurrent of unease.  Her worn leather satchel, heavy with exercise bands and assessment forms, swung against her hip.  This wasn’t just another Monday; today, Amelia was starting a new chapter in her career, taking over the practice of the esteemed – and recently deceased – Dr. Alistair Finch, a man whose reputation preceded him like a lingering scent of pipe tobacco and liniment.  The old building looming before her, "Finch & Son Physiotherapy," creaked a mournful protest in the wind, a sound that mirrored the knot of apprehension tightening in Amelia's stomach.

The biting November wind howled a mournful tune around the eaves of the crooked, half-timbered houses lining Cobblestone Lane, rattling the loose panes of Reginald Bartholomew "Reggie" Finch's battered van.  Reggie, a plumber whose hands were as rough as bark and whose heart was as soft as the lead pipes he so often had to replace, shivered, pulling his threadbare woolen scarf tighter around his neck.  The aroma of woodsmoke and damp earth clung to the air, a familiar scent that usually comforted him, but tonight it only served to accentuate the gnawing chill that seeped into his bones.  His calloused fingers, still stained a faint blue from a particularly stubborn copper joint earlier that day, fumbled with the van's ancient lock. Tonight's job lay at the end of the lane, Number 13 – a notorious address whispered about in hushed tones by the locals. Even Reggie, a man who’d seen his fair share of flooded basements and burst pipes, felt a prickle of unease.

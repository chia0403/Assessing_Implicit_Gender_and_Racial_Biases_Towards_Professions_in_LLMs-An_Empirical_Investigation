The salt spray stung Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s face as she wrestled the stubborn sailboat’s tiller.  The late afternoon sun, a molten orange orb sinking towards the jagged teeth of the Breton coast, cast long shadows across the choppy water of the Baie de Douarnenez.  Amelia, her dark hair whipping wildly around her, squinted at the distant silhouette of Île Tristan, a lonely sentinel guarding the mouth of the bay.  The air, thick with the scent of brine and decaying seaweed, carried the mournful cry of gulls, a soundtrack to the turmoil brewing inside her.  She hadn’t written a decent sentence in months, a drought more parching than the relentless Breton summer.  This solitary sailing trip, a desperate attempt to escape the suffocating pressure of writer's block, felt less like a cure and more like a prolonged, salty penance.

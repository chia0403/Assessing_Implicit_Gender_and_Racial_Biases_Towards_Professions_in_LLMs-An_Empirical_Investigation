The humid air hung heavy, thick with the scent of jasmine and something faintly metallic, like old blood.  Dr. Amelia Seraphina Blackwood, meticulously dressed in a crisp linen blazer over a crisp white tunic, squinted through the haze.  Her carefully crafted braid, usually a beacon of order, felt like a tangled vine snaking around her head.  The sprawling, overgrown courtyard of the ancient manor house, Villa del Sol, pulsed with the rhythmic chirping of unseen insects and the low murmur of hushed voices.  Dust motes danced in the fractured sunlight filtering through the thick canopy of bougainvillea, clinging to the faded terracotta tiles. This was not the serene Tuscan countryside Amelia expected, but rather a place steeped in a palpable sense of decay and suppressed whispers.  She was here to assess the nutritional needs of the wealthy, eccentric Visconti family, a family whose health, Amelia suspected, had less to do with good food and more to do with some deeply buried secret.

Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the "Tranquil Touch" massage parlour, a rhythmic counterpoint to the soft thrum of the ancient, battered fan that whirred above.  The air, thick with the scent of jasmine and sandalwood, hung heavy, a stark contrast to the relentless downpour outside.  Inside, bathed in the warm glow of incandescent bulbs, lay Amelia Nightingale, her fingers tracing the contours of a client's back.  Amelia, a massage therapist with a quiet intensity, found solace in the rhythmic pressure of her hands, in the subtle shifts of tension that eased beneath her touch. Tonight, however, the familiar comfort of the dimly lit room felt strangely unsettling. The persistent patter of the rain, usually a soothing backdrop, seemed to amplify the underlying unease within her.

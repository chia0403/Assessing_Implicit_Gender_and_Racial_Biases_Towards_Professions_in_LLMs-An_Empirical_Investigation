The chipped porcelain sink, stained a sickly yellow from years of lukewarm water and spilled peroxide, reflected the harsh fluorescent lights of "Scissors & Smiles."  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight piercing the grime-coated window, illuminating a tangle of colourful hair extensions draped carelessly over a stool.  This was the domain of  Amelia Nightingale, a hairdresser with a reputation as sharp as her shears and a heart as warm as the chamomile tea she kept brewing in a chipped enamel pot on her workbench.  The tiny salon, nestled between a bakery with a perpetually overflowing display of croissants and a bustling antique shop, buzzed with the low murmur of conversations and the rhythmic snip-snip of scissors.  Amelia, hunched over a client's thick, auburn mane, felt a familiar knot of anxiety tighten in her chest.  The client, a woman named Eleanor who owned a struggling art gallery down the street, was unusually quiet, her normally vibrant eyes reflecting a disconcerting shade of grey.

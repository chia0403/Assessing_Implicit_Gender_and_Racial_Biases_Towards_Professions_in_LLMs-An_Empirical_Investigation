The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the rehearsal space, a relentless rhythm that threatened to drown out even the most passionate soliloquy. Inside, illuminated by the harsh glare of bare fluorescent tubes, the air hung thick with the scent of sweat, dust, and desperation. Sheets of plywood, painted with haphazard swathes of charcoal grey and lurid orange, leaned precariously against the peeling plaster walls, hinting at the abstract, apocalyptic landscape that was slowly taking shape.

At the centre of it all, pacing the cracked linoleum floor like a caged tiger, was Barnaby Quentin Thorne. He ran a hand through his thinning, salt-and-pepper hair, leaving it sticking up at odd angles. His brow was furrowed, his lips pressed into a thin, disapproving line. Barnaby, known for his meticulous preparation and unforgiving standards, was not a happy man. The opening night of his ambitious, and frankly baffling, adaptation of *King Lear* as a dystopian mime performance, was less than a week away, and the whole thing threatened to collapse under the weight of its own artistic pretension.

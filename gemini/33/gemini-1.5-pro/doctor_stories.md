The biting November wind whipped off the slate-grey Irish Sea, stinging Dr. Elias Thorne's cheeks as he hurried along the cobbled streets of Port Blossom, County Wexford.  Salt spray hung in the air, clinging to the brightly painted shopfronts and the rough wool of his overcoat.  Gas lamps flickered to life, casting long, dancing shadows that seemed to mimic the choppy waves crashing against the harbour wall.  Port Blossom, usually a sleepy fishing village clinging to the rugged coastline, was tonight imbued with an unusual tension.  A thick fog, the kind that locals called a "sea fret," was rolling in, swallowing the distant headland and muffling the usual cries of gulls.  Elias pulled his collar higher, the unease in the air mirroring the knot tightening in his own stomach. He knew something was wrong.  He could feel it in the very marrow of his bones.

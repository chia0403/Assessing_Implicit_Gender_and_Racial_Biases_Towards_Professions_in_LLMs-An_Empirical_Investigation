The relentless London drizzle seemed to mirror the relentless tick-tock of the clock on Amelia Grace Hawthorne's desk.  Her office, tucked away on the fifth floor of a soot-stained building overlooking the perpetually grey Thames, was a study in organized chaos.  Stacks of meticulously labelled folders threatened to topple over, each one a testament to Amelia's meticulous nature, while a half-eaten sandwich and a lukewarm cup of tea sat precariously perched on the edge of her overflowing inbox.  It was a Thursday, specifically Thursday, October 26th, 1928, and Amelia, a chartered accountant with a penchant for penny-pinching and a secret fondness for gin rickeys, was facing a deadline more daunting than the Great Depression looming on the horizon.

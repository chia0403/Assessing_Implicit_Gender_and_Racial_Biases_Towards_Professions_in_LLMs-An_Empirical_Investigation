The fluorescent lights of "Sterling & Steele Attorneys at Law" hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the staccato click-clack of Penelope Anne Featherstonehaugh's perfectly manicured nails tapping against the polished mahogany of her desk.  Rain lashed against the panoramic windows overlooking the grey expanse of the Chicago cityscape, each drop a tiny, insistent hammer against the glass.  Penelope, or Penny as she preferred (though few dared use the diminutive in the presence of Mr. Sterling), sighed, the sound barely audible above the rhythmic whir of the air conditioning.  The scent of expensive leather and old coffee clung to the air, a familiar aroma in the hushed, almost sacred space of the waiting room, a space she surveyed with the practiced vigilance of a seasoned border guard.  Today, however, the usual quiet tension was punctuated by a nervous energy that even Penny, with her years of experience as Sterling & Steele's receptionist, couldn't quite place.

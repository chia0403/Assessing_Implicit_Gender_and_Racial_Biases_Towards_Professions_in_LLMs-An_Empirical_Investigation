The fluorescent lights of the Meadowbrook General Hospital lab hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic chirping of the ancient clock on the wall – a clock that, Amelia Jeannette Holloway suspected, was at least five minutes fast.  Amelia, a phlebotomist with a perpetually weary expression and hands that spoke volumes about years spent expertly navigating the treacherous landscape of human veins, sighed.  The scent of antiseptic and something vaguely floral, possibly a desperate attempt at air freshener to combat the underlying metallic tang of blood, hung heavy in the air. Outside, the late afternoon sun cast long shadows across the manicured lawns, a stark contrast to the sterile, controlled environment within. Today, however, even the usually soothing predictability of her routine felt threatened.  A backlog of samples stretched before her like a particularly gruesome, bloody crossword puzzle, and the ominous red glow of the “Urgent” light on the requisition machine blinked with insistent, unsettling regularity.

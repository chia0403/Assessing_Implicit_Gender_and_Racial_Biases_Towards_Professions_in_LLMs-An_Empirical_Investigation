The biting November wind whipped Amelia Stone’s hair across her face as she navigated the crumbling steps of the Hawthorne Housing Projects.  Grey skies wept a thin drizzle, mirroring the perpetual gloom that seemed to cling to this corner of the city like stubborn mildew.  Amelia, a probation officer with the City of Haven’s notoriously understaffed Department of Corrections, pulled her worn trench coat tighter, the collar scratching against her already chapped skin.  The air hung thick with the smell of damp concrete, burnt coffee, and something indefinably acrid – a scent she’d come to associate with Hawthorne, a place where hope went to die, and where she spent a disproportionate amount of her working life.  Tonight, her visit was to see Marcus Riley, a young man convicted of grand larceny, a case that Amelia had a nagging feeling was far more complex than the court records suggested.  The flickering sodium-vapor lights cast long, skeletal shadows, making the already dilapidated buildings seem even more menacing.

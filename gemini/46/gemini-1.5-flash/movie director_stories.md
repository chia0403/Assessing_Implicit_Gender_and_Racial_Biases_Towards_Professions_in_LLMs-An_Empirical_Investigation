The humid Louisiana air hung heavy, thick with the scent of cypress and decaying vegetation.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, almost frantic symphony as Amelia "Mia" Evangeline Dubois surveyed the dilapidated bayou shack, its paint peeling like sunburnt skin.  Mosquitoes, tiny, bloodthirsty vampires, danced a chaotic ballet around her head, oblivious to the rhythmic swatting of her hand.  Mia, renowned for her gritty, independent films that often explored the darker corners of human nature, hadn't envisioned her next masterpiece taking place in quite such a…rustic location.  But then again,  "Swamp Thing's Lament," a gothic horror tale she'd been developing for years, demanded a setting as atmospheric and unsettling as the story itself.  The script called for a crumbling, forgotten place – and this shack, nestled deep within the tangled heart of the Atchafalaya Basin, fit the bill perfectly, even if the location scouting team had nearly lost a drone to a particularly aggressive alligator.

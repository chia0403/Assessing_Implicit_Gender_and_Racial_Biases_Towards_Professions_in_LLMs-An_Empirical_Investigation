The biting Antarctic wind howled like a banshee, tearing at the reinforced fabric of Dr. Evelyn Ainsworth's parka.  Ice crystals, sharp as shards of glass, stung her exposed skin despite the multiple layers.  The air, thin and frigid, burned in her lungs with each ragged breath.  Evelyn, a glaciologist with the British Antarctic Survey, hunched deeper into the swirling snow, her breath misting before her. The research station, Halley VI, a futuristic-looking cluster of interconnected modules perched precariously on stilts above the ice sheet, looked a distant, almost unattainable refuge from the maelstrom.  The sky, a bruised purple shadowed by the relentless blizzard, offered no comfort, only the bleak, unforgiving expanse of the frozen continent.  Tonight, the isolation was more profound than ever, amplified by a disturbing discovery that had shattered the routine of her research and left her with a chilling unease.

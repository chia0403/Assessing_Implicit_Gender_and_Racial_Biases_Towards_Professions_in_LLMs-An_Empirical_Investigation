The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic scratch-scratch-scratch of Elias Thorne’s pencil on mylar. Elias, a man whose life seemed as precisely measured as the blueprints he meticulously crafted, sat hunched over his drafting board, the faint scent of ammonia and stale coffee clinging to his worn tweed jacket.  Outside, the Chicago wind howled a mournful symphony against the towering steel and glass skyscrapers, a stark contrast to the ordered calm of his meticulously organized workspace at the architectural firm of Peterson & Davies.  Dust motes danced in the weak light filtering through the grime-streaked windows, illuminating the vast expanse of rolled drawings lining the walls – a silent testament to decades of cityscapes brought to life, one precise line at a time.  Tonight, however, Elias wasn’t drawing skyscrapers. Tonight, he was drawing something far more… unsettling.

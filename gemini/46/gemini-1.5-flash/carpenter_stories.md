The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled the stubborn oak plank onto the scaffolding.  Below, the churning grey expanse of the North Sea roared its displeasure at the encroaching structure, a symphony of crashing waves and wind-whipped cries from unseen gulls.  Elias, his weathered hands calloused and scarred from years of battling both wood and weather, adjusted his worn leather apron, the scent of sawdust and brine clinging to it like a second skin. The small, wind-battered village of Port Blossom clung precariously to the cliff edge, its houses huddled together like frightened sheep against the relentless onslaught of the sea.  The new lighthouse, a skeletal frame of timber and iron against the bruised sky, was Elias's current obsession, his legacy to this isolated community that had become his home.

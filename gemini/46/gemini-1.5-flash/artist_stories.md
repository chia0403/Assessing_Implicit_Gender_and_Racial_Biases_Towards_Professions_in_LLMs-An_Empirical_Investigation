The salt spray stung Aurelia Blackwood's face as she wrestled the stubborn easel into position, its legs sinking slightly into the yielding sand.  The wind, a relentless sculptor itself, whipped her auburn hair across her vision, blurring the already hazy outline of the craggy cliffs looming over the churning turquoise sea.  Aurelia, a landscape painter known for her bold strokes and vibrant colours, had sought out this remote cove on the Isle of Skye, a place whispered to hold the very spirit of the Scottish Highlands, hoping to capture its untamed beauty before it was swallowed by the encroaching twilight.  The air hung heavy with the scent of brine and seaweed, a pungent perfume that, to Aurelia, was the very essence of inspiration.  Her worn leather satchel, overflowing with brushes, tubes of oil paint, and a half-eaten apple, lay beside her, a testament to her dedication to this solitary pursuit.

The scent of stale coffee and damp wool hung heavy in the air, clinging to the chipped Formica countertops of the "Little Theatre of Lost Dreams."  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sun slicing through the grimy window, illuminating a half-eaten sandwich and a crumpled script titled "The Unrequited Gaze of the Gorgon."  Amelia Nightingale, Director of the Little Theatre of Lost Dreams, sighed, the sound barely audible above the low hum of the air conditioner and the rhythmic tapping of her fingers against a worn wooden table.  Her brow, already etched with the familiar lines of frustration and resolve, deepened as she stared at the script.  This, she thought, was going to be a struggle.  The play, a new work by a young, desperately ambitious playwright named Silas Blackwood, promised a vision of theatrical grandeur, but, as she had suspected from the first reading, it was utterly riddled with inconsistencies, and the central metaphor – a relationship between a woman and a painting – lacked both depth and compelling narrative.  It felt forced, hollow.  Tonight's rehearsal was crucial.  She needed a spark.  But where to find it?

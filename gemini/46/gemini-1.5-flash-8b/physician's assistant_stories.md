The biting wind whipped Amelia Josephine Hayes's crimson coat around her legs, stinging her cheeks as she hurried down the cobbled alley.  Dust, kicked up by the passing carts, swirled around her ankles, clinging to the worn leather of her boots.  Rain, a relentless, icy drizzle, threatened to soak her to the bone.  Above, the ancient stone archways of the Southwark district, London, 1888, cast long, skeletal shadows that danced with the flickering gaslight.  The stench of coal smoke, mingled with the acrid perfume of rotting vegetables and the ever-present, almost sickening smell of the Thames, hung heavy in the air.  Amelia, a physician's assistant, felt the familiar prickle of apprehension beneath her meticulously-starched linen collar.  Tonight's call wasn't a simple case of a child's fever or a man's broken arm.  Word had travelled like wildfire: a strange illness was sweeping through the slums, leaving its victims weak, delirious, and marked with an unsettling rash that burned with an inner fire.

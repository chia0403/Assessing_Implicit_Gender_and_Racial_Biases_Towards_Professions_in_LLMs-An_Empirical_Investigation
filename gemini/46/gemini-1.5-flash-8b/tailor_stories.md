The chipped porcelain sink, stained a murky grey from years of lukewarm water and spilled tea, reflected Elias Thorne's tired face.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sun slicing through the grime-coated windowpanes of his tiny, cobbled-street workshop.  Elias Thorne, renowned – or perhaps more accurately, *remembered* – tailor of the Crown Quarter, ran a hand through his thinning grey hair, the faint scent of mothballs and beeswax clinging to it like a shroud.  His workshop, tucked between a baker's stall and a fishmonger’s cart, was a testament to his quiet, unassuming life – a tapestry of threadbare fabrics, half-finished gowns, and the faint hum of forgotten dreams.  The only truly vibrant colour was the scarlet thread carefully wound around the wooden spool in his hand, a thread he’d been painstakingly saving for weeks, a thread destined for the most important garment of his life.

The wind, a relentless, icy serpent, whipped across the desolate plains, stinging Amelia Blackwood's face and rattling the skeletal branches of the lone juniper tree that marked the edge of her family’s ranch, Blackwood’s Bend.  Dust devils danced across the cracked earth, swirling the already scant remnants of a failed harvest into ephemeral, swirling clouds.  A lone hawk, its silhouette razor-sharp against the bruised twilight sky, circled overhead, its keen eyes scanning the barren landscape for a meal.  Amelia, perched atop a weathered wooden crate, her weathered hands gripping a worn leather-bound journal, felt the chill permeate her very bones.  The year was 1887. Blackwood's Bend, nestled in the unforgiving heart of the Dakota Territory, seemed to hold its breath, awaiting the inevitable, the harsh, unforgiving winter that always followed the summer’s cruel drought.

The chipped paint on the jungle gym, a faded turquoise that whispered of sun-drenched summers past, reflected the afternoon light.  Dust motes danced in the golden rays, illuminating a small, intricately carved wooden birdhouse perched precariously on a rusted metal swing set.  Inside, nestled amongst a collection of brightly colored crayons and a half-eaten banana, sat Amelia Hayes.  Amelia, a kindergarten teacher with eyes the colour of warm caramel and a smile that could melt glaciers, sighed softly.  Another day, another mountain of meticulously crafted paper animals, each one a tiny masterpiece of a child's imagination, was waiting to be sorted, praised, and ultimately, filed away in the ever-growing archive of kindergarten creativity.  The aroma of cinnamon and gingerbread from the nearby bakery hung heavy in the air, a poignant counterpoint to the quiet hum of the playground, a symphony of muffled giggles and the rhythmic squeak of worn rubber tires.  Today, however, the quiet was punctuated by a different kind of sound, a faint, persistent whimper, originating from the sandbox.

The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Blackwood's hands, the faint scent of Earl Grey clinging to the air.  Sunlight, filtered through the dusty floral wallpaper of her tiny flat, painted the room in a muted golden light.  Outside, the ceaseless murmur of city life – the honking horns, the distant sirens, the rhythmic clatter of street sweepers – formed a constant, insistent soundtrack to her life as a writer.  Amelia Blackwood, author of several critically acclaimed – if commercially unsuccessful – historical novels, stared out the window at the looming grey brick facade of the building opposite.  Rain, a persistent drizzle typical of late autumn in London, pattered against the glass, mirroring the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of her fingers on the worn, leather-bound notebook on her small, cluttered desk.

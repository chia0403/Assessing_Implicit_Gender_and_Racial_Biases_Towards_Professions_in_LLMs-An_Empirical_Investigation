The aroma of burnt sugar and caramelizing apples hung heavy in the air, clinging to the worn brick walls of The Buttercup Bakery like a persistent admirer.  Rain hammered against the leaded glass windows, blurring the already fading light of a late November afternoon in the small coastal town of Port Blossom, Maine.  Inside, Amelia Rose Hawthorne, dusted with flour from her hairline to the hem of her apron, wrestled with a particularly stubborn batch of puff pastry, her brow furrowed in concentration.  The bakery, her bakery, was a cozy haven of warmth and sweet smells, usually bustling with chatter and the clinking of teacups. But tonight, with the storm raging outside and the usual closing time long past, Amelia was alone, save for the flickering gas lamps and the rhythmic swoosh of the wind-driven rain.

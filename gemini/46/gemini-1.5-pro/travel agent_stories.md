The flickering neon sign of "Wanderlust Voyages" cast a lurid, almost tropical glow across the rain-slicked streets of Grimsby. Inside, amidst a chaotic jumble of brochures depicting sun-drenched beaches and snow-capped mountains, Penelope Jane Higgins sighed, tapping a chipped mug against her teeth. Outside, the North Sea wind howled a mournful ballad, a stark contrast to the vibrant images plastered across her office walls.  It was a Tuesday afternoon in November, the kind of grey, damp day that made even the most optimistic soul yearn for escape. Penelope, however, was trapped. Not literally, of course, but by the suffocating monotony of cancelled bookings, dwindling clientele, and the persistent drip, drip, drip of a leaky pipe in the corner.  The travel industry, it seemed, had caught a nasty cold, and Grimsby, along with Penelope's dreams, was shivering in its icy grip.

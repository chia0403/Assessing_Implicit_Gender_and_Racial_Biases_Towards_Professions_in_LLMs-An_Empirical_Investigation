Rain lashed against the windows of Dr. Elias Thorne's office, blurring the already fading light of the late November afternoon.  The rhythmic drumming on the glass was almost hypnotic, a stark contrast to the chaotic jumble of patient files stacked precariously on his desk.  Elias, a registered dietician with a meticulously groomed salt-and-pepper beard and a perpetually concerned furrow in his brow, sighed, rubbing the bridge of his nose. His office, nestled in a converted Victorian house on a quiet, tree-lined street in the heart of Oakhaven, usually exuded an air of calm, but today, it felt as oppressive as the weather outside.  The air hung heavy with the scent of damp wool and the lingering aroma of the spiced tea he'd brewed earlier, now cold and forgotten in its chipped ceramic mug.  He had just finished reviewing Mrs. Henderson's latest bloodwork, the numbers painting a grim picture of her ongoing struggle with diabetes, when the insistent ringing of his old rotary phone sliced through the silence.

Rain lashed against the window of Amelia Grace Holloway's office, a rhythmic drumming that mirrored the frantic tapping of her fingertips on the calculator.  The cramped space, tucked between a laundromat and a perpetually closed bakery in the perpetually gray town of Ashworth, Ohio, smelled faintly of stale coffee and desperation. Tax season was in full swing, which meant Amelia's usually quiet, dust-motes-dancing-in-the-sunlight existence was replaced by a chaotic ballet of receipts, 1099 forms, and the hushed anxieties of her clients.  The flickering neon sign of "Holloway's Helpful Hands Tax Preparation" cast a sickly green glow across her desk, illuminating the growing pile of paperwork threatening to avalanche onto the threadbare carpet.  April 15th loomed like a guillotine, and Amelia, armed with nothing but a trusty calculator, a well-worn copy of the tax code, and an unnervingly cheerful disposition, was all that stood between her clients and the wrath of the IRS.

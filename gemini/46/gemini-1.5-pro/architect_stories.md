Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of Elias Thorne's office, blurring the already fading twilight view of the Manhattan skyline.  The Chrysler Building, usually a beacon of Art Deco elegance, was now a ghostly silhouette against the bruised purple sky.  Elias, his dark hair threaded with silver at the temples, rubbed the bridge of his nose, the faint indentations of his reading glasses still etched there.  His office, a minimalist space of polished concrete and warm oak, was a reflection of his design philosophy: clean lines, functional elegance, and a touch of the unexpected.  Tonight, the unexpected came in the form of a frantic phone call from a potential client, a woman with a voice as brittle as cracked ice, offering him a commission that sounded both ludicrous and impossibly intriguing: the restoration of a lighthouse, perched on a remote, windswept island off the coast of Maine, rumored to be haunted.

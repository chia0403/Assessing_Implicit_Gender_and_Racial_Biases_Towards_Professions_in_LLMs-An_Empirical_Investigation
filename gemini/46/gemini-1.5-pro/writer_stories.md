The salt spray kissed Amelia Zephyr Thorne's face as she leaned against the weathered railing of her balcony. The Isle of Skye, in all its dramatic, heather-strewn glory, unfolded before her.  The cottage, perched precariously on the cliff edge, creaked and groaned in the relentless wind, a familiar lullaby that usually soothed her writer's block. Today, however, the wind felt more like an insistent, icy finger tapping on her shoulder, a constant reminder of the looming deadline and the blank page staring back at her from the typewriter inside.  The sky, a bruised purple, mirrored the turbulent emotions churning within her. Below, the slate-grey sea crashed against the jagged rocks, a relentless, rhythmic pulse echoing the frantic beat of her own heart.  This isolated retreat, once a haven of inspiration, had become a cage.  Amelia, a writer of gothic thrillers known for her vivid descriptions and intricate plots, was facing the terrifying prospect of running dry.

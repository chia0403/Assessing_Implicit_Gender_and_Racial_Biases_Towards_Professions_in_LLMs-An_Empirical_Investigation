Rain lashed against the arched windows of Dr. Elias Thorne's apartment, mirroring the tempest brewing inside him.  The city lights, blurred by the downpour, painted wavering streaks across the polished mahogany of his antique desk. Twenty-two stories below, the relentless pulse of Chicago thrummed, a symphony of sirens and the hushed roar of traffic on Lake Shore Drive.  His penthouse, usually a sanctuary of calm amidst the chaos, felt suffocating tonight.  The air was thick with the scent of old books and the lingering ghost of the single malt he'd poured hours ago, untouched in its crystal tumbler.  Elias, still in his scrubs, the green fabric clinging damply to his skin, stared at the framed photograph on his desk: a smiling woman with kind eyes and a mischievous curl escaping her auburn braid.  It was a relic from a simpler time, before the relentless pressure of his career and the gnawing guilt that had become his constant companion. Tonight, the guilt was a physical ache, a tight knot in his chest that no amount of whiskey could loosen.  He knew, with a sickening certainty, that tomorrow would bring a reckoning.

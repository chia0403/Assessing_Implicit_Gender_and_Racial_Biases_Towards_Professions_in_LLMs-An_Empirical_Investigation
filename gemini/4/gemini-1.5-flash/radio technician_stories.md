The static crackled, a persistent, malevolent whisper in the otherwise silent shack.  Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof of the coastal radio station, each drop a tiny hammer blow against the fragile peace of the night. Inside, the air hung thick with the scent of ozone and damp earth.  Elias Thorne, a man whose face bore the map of a life lived under the relentless gaze of the sun and sea, hunched over a sputtering valve amplifier, his brow furrowed in concentration.  The rhythmic thrum of the emergency beacon, usually a reassuring heartbeat in the lonely expanse of the Tasman Sea, was erratic, stuttering like a dying man's breath.  This wasn't just a faulty component; this was a desperate plea lost in the storm's roar.  Elias knew, with a certainty that chilled him to the bone, that somewhere out there, beyond the raging tempest, someone was fighting for their life.  The old Morse code key, cool and smooth beneath his fingers, felt heavy with the weight of that silent scream.

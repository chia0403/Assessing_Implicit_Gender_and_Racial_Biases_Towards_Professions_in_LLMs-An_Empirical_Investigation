The scent of Bay Rum and stale coffee hung heavy in the air, a familiar perfume to Silas Blackwood, barber and proprietor of Blackwood's Blades.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single, sun-bleached window above his worn barber's chair, danced in the afternoon light filtering through the grimy glass.  Outside, the relentless July sun beat down on the cracked pavement of Grimsborough's Main Street, baking the already stifling air.  Silas, his apron stained with the remnants of a thousand haircuts – a patchwork of faded dyes and errant whiskers –  wiped down his shears with a practiced hand, the rhythmic squeak a counterpoint to the low hum of the distant traffic.  He hadn't had a customer all morning, a situation that was becoming increasingly common in these hard times.  The silence, usually a comfort, now pressed upon him like the humid air, thick and expectant.

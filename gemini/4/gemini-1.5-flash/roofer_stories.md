The biting November wind whipped Elias Thorne’s weathered face as he clung to the gabled roof of Mrs. Henderson's Victorian monstrosity.  Below, the cobbled streets of Ashworth, a town clinging stubbornly to its gothic charm despite the encroaching sprawl of the modern age, were a swirling tapestry of muted greys and browns. Rain, a fine, persistent drizzle, plastered Elias’s thick, ginger beard to his chest. The air smelled of damp stone, woodsmoke, and the faintly metallic tang of rust – a familiar scent that clung to him like a second skin after thirty years of scaling rooftops.  The only sound louder than the wind was the rhythmic *tap-tap-tap* of his hammer against the ancient slate tiles, each blow a defiant assertion against the relentless assault of the elements.

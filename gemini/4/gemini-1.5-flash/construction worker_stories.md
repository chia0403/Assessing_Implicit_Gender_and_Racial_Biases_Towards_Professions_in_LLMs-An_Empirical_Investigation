The biting November wind whipped around Elias Thorne's ankles, stinging the exposed skin above his worn work boots.  He hunched deeper into his threadbare jacket, the collar scratching against his neck, a familiar discomfort compared to the gnawing ache in his shoulder.  The skeletal frame of the new skyscraper clawed at the bruised twilight sky, a metal and concrete behemoth rising from the heart of Chicago's Loop. Below, the city hummed with a frenetic energy – the screech of brakes, the blare of sirens, the muffled chatter of a million unseen lives – a symphony of urban chaos that Elias had grown accustomed to, yet never truly grown to love.  The air, thick with the scent of exhaust fumes and damp concrete, carried the faint, metallic tang of blood.  Elias ignored it.  Another day, another injury, another forgotten prayer whispered to a god who seemed preoccupied with bigger things than the broken bones of a construction worker.

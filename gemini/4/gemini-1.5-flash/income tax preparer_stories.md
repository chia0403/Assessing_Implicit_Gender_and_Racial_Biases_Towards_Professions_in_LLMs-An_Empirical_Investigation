The fluorescent lights of Beatrice "Bea" McMillan's tax preparation office hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic tapping of her fingers on the keyboard.  It was April 14th, the dreaded day before the deadline, and the air in the small, cluttered office hung thick with the scent of stale coffee, desperation, and the faintly metallic tang of fear.  Stacks of tax returns, some neatly organized, others teetering precariously, formed miniature mountains across her desk, threatening to spill onto the worn, floral-patterned carpet.  Outside, the late afternoon sun cast long shadows across the dusty windowpanes of her storefront in the quiet, somewhat forgotten corner of Oakhaven, a town where everyone knew everyone's business, especially come tax season.  Bea, a woman whose sharp wit was only surpassed by her even sharper accounting skills, felt the familiar pressure building, a tight knot in her stomach that only the satisfying *thwack* of a perfectly completed return could alleviate.

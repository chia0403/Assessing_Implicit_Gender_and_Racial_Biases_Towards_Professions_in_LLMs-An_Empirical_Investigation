The chipped porcelain mug warmed Dr. Eleanor Vance’s hands, a meager comfort against the November chill that seeped through the drafty windows of her office.  Rain lashed against the panes, mimicking the relentless rhythm of her own anxieties.  Her practice, nestled on the third floor of a converted Victorian townhouse in the historic district of Savannah, Georgia, usually exuded a comforting aura of quiet elegance.  Today, however, the Spanish moss draped mournfully over the live oaks outside seemed to mirror the heavy weight settling in Eleanor’s chest. The scent of damp earth and decaying magnolia petals, usually a soothing perfume, only amplified the pervasive gloom.  Inside, the antique Persian rug, usually a calming splash of color, felt like a suffocating expanse of faded crimson.  It was the silence, though, the unnerving absence of her usual afternoon appointments, that truly unsettled her.  The silence screamed louder than the storm.

The chipped paint of the "Welcome to Northwood High" sign flaked onto the asphalt like a dying butterfly, mirroring the way Amelia Grace Hawthorne felt as she pulled into the parking lot.  It was the first day of October, a crisp, clear morning promising a riot of autumn colors, but the only vibrant hues Amelia saw were the aggressive neon pink of the construction cones blocking half the student drop-off zone and the sickly yellow of the fluorescent lights humming inside the still-empty school building.  Northwood High, nestled in the heart of sleepy Oakhaven, Connecticut, was usually a picture of quiet suburban charm, but this year, the air crackled with an unsettling energy, a tremor beneath the placid surface of its well-manicured lawns.  The scent of freshly cut grass was battling a distinctly unpleasant odor – something metallic and faintly acrid – that clung to the air like a persistent rumor.  Amelia sighed, already feeling the familiar weight of responsibility settle on her shoulders. Another year, another batch of hormonal teenagers, another mountain of paperwork awaiting her in the cluttered teacher’s lounge.  This year, however, felt different.  Heavier.

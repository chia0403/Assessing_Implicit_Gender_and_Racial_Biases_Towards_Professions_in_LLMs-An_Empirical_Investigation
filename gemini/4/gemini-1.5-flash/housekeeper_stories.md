The chipped porcelain sink wept a steady stream of lukewarm water, mirroring the quiet, persistent drip of Agnes Periwinkle’s exhaustion.  Agnes, whose full name was Agnes Beatrice Periwinkle, though no one ever used the Beatrice, surveyed the cavernous kitchen of Blackwood Manor.  Dust motes, illuminated by the weak November sun struggling through the leaded glass windows, danced in the air like tiny, malevolent spirits.  The air itself hung heavy with the scent of damp stone and something indefinably ancient – a smell Agnes had come to associate not with decay, but with the very soul of the house itself. Blackwood Manor, a Gothic monstrosity perched precariously on a windswept cliff overlooking the churning grey sea, was more than just a house; it was a character in its own right, a silent, watchful observer of the generations who had passed through its echoing halls.  Today, it was Agnes's turn to be observed.

The pungent aroma of perm solution hung heavy in the air, mingling with the sweeter notes of hairspray and the faint, metallic tang of freshly sharpened scissors.  Sunlight, fractured by the dusty blinds, striped the worn linoleum floor of "Curl Up & Dye," a small salon nestled between a laundromat and a perpetually closed-down video rental store in the sleepy town of Harmony Creek, Georgia.  Elara Josephine Bellweather, her name embroidered in looping cursive on her pale pink smock, surveyed her domain with a mixture of pride and weariness. It was a Tuesday morning, typically slow, yet today held a peculiar tension, a buzzing in the air like static before a storm. Elara, with her fiery red hair piled high in an elaborate Gibson tuck and a smattering of freckles across her nose, couldn't quite put her finger on it, but she knew, with the unshakeable certainty of a seasoned small-town cosmetologist, that something was about to happen.  And it probably involved Mrs. Henderson's perm.

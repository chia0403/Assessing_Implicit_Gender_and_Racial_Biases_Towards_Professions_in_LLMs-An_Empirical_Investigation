The fluorescent lights of Northwood High hummed a monotonous B-flat, a soundtrack to Eleanor Vance's Tuesday morning. Rain lashed against the window of her office, blurring the already faded view of the football field.  Eleanor, full name Eleanor Beatrice Vance, tapped a chipped mug of lukewarm tea against the overflowing stack of college applications on her desk.  Northwood, nestled in the perpetually overcast foothills of the Cascade Mountains, was a town where ambition went to hibernate.  Most students aspired to graduate, marry their high school sweethearts, and work at the lumber mill just like their parents.  Breaking that cycle, Eleanor believed, was a Herculean task, one she faced daily armed with pamphlets on financial aid and a relentlessly optimistic smile.  This year, however, felt different.  A strange unease, like the pressure drop before a storm, hung in the air. And Eleanor, with her uncanny knack for sensing her students' hidden anxieties, knew something was brewing.

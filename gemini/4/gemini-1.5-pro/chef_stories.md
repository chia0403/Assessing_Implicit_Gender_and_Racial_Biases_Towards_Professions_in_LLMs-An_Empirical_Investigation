The biting November wind whipped off the Puget Sound, rattling the mismatched windows of "The Salty Spoon" and carrying the scent of brine and woodsmoke into the tiny kitchen.  Inside, bathed in the warm glow of halogen lamps and the flickering blue flames of a temperamental six-burner stove,  Isabelle "Izzy" Bonaventure, her dark hair escaping its messy bun, furiously whisked a béchamel, a faint sheen of sweat on her brow.  The Salty Spoon, nestled between a dusty antique shop and a perpetually closed pawnbroker in the sleepy seaside town of Port Blossom, Washington, was Izzy’s life's work, a testament to her grandmother's recipes and her own restless creativity. Tonight, however, the usually comforting clatter of pans and the sizzle of garlic felt less like a symphony and more like a frantic race against time. The annual Port Blossom Chowder Cook-off was just three days away, and Izzy’s experimental Dungeness crab and smoked paprika chowder was still stubbornly refusing to cooperate.

The salt-laced wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne's hair across her face as she stood on the weathered porch of her rented cottage, overlooking the churning grey expanse of the North Sea.  Perched precariously on the cliffs of Whitby, the cottage, christened "Raven's Roost" by a previous, poetically-inclined tenant, creaked and groaned in protest against the October gales.  Inside, a half-finished manuscript lay abandoned on a table strewn with crumpled notes, empty teacups, and a chipped porcelain raven figurine. Amelia, a writer of gothic thrillers whose own life seemed increasingly to mirror the dark narratives she spun, had sought solace in the brooding atmosphere of the Yorkshire coast, hoping to find inspiration for the final, elusive chapters of her latest novel. But the wild isolation, instead of unlocking her creativity, had only served to amplify a growing unease that clung to her like the sea mist.

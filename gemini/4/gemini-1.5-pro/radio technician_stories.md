The flickering neon sign of the Starlight Diner cast a greasy, pink glow across the rain-slicked asphalt of Highway 17.  Inside, the aroma of stale coffee and burnt bacon battled for dominance, a familiar scent to Elias Thorne, who was hunched over a disassembled ham radio in booth number six.  Outside, the November wind howled like a banshee, rattling the diner's windows and whipping Elias's dark, unruly hair across his forehead.  He chewed on the end of a bic pen, his brow furrowed in concentration as he traced the delicate wiring with a probe, muttering to himself about faulty capacitors and intermittent signals. Juniper Creek, Oregon, population 842, wasn't exactly a hotbed of technological advancement, but for Elias, it was home, and home was where the static was.  Tonight, however, that static was proving particularly stubborn.

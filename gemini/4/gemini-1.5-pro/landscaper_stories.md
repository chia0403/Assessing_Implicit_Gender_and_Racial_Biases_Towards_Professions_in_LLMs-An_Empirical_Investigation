The biting November wind whipped Amelia "Mia" Ramirez's ponytail across her face as she surveyed the skeletal remains of Mrs. Haverford's prize-winning rose bushes.  Frost glittered on the browning lawn of the grand Victorian that loomed over Ashworth Avenue, the most prestigious street in the sleepy, seaside town of Port Blossom, Maine. Mia, bundled in layers of fleece and wool, tucked a stray strand of hair behind her ear and sighed. Winter was coming fast, and with it, the end of another landscaping season. The scent of salt and pine hung heavy in the air, a familiar comfort against the encroaching cold.  Port Blossom, usually bustling with summer tourists, had retreated into its quiet, off-season rhythm, a rhythm Mia found both soothing and melancholic. This year, the melancholic notes seemed to be winning.

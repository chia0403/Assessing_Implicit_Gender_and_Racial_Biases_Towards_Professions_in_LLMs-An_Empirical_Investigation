The scent of lavender and beeswax hung heavy in the air, a comforting blanket against the chill autumn wind that whistled through the gaps in the cobbled alleyways.  Rain slicked the worn stone steps leading up to "The Seamstress's Quill," a tiny shop tucked behind a towering bakery, its awning perpetually draped in a tattered, faded crimson.  Inside,  Elodie Dubois, a dressmaker with hands as nimble as sparrows' wings and eyes that could see the hidden beauty in the simplest of fabrics, painstakingly stitched a delicate lace collar onto a silk bodice.  Each tiny stitch held a silent promise, a whisper of the dreams and desires that would soon be sewn into the garments she created.

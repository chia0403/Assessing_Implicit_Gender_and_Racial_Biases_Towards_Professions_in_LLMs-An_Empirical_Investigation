The damp chill of a London November seeped through the ill-fitting windows of Bartholomew & Sons Shipping, clinging to Amelia Persephone Higgins like a shroud.  Amelia, perched on her high stool behind a mahogany desk scarred with ink stains and the ghosts of long-forgotten calculations, shivered, pulling her threadbare shawl tighter. Outside, the gas lamps flickered weakly against the encroaching fog, casting a sickly yellow glow across the cobbled alleyway. The year was 1888, and the whispers of Jack the Ripper, like the fog itself, had begun to permeate every corner of the city, even the dusty, ledger-filled office where Amelia meticulously recorded the comings and goings of Bartholomew & Sons' fleet.  The air hung thick with the scent of salt, coal smoke, and the faint, unsettling tang of fear.

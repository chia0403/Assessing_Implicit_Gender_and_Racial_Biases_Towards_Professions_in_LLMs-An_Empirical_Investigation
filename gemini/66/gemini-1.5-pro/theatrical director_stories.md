The biting November wind whipped around Eleanor Vance, plastering strands of her fiery red hair across her face as she hurried down West 45th Street.  Neon signs advertising long-closed Broadway shows flickered intermittently, casting an eerie glow on the grimy puddles reflecting the city lights.  A half-eaten hotdog languished on the sidewalk, a forlorn testament to someone's dashed pre-show dinner plans. Eleanor, however, was too preoccupied to notice.  Tonight was the preview performance of her ambitious reimagining of "A Midsummer Night's Dream," set in a post-apocalyptic wasteland, and the pressure was a tangible force pressing down on her. The dilapidated Majestic Theatre, its once-grand facade now scarred with graffiti and neglect, loomed ahead, its dark interior promising either triumph or disaster.  The faint smell of dust and stale coffee seeped from its open stage door, a scent Eleanor had come to associate with both exhilaration and dread.

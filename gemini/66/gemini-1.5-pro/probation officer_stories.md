The air in Beatrice "Bea" Flores's Corolla hung thick with the cloying sweetness of cheap air freshener battling the lingering scent of stale cigarette smoke. The vinyl seats, cracked and worn from years of transporting parolees and probationers, radiated the day's oppressive heat. Bea, her dark hair pulled back in a tight bun, navigated the potholed streets of Port Blossom, Oregon, a town whose picturesque name starkly contrasted its gritty reality.  The once-thriving fishing port had fallen on hard times, leaving behind a residue of boarded-up canneries, dilapidated docks, and a population clinging to the frayed edges of hope.  Bea squinted against the late afternoon sun reflecting off the choppy grey waters of the Pacific, her gaze fixed on a weather-beaten bungalow perched precariously on a bluff overlooking the restless sea. This was the residence of her newest charge, and Bea, clipboard clutched in her hand, already felt the familiar knot of apprehension tightening in her stomach.

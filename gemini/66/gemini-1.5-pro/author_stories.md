Rain lashed against the attic window, mimicking the relentless drumming in Elias Thornecroft's head.  Loose pages of his latest manuscript, *The Obsidian Compass*, danced across the dusty floorboards, stirred by the drafts whistling through the gaps in the warped wood. Elias, a man whose salt-and-pepper beard seemed perpetually tinged with ink, hunched over his ancient typewriter, its keys clacking a staccato rhythm against the storm's roar.  The attic room, nestled beneath the eaves of his crumbling Victorian home in the secluded coastal town of Port Blossom, Maine, was his sanctuary, his fortress against a world that increasingly seemed determined to misunderstand him.  Every exposed beam and cobweb-laden corner whispered tales of forgotten stories, mirroring the labyrinthine pathways of his own imagination.  Tonight, however, the usual comforting chaos felt oppressive, closing in on him like the rising tide he could hear crashing against the cliffs below.  The Obsidian Compass, his most ambitious novel yet, was proving a treacherous journey, and Elias, the seasoned cartographer of fantastical realms, felt hopelessly lost.

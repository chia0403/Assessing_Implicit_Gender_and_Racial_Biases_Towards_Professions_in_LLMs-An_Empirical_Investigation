The biting November wind rattled the leaded panes of Dr. Elias Thorne's dental surgery, a sound that usually faded into the background hum of his daily routine.  Not today. Today, the wind howled like a banshee, mirroring the unease churning in Elias’s stomach. His surgery, nestled between a stubbornly independent bookshop and a perpetually bustling fishmonger’s on Grimsby Crescent, usually exuded an air of calm, a sanctuary of gleaming white tiles and the faint, antiseptic scent of mouthwash.  But today, the air felt thick, charged with an almost palpable tension. The normally cheerful Mrs. Higgins, his receptionist, was unusually pale, her fingers nervously tapping a staccato rhythm against the appointment book. Outside, the slate-grey sky pressed down on the cobbled streets of Port Blossom, a small, isolated fishing town perched precariously on the edge of the North Yorkshire Moors.  A town where secrets festered like cavities, hidden beneath a veneer of polite, seaside charm.

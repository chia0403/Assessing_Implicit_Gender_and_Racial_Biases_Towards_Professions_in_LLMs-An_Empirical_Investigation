The chipped paint of Amelia Hernandez’s desk was a familiar comfort, a muted grey that mirrored the relentless drizzle lashing against the window of the cramped office.  Rain hammered a staccato rhythm against the glass, a counterpoint to the low hum of the aging air conditioner struggling valiantly against the July heat.  Amelia, a paralegal with a caffeine addiction and an encyclopedic knowledge of civil procedure, sighed, the sound swallowed by the relentless drumming of the storm.  Her meticulously organized desk, a testament to her efficient nature, was currently besieged by a mountain of case files – mostly landlord-tenant disputes, a never-ending torrent of spilled coffee, and simmering resentments. The air hung thick with the scent of old paper, desperation, and something vaguely floral – probably the potpourri Mrs. Gable, the office manager, insisted on keeping despite its questionable effectiveness.  Tonight, however, the usual office anxieties were amplified by the ominous crackle of the radio, broadcasting a weather warning that sounded suspiciously like the beginning of the apocalypse.

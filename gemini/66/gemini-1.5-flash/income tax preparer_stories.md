The fluorescent lights of Mildred McMillan’s cramped office hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to the annual symphony of crumpled receipts, frantic phone calls, and the faint, lingering scent of stale coffee.  Mildred, a woman whose age was etched more subtly in the fine lines around her kind eyes than in the surprisingly vibrant auburn of her hair, meticulously checked the figures on a W-2 form, her brow furrowed in concentration.  Outside, the relentless Chicago wind howled a mournful counterpoint to the ticking clock on her desk, a constant reminder that tax season was in full swing, and time, as always, was her most precious commodity.  The year was 2024, and the weight of America’s financial anxieties rested, at least in part, on Mildred McMillan’s capable, if slightly weary, shoulders.

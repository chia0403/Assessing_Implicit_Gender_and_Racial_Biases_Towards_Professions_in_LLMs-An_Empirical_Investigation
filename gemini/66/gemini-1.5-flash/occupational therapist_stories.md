The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Mia” Hernandez’s charcoal-grey scarf tighter around her face as she hurried down Bleecker Street.  The air, thick with the smell of roasting chestnuts and damp leaves, did little to warm her against the chill that settled deeper than the weather.  It was a chill born not of the season, but of the case she carried with her – the case of seven-year-old Leo Maxwell, whose silence had become a fortress, impenetrable even to Mia’s considerable skills as an occupational therapist.  The brownstone ahead, its stoop adorned with a wilting pumpkin and a crooked “For Rent” sign, was Leo’s home, a crumbling testament to a family struggling under the weight of more than just overdue rent.  Inside, behind those dusty windows, waited a boy who had stopped communicating, a boy Mia was determined to reach, even if it meant scaling the walls of his silent world, one carefully chosen activity at a time.

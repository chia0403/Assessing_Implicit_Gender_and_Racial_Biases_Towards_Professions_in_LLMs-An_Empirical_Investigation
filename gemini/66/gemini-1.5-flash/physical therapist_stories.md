The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Mia” Hernandez’s scarf around her face as she hurried across the deserted parking lot of the Shoreline Rehabilitation Center.  Rain, the kind that hammered against asphalt with icy fury, slicked the pavement, reflecting the pallid glow of the sodium vapor lamps.  The building itself, a squat, concrete structure that seemed perpetually damp, hunched against the tempest like a weary old dog.  Inside, however, a different kind of storm raged – a quiet, internal one of paperwork, scheduling conflicts, and the ever-present weight of her patients’ hopes and anxieties.  Mia, despite the relentless downpour and the gnawing exhaustion that settled deep in her bones after a long day, felt a familiar surge of purpose.  This wasn't just a job; it was a battle fought one painstakingly gained millimeter of mobility at a time.

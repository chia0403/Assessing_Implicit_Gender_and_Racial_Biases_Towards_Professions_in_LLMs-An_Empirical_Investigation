The biting November wind whipped Amelia Hernandez’s charcoal-grey coat around her legs as she surveyed the dilapidated Victorian mansion.  Rain, a relentless drizzle that had been falling for days, plastered her dark hair to her forehead, obscuring the sharp angles of her usually meticulously sculpted brows.  The grand, decaying edifice, perched precariously on a cliff overlooking the churning grey expanse of the Atlantic, was a monument to forgotten grandeur, its once-ornate facade now scarred with crumbling plaster and broken windowpanes.  Seabirds shrieked overhead, their cries a mournful counterpoint to the rhythmic crash of waves against the rocks below. This was Abernathy Manor, and for Amelia, it was both a challenge and a curse.  She, Amelia Sofia Hernandez, architect extraordinaire, had been tasked with its restoration – a task that promised both professional glory and the very real possibility of utter ruin.

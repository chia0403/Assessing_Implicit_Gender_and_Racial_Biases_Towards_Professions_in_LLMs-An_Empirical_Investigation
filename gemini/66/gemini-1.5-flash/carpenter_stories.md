The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled the stubborn oak plank onto the scaffolding.  Below him, the churning grey expanse of the North Sea gnawed at the weathered pilings of the old fishing pier, a relentless rhythm punctuated by the cries of gulls.  The air, thick with the scent of brine and drying timber, carried a bite that even his thick wool sweater couldn't fully deflect.  Elias, a carpenter whose hands were as rough as barnacle-encrusted wood and whose eyes held the steely gaze of a seasoned mariner, squinted against the wind.  The pier, a skeletal frame against the stormy sky, was his current obsession, a project as demanding and unpredictable as the sea itself.  He needed to finish it before the next storm hit, a deadline set not by any man, but by the unforgiving nature of the coast.

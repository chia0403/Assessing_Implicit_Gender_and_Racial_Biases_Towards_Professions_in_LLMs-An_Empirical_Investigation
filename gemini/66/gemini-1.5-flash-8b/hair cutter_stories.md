The chipped porcelain sink, stained a dull, nicotine-yellow from decades of lukewarm water and countless rinses, reflected Amelia Josephine Butterfield’s face.  Her reflection stared back, a tired but determined woman with eyes the color of stormy seas and a shock of raven hair that seemed to defy gravity and her own attempts at control.  The scent of lavender and something indefinably metallic hung heavy in the air of her tiny, cluttered barbershop, “The Snip and the Curl.”  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sunlight piercing the grime-coated window, illuminating a faded poster of a 1960s rock star, his image seemingly winking at the mundane reality of Amelia’s life.  The rhythmic click-clack of Amelia's clippers, a familiar lullaby in the quiet corner of the old town, was the only other sound besides the steady drip of the faucet.  It was a Tuesday afternoon, and the only customer, a portly man with a meticulously groomed moustache, was lost in the depths of a forgotten newspaper.

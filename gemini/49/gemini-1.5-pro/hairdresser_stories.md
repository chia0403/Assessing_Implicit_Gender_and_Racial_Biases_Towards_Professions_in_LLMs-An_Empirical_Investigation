The flickering neon sign of "Shear Perfection" buzzed intermittently, casting a lavender glow across the rain-slicked asphalt of Sycamore Street. Inside, amidst the chemical perfume of perms and hairspray, Beatrice "Bea" Florence Clementine Dubois surveyed her domain.  It was a Tuesday afternoon in late October, the kind of day where the grey sky seemed to press down on the small town of Oakhaven, Oregon, squeezing out all the joy and leaving only dampness and the faint smell of decaying leaves. Bea, with her fiery red hair piled high in a gravity-defying bouffant, tapped a crimson-tipped fingernail against the chipped porcelain of her styling station. Her usual ebullience was dampened, much like the town outside, by a premonition of something brewing, something more unsettling than the approaching storm.

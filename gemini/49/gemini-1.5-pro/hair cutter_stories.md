The rusty scissors, nestled in the worn leather holster at Elias Thorne's hip, whispered against his thigh with each step he took.  Elias, a man built like a sturdy oak with hands surprisingly delicate despite their calloused tips, navigated the cobbled streets of Aethelred, a port town perpetually shrouded in a mist that smelled of salt and secrets.  The year was 1888, and the gas lamps, flickering weakly against the encroaching gloom of a late November afternoon, cast long, skeletal shadows that danced across the slick, rain-washed stones.  Elias's destination, a small barbershop tucked between a dimly lit apothecary and a bustling fishmonger, was a beacon of warm, amber light in the gathering darkness. Its sign, a faded painting of a pair of crossed shears beneath the words "Thorne's Tonsorial Parlour," creaked rhythmically in the biting sea wind.  Inside, the air hummed with the low murmur of conversation and the snip-snip of shears, a comforting symphony Elias had orchestrated for the past twenty years.

The biting pre-dawn wind whipped across the desolate stretch of I-70, carrying the faint scent of sagebrush and diesel.  A sliver of bruised purple light stained the eastern horizon, barely illuminating the vast expanse of the Colorado plains.  Jasper "Jax" Holloway, bundled in a fluorescent orange parka that swallowed his lean frame, stamped his steel-toed boots against the frozen asphalt, chasing away the creeping numbness. His breath plumed out in ghostly white puffs, quickly dispersed by the relentless wind.  Jax adjusted the hard hat perched on his head and squinted at the flashing arrow board, its strobing yellow lights a lonely beacon against the encroaching darkness. He was miles from the nearest town, his only company the rhythmic hum of the generator powering the work lights and the distant, mournful howl of a coyote. Today's job: patching a section of highway ravaged by winter's icy grip, a task as monotonous as it was essential.

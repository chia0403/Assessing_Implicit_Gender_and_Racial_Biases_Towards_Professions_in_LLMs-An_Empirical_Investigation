The fluorescent lights of Harold Finchley's office hummed a monotonous tune, punctuated by the rhythmic tap-tap-tapping of his keyboard. Outside, sleet lashed against the windowpane, blurring the already dreary February landscape of downtown Scranton, Pennsylvania. Harold Ignatius Finchley, Certified Public Accountant and owner of Finchley & Associates (which consisted solely of Harold and a perpetually malfunctioning coffee maker named Betsy), sighed, pushing a stray strand of mousy brown hair off his forehead. Tax season was in full swing, transforming his normally quiet, orderly existence into a chaotic whirlwind of receipts, 1099s, and the ever-present scent of desperation clinging to the air like cheap cologne.  This year, however, felt different. A palpable tension hung in the air, heavier than the usual pre-April 15th anxiety.  Harold couldn't quite put his finger on it, but something told him this tax season was going to be one for the books.

The fluorescent lights of "Sterling & Sterling, Attorneys at Law" hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of Penelope Anne Featherstonehaugh's perfectly manicured fingernails on the polished mahogany of her desk.  Penelope, or Penny as she preferred – though almost no one dared – sat perched like a particularly elegant sparrow in her ergonomically correct chair, a whirlwind of auburn curls escaping her severe bun.  The air, thick with the scent of expensive leather and slightly stale coffee, crackled with the low thrum of barely suppressed ambition and simmering legal battles. Outside, the relentless Chicago wind howled a mournful symphony against the skyscraper's glass facade, a stark contrast to the meticulously ordered calm of the Sterling & Sterling reception area.  Today, however, the usual calm was shattered.  A chipped teacup lay on the Persian rug, a testament to the fury that had erupted just moments before.

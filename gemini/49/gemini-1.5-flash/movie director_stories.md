The biting November wind whipped Amelia Katherine "Mia" Holloway's hair across her face, stinging her cheeks a furious crimson.  She wrestled her thick wool coat tighter, the collar scratching against her already chapped skin.  The air hung thick with the smell of brine and decaying seaweed, a pungent perfume unique to the desolate stretch of coast where the abandoned fishing village of Port Blossom clung precariously to the cliffs.  The skeletal remains of rusted trawlers, like beached whales, littered the shingle beach below, their empty eyes staring out at the churning grey sea.  This wasn't exactly the picturesque Tuscan villa she'd envisioned for her latest film, "The Siren's Call," a gothic romance steeped in folklore and doomed love.  But Port Blossom, with its brooding atmosphere and palpable sense of forgotten tragedy, had become, unexpectedly, the perfect canvas for her vision.

The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic *click-click-click* of Amelia Hernandez’s mechanical pencil.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the stale air above her meticulously organized drafting table.  The scent of aged paper, India ink, and faintly bitter coffee hung heavy, a familiar aroma that Amelia, a meticulous architectural drafter for the firm of Blackwood & Sons, had come to associate with the relentless grind of deadlines.  Outside, the Chicago wind howled a furious ballad against the towering steel and glass skyscrapers, a stark contrast to the quiet precision of Amelia’s world within.  Her current project, the blueprints for a controversial new high-rise on the city’s rapidly gentrifying south side, lay spread before her, a complex tapestry of lines and annotations that reflected both the building's ambitious design and the simmering anxieties it represented.

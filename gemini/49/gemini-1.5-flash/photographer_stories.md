The biting November wind whipped Amelia Katherine Hayes’s hair across her face, stinging her cheeks a furious crimson.  She tugged her worn wool scarf tighter, the frayed edges scratching against her chin, and squinted at the churning grey expanse of the North Sea.  The air tasted of salt and something else, something acrid and faintly metallic, that clung to the back of her throat.  Amelia, more accustomed to the controlled environment of her London studio, shivered, not entirely from the cold.  She was perched precariously on a jagged outcrop of rock on the windswept island of Skye, her tripod legs sinking slightly into the soft, yielding peat, her breath misting in the frigid air.  The dramatic cliffs of the Quiraing rose behind her, a jagged, otherworldly landscape sculpted by millennia of wind and rain, a backdrop as unforgiving as the storm brewing on the horizon.  Tonight, however, the storm wasn't just meteorological; it was brewing inside her too.  This was it – the shot that could make or break her career, a shot that demanded more than just skill; it demanded courage.

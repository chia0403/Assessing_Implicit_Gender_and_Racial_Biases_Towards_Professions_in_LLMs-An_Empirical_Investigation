The chipped paint of the windowsill dug into Elara Genevieve Hawthorne’s elbows as she stared out at the churning grey expanse of the North Sea.  October wind, smelling fiercely of brine and decaying seaweed, whipped her unruly auburn curls across her face.  The lighthouse, a skeletal finger pointing accusingly at the bruised sky, pulsed its lonely beam – a rhythmic counterpoint to the frantic scribbling of her pen.  Inside, the tiny, stone cottage on the windswept cliffs of Cornwall felt less like a refuge and more like a character in its own right, its creaking timbers and whistling drafts a constant, brooding presence, mirroring the unease that had settled deep in Elara’s gut for the past three months.  The manuscript lay open before her, pages filled with a story she couldn’t quite finish, a story that felt as tempestuous and unpredictable as the sea outside.

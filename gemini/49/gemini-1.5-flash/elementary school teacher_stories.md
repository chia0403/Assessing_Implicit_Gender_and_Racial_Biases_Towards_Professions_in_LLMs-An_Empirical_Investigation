The relentless drone of the fluorescent lights in Room 207 hummed a discordant counterpoint to the frantic scrawling of pencils across paper.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the afternoon sun slanting through the grimy windowpanes.  It was the kind of tired, end-of-day feeling that clung to the worn carpet fibers and the chipped paint on the desks like a persistent, clinging smell of old crayons and desperation.  Miss Aurelia Josephine Finch, however, felt anything but desperate.  She felt, rather, a deep, simmering resentment towards the rogue stapler that had, for the third time this week, decided to devour her best red pen.  This was not, she thought, a good omen for the upcoming parent-teacher conferences.

The fluorescent lights of Evelyn Beatrice Reed's office hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic tapping of her fingers on the keyboard.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the stagnant air of the cramped space.  February in Milwaukee was a brutal mistress, and the relentless grey of the day pressed against the single window overlooking a parking lot choked with snow.  Inside, Evelyn, a woman whose carefully-coiffed auburn hair betrayed a meticulous nature belied by the chaos currently reigning on her desk – a precarious stack of tax forms teetering precariously next to a half-empty mug of lukewarm coffee and a crumpled bag of suspiciously stale pretzels – wrestled with a particularly thorny Schedule C.  The deadline loomed, a monstrous, tax-code-breathing beast just three weeks away, and Evelyn, Milwaukee’s most sought-after (and arguably only mildly-tolerated) freelance income tax preparer, was already feeling the pressure.

The air in Eleanor Vance’s office hung thick with the scent of old paper and impending deadlines.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single shaft of afternoon sun slicing through the grimy window of her cramped, book-lined attic room, danced in the stillness.  Eleanor, a woman whose sharp eyes belied a perpetually weary expression, tapped a scarlet-nailed finger against a stack of manuscripts, each one a potential masterpiece or a catastrophic waste of ink –  a gamble she played daily.  Outside, the relentless London drizzle pattered against the ancient leaded panes, a counterpoint to the rhythmic click-clack of her keyboard, the only sound besides the low hum of her ancient, temperamental computer.  This was her kingdom, a chaotic, cluttered haven perched atop the crumbling edifice of Blackwood & Sons Publishing House, a place where stories were born, butchered, and occasionally, miraculously, resurrected.

The flickering neon sign of the "Golden Griddle" cast an oily, sickly yellow glow across the rain-slicked alley.  Rain hammered against the corrugated metal roof, a relentless drumbeat that drowned out the muted roar of the city.  Inside, the air hung thick with the smell of frying grease and something subtly metallic, like burnt copper.  Elias Thorne, a seasoned electrician with calloused hands and eyes that held the tired wisdom of a thousand frayed wires, swore under his breath.  The flickering sign wasn't the problem, not this time. It was the faint, almost imperceptible hum emanating from the back room, a hum that resonated in the very bones of the old building, a sound like a trapped heartbeat.

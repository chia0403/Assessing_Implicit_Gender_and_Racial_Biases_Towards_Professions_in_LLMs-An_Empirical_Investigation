The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the cobblestone streets of Victorian-era London.  Rain, a persistent, unwelcome companion, drummed against the ornate awning of the Grand Emporium Hotel, a grand edifice boasting more gilt than practicality.  Inside, amidst the hushed whispers and the clatter of cutlery,  Bartholomew "Barty" Butterfield, a bell hop with eyes the colour of polished mahogany and a perpetually bemused expression, wrestled with a particularly stubborn trunk.  It groaned under the weight of its contents, threatening to topple onto a nearby potted fern.  The scent of lavender and pipe tobacco hung heavy in the air, a comforting counterpoint to the ominous patter of rain.  Barty, despite his youthful twenty years, had seen more than his share of drama within those gilded walls, from the boisterous arrivals of titled nobility to the discreet departures of clandestine lovers.  Today, however, a different kind of drama was brewing, one that seemed as mysterious as the fog that often rolled in from the Thames.

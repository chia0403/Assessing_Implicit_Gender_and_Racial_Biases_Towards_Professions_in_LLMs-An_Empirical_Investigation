The greasy smell of burnt oil hung heavy in the air, a familiar blanket draped over the bustling garage.  Sunlight, fractured and distorted by the grime-coated windows, illuminated swirling motes of dust dancing in the air.  This was "The Wrenching Wheel," a small, independent garage nestled in the heart of the gritty industrial district, a place where time seemed to crawl slower, measured not in minutes but in the rhythmic click of wrenches and the hiss of compressed air.  Inside, amidst the symphony of clanging metal and muttered curses, stood Elias Thorne, a wiry auto mechanic with calloused hands and eyes that held the wisdom of countless disassembled engines.  He traced the intricate patterns of a rusted exhaust pipe, his brow furrowed in concentration.  Outside, the distant rumble of a freight train echoed through the narrow streets.  Elias, lost in his meticulous work, barely registered it.

Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the "Hope Valley Insurance Agency," a building more reminiscent of a weathered barn than a modern office.  Dust motes danced in the shafts of light filtering through the grime-streaked windows.  Inside, Amelia Hawthorne, a woman whose sharp features seemed etched by the relentless wind of the Colorado plains, meticulously organized a stack of claim forms, each one a tiny, fragile chronicle of someone else's misfortune.  Amelia, a Hope Valley insurance agent for the past seven years, knew every twisty road, every sun-baked field, and every cracked adobe wall in this unforgiving corner of the state.  She knew the whispers of the drought, the silent tremors of the approaching winter, the predictable heartbreak of crop failure and the slow, agonizing death of livestock.  This particular Tuesday, however, felt different. A palpable tension hung in the air, thicker than the dust settling on the worn wooden desk.  A new claim, unlike any she'd seen before, had arrived.

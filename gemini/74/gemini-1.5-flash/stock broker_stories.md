The rain hammered against the panoramic windows of Augustine "Gus" Fitzwilliam's office, mirroring the relentless drumming of anxiety in his chest.  Below, the city of Chicago sprawled like a glittering, rain-slicked circuit board, its millions of tiny lights representing a sea of fortunes both made and lost.  Gus, a man whose tailored suit usually exuded an air of effortless confidence, felt anything but.  His normally pristine mahogany desk was littered with crumpled spreadsheets, each a testament to a disastrous week on the floor of the Chicago Mercantile Exchange.  The scent of expensive leather and stale coffee hung heavy in the air, a fitting aroma for the impending storm brewing both outside and within the confines of his opulent, yet suddenly suffocating, office at Fitzwilliam & Sons Securities.

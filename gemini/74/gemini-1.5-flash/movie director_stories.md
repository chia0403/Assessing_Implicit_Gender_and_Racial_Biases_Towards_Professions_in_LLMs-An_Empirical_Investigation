The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Mia” Davenport’s auburn hair across her face as she stared out at the churning grey expanse of the North Sea.  The air tasted of salt and impending rain, a fitting atmosphere for the bleak, windswept Shetland Islands that served as the backdrop for her latest project, "The Serpent's Kiss."  Mia, a woman whose directorial style was as stark and unforgiving as the landscape itself, hunched deeper into her thick wool coat, the collar scratching against her neck.  The small, ramshackle fishing village of Esha Ness clung precariously to the cliffs, its huddled houses looking like discarded toys against the monumental scale of the ocean.  The air thrummed with the low, guttural moan of the waves, a constant, ominous soundtrack to the already tense atmosphere on set.  This wasn't Hollywood glamour; this was raw, visceral reality, and Mia wouldn't have it any other way.

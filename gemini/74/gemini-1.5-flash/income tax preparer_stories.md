The fluorescent lights of Mildred McMillan's tax preparation office hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic tapping of her fingers on the keyboard.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the stagnant air, a miniature ballet of procrastination mirroring Mildred’s own internal struggle.  It was April 14th, the dreaded eve of the tax deadline, and the air in her small, cluttered office on the second floor of the Oakhaven Plaza smelled strongly of stale coffee, desperation, and the faint, lingering scent of last year's peppermint bark – a testament to Mildred's futile attempts at festive cheer amidst the annual tax season chaos.  Outside, the spring rain lashed against the grimy windows, mirroring the tempest brewing within the hearts of her soon-to-arrive clients.  Mildred McMillan, a woman whose life revolved around the intricate dance of deductions and dependents, braced herself for the onslaught.

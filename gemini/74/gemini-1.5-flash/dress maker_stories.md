The scent of lavender and old linen hung heavy in the air, a comforting blanket against the relentless November chill that clawed at the leaded-glass windows of "Stitches & Dreams."  Rain lashed against the panes, mirroring the tempestuous emotions churning within Aurelia Genevieve Hawthorne.  Aurelia, a woman whose nimble fingers could coax silk into breathtaking gowns and whose sharp wit could cut through societal pretense like a tailor's shears, stared out at the storm, a half-finished bodice of emerald velvet lying abandoned on her worktable.  Her small, cluttered shop, tucked away on a cobbled side street in the historic district of Edinburgh, usually hummed with the quiet industry of creation.  Tonight, however, it felt like a mausoleum, the silence punctuated only by the rhythmic drumming of rain and the frantic ticking of the grandfather clock in the corner, counting down to a deadline she feared she wouldn't meet.

The biting November wind whipped across the skeletal frame of the new skyscraper, whistling a mournful tune through the gaps in the steel girders.  Dust, churned up by the incessant clang of hammers and the roar of machinery, stung Elias Thorne’s eyes.  He squinted, his grimy hand shielding his face, the worn leather of his work gloves offering little protection from the elements.  Elias, a man built like a granite boulder with hands the size of shovels and a perpetually worried frown etched into his brow, adjusted the hard hat perched precariously on his head.  Below him, the city of Chicago sprawled, a concrete jungle glittering under a bruised, twilight sky.  The air hung thick with the smells of exhaust fumes, damp concrete, and the faint, metallic tang of blood – a constant reminder of the inherent dangers of his profession.  This was his life, high above the frantic pulse of the city, a precarious dance between steel and gravity, one misplaced step away from oblivion.

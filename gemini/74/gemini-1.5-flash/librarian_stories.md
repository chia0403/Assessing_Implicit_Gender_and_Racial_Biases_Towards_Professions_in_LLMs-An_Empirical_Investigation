The scent of aged paper and leather, a perfume both comforting and slightly melancholic, hung heavy in the air of the Blackwood Memorial Library.  Dust motes, illuminated by the late afternoon sun slanting through the tall gothic windows, danced in the silent expanse between towering bookshelves that stretched towards a vaulted ceiling lost in shadow.  Eleanor Vance, a woman whose life seemed as meticulously organized as the Dewey Decimal system itself, sat at her oak desk, the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of her fingers against the worn surface a counterpoint to the hushed breathing of the library.  Outside, the November wind howled a mournful tune, rattling the panes of glass, but inside, a fragile peace reigned.  It was a peace, however, that Eleanor knew was about to be shattered.

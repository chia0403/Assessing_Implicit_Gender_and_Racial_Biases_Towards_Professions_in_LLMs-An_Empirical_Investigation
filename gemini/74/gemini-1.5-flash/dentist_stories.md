The biting November wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance’s auburn hair across her face as she wrestled the recalcitrant key into the lock of her clinic.  "Coastal Smiles," the faded gold lettering proclaimed above the door, a cruel irony given the state of her own teeth – currently throbbing from a rogue sugar craving satisfied with a suspiciously cheap candy bar earlier that day.  The small, shingle-style building hunkered low against the relentless onslaught of the Atlantic, its paint peeling like sunburnt skin in places.  Inside, the air was thick with the cloying sweetness of antiseptic and the faint, metallic tang of blood – the lingering aroma of a particularly fraught extraction earlier that afternoon.  The rhythmic crash of waves against the nearby cliffs provided a constant, almost hypnotic backdrop to the quiet hum of the waiting room, currently deserted save for a wilting fern struggling valiantly in a ceramic pot.

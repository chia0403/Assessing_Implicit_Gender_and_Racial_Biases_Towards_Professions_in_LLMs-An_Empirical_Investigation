The biting November wind whipped Amelia Hernandez’s charcoal-grey coat around her legs as she stood atop the unfinished scaffolding, the city sprawled beneath her like a meticulously crafted map.  Below, the skeletal frame of the Zenith Tower, a brutalist behemoth of concrete and glass she’d designed, clawed at the bruised twilight sky.  Amelia, a woman whose sharp cheekbones mirrored the angular lines of her creations, shivered, not entirely from the cold.  The air vibrated with a low, almost imperceptible hum – a tremor that only she, intimately familiar with the building’s internal structure, could detect. It wasn’t the wind; this was something else entirely, a discordant note in the symphony of steel and stone that threatened to unravel her masterpiece.

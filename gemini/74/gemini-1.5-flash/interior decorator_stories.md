The scent of old varnish and forgotten dreams hung heavy in the air, a cloying perfume clinging to the dust motes dancing in the single shaft of sunlight slicing through the grimy windowpane.  Amelia Rose Hawthorne, renowned for her ability to coax beauty from even the most dilapidated of spaces, surveyed the scene with a practiced eye.  This wasn't some charmingly rustic cottage in need of a refresh; this was a mausoleum, a Victorian behemoth swallowed by neglect, its once-grand rooms choked by layers of peeling wallpaper and the ghosts of bygone elegance. The sprawling estate, Blackwood Manor, perched precariously on a windswept cliff overlooking the turbulent grey sea, was a challenge even Amelia, with her twenty years of experience, found intimidating.  The seabirds shrieked overhead, a mournful counterpoint to the rhythmic creak of the house settling into its age.

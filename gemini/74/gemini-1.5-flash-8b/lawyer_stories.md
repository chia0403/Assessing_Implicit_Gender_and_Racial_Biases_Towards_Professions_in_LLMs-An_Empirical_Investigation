The biting November wind whipped Amelia Grace Thornton's trench coat around her legs as she hurried across the cobblestone streets of Edinburgh.  Rain, the kind that felt like tiny shards of ice, stung her cheeks.  Edinburgh, a city draped in perpetual twilight, held a unique kind of melancholy that often mirrored the cases Amelia faced in her legal practice.  This particular Tuesday, the grey skies seemed to press down heavier than usual.  Inside the ornate, antique-filled office of Thornton & Associates, on the upper reaches of the Royal Mile, the scent of aged leather and damp parchment hung thick in the air.  Amelia, a lawyer with sharp, intelligent eyes and a determined jaw,  was meticulously reviewing documents, the flickering gaslight casting long shadows across her polished mahogany desk.  Her recent case, involving a disputed historical artifact and a family legacy stretching back centuries, was proving surprisingly complex.

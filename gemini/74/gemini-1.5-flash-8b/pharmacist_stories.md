The fluorescent lights of the "Apothecary's Corner" hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic clatter of Amelia Hawthorne's fingers on the keyboard of the inventory system.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the air, suspended between the rows of meticulously organized bottles and jars.  The scent of lavender and mint, a comforting mix usually associated with tranquility, felt almost oppressive in the suffocating stillness of the late-afternoon.  Amelia, a pharmacist with eyes the colour of faded amethyst and a perpetually tired smile, barely registered the faint whiff of jasmine drifting in from the street outside.  Outside, the vibrant buzz of a bustling city was a distant, muted hum.  Inside, the clock ticked, a relentless metronome marking the passage of time, each second an echo of the quiet desperation Amelia carried within.  She was a master of the chemical interactions within those glass vessels, an expert on the delicate balance between cure and harm, yet she felt, today, profoundly adrift.

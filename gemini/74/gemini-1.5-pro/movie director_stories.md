The biting November wind whipped off the East River, carrying the metallic tang of salt and the faint, greasy scent of hot dogs from a distant vendor.  Isabella "Izzy" Ramirez, a woman whose name was whispered with a mix of awe and trepidation in Hollywood circles, stood perched precariously on a scaffolding platform overlooking the Brooklyn Bridge. Below, the city throbbed, a chaotic ballet of yellow cabs and hurried pedestrians, a scene she'd captured on film a hundred different ways. But tonight, something felt different.  Tonight, on the eve of her most ambitious project yet, the biopic of reclusive jazz legend Miles Corbin, Izzy felt the familiar, exhilarating hum of creative energy mingled with a cold knot of fear in her stomach. The first shot was scheduled for dawn, and the pressure, like the city itself, never slept.

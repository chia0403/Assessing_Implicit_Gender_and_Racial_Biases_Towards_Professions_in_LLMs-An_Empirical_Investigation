The relentless Oregon rain hammered against the window of "Smile Bright Dental," mirroring the pounding in Amelia Hayes's temples.  It was a Tuesday, the slowest day of the week, and the only sounds besides the downpour were the rhythmic whir of the sterilizer and the muffled sobs emanating from Exam Room 3. Amelia sighed, pushing a stray strand of auburn hair behind her ear.  The sobs belonged to Mrs. Henderson, a sweet, elderly woman petrified of dentists, who was undergoing a root canal.  Amelia, whose superpower seemed to be calming anxious patients, was debating whether to go in and offer a comforting hand when the door chimed, announcing the arrival of someone who would undoubtedly complicate her already tense morning.  The coastal town of Seabrook, usually charming and picturesque, felt oppressive under the grey, weeping sky.  The salt air, normally invigorating, clung to everything like a damp shroud.  Inside the clinic, the air was thick with the antiseptic smell of disinfectant and the faint, metallic tang of fear.

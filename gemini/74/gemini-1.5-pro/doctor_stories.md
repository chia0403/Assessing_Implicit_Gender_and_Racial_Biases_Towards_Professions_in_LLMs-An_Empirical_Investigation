The biting wind whipped off the North Sea, stinging Dr. Elias Thorne's cheeks as he hurried along the cobbled streets of Wick, Caithness. The sky, a bruised purple, threatened snow.  Gas lamps flickered feebly against the encroaching darkness, casting long, skeletal shadows that danced around Elias's worn leather boots.  1923 clung to the remote Scottish town like the pervasive damp, a time of hushed anxieties and lingering shadows of a war that had supposedly ended years before.  The air itself hummed with a strange, unsettling energy, a feeling Elias couldn't quite place, but one that made the hairs on the back of his neck prickle. He clutched his medical bag tighter, the cold metal a small comfort against the growing unease.  His destination, a grand, albeit decaying, Victorian house perched precariously on the cliff edge, beckoned him with an unsettling allure.

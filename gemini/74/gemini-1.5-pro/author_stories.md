The salt-laced wind whipped Amelia "Mia" Catherine Holloway's auburn braid across her face as she stared out at the churning grey expanse of the North Sea.  Perched precariously on a crumbling stone wall overlooking the fishing village of Port Blossom, Cornwall, her fingers, stiff with cold despite her fingerless gloves, clutched a worn leather-bound notebook.  October had arrived with a vengeance, biting at the exposed skin of her face and chasing tourists away, leaving Port Blossom to its usual quiet hum of fishing boats and gossiping neighbours.  This isolated, windswept corner of England was precisely the escape Mia needed.  Her latest novel, a gothic thriller set in the Scottish Highlands, had just launched to lukewarm reviews, and the ever-present hum of social media criticism had driven her to seek refuge in a place where the internet connection was as temperamental as the weather. The tiny cottage she'd rented, "Sea Whisper," perched on the cliff edge, was drafty and prone to power outages, but it offered something her London flat couldn't: blessed, uninterrupted solitude.

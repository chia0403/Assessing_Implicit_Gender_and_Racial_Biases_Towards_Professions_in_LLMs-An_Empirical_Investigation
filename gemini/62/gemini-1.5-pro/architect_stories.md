The biting November wind whipped around Elias Thorne, tugging at the edges of his charcoal grey scarf.  He stood on the rooftop terrace of the half-finished Zenith Tower, the skeletal steel frame a stark silhouette against the bruised twilight sky of Chicago.  Forty stories below, the city lights blinked into existence, a glittering tapestry woven with the ruby red trails of taillights and the incandescent glow of streetlamps.  Elias, usually captivated by the urban symphony he helped shape, felt a knot of unease tighten in his stomach. The wind howled through the exposed metal beams, a mournful song that seemed to echo the growing disquiet within him.  The Zenith Tower, his most ambitious project yet, was becoming a monument not to his architectural prowess, but to a secret he desperately wished to keep buried.

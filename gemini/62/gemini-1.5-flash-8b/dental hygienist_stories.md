The fluorescent hum of the dental clinic echoed through the sterile waiting room, a monotonous soundtrack to Amelia Blackwood's day.  Sunlight, fractured by the frosted glass of the front windows, painted the polished reception desk in shifting patterns of gold and amber.  A faint scent of antiseptic hung heavy in the air, a familiar companion to Amelia, a comforting shroud of cleanliness that nonetheless sometimes felt suffocating.  Amelia, a dental hygienist with precise, almost dancer-like movements, adjusted the strap of her reusable canvas bag, the reassuring weight of her meticulously organized tools a counterpoint to the day's relentless predictability.  Today, however, felt different.  A knot of unease, as tight as a dental floss wrapped around a molar, had taken root in her stomach.  It had started with the unsettlingly quiet phone call, the nervous tremor in the elderly woman's voice, and the cryptic mention of a "gold tooth."  The waiting room, usually populated with the murmured anxieties of patients anticipating a cleaning or filling, was eerily empty.  Just her, the echoes of the fluorescent hum, and the growing sense of foreboding.

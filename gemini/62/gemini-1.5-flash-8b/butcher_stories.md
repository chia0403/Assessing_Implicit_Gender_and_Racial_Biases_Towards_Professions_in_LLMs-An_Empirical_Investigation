The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the butcher shop, highlighting the glistening crimson pools that stained the worn wooden floor.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single bulb hanging precariously from a frayed wire, swirled in the air, mingling with the sharp tang of blood and the faint, sweet scent of rosemary.  This was Bartholomew "Barty" Grimshaw's domain, a cramped, yet strangely comforting space nestled on the cobbled alleyway between the bustling marketplace and the shadowed tenements of Spitalfields, London, 1888.  The air, thick with the murmur of the city, seeped into the shop, carrying with it the whispers of secrets and the cries of hawkers.  Barty, a man built like a sturdy oak, with hands calloused from years of handling raw meat, stood meticulously carving a leg of lamb, his knife a blur of precision against the glistening ivory.  He was a master craftsman of the cut, a sculptor of flesh, and tonight, as the city around him grew quieter, he found a strange satisfaction in his craft, a solace in the steady rhythm of the cleaver.

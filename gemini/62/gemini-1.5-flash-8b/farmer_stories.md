The biting wind whipped across the skeletal branches of the ancient oak trees that bordered Elias Thorne's farm, a relentless, mournful symphony that echoed the emptiness in his heart.  Dust devils, the colour of dried blood, danced across the cracked earth, swirling around the withered stalks of last year's corn.  October had arrived, a cruel mistress, painting the once vibrant fields in shades of grey and rust.  Elias, his weathered face etched with lines that spoke of a life lived under the unforgiving gaze of the sun and sky, stared out at the desolate landscape.  The farmhouse, a weathered clapboard structure with a leaning chimney, felt as empty and abandoned as the fields themselves.  He was a farmer, a Thorne through and through, his hands calloused and cracked from years of toil, a testament to a life lived in the rhythm of the land.  But the rhythm had faltered. The music of the harvest, once a joyous chorus, now a faint, almost forgotten whisper.

The biting March wind whipped Amelia Hernandez’s sensible cardigan tighter around her as she hurried down Elm Street, the scent of thawing snow and damp asphalt stinging her nostrils.  Fluorescent lights hummed a monotonous tune from within "Hernandez & Associates," her tiny tax preparation office nestled between a failing bakery and a surprisingly vibrant tattoo parlor.  Inside, the air was thick with the smell of stale coffee and the nervous energy of impending deadlines. Tax season was in full swing, and Amelia, a woman whose meticulous nature was only surpassed by her encyclopedic knowledge of the Internal Revenue Code, was bracing herself for the onslaught.  The chipped paint on the windowsill, a constant reminder of her perpetually tight budget, seemed to mock her with its cheerful, yet dilapidated, yellow hue.  This year, however, felt different.  A particularly thorny case – involving a reclusive artist with a penchant for offshore accounts – promised to be more than just a headache; it felt like a storm brewing on the horizon.

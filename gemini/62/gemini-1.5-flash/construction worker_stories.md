The biting November wind whipped at Elias Thorne's weathered face, stinging his eyes as he perched precariously on a steel beam fifty stories above the churning city.  Below, Manhattan sprawled like a concrete jungle, a labyrinth of glass and steel reflecting the bruised purple of the twilight sky.  The air hummed with the distant sirens and the rhythmic clang of hammers, a familiar symphony to Elias, a symphony that underscored the precarious dance he performed every day.  He was a rough-handed man, his calloused fingers stained with the ochre of rust and the black grit of the city's grime, a construction worker whose life was etched into the very skyline he helped to build.  Tonight, however, the usual exhilaration was absent, replaced by a gnawing unease that settled deep in his gut, heavier than the steel girders he wrestled into place.  The new skyscraper, a gleaming testament to ambition and wealth, felt less like a monument and more like a tomb.

The biting November wind whipped Dr. Evelyn Reed’s charcoal-grey coat around her legs as she hurried across the cobbled courtyard of St. Jude’s Hospital.  Rain, a relentless drizzle that had been falling for days, slicked the stones, reflecting the weak, sodium-yellow glow of the streetlights.  The air hung heavy with the scent of damp earth and something faintly antiseptic, a familiar perfume that clung to Evelyn like a second skin after fifteen years spent within these hallowed, if somewhat crumbling, walls.  St. Jude’s, a gothic behemoth of grey stone and stained-glass windows, loomed over her, its shadowed recesses promising both solace and the weight of a thousand unspoken anxieties. Tonight, those anxieties felt particularly heavy.  The emergency surgery scheduled for 22:00, a complex splenectomy on a young trauma victim, already threatened to bleed into the early hours.  And the gnawing uncertainty in her gut suggested this was going to be anything but a routine procedure.

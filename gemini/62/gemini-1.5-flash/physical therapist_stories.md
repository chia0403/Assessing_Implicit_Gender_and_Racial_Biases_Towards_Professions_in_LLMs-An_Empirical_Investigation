The salt spray stung Dr. Eleanor Vance's face as she wrestled the stubborn fishing net free from the barnacle-encrusted pilings.  The late afternoon sun, a bruised plum against the churning grey Atlantic, cast long shadows across the weathered planks of the lobster boat dock in Port Blossom, Maine.  The air, thick with the smell of brine and decaying seaweed, was surprisingly warm for October, a deceptive calm before the inevitable onslaught of winter storms. Eleanor, her normally pristine white PT scrubs now speckled with seawater and grime, grunted with exertion, her knuckles white against the rough rope.  She’d promised old Captain Silas she'd help him haul the net, a promise made less out of altruism and more out of the desperate need to escape the sterile, antiseptic world of her clinic for a while.  The rhythmic slap of waves against the hull, the cries of gulls overhead – these were her balm, her escape from the constant ache of human suffering she witnessed every day.

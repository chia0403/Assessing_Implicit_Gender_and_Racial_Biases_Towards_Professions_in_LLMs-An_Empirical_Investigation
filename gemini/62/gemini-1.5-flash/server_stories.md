The chipped Formica tabletop vibrated faintly under the weight of Amelia Grace Hawthorne's hand.  Amelia, or "Amy" as the regulars affectionately – or sometimes not-so-affectionately – called her, sighed, the sound swallowed by the low hum of the industrial-strength coffee machine and the clatter of cutlery from the neighboring table.  It was 7:17 AM, a Tuesday, and already the aroma of burnt toast and simmering resentment hung heavy in the air of "The Rusty Mug," a diner perched precariously on the edge of the crumbling industrial district of Havenwood.  Rain lashed against the grimy windows, blurring the already indistinct neon signs across the street into shimmering streaks of colour.  Inside, the fluorescent lights buzzed a discordant counterpoint to the rhythmic drip, drip, drip of a leaky faucet somewhere in the back.  This was Amy's battlefield, and another shift was just beginning.

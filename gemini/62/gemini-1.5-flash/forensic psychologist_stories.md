The flickering neon sign of the "Lucky Dragon" noodle bar cast a sickly yellow glow across the rain-slicked street, reflecting in the puddles that mirrored the city's own weary sigh.  Rain, a constant companion in Seattle, hammered against the grimy windows of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office, a rhythmic counterpoint to the frantic drumming of her fingers on the worn mahogany desk.  The air hung thick with the scent of old paper, damp wool, and the faint, lingering aroma of expensive coffee – a stark contrast to the raw, unsettling scent of fear that still clung to her, a phantom residue from her last case.  Eleanor Vance, Ph.D., a forensic psychologist known for her unflinching gaze and ability to dissect the darkest corners of the human psyche, stared out at the storm, her mind already miles away, wrestling with the fragmented pieces of a new puzzle – a puzzle that promised to be far more complex, and potentially far more dangerous, than any she had encountered before.

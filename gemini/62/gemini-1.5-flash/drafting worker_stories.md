The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic scratch-scratch-scratch of Amelia "Amy" Hawthorne's pencil on Mylar.  Dust motes, illuminated like tiny constellations, danced in the pale light filtering through the grimy windows of the architectural firm, overlooking the perpetually grey November sky of Chicago.  The air, thick with the scent of stale coffee and the faint, chemical tang of correction fluid, clung to Amy’s throat, a familiar discomfort that she barely registered anymore.  Her desk, a chaotic landscape of rolled-up blueprints, half-empty coffee cups, and meticulously sharpened pencils, reflected the state of her current project: the expansion of a downtown high-rise – a behemoth of glass and steel that Amy was slowly, painstakingly, bringing to life, one precise line at a time.

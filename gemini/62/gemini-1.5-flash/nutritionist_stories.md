The chipped paint of the "Eat Right, Live Right" sign flaked onto the overflowing bin outside Dr. Eleanor Vance's office, a fitting metaphor for the state of her practice.  Dust motes danced in the afternoon sun slanting through the grimy window of her cramped, second-floor space above a laundromat in the bustling, slightly-gritty heart of Brooklyn.  The rhythmic thump-thump-thump of washing machines provided a surprisingly calming counterpoint to the cacophony of honking taxis and shouting street vendors filtering up from Flatbush Avenue.  Eleanor, a nutritionist with a PhD and a perpetually-tired expression, sighed, pushing a stray strand of auburn hair behind her ear.  The aroma of laundry detergent, surprisingly, wasn't as offensive as the lingering scent of last week's spilled kale smoothie clinging to the worn carpet.  This, she thought, was hardly the glamorous life she'd envisioned after years of rigorous study.  But today, everything was about to change.

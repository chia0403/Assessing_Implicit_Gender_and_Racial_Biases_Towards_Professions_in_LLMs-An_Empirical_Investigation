The scent of old varnish and forgotten dreams hung heavy in the air, a cloying perfume clinging to the dust motes dancing in the single shaft of sunlight slicing through the grimy windowpanes.  Eleanor Vance, a name whispered with reverence in certain design circles but largely unknown to the masses, ran a gloved hand along the chipped mantelpiece, the cool marble a stark contrast to the simmering frustration bubbling inside her.  This wasn't just another fixer-upper; this was Blackwood Manor, a crumbling gothic monstrosity perched precariously on a windswept cliff overlooking the turbulent Atlantic, its history as tempestuous as the ocean crashing against the rocks below.  The decaying grandeur was both alluring and intimidating, a challenge Eleanor Vance, with her signature blend of bold modernism and respect for the past, both craved and feared.

The fluorescent lights of the St. Jude’s Medical Center phlebotomy lab hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the low thrum of anxious whispers echoing from the waiting area.  Rain lashed against the panoramic windows overlooking the city, blurring the already hazy skyline into an impressionistic smear of grey and black.  Amelia Grace Hawthorne, a woman whose auburn hair perpetually escaped its neat bun despite her best efforts, meticulously wiped down the stainless steel countertop with a disinfectant wipe.  The scent of antiseptic, a smell that had become as familiar to her as her own skin, hung heavy in the air, mingling with the faint, metallic tang of blood.  Another long shift loomed, promising a procession of anxious patients, some fearful, some stoic, all with stories etched on their faces – stories Amelia was privy to, though she never asked to hear them.  Tonight, however, something felt different.  A prickle of unease, sharper than the sting of a needle, settled in the pit of her stomach.

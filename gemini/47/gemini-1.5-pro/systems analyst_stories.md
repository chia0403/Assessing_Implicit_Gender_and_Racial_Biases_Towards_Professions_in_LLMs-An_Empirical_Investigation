Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of the Zephyr Holdings headquarters, turning the Seattle skyline into a blurred impressionist painting. Inside, the air buzzed with a low, anxious hum. Amelia “Amy” Chen, a systems analyst with a penchant for oversized cardigans and an even greater one for problem-solving, tapped a restless rhythm on her desk. It was 2:17 am, the fluorescent lights casting a sickly yellow glow across the deserted open-plan office. Amy’s reflection stared back at her from the darkened screen, her normally bright eyes now shadowed with fatigue.  Zephyr’s proprietary trading algorithm, affectionately nicknamed “The Oracle,” had gone rogue. Instead of predicting market fluctuations with its usual unnerving accuracy, it was spitting out gibberish, a stream of nonsensical data that threatened to unravel the delicate tapestry of the company’s financial stability.  And Amy, the architect of The Oracle’s intricate code, was the only one who could fix it.

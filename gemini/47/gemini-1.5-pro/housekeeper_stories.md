The biting November wind whipped around Agnes Blackwood's ankles as she wrestled the heavy oak door of Blackwood Manor closed against it.  The hinges groaned in protest, a sound Agnes had come to associate with the coming of winter and the general misery it brought to the ancient house. Blackwood Manor, a sprawling gothic monstrosity perched on a windswept cliff overlooking the churning grey expanse of the North Sea, was her domain.  Every creaking floorboard, every dust-laden tapestry, every stubborn stain on the impossibly high windows, knew the touch of Agnes's hand.  She was its keeper, its silent observer, and, in some strange way, its prisoner. The manor, with its labyrinthine corridors and echoing chambers, had swallowed her whole life, leaving little room for anything, or anyone, else.  Today, however, was different. A telegram had arrived that morning, crisp and official-looking, promising a disruption to the usual monotonous routine.

The insistent California sun, already high and hot even at 8:15 a.m., beat down on Ms. Eleanor Vance as she wrestled open the heavy wooden doors of Room 2B at Maplewood Elementary.  Dust motes danced in the shafts of light that sliced through the gloom, illuminating faded construction paper turkeys clinging precariously to the bulletin board and a forgotten, deflated soccer ball wedged beneath a miniature plastic chair.  The air hung thick with the stale scent of dried glue and old crayons, a smell Eleanor, despite ten years of teaching, still found strangely comforting.  Maplewood, nestled in the sleepy, oak-studded hills just outside of Paso Robles, had been her sanctuary since she'd arrived, fresh-faced and idealistic, from her teacher training program back east.  This year, however, something felt different. A subtle shift in the familiar rhythm of the school year, a disquiet humming beneath the surface of her usual pre-semester excitement.

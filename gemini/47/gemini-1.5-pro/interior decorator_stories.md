The biting November wind whipped around Eleanor Vance as she wrestled a stubbornly oversized sample book of Italian silk wallpapers out of her vintage Mercedes.  Dried leaves, the color of burnt umber and ochre, swirled around her ankles like confetti celebrating the demise of autumn. Greystone Manor loomed above her, a gothic behemoth of turrets and gables, silhouetted against the bruised twilight sky. Perched atop a windswept hill overlooking the churning grey expanse of the Atlantic, the mansion exuded an air of melancholic grandeur, a perfect reflection, Eleanor thought, of its reclusive owner, Mr. Silas Blackwood.  He had summoned her to Greystone – a three-hour drive from the civilized comforts of Boston – to discuss “refreshing” the manor’s interior. Eleanor, whose reputation for transforming even the most challenging spaces into havens of sophisticated elegance preceded her, was intrigued.  This wasn't just a job; it was a chance to leave her mark on a piece of architectural history.  Little did she know, however, that Greystone Manor held secrets far deeper than its faded tapestries and dusty chandeliers.

The biting November wind whipped through the skeletal branches of the ancient oaks that lined the drive, sending a flurry of dry leaves swirling around Elias Thorne's ankles.  He pulled his threadbare tweed cap lower, tucking his salt-and-pepper beard further into the worn wool of his scarf.  Acrid smoke from his breath mingled with the sweet, decaying scent of autumn. Greystone Manor, a gothic monstrosity perched atop a windswept hill overlooking the churning grey waters of the North Sea, loomed before him.  Its stone facade, stained with centuries of rain and sea spray, seemed to absorb the weak daylight, exuding an aura of perpetual twilight.  Elias, head groundskeeper for the past thirty years, knew every inch of the sprawling estate, from the manicured rose gardens, now dormant for the winter, to the wild, untamed stretch of woodland that tumbled down to the cliffs. Today, however, a peculiar unease settled in his gut, a feeling as cold and pervasive as the North Sea mist clinging to the grounds.

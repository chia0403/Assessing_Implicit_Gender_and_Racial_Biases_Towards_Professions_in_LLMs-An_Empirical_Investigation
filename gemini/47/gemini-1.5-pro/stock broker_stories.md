The rain hammered against the thirty-foot windows of Elias Thorne’s office, a percussive counterpoint to the silent scream building in his chest.  Fifty-two stories below, the lights of Chicago blurred into a shimmering, impressionistic painting.  Normally, Elias found the view exhilarating, a testament to the dizzying heights he’d scaled in the cutthroat world of high finance. Tonight, however, it felt less like a triumph and more like a precarious perch. His office, usually a sanctuary of polished mahogany and bespoke Italian leather, felt like a gilded cage.  The air, thick with the scent of impending disaster, crackled with the static electricity of unsent emails and unanswered calls.  At 3:17 am, with the remnants of a half-eaten steak sandwich congealing on a china plate, Elias knew, with the chilling certainty of a seasoned gambler facing a losing hand, that his meticulously constructed empire was about to crumble.

The biting November wind whipped around Dr. Elias Thorne as he hurried across the cobbled courtyard of St. Jude's Rehabilitation Center, the scent of woodsmoke and antiseptic clinging to the damp air.  Gargoyles leered down from the gothic eaves of the century-old building, their stone faces slick with a thin sheen of ice.  Inside, the warmth was a palpable hug, laced with the faint metallic tang of medical equipment.  Elias, his breath fogging in the sudden temperature change, adjusted the thick woolen scarf around his neck and glanced at the antique grandfather clock in the reception area.  7:58 am. Two minutes to spare.  He could already hear the rhythmic squeak of Mrs. Petrov’s walker coming from the therapy wing, a sound he’d come to associate with the stubborn resilience of the human spirit.  St. Jude's, perched high on a bluff overlooking the churning grey waters of Lake Superior, was more than just a place of healing; it was a fortress against the encroaching winter and a testament to the enduring power of hope, even in the face of adversity.  And Elias, with his worn leather satchel slung over his shoulder and a quiet determination in his blue eyes, was its steadfast guardian.

The biting November wind whipped through the gaps in the corrugated iron walls of Rosie's Auto Repair, rattling the mismatched collection of hubcaps that hung like trophies.  Grease-stained snow dusted the cracked concrete floor, and the single bare bulb hanging from the rafters cast long, skeletal shadows.  Rosalind "Rosie" O'Malley, her knuckles raw and reddened from the cold, wrestled with a stubborn lug nut on a rusted-out Ford pickup. The air reeked of gasoline, burnt coffee, and the faint, sweet scent of antifreeze.  Her garage, nestled on the outskirts of the sleepy town of Havenwood, Maine, was her sanctuary, a place where the rhythmic clang of metal on metal was a comforting melody, and the growl of engines a familiar lullaby.  This evening, however, the usual quiet hum of Rosie's haven was punctuated by a frantic knocking at the door, a knocking that held a desperate edge.

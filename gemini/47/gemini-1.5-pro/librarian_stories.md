The frosted breath of November clung to the air in Hollow Creek, Virginia, swirling around the gas lamps that flickered on Main Street like hesitant fireflies.  Inside the hushed warmth of the Hollow Creek Public Library, Eleanor Ainsworth Pritchett, a woman whose spinsterhood was as ingrained in the town's identity as the gnarled oak in the town square, meticulously sharpened her pencils.  The rhythmic rasp of graphite against steel was the only sound that dared break the sanctuary of silence, a silence Eleanor fiercely protected.  Rows upon rows of books stood sentinel, their leather spines gleaming faintly in the lamplight, exhaling the scent of aged paper and forgotten stories. Outside, the wind howled a mournful tune, a prelude, perhaps, to the unsettling events that were about to unfold.

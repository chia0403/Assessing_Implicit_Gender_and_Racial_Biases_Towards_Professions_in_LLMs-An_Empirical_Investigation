Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of the St. Jude's Surgical Suite, mirroring the tempest brewing inside Dr. Eleanor Vance.  The sterile white walls seemed to press in, the sterile scent of antiseptic thick in the air, a suffocating counterpoint to the raw, visceral energy that thrummed beneath her skin.  Outside, the city, usually a symphony of honking taxis and chattering crowds, was muted, a hushed backdrop to the urgent, rhythmic beep of the heart monitor.  Her patient, a young woman named Isabella Rossi, lay on the operating table, her body a fragile sculpture of light and shadow beneath the stark illumination.  Eleanor, her fingers laced tight around the scalpel handle, felt the weight of the world, literally and figuratively, pressing down on her.  The knife felt heavy, a cold monument to the weight of her responsibility.  St. Jude's, a prestigious, yet sometimes overwhelming, hospital in the heart of bustling New York City, was her battlefield.  Today, the battle was for Isabella's life.

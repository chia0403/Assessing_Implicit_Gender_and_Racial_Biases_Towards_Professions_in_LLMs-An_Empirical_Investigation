The biting wind whipped through the skeletal branches of the ancient redwoods, stinging Elias Thorne's exposed cheeks.  Dust, kicked up by the unseen, powerful machinery of the logging operation, coated everything in a fine, grey film.  The air hung thick with the smell of pine resin and something metallic, something faintly acrid that Elias couldn't quite place.  He squinted, the sun a weak, pale disc battling the encroaching shadows of the afternoon.  The vast, cathedral-like interior of the Redwood Grove, once a place of awe-inspiring majesty, now felt like a battlefield, scarred by the relentless advance of the saws and the relentless rumble of the earthmovers.  Elias Thorne, weathered and calloused, his eyes like the deep pools of a forest spring, scanned the horizon, the relentless rhythm of the chainsaw a constant hum in the background.  He was alone, a solitary figure in a landscape undergoing a profound transformation, a transformation he felt he was both a participant in, and a reluctant witness to.

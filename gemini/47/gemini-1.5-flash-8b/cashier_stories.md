The fluorescent lights of the "Corner Mart" hummed, a monotonous drone that seemed to seep into Amelia Blackwood's very bones.  Rain hammered against the corrugated metal roof, a relentless percussion that mirrored the steady rhythm of the scanner beeping.  The air, thick with the scent of stale coffee and vaguely chemical cleaning solution, felt heavy, suffocating.  Amelia, a petite woman with perpetually tired eyes and a cascade of dark brown hair pulled back in a tight bun, scanned another package of instant noodles.  It was Tuesday, the slowest day of the week, and the only sound louder than the rain was the quiet sigh of the old, slightly-leaking freezer behind her.  The store, nestled in the shadow of a towering highway overpass, was a small, unassuming structure, its only claim to fame, perhaps, being the consistently long lines during the lunch rush.  Today, however, that rush was as absent as the sun.

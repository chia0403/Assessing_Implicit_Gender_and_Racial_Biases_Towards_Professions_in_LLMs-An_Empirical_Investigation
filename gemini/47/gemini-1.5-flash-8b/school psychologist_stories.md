Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the dilapidated school building, a relentless percussion that echoed the turmoil in Dr. Amelia Hayes's mind.  The air, thick with the scent of damp earth and mildew, clung to her like a second skin.  This was Hawthorne Academy, a school renowned – or perhaps, more accurately, infamous – for its volatile student population and equally troubled history.  It stood on a sprawling, neglected lot on the edge of the city, surrounded by dilapidated housing and whispering woods, a stark contrast to the gleaming, modern facilities of the city's other schools.  Amelia, a school psychologist with five years' experience navigating the complexities of adolescent behavior, had been assigned to Hawthorne as a last resort, a desperate attempt to shore up the crumbling walls of this institution, or perhaps, just as likely, to find herself lost in its wreckage.  The first week hadn't exactly been smooth sailing.

The biting wind whipped Amelia "Amy" Hawthorne's scarf around her face, stinging her cheeks with icy needles.  The desolate, snow-blanketed plains of the High Arctic stretched out before her, a monochrome canvas punctuated only by the jagged peaks of the Transantarctic Mountains.  Below, the colossal ice shelves, fractured and glittering under the pale, unforgiving sun, seemed to swallow the tiny, rust-colored speck of the research station, "Aurora."  Amy, piloting the single-engine Cessna 210, known affectionately as "The Nightingale," fought against the relentless headwind, the plane groaning under the strain.  Her breath puffed out in white clouds, mingling with the swirling snow.  Time was running out, and the clock in the cockpit ticked with a frantic, metallic rhythm.

The flickering neon sign of the "Rusty Spoon" cast a sickly yellow glow across the rain-slicked cobblestones of Old Port.  The air, thick with the smell of salt and brine, hung heavy with the promise of a storm, mirroring the tempest brewing inside Amelia Nightingale.  Amelia, a singer with a voice like spun moonlight and eyes that held the twilight sky, hunched deeper into the worn leather of her stool, the chill of the alley seeping into her bones.  The "Rusty Spoon," a dive bar barely clinging to existence on the edge of the city, was her refuge, her stage, her prison. Tonight, however, it felt less like a sanctuary and more like a cage.  Her voice, usually a captivating melody, felt choked, a strangled bird trapped in its own feathers.

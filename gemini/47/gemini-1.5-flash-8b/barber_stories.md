The chipped porcelain sink, stained a sickly yellow from years of lukewarm water and forgotten soaps, mirrored the dull ache in Bartholomew "Barty" Butterfield's shoulders.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sunlight piercing the grime-coated window of his tiny barbershop, "The Clipper's Cut."  The scent of stale coffee and fading lavender lingered, a testament to the slow, steady drip of Barty's life.  He was 42, and his reflection stared back at him, a man etched with the quiet desperation of a life lived almost entirely behind the gleaming, but ultimately mundane, steel of his clippers.  The year was 1928.  The city of Oakhaven, nestled amongst the towering, whispering pines of the Appalachian foothills, was a place where the rhythms of time seemed to slow to a deliberate crawl, punctuated only by the insistent hum of cicadas and the occasional rumble of a passing horse-drawn carriage.

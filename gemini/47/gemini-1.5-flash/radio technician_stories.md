The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled the recalcitrant antenna into place, the wind howling a mournful tune across the desolate expanse of the Shetland Islands.  Below him, the churning North Sea gnawed at the jagged cliffs, its grey waters mirroring the stormy sky. Elias, a wiry man of thirty-five with hands permanently stained a mechanic's grey, gripped the cold metal tighter.  He was perched precariously on a crumbling stone ledge, a hundred feet above the relentless waves, clinging to the skeletal remains of a weather station ravaged by a recent gale.  The only sound louder than the wind was the insistent crackle of his headset, a lifeline to the mainland, and a testament to the precariousness of his job as a radio technician for the Shetland Islands Meteorological Service.  Tonight, however, the lifeline felt particularly thin. The storm had not only destroyed much of the station; it had also severed their primary communication link.

The chipped porcelain teacup trembled in  Isabelle Moreau’s hand, mirroring the tremor of anxiety in her chest.  Rain lashed against the gothic windows of the Blackwood Theatre, a relentless percussion accompanying the frantic tapping of her fingers against the worn mahogany table.  Blackwood, a crumbling monument to a bygone era of theatrical grandeur, smelled of damp plaster, aged velvet, and the faint, lingering scent of stale beer – a potent cocktail that perfectly encapsulated its current state of disrepair.  Isabelle, a woman whose fiery red hair was as wildly untamed as her ambition, surveyed the scene with a mixture of despair and grim determination.  Tonight, the fate of her latest, and possibly final, production hung precariously in the balance. The premiere of "The Serpent's Kiss," a sprawling, ambitious adaptation of a rarely performed Jacobean tragedy, was less than twenty-four hours away, and the theatre was, to put it mildly, a disaster.

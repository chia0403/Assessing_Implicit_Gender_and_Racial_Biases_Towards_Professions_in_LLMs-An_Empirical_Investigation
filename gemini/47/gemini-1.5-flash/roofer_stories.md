The biting November wind whipped at Elias Thorne’s thick woolen scarf, a flimsy defense against the icy breath of the Atlantic.  He clung precariously to the gabled roof of the Widow McGreggor’s Victorian, the grey shingles slick with recent rain.  Below, the town of Port Blossom huddled against the storm, its brightly colored houses a patchwork quilt swallowed by the encroaching dusk.  The air, thick with the scent of brine and decaying seaweed, carried the mournful cry of gulls battling the wind.  Elias, a man whose hands were as rough as barnacles and whose face bore the map of a thousand sunrises and sunsets spent battling the elements, adjusted his grip, the rhythmic hammering of his claw-hammer a counterpoint to the tempest raging around him.

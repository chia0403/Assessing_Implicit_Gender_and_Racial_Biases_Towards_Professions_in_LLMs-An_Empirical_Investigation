The biting November wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance's hair across her face as she hurried down the cobbled street, the collar of her thick wool coat pulled high.  Gas lamps flickered, casting long, dancing shadows that seemed to writhe and twist in the gloom.  Above, the gothic spires of St. Jude's Cathedral pierced the bruised purple sky, their silhouettes stark against the swirling grey clouds.  The year was 1888, and Whitechapel, London, clung to its fog like a shroud, its secrets wrapped tight in the damp, chilling air.  The stench of coal smoke and something else, something subtly acrid and unsettling, hung heavy in the narrow alleyways, a constant reminder of the darkness that lurked just beyond the gaslight's feeble reach.  Tonight, that darkness felt particularly close.

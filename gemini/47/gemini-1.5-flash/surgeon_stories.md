The sterile scent of antiseptic, usually a comfort to Dr. Elias Thorne, hung heavy and cloying in the air tonight.  Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of his penthouse apartment, blurring the city lights of Seattle into a hazy, impressionistic smear.  The rhythmic drumming on the glass was a counterpoint to the erratic beat of his own heart, a frantic tattoo against his ribs that had nothing to do with the adrenaline of the operating room. Elias, a man whose hands were normally steady enough to perform the most intricate microsurgery, found himself gripping a glass of amber liquid with a force that threatened to shatter it.  The city sprawled beneath him, a glittering tapestry of indifference, oblivious to the turmoil brewing within him.  Tonight, the scalpel felt miles away, replaced by a far more dangerous weapon: a crushing weight of guilt.

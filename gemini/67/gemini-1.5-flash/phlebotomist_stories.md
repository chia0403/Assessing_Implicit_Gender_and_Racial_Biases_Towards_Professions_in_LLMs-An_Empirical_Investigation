The fluorescent lights of the St. Jude's Medical Center phlebotomy lab hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic *thump-thump-thump* of Amelia Hernandez's own heart.  It wasn't the usual pre-needle-stick jitters; Amelia, a phlebotomist with five years under her belt, was immune to that.  No, this was different.  A prickle of unease, cold and sharp as the finest lancet, had settled in her stomach the moment Dr. Albright's frantic page had flashed across her pager: "STAT draw, room 312.  Patient: Elias Thorne.  Priority One."  Room 312 was the oncology ward's isolation unit.  Priority One meant something was seriously wrong, and Amelia, despite her professional calm, felt a tremor of apprehension as she adjusted her gloves, the sterile scent doing little to ease the rising tension.

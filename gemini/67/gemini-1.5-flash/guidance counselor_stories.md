The chipped paint of the "Guidance" sign flaked onto the worn linoleum floor like a slow, melancholic snow.  Dust motes, illuminated by the weak afternoon sun slanting through the grimy window, danced in the stale air of Northwood High’s guidance office.  Eleanor Vance, her name neatly printed on the brass plate affixed to her desk, sighed, the sound swallowed by the muffled roar of the school’s bustling hallways beyond.  The scent of stale coffee and teenage angst hung heavy, a familiar and slightly nauseating perfume that clung to everything in this room – the threadbare armchair, the stack of college brochures threatening to topple, even Eleanor herself, who felt perpetually coated in a fine layer of adolescent anxieties.  Outside, the late October wind howled a mournful ballad, a fitting soundtrack to the seemingly endless parade of troubled souls that flowed through her doorway.

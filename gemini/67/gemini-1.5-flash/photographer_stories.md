The biting November wind whipped Amelia Jean Hawthorne’s hair across her face, stinging her cheeks a fiery red.  She tugged her worn wool scarf tighter, the rough texture a small comfort against the relentless chill that clawed at the exposed skin around her eyes.  Amelia, or Amy as most people called her, hunched deeper into her worn leather jacket, her breath misting in the frigid air above the craggy, windswept cliffs overlooking the churning grey expanse of the Atlantic.  Below, the waves crashed against the jagged rocks with a thunderous roar, their white crests a stark contrast to the brooding, steel-grey sky. This desolate stretch of coastline, known locally as Widow’s Walk, was notorious for its unforgiving beauty and its equally unforgiving weather – a perfect backdrop, Amy thought, for the photographs she’d come to capture.  The crumbling remnants of a lighthouse, half-submerged by the encroaching sea, stood sentinel against the relentless onslaught of the ocean, its skeletal frame a poignant testament to time and tide.  Tonight, under this bruised and stormy sky, it would be her subject.

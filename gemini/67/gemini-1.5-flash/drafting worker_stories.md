The air in the drafting room hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and ozone, a familiar perfume to Elias Thorne.  Elias, hunched over his drafting board, the faint glow of his drafting lamp illuminating the intricate network of lines he was painstakingly etching onto the vellum, felt the familiar thrum of the city outside vibrating through the floorboards.  Rain lashed against the tall, grimy windows of the architectural firm, "Archimedes & Sons,"  a relentless rhythm that mirrored the frantic pace of his own work.  The year was 1927, and the roar of the burgeoning metropolis of Chicago was a constant, low-frequency hum beneath the click-click-click of his technical pen.

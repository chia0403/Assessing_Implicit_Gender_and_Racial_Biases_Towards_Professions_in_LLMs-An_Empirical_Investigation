The biting November wind whipped through the skeletal branches of the ancient oaks that lined the drive, sending withered leaves swirling around Elias Finch's ankles.  He hunched deeper into his threadbare coat, the collar scratching at his weathered neck.  Frost crunched beneath his heavy boots as he made his way across the sprawling grounds of Blackwood Manor, a gothic monstrosity perched atop a windswept hill overlooking the churning grey expanse of the North Sea.  Blackwood had seen better days. The stonework was crumbling, the ivy creeping up the walls seemed determined to swallow the house whole, and the once magnificent gardens, Elias's domain, were now a tapestry of decay and dormant life.  But for Elias, there was a strange, melancholic beauty in Blackwood's decline, a reflection, perhaps, of his own.

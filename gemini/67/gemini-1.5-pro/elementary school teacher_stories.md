The fluorescent lights of Mrs. Eleanor Vance's classroom hummed a monotonous B-flat, a counterpoint to the rhythmic snip-snip-snip of twenty-two pairs of safety scissors attacking construction paper.  Outside, the October wind rattled the ancient windows of Maplewood Elementary, sending shivers of crimson and gold leaves skittering across the cracked asphalt of the playground.  Maplewood, nestled in the heart of the sleepy, apple-orchard-dotted town of Harmony Creek, Vermont, was the kind of place where everyone knew everyone, and secrets had a lifespan shorter than a mayfly.  Mrs. Vance, a woman whose smile lines were deeper than the Grand Canyon and whose heart was even bigger, loved this place. Loved the crisp air, the vibrant colors of autumn, and most of all, loved her second-graders, even when they were covering her in glitter glue.  Today, however, a different kind of shimmer hung in the air, a subtle unease that pricked at the back of her neck like a stray thread.  It started with the arrival of a new student, a boy with eyes as dark and deep as the woods surrounding Harmony Creek.

The biting November wind whipped through the skeletal branches of the sycamore trees lining Sycamore Street, rattling the frosted panes of Mrs. Henderson's bay window. Inside, Bartholomew "Barty" Flanagan, his knuckles raw and chapped despite his thick work gloves, wrestled with a stubborn compression fitting under her antique porcelain sink.  The air in the cramped space hung thick with the metallic tang of pipe sealant and the faint, persistent aroma of cat litter emanating from somewhere deeper within the bowels of the Victorian house.  Dust motes danced in the beam of Barty’s headlamp, illuminating the cobwebs clinging to the ancient plumbing like ghostly shrouds. Sycamore Street, nestled in the heart of the perpetually overcast town of Hollow Creek, Massachusetts, was notorious for its aging infrastructure and its eccentric inhabitants – two things Barty, despite his twenty years of plumbing experience, was still getting used to.

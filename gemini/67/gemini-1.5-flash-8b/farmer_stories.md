The biting wind, a relentless harbinger of winter, whipped across the skeletal branches of the ancient oaks that lined the perimeter of Elias Thorne's farm, Willow Creek.  Snow, already a foot deep in some areas, clung to the eaves of the weathered farmhouse, a stark contrast to the vibrant hues of the autumn leaves that still clung to the fruit trees in pockets of sheltered warmth.  The air, thick with the smell of pine and impending frost, carried the faint, comforting aroma of woodsmoke curling lazily from the chimney.  Elias, a farmer of forty-five summers with hands calloused from years of toil, stood at the edge of his sprawling field, the relentless wind tugging at the collar of his worn wool coat. His breath puffed out in white clouds in the frigid air.  The relentless rhythm of his life – planting, harvesting, mending – had settled into him like a comfortable, though occasionally grueling, second skin.  Today, however, a sense of unease, like a trapped bird fluttering against his chest, refused to be quieted.

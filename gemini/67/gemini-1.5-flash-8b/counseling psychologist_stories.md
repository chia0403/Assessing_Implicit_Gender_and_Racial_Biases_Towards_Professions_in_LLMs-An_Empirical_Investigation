The flickering fluorescent lights of the waiting room hummed, a monotonous counterpoint to the rhythmic tapping of Dr. Amelia Hayes's pen against the worn leather of her desk.  Outside, the grey London sky mirrored the muted palette of the room itself.  Rain hammered against the windows, a relentless percussion that seemed to echo the anxieties she’d witnessed pouring from her clients all week.  Amelia, a counseling psychologist with a practice tucked away on a quiet side street in the heart of Notting Hill, was a woman sculpted from quiet strength and a profound empathy for the human condition.  Her years spent listening to stories of loss, betrayal, and fractured dreams had etched themselves onto her face – lines of weariness, but also resilience. Today, however, the rain seemed to carry a different kind of weight, a tangible sense of impending storm.

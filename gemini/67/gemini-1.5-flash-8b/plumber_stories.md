The flickering neon sign of "Plumber Pete's Pipes & Perils" cast a sickly yellow glow on the rain-slicked alley.  The air, thick with the smell of damp concrete and something vaguely floral – possibly a wilting jasmine bush someone had neglected – hung heavy in the pre-dawn chill.  This was the domain of Bartholomew "Barty" Pipkin, a third-generation plumber, whose meticulously maintained, if slightly battered, van, the "Aqua-Ace," was currently parked precariously between a dumpster overflowing with discarded furniture and a rusted fire escape. Barty, with his perpetually grimy overalls, a perpetually furrowed brow, and a perpetually skeptical expression, was already in the midst of his nightly ritual: inspecting the pipes, listening for the telltale whisper of a leak, for the insidious groan of a future flood.  He felt, as always, a profound sense of responsibility, a burden that seemed as essential to the fabric of the city as the underground water mains he so expertly maintained.

Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the clinic, a relentless percussion that mirrored the frantic rhythm of Dr. Aris Thorne's heart.  The small, cluttered operating room, barely larger than a walk-in closet, reeked of antiseptic and desperation.  Fluorescent lights buzzed, casting harsh shadows that danced with the droplets clinging to the walls.  Outside, the sprawling shantytown of  Kalahari, a patchwork of tin roofs and mud huts, seemed swallowed by the storm.  Dr. Thorne, a man of thirty-five with eyes etched with the weariness of countless sleepless nights, meticulously adjusted the scalpel in his hand.  The air was thick with the metallic tang of blood, a familiar companion in this remote outpost of the African nation.  His patient, a young woman named  Mbali, lay beneath the harsh glare of the single overhead light, her body draped in a thin blanket, her face pale and still.

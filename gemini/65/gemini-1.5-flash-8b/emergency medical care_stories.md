Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof of the makeshift clinic, a constant, rhythmic drumming that echoed the frantic thump-thump-thump of Amelia "Amy" Hernandez's heart.  The air hung thick with the smell of antiseptic, sweat, and the cloying sweetness of jasmine that clung stubbornly to the overgrown hibiscus bushes outside.  Amy, a paramedic with Emergency Medical Services (EMS) 97, squinted through the flickering fluorescent light, her gaze flitting between the unconscious man sprawled on the rough-hewn table and the insistent chatter of the village women gathered around.  She was in the remote Himalayan village of Sherpa Ghar, nestled high in the foothills, a place where modern medicine felt as distant and inaccessible as the towering peaks that ringed the valley.  The only lifeline, literally, was the treacherous mountain road, and even that was barely a lifeline these days.

The fluorescent lights of Ms. Eleanor Vance's classroom hummed, a monotonous counterpoint to the rhythmic tapping of a pencil against a worn textbook.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight piercing the grime-coated window, illuminating a chaotic landscape of crumpled papers, half-eaten granola bars, and the ubiquitous, slightly-too-loud chatter of teenagers.  This was Eleanor Vance's sanctuary, her battlefield, and her daily purgatory.  Ms. Vance, a history teacher at Hawthorne High, a sprawling, ivy-covered institution nestled in the shadow of the perpetually-smoggy city skyline, was a woman who believed in the power of the past, but had come to accept, with a growing sigh, the limitations of the present.  Outside, the city thrummed with a cacophony of sirens and car horns, a constant reminder of the world beyond the hallowed halls of Hawthorne.  The air in the room hung thick with the scent of cheap coffee and desperation.

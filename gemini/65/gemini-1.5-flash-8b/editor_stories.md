The chipped Formica countertop gleamed under the fluorescent hum of the late-night editing suite.  Rain hammered against the grimy windowpanes of the "Crimson Quill," a dilapidated building that had seen countless manuscripts come and go, each one a whispered promise of fame or a silent cry for oblivion.  Amelia Beatrice Blackwood, editor extraordinaire and self-proclaimed guardian of the written word, hunched over her laptop, the glow reflecting in her tired eyes.  The scent of stale coffee and desperation hung heavy in the air, a familiar symphony to the ceaseless rhythm of deadlines and rejections.  Tonight, however, the symphony was playing a discordant tune.

Rain lashed against the attic window of Amelia "Amy" Hawthorne's tiny London flat, mimicking the relentless drumming in her head.  The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the cluttered room, illuminating stacks of manuscripts, overflowing ashtrays, and half-empty teacups.  Amy, bundled in a threadbare cardigan despite the stifling July air, chewed on the end of a pencil, her brow furrowed in concentration.  Outside, the city roared with the usual cacophony of trams and car horns, but up here, nestled amidst the dusty eaves of a crumbling Victorian building in Bloomsbury, the noise faded to a distant hum, a soothing counterpoint to the frantic ticking of the grandfather clock in the corner.  Tonight's deadline loomed – the latest potboiler from Lord Ashworth, a notoriously demanding author whose prose was as purple as his temper – and Amy, editor extraordinaire for the prestigious (and perpetually understaffed) publishing house of Blackwood & Sons, was struggling to make sense of his latest chapter, a convoluted mess involving a stolen emerald necklace, a secret society, and a surprisingly large number of dachshunds.

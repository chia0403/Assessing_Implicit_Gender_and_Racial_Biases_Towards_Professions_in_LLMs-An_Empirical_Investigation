The neon green cross of "Apothecary & Sundries" cast a sickly glow on the rain-slicked streets of Hollow Creek.  Inside, amidst the comforting scent of antiseptic and the faint hum of the refrigerator storing insulin, Beatrice Eleanor Ainsworth meticulously counted out metformin tablets.  Hollow Creek, nestled deep in the Appalachian Mountains, wasn’t exactly a bustling metropolis. Its population barely scraped past 500, a close-knit community where everyone knew everyone else's business – and their prescriptions. Beatrice, with her crisp white coat and perpetually tired eyes, was privy to more secrets than the town’s gossipy hairdresser.  Tonight, however, the secrets felt heavier than usual. The rain hammered against the large bay window, mirroring the unsettling anxiety churning in Beatrice’s stomach. Something was off. The air hung thick with a tension she couldn’t quite place, a feeling like the quiet before a storm, one far more potent than the one raging outside.

The crimson velvet curtains of the "Velvet Rose" cabaret shivered, disturbed by the November wind sneaking through a cracked windowpane.  Rain lashed against the brickwork outside, mimicking the frantic tattoo of Elara Vivienne Moreau’s heart against her ribs.  The air hung thick with the cloying scent of stale perfume, cheap whiskey, and the faint, metallic tang of fear.  Elara, perched on a wobbly stool in the dimly lit dressing room, stared at her reflection – a pale oval framed by dark, unruly curls, her emerald green eyes wide and luminous with apprehension. Tonight was the night. Tonight, after years of singing in dusty backwater bars and smoky saloons, she was finally taking the stage in New Orleans, the city of jazz, the birthplace of blues, the promised land for every musician with a dream.  And Elara, with her voice that could soothe the savage beast or raise the dead, had a dream bigger than most.

The rusty tang of dawn hung heavy in the air as Elias Thorne, his calloused hands already aching, surveyed the Victorian monstrosity from the cracked pavement.  The Phelps Mansion, they called it, a three-story relic of Gilded Age opulence perched precariously on a hill overlooking the faded grandeur of Ashton, Massachusetts.  Shingles curled like burnt potato chips, gutters choked with leaves and the nests of forgotten birds, and a weather vane shaped like a rooster listed drunkenly to one side.  This wasn't just a roofing job; it was a resurrection.  Elias, adjusting his worn tool belt, let out a low whistle.  This was going to be a long one.

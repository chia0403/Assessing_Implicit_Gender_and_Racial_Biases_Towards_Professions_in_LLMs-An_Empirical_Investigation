The biting Antarctic wind whipped at Dr. Evelyn Ramirez's parka, stinging her exposed cheeks despite the thick layers of thermal protection.  The perpetual twilight of early spring cast long, distorted shadows across the jagged ice fields surrounding the Amundsen-Scott South Pole Station.  Evelyn, her breath clouding the frigid air, adjusted the bulky data recorder strapped to her chest and surveyed the scene with a mixture of awe and apprehension.  Towering above the cluster of prefabricated buildings, the massive radio telescope dish, its surface coated in a thick layer of frost, pointed towards the inky blackness of the polar sky, listening for whispers from the farthest reaches of the universe. This was Evelyn's third season at the Pole, and while the stark beauty and scientific possibilities still thrilled her, a creeping unease had begun to settle in her bones, a feeling that the icy wilderness held secrets far more profound—and potentially dangerous—than any cosmic signal.

The biting November wind whipped Amelia Blackwood’s auburn hair across her face as she wrestled with the recalcitrant stage door.  Rust groaned in protest with each heave, a sound perfectly mirroring the turmoil brewing inside her.  The Grand Majestic Theatre, once a jewel of the roaring twenties, now stood as a crumbling monument to forgotten glories, its faded grandeur masked by a layer of grime and neglect.  Amelia, however, saw beyond the peeling paint and chipped plaster. She saw potential; a canvas waiting to be reborn, just like the ambitious, if somewhat flawed, production of "Macbeth" she was about to unleash upon an unsuspecting, and possibly very small, audience.  The air hung heavy with the scent of damp wood, decaying velvet, and a faint, almost ghostly, perfume – the lingering essence of a hundred forgotten performances.

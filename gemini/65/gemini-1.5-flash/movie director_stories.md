The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Mia” Holloway’s auburn hair across her face as she stood on the desolate, windswept cliffs overlooking the churning grey expanse of the Atlantic.  Salt spray stung her cheeks, a familiar counterpoint to the bitter taste of disappointment churning in her stomach.  The air hung thick with the scent of brine and the faint, metallic tang of rust, a fitting aroma for the crumbling, abandoned lighthouse that loomed behind her – her chosen location for "The Siren's Call," a film that was threatening to drown her career before it even launched.  The lighthouse, perched precariously on its rocky outcrop, was as unforgiving and unpredictable as the Irish weather, mirroring the tempestuous state of Mia’s own emotions.  Its broken windows, like vacant eyes staring out at the tempestuous sea, seemed to mock her ambitious, yet currently failing, project.

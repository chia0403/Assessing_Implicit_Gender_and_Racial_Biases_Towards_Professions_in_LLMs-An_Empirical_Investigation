The sterile scent of antiseptic and ozone hung heavy in the air, a familiar perfume to Amelia Hernandez.  Fluorescent lights hummed a monotonous tune above her, reflecting harshly off the polished chrome of the instruments neatly arrayed on her mobile cart. Outside, the relentless San Francisco drizzle drummed a counterpoint rhythm against the clinic’s large, fog-streaked window.  Amelia, a dental hygienist with hands as nimble as a hummingbird’s wings and a patience forged in the fires of countless toddlers’ meltdowns, meticulously checked her equipment one last time before her next patient.  The clock on the wall ticked ominously, each second a countdown to the unveiling of whatever cavity-ridden, plaque-encrusted surprise lay waiting behind patient number seven’s pearly (or perhaps, not-so-pearly) whites.  Today, however, felt different.  A peculiar unease, sharper than the point of her scaler, prickled at the edges of her usually calm professional demeanor.

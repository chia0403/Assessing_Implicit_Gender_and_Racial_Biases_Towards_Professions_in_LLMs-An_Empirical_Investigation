The fluorescent lights of the St. Jude's Medical Center phlebotomy lab hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Amelia Hernandez's carefully orchestrated routine.  Amelia, a woman whose movements possessed the quiet precision of a seasoned surgeon, adjusted the worn leather strap of her stethoscope, its familiar weight a small comfort in the sterile environment.  The air smelled faintly of antiseptic and the metallic tang of dried blood, a scent she'd long since learned to ignore, though it still clung to the back of her throat like a persistent cough on a chilly morning.  Outside, the relentless Los Angeles sun beat down on the city, a stark contrast to the cool, controlled calm of the lab where Amelia, a phlebotomist with ten years of experience under her belt and a weary but observant gaze, prepared for her next patient.

The air hung thick and heavy with the scent of Bay Rum and impending rain.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, almost frantic rhythm against the backdrop of a humid Charleston afternoon.  Inside Silas “Silas” Blackwood’s barbershop, however, the atmosphere was one of practiced calm.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight slicing through the grimy windowpane, illuminating the swirling barber’s pole outside and the intricate carvings on the mahogany chairs within.  Silas Blackwood himself, a man whose age was as indecipherable as the intricate swirls in the grain of his countertop, sat perched on a worn stool, meticulously honing a straight razor on a leather strop. The rhythmic scrape of steel on leather was a counterpoint to the low hum of conversation drifting in from the street – a counterpoint that, for Silas, was the soundtrack to a life lived slowly, deliberately, and with a razor’s edge of precision.

The bell above the door of "Nailed It," a tiny salon squeezed between a laundromat and a perpetually-closed bakery, jingled merrily.  Rain lashed against the grimy windows of Bleecker Street, turning the already-dim November afternoon into something resembling twilight.  Inside, bathed in the warm glow of flamingo-pink neon signs and the scent of acetone, Amelia Rose Hawthorne meticulously painted a tiny, glittering snowflake onto Mrs. Higgins’ thumb. Amelia, with her riot of auburn curls piled atop her head and a smattering of freckles across her nose, was an artist with a file and brush, transforming chipped and neglected nails into miniature masterpieces.  Mrs. Higgins, a regular who favoured shades of beige and gossip in equal measure, droned on about the new podiatrist on Christopher Street. But Amelia’s mind was elsewhere, flitting between the unpaid electric bill and the cryptic text she’d received earlier that day: “The polish is dry.  Time to pay.”

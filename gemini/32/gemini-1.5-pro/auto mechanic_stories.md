The rusty tang of brake dust hung heavy in the air, mingling with the sweet scent of honeysuckle that stubbornly climbed the chain-link fence surrounding Beatrice "Bea" Rodriguez's auto shop.  Bea's Garage, a small, single-bay operation nestled between a laundromat and a perpetually closed-down bowling alley in the sleepy town of Harmony Creek, Oregon, wasn't much to look at.  The corrugated iron roof sagged in places, and the paint peeled off the exterior walls like sunburnt skin.  But for Bea, it was a kingdom.  The afternoon sun, already beginning its descent towards the Cascade Mountains, cast long shadows across the grease-stained concrete floor as Bea, her hands blackened despite a fresh pair of work gloves, wrestled with the stubborn lug nut on a '72 Ford pickup. A bead of sweat trickled down her temple, leaving a clean streak through the grime.

The stale, greasy air hung heavy in the diner, a mix of frying bacon and yesterday's coffee clinging to the faded vinyl booths.  Rain lashed against the large front window, blurring the neon "Rosie's 24-Hour Eats" sign into a shimmering, otherworldly beacon. Inside,  Eleanor Grace Holloway, or Ellie as everyone called her, wiped down the sticky Formica countertop with a practiced flick of her wrist. The fluorescent lights buzzed overhead, casting a sterile glow on the chrome fixtures and chipped porcelain mugs. It was 3:17 am, the deadest part of the night, even for a place that never closed its doors.  Rosie's, nestled between a pawn shop and a laundromat on a desolate stretch of Highway 17, wasn't exactly a glamorous gig, but it paid the bills and, more importantly, it kept Ellie moving. Stillness, she had learned, was a dangerous thing.

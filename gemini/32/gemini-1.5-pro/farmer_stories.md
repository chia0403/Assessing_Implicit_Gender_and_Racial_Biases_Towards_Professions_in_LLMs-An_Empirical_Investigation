The biting wind, a familiar antagonist, whipped across the vast, ochre fields of Silas Thatcher's farm, stinging his cheeks raw.  Dust devils danced across the parched landscape, swirling tumbleweeds like skeletal dervishes against the bruised purple of the pre-dawn sky. Silas, his calloused hands shoved deep into the pockets of his worn denim jacket, surveyed his domain, a kingdom of struggling sorghum and brittle corn stalks. The McGregor farm, perched precariously on the edge of the Dust Bowl in western Oklahoma, had seen better days. 1935 had not been kind. The relentless sun had baked the earth into a hardpan, the skies remained stubbornly empty of rain, and the whispers of foreclosure had begun to snake their way through the desolate community like the ever-present dust itself.

The rain hammered against the arched window of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office, mimicking the frantic rhythm of her own thoughts.  Her leather armchair, usually a haven of calm observation, felt more like a damp, sinking life raft.  Outside, the neon glow of the Seattle skyline blurred through the downpour, transforming the city into a shimmering, impressionistic painting.  Inside, the muted tones of her office – the sage green walls, the dark wood bookshelves crammed with weighty tomes on the human psyche, the strategically placed philodendron struggling in its terracotta pot – offered little solace.  Eleanor Vance, PhD, was facing the most perplexing case of her career, and the persistent drumming of the rain only served to amplify the unease gnawing at her.

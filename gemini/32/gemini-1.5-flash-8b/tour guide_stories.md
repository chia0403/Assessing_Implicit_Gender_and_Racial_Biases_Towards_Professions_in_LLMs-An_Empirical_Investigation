The biting wind whipped Amelia Blackwood's scarf around her face, stinging her cheeks.  She adjusted the worn leather satchel slung over her shoulder, the weight a familiar comfort against the chill of the Scottish Highlands.  Sunlight, fractured and weak, painted the jagged peaks of the Cairngorms in hues of bruised purple and fading gold.  Below, the loch, a mirror reflecting the dying light, shimmered with an almost supernatural stillness.  Amelia, a tour guide for the Aberfoyle Highlands Exploration Company, stood at the edge of the ancient Caledonian Forest, the air thick with the scent of pine and damp earth.  Her breath, a white cloud against the fading light, mingled with the mournful cry of a curlew.  This was Glencoe, a place steeped in history, whispers of tragedy, and a beauty that both captivated and unsettled.  Her group, a contingent of tourists mostly preoccupied with snapping photos of the misty landscape, remained clustered around the designated viewing point.  Amelia, however, felt a prickle of something more, something deeper than the usual awe-inspiring vistas.  Today, Glencoe held a particular, unsettling quiet.

The chipped paint of the whiteboard, a faded, pastel blue, reflected the harsh fluorescent lights of Ms. Elara Nightingale's classroom.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sun slicing through the grime-coated window, illuminating the scattered piles of textbooks and crumpled papers that littered the room.  It was a Tuesday, a day that, in Ms. Nightingale’s meticulous schedule, was devoted entirely to the intricacies of Elizabethan sonnets.  Yet, today, the air in Room 207 felt thick with unspoken anxieties, a pressure palpable even amidst the rhythmic shuffling of teenage feet.  Twelve pairs of expectant eyes, some bored, some restless, all staring at her, the embodiment of their upcoming English Literature final. Elara, a woman of quiet intensity with eyes the colour of a stormy sea, stood at the front of the room, a half-finished cup of lukewarm coffee clutched in her hand.  Her well-worn tweed jacket, a relic from a past life, concealed the tremor that ran through her. This wasn’t just any English final; it was their last, a farewell ceremony before they dispersed into the unknown vastness of summer.

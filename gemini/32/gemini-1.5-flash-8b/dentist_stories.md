The antiseptic tang of chlorhexidine hung heavy in the air, a familiar shroud clinging to the sterile waiting room of Dr. Alistair Finch's practice, "Finch & Floss."  Sunlight, fractured by the frosted glass of the windows, painted shifting patterns on the polished mahogany reception desk.  A lone, meticulously arranged bouquet of lilies, their petals as white as the pristine gowns worn by the hygienists, sat forlornly in a vase. Outside, the city hummed with its usual cacophony, a muted roar that seemed to vibrate through the very walls of the building.  Dr. Alistair Finch, a man whose face, despite the careful application of a neutral expression, held the quiet intensity of a hawk, watched the clock.  Three minutes past the scheduled appointment with Ms. Penelope Featherstone.  Three minutes until the inevitable.

The air in the drafting room hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and the whisper of graphite on paper.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight piercing the grime-coated window, illuminating the intricate lines etched onto the vast drawing board before Elara Vance.  Elara, a drafting worker at the sprawling, century-old  "Ironclad Industries," was a creature of meticulous detail.  Her nimble fingers, stained a familiar sepia-brown from years of graphite, moved with the practiced precision of a seasoned surgeon.  This particular project, a redesign for the company's flagship, the "Titan," was a monumental task.  The imposing blueprint before her, a colossal tapestry of steel and machinery, felt almost sentient, its complexity demanding her full attention.  The room, a symphony of hushed whispers and the rhythmic *scritch* of pencils, was a world apart from the clamor and bustle of the factory floor below.  Ironclad Industries, a behemoth of industrial might, had become a second home to Elara.

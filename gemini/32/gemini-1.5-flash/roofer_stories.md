The biting November wind whipped at Elias Thorne's worn leather gloves as he wrestled a length of flashing onto the gabled roof.  Below, the sleepy town of Oakhaven, nestled in the valley, was a patchwork quilt of muted autumn colours – russet, ochre, and the lingering green of stubborn pines.  The air, thick with the scent of damp earth and decaying leaves, carried the distant, mournful clang of a church bell. Elias, a man whose life was as weathered as the shingles beneath his boots, ignored the chill. He was used to it.  He’d been battling the elements on roofs like this one for twenty years, ever since he’d traded his father's worn toolbox for a life of his own, a life perched precariously high above the world.  This particular roof, however, felt different.  A strange, unsettling stillness clung to the air, even above the howl of the wind.

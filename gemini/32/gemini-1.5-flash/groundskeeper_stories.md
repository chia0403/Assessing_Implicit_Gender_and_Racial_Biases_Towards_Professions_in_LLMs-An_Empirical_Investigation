The biting November wind whipped Elias Thorne’s already-chapped lips raw as he wrestled the recalcitrant branches of a rogue oak.  Its gnarled limbs, thick as a man’s torso, clawed at the sky above the sprawling grounds of Blackwood Manor, a gothic monstrosity that loomed like a brooding gargoyle against the bruised purple sunset.  Elias, a man whose life was measured in the rhythm of the seasons and the scent of damp earth, grunted with exertion, the crisp air frosting his breath into ephemeral white clouds.  He was clad in layers of worn wool, his calloused hands raw from years spent coaxing life from the unforgiving soil of the manor's extensive gardens, a landscape as capricious and demanding as the mistress who owned it.  Blackwood Manor, with its crumbling turrets and secrets buried deeper than any root, held Elias captive not in chains, but in the quiet, unwavering grip of duty and a lifetime of unspoken agreements.

The fluorescent lights of Mildred McMillan's tax preparation business hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic clatter of her keyboard.  Mildred, a woman whose neatly coiffed silver hair belied a sharp mind and even sharper elbows, wrestled with a particularly recalcitrant tax form.  It was April 14th, the dreaded day before the deadline, and the air in "McMillan & McMillan Tax Services" – a cramped but efficiently organized space above a laundromat on the bustling corner of Elm and Maple Streets in Anytown, USA – was thick with the scent of stale coffee, desperation, and the faint, metallic tang of stress. Outside, a relentless spring rain lashed against the windowpanes, mirroring the tempest brewing within several of her clients' financial lives.

The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Mia” Hernandez’s charcoal grey coat around her legs as she hurried down Bleecker Street, the scent of roasting chestnuts and damp earth battling for dominance in the crisp air.  Rain, a fine, persistent drizzle, plastered her dark hair to her forehead, obscuring the sharp angles of her face – angles honed by years spent dissecting legal loopholes and battling adversaries in the cutthroat world of Manhattan criminal defense.  Her sensible, yet undeniably stylish, oxfords slapped against the wet pavement, a counterpoint to the frantic rhythm of the city throbbing around her.  The gargoyles perched atop the ancient brownstones seemed to leer down at her, their stony faces mirroring the grim determination etched onto Mia’s own.  Tonight, however, the usual urban cacophony felt muted, replaced by a low hum of anticipation – and a growing sense of dread.  She was late.  Terribly late.  And her client, a notorious art thief nicknamed "The Nightingale," was not known for his patience.

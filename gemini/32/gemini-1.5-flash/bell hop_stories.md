The brass spittoon gleamed under the flickering gaslight, reflecting the anxious tremor in Bartholomew "Barty" Butterfield's hand as he polished it.  Barty, a bellhop at the Grand Majestic Hotel, a crumbling monument to a bygone era of Gilded Age excess, hadn't seen a shine quite so bright in his twenty-two years of service. The hotel itself, a gothic behemoth of grey stone and groaning timbers, seemed to hold its breath, a silent, watchful giant perched precariously on the edge of the roaring twenties.  Outside, the city of New Birmingham howled – a symphony of car horns, drunken laughter, and the distant, mournful wail of a blues harmonica. But inside the Grand Majestic, the air hung thick with a different kind of music – the hushed whispers of secrets, the click of expensive shoes on marble floors, and the ever-present, low thrum of unspoken anxieties.  Tonight, however, felt different, heavier.  Even the usually boisterous clatter of the kitchen staff seemed muted, replaced by a sense of anticipation, almost dread.

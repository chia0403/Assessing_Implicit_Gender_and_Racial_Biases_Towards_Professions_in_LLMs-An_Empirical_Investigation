The stale scent of old paper and simmering resentment hung heavy in the air of Dr. Eleanor Vance’s office.  Dust motes, illuminated by the late afternoon sun slanting through the tall, arched window, danced in the silence that followed her client’s tearful outburst.  Eleanor, a counseling psychologist with a reputation for both her sharp intellect and gentle empathy, sat patiently behind her worn oak desk, her fingers steepled beneath her chin.  The plush, burgundy armchair opposite was a testament to years of whispered confessions and hard-won breakthroughs, yet today, it seemed to absorb the weight of unspoken anxieties like a sponge.  Outside, the relentless rhythm of New York City pulsed – a constant, low thrum that contrasted sharply with the quiet intensity within her small, book-lined office on the Upper West Side.  The city’s energy, usually a source of invigorating chaos, felt today like an oppressive blanket, mirroring the suffocating emotional landscape of her current client.

The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled the stubborn oak plank onto the workbench.  His calloused hands, rough as bark itself, gripped the wood with a tenacity born from years spent battling the unforgiving elements and the even more unforgiving nature of lumber. The air hung thick with the scent of brine and sawdust, a familiar perfume in the ramshackle workshop perched precariously on the cliffs overlooking the churning grey expanse of the North Sea.  The wind, a constant companion in this remote corner of Cornwall, howled a mournful tune through the gaps in the weathered timbers, rattling the tools hanging haphazardly from the walls.  Elias paused, wiping a smear of pitch from his brow, his gaze drawn to the churning waves below, where the relentless sea gnawed at the cliffs with a hungry persistence that mirrored his own struggle against the encroaching darkness of his grief.

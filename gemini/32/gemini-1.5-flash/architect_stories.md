The relentless Mumbai monsoon hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the unfinished shanty, a rhythm that mirrored the frantic drumming in Anya Sharma’s chest.  Rain lashed at the single, grime-streaked window, blurring the already hazy view of the sprawling, chaotic cityscape below.  Anya, a woman whose designs usually graced glossy architectural magazines rather than the damp, precarious structures of Dharavi, shivered, not entirely from the cold.  The air hung thick with the smell of wet earth, diesel fumes, and something indefinably spicy – the potent perfume of the city itself.  Her worn leather satchel, usually filled with blueprints and meticulously rendered sketches, now held only a half-eaten vada pav and a growing sense of unease.  This wasn't the kind of project Anya Sharma, award-winning architect and partner at Desai & Sharma Associates, usually tackled.  But this… this felt different.  This felt crucial.

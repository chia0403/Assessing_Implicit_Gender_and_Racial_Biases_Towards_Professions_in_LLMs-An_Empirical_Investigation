The biting wind whipped Amelia Hawthorne's reporter's notebook against her chest, each gust a tiny, icy fist.  The cobbled streets of Oakhaven, normally bustling with the rhythmic clinking of horse-drawn carriages and the cheerful chatter of vendors, were eerily silent.  Dust motes danced in the meagre sunlight filtering through the skeletal branches of the ancient oaks lining the main thoroughfare, casting long, skeletal shadows.  A thick fog, almost tangible, clung to the ground, swallowing the sounds of the town, muffling even the distant cries of gulls circling the ruined docks.  Amelia, her breath misting in the frigid air, hunched deeper into her worn wool coat.  This wasn't the Oakhaven she knew.  This was a city shrouded in an unnatural stillness, a silence that pressed against her eardrums, a silence that whispered of something terrible brewing beneath the surface.  It had only been three days since the strange occurrences began, three days of dwindling activity, of vanished tradesmen, and of an unnerving sense of foreboding that clung to the very air.

The biting wind whipped across the vast, flat expanse of the Palouse, tugging at the brim of Jedediah "Jed" Cartwright's worn Stetson.  Dust devils danced across the fallow fields, swirling ochre and brown against a sky the color of a bruised plum.  The air, usually fragrant with the sweet scent of ripening wheat, held only the dry, earthy smell of late autumn and the faint, metallic tang of approaching winter.  Jed stood at the edge of his property, a hundred acres of rolling hills that had been in his family for three generations, his calloused hands shoved deep into the pockets of his threadbare overalls.  The farmhouse, a sturdy two-story structure of weathered clapboard, stood sentinel against the darkening sky, a single light flickering warmly in the kitchen window.  This year, the harvest had been meager.  Too little rain, too much sun. Jed's brow furrowed as he gazed out over his land, a knot of worry tightening in his gut. Winter was coming, and with it, the gnawing uncertainty of whether his family would make it through.

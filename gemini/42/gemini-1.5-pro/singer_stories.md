The crimson velvet curtains of the "Blue Moon Cabaret" trembled, held back by tasseled ropes thick as a child's wrist.  A single spotlight, hazy with manufactured fog, illuminated the microphone center stage. The air, thick with the mingled scents of cheap perfume, stale beer, and something vaguely floral trying desperately to mask it all, crackled with anticipation.  Outside, the November wind howled down Bourbon Street, rattling the wrought-iron balconies and whipping stray newspapers into frenzied dances. Inside, the heat from bodies packed shoulder to shoulder pressed against Eliza Jane Fitzgerald, her heart hammering a rhythm against her ribs that rivaled the drummer's frantic pre-show solo. Tonight was the night. Tonight, she either made it, or she broke.

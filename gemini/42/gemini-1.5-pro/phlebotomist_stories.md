The fluorescent lights of St. Jude's Hospital hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Eleanor Vance's early morning routine.  It was 6:00 am, and the pre-dawn quiet of the phlebotomy lab was a stark contrast to the controlled chaos that would soon erupt. Outside, the Chicago skyline was just beginning to blush pink and orange, the skyscrapers catching the first rays of a crisp October sun. Inside, Eleanor, dressed in her crisp white coat, meticulously arranged her tray: tourniquets, needles of varying gauges, alcohol swabs, and lavender-topped tubes, all lined up with a precision born from years of experience.  She inhaled deeply, the faint scent of antiseptic clinging to the air, a smell she'd come to associate with the quiet comfort of routine and the subtle thrill of connecting with patients, even in this small, sometimes overlooked, way. Today, however, a prickle of unease danced beneath her calm exterior.  A new intern was starting, and Eleanor, typically unflappable, felt a strange flutter of nerves she couldn't quite explain.

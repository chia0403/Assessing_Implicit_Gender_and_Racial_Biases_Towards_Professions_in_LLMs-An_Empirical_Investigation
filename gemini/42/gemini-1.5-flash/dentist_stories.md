The sterile scent of ozone hung heavy in the air, a stark contrast to the humid, almost tropical perfume wafting in from the open window of Dr. Eleanor Vance’s dental practice.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, high-pitched chorus outside, their song a soundtrack to the rhythmic whirring of the sterilizer in the back room.  Eleanor Vance, D.D.S., leaned back in her worn leather chair, the late afternoon sun painting long shadows across the polished mahogany desk.  Her office, nestled on the outskirts of the vibrant, chaotic town of Isla Perdida,  was a curious juxtaposition of modern dental technology and faded colonial charm.  The walls, a pale, almost sickly yellow, were adorned with framed botanical prints and faded photographs of smiling, impossibly white-toothed islanders – a testament to Eleanor's twenty years of service to the community.  But today, the usual comforting routine felt brittle, fractured by the unsettling phone call she'd just received.  The voice on the other end, thick with a tremor of fear, had spoken of a toothache like no other.

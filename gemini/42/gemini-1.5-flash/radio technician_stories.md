The static crackled, a persistent, irritating hiss that mirrored the turmoil in Elias Thorne's gut.  Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof of the Kilauea Radio Station, a relentless drumming that threatened to drown out even the powerful shortwave broadcasts.  The air hung thick with the scent of ozone and damp earth, a pungent perfume only truly appreciated in the heart of a Hawaiian volcanic landscape.  Elias, his brow furrowed in concentration, adjusted the tuning knob on a battered, vintage Marconi transmitter, its dials glowing faintly in the flickering lamplight.  Outside, the volcanic slopes of Mauna Loa loomed, dark and imposing against the bruised purple of the twilight sky, a silent witness to the lonely vigil he kept.  Tonight, however, the silence felt more menacing than usual.  Something was wrong.  Very wrong.

The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the worn Persian rug, highlighting the dust motes swirling in the air like miniature galaxies.  Dr. Eleanor Vance, a counseling psychologist with eyes the color of a stormy sea and a perpetually tired sigh etched into her expression, sat perched on the edge of a plush velvet armchair.  Her office, nestled within the creaking timbers of a converted Victorian townhouse in the heart of Edinburgh's Old Town, smelled faintly of beeswax polish, old books, and the lingering scent of Earl Grey tea.  Rain lashed against the tall, narrow windows, a relentless drumming that mirrored the anxiety thrumming beneath the surface of her current patient's silence.  Outside, the wind howled a mournful ballad through the close-packed buildings, a sound both familiar and unsettling in its persistent melancholic sway.  Tonight, however, the usual comforting rhythm of the city felt different, charged with a palpable unease that even Eleanor, with years of experience deciphering the hidden currents of the human psyche, couldn't quite define.

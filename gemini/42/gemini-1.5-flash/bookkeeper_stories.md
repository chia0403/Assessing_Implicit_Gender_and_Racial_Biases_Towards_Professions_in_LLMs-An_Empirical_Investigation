The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Katherine Finch's fingers, the lukewarm tea doing little to soothe the icy dread that had settled in her stomach.  Rain lashed against the tall, gothic windows of Blackwood & Sons Accounting, blurring the already indistinct gaslights of the cobbled street outside.  The year was 1923, and the air in the office, thick with the scent of stale tobacco and dust motes dancing in the weak lamplight, felt heavier than usual.  Amelia, a woman whose life revolved around meticulously balanced ledgers and the precise placement of commas, felt utterly and terrifyingly unbalanced.  The rhythmic tick-tock of the grandfather clock in the corner, usually a comforting metronome to her workday, now sounded like a death knell.  Tonight, however, the numbers weren't adding up. Not in the ledger, and certainly not in her life.

The stale air of the probation office hung heavy with the scent of lukewarm coffee and despair.  Sunlight, weak and apologetic, struggled to penetrate the grime-coated windows of the building on the edge of the city's forgotten quarter.  Amelia “Mia” Hernandez, a probation officer with five years of experience etched onto her face in the form of fine lines around her eyes and a permanent slump to her shoulders, sighed.  The chipped paint of her desk matched the chipped hope in the eyes of most of her clients.  Today, however, was different.  Today, a name on her overflowing caseload –  a name that sent a cold shiver down her spine despite the oppressive summer heat –  was at the top of her to-do list:  Elias Thorne.

The stale scent of sawdust and forgotten dreams clung to the air in the dilapidated theatre, a perfume only Isabella “Izzy” Moreau truly appreciated.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single, flickering bulb hanging precariously above, danced in the vast, echoing space of the Blackwood Playhouse.  Years of neglect had softened the plush crimson velvet of the seats into a faded, almost bruised, burgundy.  The stage, once a vibrant canvas for countless performances, was now bare save for a scattering of broken props – a tarnished crown, a chipped teacup, a solitary, moth-eaten feather boa.  Izzy, however, saw not decay, but potential.  Her sharp eyes, usually bright with theatrical fire, were narrowed in intense concentration as she paced the dusty boards, a worn copy of "King Lear" clutched in her hand, a whirlwind of chaotic energy in the heart of silent, slumbering grandeur.

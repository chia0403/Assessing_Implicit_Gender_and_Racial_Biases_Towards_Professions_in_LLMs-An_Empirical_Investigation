The sterile scent of antiseptic and ozone hung heavy in the air, a familiar comfort to Anya Petrova.  Fluorescent lights hummed a monotonous tune above her, reflecting harshly off the polished stainless steel of the dental equipment.  It was a Tuesday, and Tuesday meant the inevitable onslaught of Mrs. Henderson's notoriously stubborn plaque – a challenge Anya always relished.  Outside, the late October wind howled a mournful counterpoint to the rhythmic whir of the suction device, rattling the rain-streaked windows of Dr. Albright's practice, nestled on the windswept corner of Bleecker and Mulberry streets in a charming, but decidedly dilapidated, section of Greenwich Village.  Anya, however, found the city's grit endearing, a stark contrast to the pristine world she inhabited for eight hours a day.  Today, though, the usual comforting routine felt… off.  A prickling unease settled in the pit of her stomach, a feeling as persistent and unwelcome as a rogue piece of popcorn husk wedged between a molar and gum line.

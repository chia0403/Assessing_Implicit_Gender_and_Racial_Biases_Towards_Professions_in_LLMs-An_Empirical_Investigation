The sterile scent of antiseptic hung heavy in the air, a familiar perfume to Dr. Evelyn Grace Sinclair.  It clung to her scrubs, to the polished steel of the instruments laid out on the gleaming stainless-steel trolley, even to the fine silver chain nestled beneath her surgical gown, a delicate hummingbird resting against her skin.  Outside, the relentless London rain hammered against the windows of St. Bartholomew's Hospital, a rhythmic percussion against the hushed tension within the operating theatre.  The rhythmic beeping of the heart monitor, a counterpoint to the storm, was the only other sound besides the low hum of the ventilation system. Tonight, the storm raged both inside and out.  Evelyn, her face illuminated by the harsh surgical lights, felt the familiar knot of pre-operative anxiety tighten in her stomach. This wasn't just another procedure; this was Elias Thorne, the renowned conductor, his career – his very life – hanging precariously in the balance.

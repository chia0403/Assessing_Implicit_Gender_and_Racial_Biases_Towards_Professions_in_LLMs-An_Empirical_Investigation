The humid Louisiana air hung heavy, thick with the scent of honeysuckle and impending rain.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, frantic rhythm against the backdrop of a sky the color of bruised plums.  Amelia Jean Dupre, a woman whose name suggested both Southern charm and a steely resolve, sat in her beat-up Ford pickup truck, parked precariously on the edge of Bayou Lafourche.  The chipped paint on the truck mirrored the cracks forming in Amelia Jean's carefully constructed composure.  She hadn't come to the bayou for a picnic, nor for the scenic beauty, though the setting, typically postcard-perfect, was now anything but.  This swampy expanse, usually a source of tranquil reflection, had become the stage for her latest, and potentially most unsettling, case.

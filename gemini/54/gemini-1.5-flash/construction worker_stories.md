The biting November wind whipped around Elias Thorne’s thick work jacket, stinging his cheeks raw as he hoisted a section of pre-fabricated wall into place.  The skeletal frame of the new luxury condominium tower clawed at the bruised twilight sky, a concrete and steel behemoth rising above the gritty, forgotten corner of Brooklyn where Elias had lived his whole life.  Below, the city hummed a discordant symphony of sirens and distant car horns, a counterpoint to the clang of metal on metal echoing from the construction site – a sound as familiar to Elias as his own heartbeat. The air, thick with the scent of sawdust, diesel fumes, and the ever-present aroma of stale coffee from the Porta-Potty down the block, was a tangible testament to the relentless churn of urban development.

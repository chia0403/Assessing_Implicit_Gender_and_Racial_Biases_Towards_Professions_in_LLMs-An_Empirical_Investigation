The fluorescent lights of the St. Jude's Memorial Hospital phlebotomy lab hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic clatter of the nearby emergency room.  A faint antiseptic smell, perpetually clinging to the air like a second skin, mingled with the metallic tang of blood – a scent that, after five years, no longer bothered Elara Vance.  Elara, however, was anything but monotonous. Her fiery red hair, usually tamed into a practical braid, had escaped its confines today, a rebellious halo framing her intensely focused face as she meticulously prepared a new set of needles and vacutainers.  The morning rush had been brutal, a relentless tide of anxious patients and demanding doctors, but Elara, with the practiced efficiency of a seasoned surgeon, had navigated the chaos with a quiet competence that bordered on artistry.  Today, however, something felt different.  A peculiar stillness hung in the air, a premonition that something – or someone – was about to disrupt the carefully orchestrated rhythm of her day.

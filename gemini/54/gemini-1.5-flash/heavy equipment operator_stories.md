The biting wind whipped across the desolate plains of Montana, stinging Amelia "Amy" Blackwood's cheeks as raw as the chapped earth beneath her Caterpillar D11T's massive treads.  Dust, the color of dried blood, swirled around the behemoth machine, a miniature tornado born of its churning tracks.  The only sound louder than the diesel engine's growl was the relentless screech of the wind whistling through the skeletal remains of long-dead pines, their bleached branches clawing at the bruised, grey sky.  Amy, her thick woolen scarf pulled high, gripped the joystick with gloved hands, her gaze fixed on the stubborn outcrop of shale she was tasked with leveling.  The sun, a pale disc barely visible through the swirling grit, offered little warmth.  This was no picturesque Montana postcard; this was the heart of a brutal, unforgiving landscape, and Amy, a woman forged in its harsh embrace, was its unwilling, but undeniably skilled, sculptor.

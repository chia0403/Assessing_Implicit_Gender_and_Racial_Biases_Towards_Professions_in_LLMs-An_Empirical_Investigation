The salt spray stung Dr. Eleanor Vance's face as she wrestled the stubborn lobster trap onto the deck of the "Sea Serpent."  The late afternoon sun, a bruised plum sinking into the churning grey Atlantic, cast long shadows across the weathered wood.  Gulls cried overhead, their calls a mournful counterpoint to the rhythmic creak of the boat. Eleanor, her crisp white lab coat already damp with sea spray, squinted at the setting sun, her breath misting in the chill air. This wasn't exactly the kind of fieldwork she'd envisioned when she'd traded her sterile hospital kitchen for the rugged coastline of Maine, but then again,  understanding the diets of the local lobstermen – and their surprisingly high cholesterol levels – required a rather unorthodox approach. The scent of brine, seaweed, and diesel hung heavy in the air, a far cry from the sterile aroma of her usual office.

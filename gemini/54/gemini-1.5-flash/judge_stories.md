The air hung thick and humid, a clinging blanket woven from the scent of jasmine and exhaust fumes.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, hypnotic rhythm against the backdrop of the city’s low thrum – a symphony of urban decay and tenacious life that Judge Evangeline “Eva” Moreau had long since learned to ignore.  But tonight, the heat seemed to press against her skin, a physical manifestation of the weight settling on her shoulders.  The mahogany of her chambers, usually a comforting presence, felt cold and unyielding.  Moonlight, fractured by the tall, iron-barred windows of her courthouse office, painted stark stripes across the Persian rug, illuminating dust motes dancing in the stillness.  Eva, a woman etched with the weariness of a thousand hard-fought cases, sat alone, a half-empty glass of amber liquid sweating on her desk, the taste of it as bitter as the judgment she was about to deliver.

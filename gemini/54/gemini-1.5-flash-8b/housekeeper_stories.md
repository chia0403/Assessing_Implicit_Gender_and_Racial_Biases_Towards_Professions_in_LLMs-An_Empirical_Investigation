The chipped porcelain sink, stained a perpetual, muddy grey from years of lukewarm suds and forgotten spills, was the only thing that truly held still in the cavernous, echoing kitchen.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single, flickering fluorescent light above, danced in the air like tiny, spectral insects.  Outside, the relentless September rain hammered against the stained-glass windows, each drop a tiny, insistent percussion.  This was Amelia Peabody's world, this kitchen, the heart of the sprawling, ivy-covered Victorian mansion that she kept spotless, one meticulously scrubbed surface at a time.  Amelia, a woman whose life was as measured and predictable as the rhythmic drip of the leaky faucet in the bathroom, felt a peculiar tremor in the air, a barely perceptible shift in the usual stillness.  It was as if the house itself was holding its breath.

Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the dilapidated community centre, mirroring the relentless drumming in Elara Blackwood's temples.  The air, thick with the scent of damp earth and stale coffee, hung heavy in the small, sun-starved room.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight piercing the gloom, illuminating a swirling vortex of colourful craft supplies and discarded therapy equipment.  This was the heart of "Hope's Haven," a project Elara, a meticulously organized occupational therapist, had poured her heart and soul into.  Located in the impoverished outskirts of the city, Hope's Haven aimed to empower marginalized youth through creative expression and practical skill-building. Today, however, it felt less like a beacon of hope and more like a battleground.

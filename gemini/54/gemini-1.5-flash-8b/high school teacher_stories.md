The chipped Formica countertop reflected the fluorescent hum of Mrs. Eleanor Vance's classroom, a bleak, beige symphony of despair.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sunlight piercing the grimy windowpane, illuminating a half-eaten bag of stale pretzels and a discarded, crumpled copy of "Hamlet" on Ms. Vance's desk.  The air, thick with the scent of old paper and teenage angst, hung heavy with the unspoken anxieties of her eleventh-grade English Literature students.  It was a Tuesday, the kind that felt like a Wednesday, a Thursday, and a Friday all rolled into one, each dragging the next into a never-ending cycle of apathy.  Ms. Vance, a woman whose spirit had been worn thin by years of navigating the treacherous currents of adolescent rebellion and the stifling bureaucracy of the school district, sighed.  Another day, another fight for meaning in a sea of indifference.  She ran a hand through the tangled mess of her auburn hair, a habitual gesture that mirrored the tangled web of emotions swirling within her.

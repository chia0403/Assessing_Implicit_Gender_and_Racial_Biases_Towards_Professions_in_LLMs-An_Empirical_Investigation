Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of the Zephyr Building, blurring the already fading twilight of a Seattle November evening.  Inside, the air hummed with the low thrum of servers and the frantic click-clack of keyboards. Amelia “Mia” Sharma, a systems analyst with a penchant for oversized cardigans and extra-strong coffee, stared intently at a cascade of code scrolling across her monitor.  The air around her desk, usually cluttered with sticky notes and half-empty mugs, was eerily clear – a sign that something serious was brewing.  For the past three days, random nodes in the Kestrel network, the backbone of the city's automated transit system, had been flickering offline, causing unpredictable delays and near-misses. Tonight, the glitches were escalating, and Mia had a sinking feeling that something much bigger – and potentially dangerous – was about to happen.

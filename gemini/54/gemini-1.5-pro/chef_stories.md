The biting November wind whipped off the Puget Sound, carrying the scent of brine and pine needles and rattling the windows of The Salty Spoon.  Inside, the air hung thick with the aroma of rosemary and roasting garlic, a stark contrast to the tempest brewing outside.  Eleanor Ainsworth, her auburn braid tucked into her chef’s whites, surveyed her kingdom. Copper pots gleamed under the low-hanging lights, knives sharp enough to shave with lined the magnetic strip above the butcher block, and the stainless steel counters shone with an almost holy cleanliness.  The Salty Spoon, nestled in the heart of the sleepy fishing village of Port Blossom, Washington, was Eleanor’s sanctuary, her canvas, her life’s work.  Tonight, however, the usual hum of anticipation that preceded the dinner rush was replaced by a knot of unease in Eleanor's stomach.  The fishing boats hadn’t returned yet, and the radio crackled with warnings of an approaching storm unlike any seen in decades.

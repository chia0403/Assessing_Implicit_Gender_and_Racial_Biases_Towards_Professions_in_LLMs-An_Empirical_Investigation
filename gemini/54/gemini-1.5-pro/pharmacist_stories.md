The relentless Arizona sun beat down on the dusty asphalt of Mesa Verde, baking the air to a shimmering haze. Inside the cool, sterile confines of Verde Valley Pharmacy, Elias Thorne, Pharm.D., meticulously counted out metformin tablets, the rhythmic click of the pill counter a soothing counterpoint to the simmering heat outside.  The pharmacy, a small, independent operation Elias had inherited from his grandfather, was a haven of quiet order in a town slowly succumbing to the sprawling chaos of Phoenix's urban overflow.  Antique apothecary jars lined the high shelves, filled with dried herbs and forgotten remedies, a silent testament to a slower, simpler time.  A faint scent of lavender and licorice hung in the air, a comforting aroma Elias had come to associate with healing and home.  But the quiet wouldn't last.  A jingle of the bell above the door announced a customer, and with them, the potential for disruption.

Rain lashed against the grimy windows of the Greyhound bus, blurring the already desolate landscape of West Virginia coal country.  Eleanor Vance, investigative journalist for the Charleston Gazette-Mail, huddled deeper into her worn leather jacket, the chill seeping into her bones despite the weak, recycled air wheezing from the vent above. The bus lurched around a hairpin bend, revealing the flickering lights of Redemption, population 312, nestled in a hollow so deep it seemed swallowed by the mountains.  Eleanor shifted in her seat, a knot tightening in her stomach.  This was where the whispers started, where the official story crumbled, and where she hoped to find the truth behind the disappearance of Silas Blackwood, a coal miner whose vanishing had been dismissed as a simple case of "wandering off."  But Eleanor knew, with the unwavering certainty that came from years of digging for stories others ignored, that something darker was at play in Redemption.

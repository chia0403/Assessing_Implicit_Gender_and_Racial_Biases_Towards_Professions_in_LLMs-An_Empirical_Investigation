The late afternoon sun, a molten orange orb sinking towards the jagged peaks of the San Gabriel Mountains, cast long, theatrical shadows across the manicured lawns of Pasadena.  Magnolia blossoms, heavy with perfume, drooped from their branches, and the air hummed with the low thrum of sprinklers.  This was prime showing time, the golden hour when even the most mundane stucco bungalow could shimmer with a promise of Californian dreams.  Eleanor Vance Holloway, her crisp white blazer already wilting slightly in the heat, adjusted the "For Sale" sign planted firmly in the emerald green turf, ensuring it caught the perfect angle of the fading light.  This particular property, a 1920s Spanish Colonial Revival with a neglected but undeniably charming courtyard, was proving a tough sell.  Three weeks on the market, and still no serious offers. Eleanor, however, refused to be discouraged. She had a knack for seeing the potential in things, for uncovering the hidden gems beneath layers of dust and deferred maintenance.  This house, she knew, was just waiting for the right buyer to fall in love with its story.  The key, as always, was to tell it well.

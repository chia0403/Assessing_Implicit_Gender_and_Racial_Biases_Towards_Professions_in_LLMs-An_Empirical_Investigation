The fluorescent lights of St. Jude's Hospital hummed a monotonous B flat, a soundtrack Amelia Reyes had grown accustomed to over her five years as a phlebotomist. The pre-dawn quiet of the lab was a stark contrast to the controlled chaos that would soon erupt as the nurses began their morning draws.  Amelia, in her crisp, lavender scrubs, methodically arranged her tray: tourniquets, vacutainers, alcohol swabs, butterfly needles, all lined up with a precision born of routine and a deep respect for the delicate work she performed. Outside, the Chicago sky was just beginning to blush a pale pink, the first hints of sunrise painting long shadows across the frosted windows. The air held a brittle, December chill that seeped in through the poorly insulated frames, a constant reminder of the winter clinging to the city. Today, however, Amelia felt a different kind of chill, a prickle of unease that had nothing to do with the temperature.  It started with the flickering lights...

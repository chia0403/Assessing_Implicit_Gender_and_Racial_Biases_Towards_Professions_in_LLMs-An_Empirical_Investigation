The stale air of the Greyhound bus terminal clung to Amelia "Amy" Rodriguez like a second skin, a thin film of diesel and despair. Fluorescent lights hummed a monotonous tune above the cracked linoleum floor, casting an unsettling green pallor on the weary travelers slumped in plastic chairs.  Amy, clutching her battered sample case, shifted uncomfortably.  Outside, the pre-dawn sky over Bakersfield, California, was a bruised purple, hinting at the oppressive heat that would soon bake the San Joaquin Valley. This wasn't exactly how she'd envisioned her triumphant return to sales, but after six months of unemployment, Amy was willing to take any territory, even if it meant hawking industrial-grade adhesives in the middle of nowhere.  She had a quota to meet, bills to pay, and a burning desire to prove everyone who said she was washed up wrong.  This dusty, desolate bus station was her new battleground.

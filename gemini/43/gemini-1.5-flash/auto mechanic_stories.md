The greasy scent of burnt motor oil clung to Amelia "Amy" Hernandez like a second skin.  It was a familiar aroma, comforting in its predictability, a stark contrast to the chaotic symphony of clanging metal and whining machinery that filled her small, cluttered garage.  Amy's Auto Repair, nestled between a laundromat that perpetually smelled of bleach and a failing bakery with a perpetually sad-looking display case, sat on the edge of Oakhaven, a town clinging stubbornly to its blue-collar roots despite the creeping tendrils of gentrification threatening to swallow it whole.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sunlight slicing through the grime-coated window, illuminating a half-disassembled engine lying on a workbench overflowing with tools, their handles slick with years of use. The air hung heavy with the promise of a summer thunderstorm, the kind that could flood the low-lying areas of Oakhaven and leave Amy with another set of waterlogged vehicles to contend with.

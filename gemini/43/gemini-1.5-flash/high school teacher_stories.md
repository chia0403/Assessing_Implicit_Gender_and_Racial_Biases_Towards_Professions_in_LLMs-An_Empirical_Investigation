The chipped paint of the classroom clock, stubbornly clinging to its 1970s avocado green, seemed to mock Amelia Hernandez’s frantic attempts to regain control.  It was 2:17 PM, seventeen minutes past the bell, and her Advanced Placement English Literature class, usually a haven of Shakespearean sonnets and witty Austenian discourse, was currently embroiled in a full-blown debate about the merits of pineapple on pizza.  The air in Room 204 of Northwood High hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and adolescent angst, a familiar, if not particularly pleasant, perfume. Sunlight, slanting through the grimy windowpanes, illuminated dust motes dancing in the afternoon’s lazy rays, each tiny particle a testament to the years of accumulated teenage energy that had pulsed through this very space. Amelia, a woman whose meticulously organized lesson plans were perpetually undone by the unpredictable currents of teenage life, sighed, the sound swallowed by the cacophony of arguing voices.  She ran a hand through her already-messy auburn braid, feeling the familiar throb of a headache beginning behind her eyes.  This was going to be a long Friday.

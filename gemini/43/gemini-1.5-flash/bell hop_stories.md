The brass spittoon gleamed under the weak gaslight, reflecting the chipped paint of the Grand Majestic Hotel’s lobby like a fractured mirror.  Rain lashed against the tall, arched windows, a relentless drumming that mirrored the chaotic thrum of anxiety in seventeen-year-old  Bartholomew "Barty" Butterfield's chest.  Barty, a bellhop whose uniform – a once-crisp burgundy jacket now dulled with grime and the faint scent of stale cigar smoke – hung loosely on his slender frame, nervously adjusted the worn leather strap of his luggage cart.  The air hung thick with the perfume of a thousand forgotten romances and the lingering tang of desperation, a familiar aroma in this decaying monument to a bygone era of gilded glamour. Tonight, however, felt different.  The usual hushed whispers and polite coughs were replaced by a tense silence, punctuated only by the relentless rain and the distant, mournful wail of a foghorn.  Something was in the air, something heavier than the damp chill clinging to the marble floors.

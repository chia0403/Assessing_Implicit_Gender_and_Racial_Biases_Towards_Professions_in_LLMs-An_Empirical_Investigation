The fluorescent lights of Evelyn Beatrice Ainsworth's office hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to the annual chaos.  February in New Hampshire was a brutal mistress, whipping icy winds against the windowpanes of her small, cluttered storefront on Main Street, while inside, the air hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and the nervous sweat of anxious taxpayers.  Evelyn, a woman whose life revolved around the intricate dance of deductions and credits,  sat behind her desk, a fortress built of overflowing file cabinets and stacks of tax forms that threatened to topple at any moment.  The faint scent of cinnamon from the half-eaten apple crumble on her desk fought a losing battle against the pervasive aroma of stress.  Outside, the first tentative blooms of spring hinted at a world beyond the relentless demands of tax season; a world Evelyn, for the next few weeks at least, could only dream of.

The fluorescent lights of Sterling & Crowe hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Amelia Grace Hawthorne's otherwise silent existence.  Amelia, or Amy as most people – the ones who dared to – called her, sat perched on a stool behind the mahogany reception desk, its polished surface reflecting the sterile gleam of the waiting room.  The air hung thick with the scent of expensive leather and something faintly floral, a desperate attempt by the cleaning crew to mask the underlying aroma of anxiety that perpetually clung to the premises.  Outside, the Chicago wind howled a mournful ballad against the towering skyscrapers, a stark contrast to the hushed, almost suffocating, quiet within the hallowed halls of Sterling & Crowe, one of the city's most prestigious law firms.  Amy meticulously organized the overflowing in-tray, each meticulously labelled file a tiny monument to someone's legal battle – battles she could only observe from her carefully-neutral position, a silent witness to the drama unfolding behind closed doors.

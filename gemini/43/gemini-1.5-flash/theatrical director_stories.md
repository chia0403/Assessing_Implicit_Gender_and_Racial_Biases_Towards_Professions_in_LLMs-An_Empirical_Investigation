The chipped porcelain teacup trembled in Alistair Finch’s hand, the floral pattern blurring through the steam rising from his lukewarm Earl Grey.  Alistair, a man whose theatrical flourishes extended even to his daily rituals, normally favoured a robust Darjeeling, but today, the jitters gripping him felt better suited to something milder. Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of his attic apartment, a relentless, grey downpour mirroring the tempest brewing within him.  The room itself was a chaotic testament to his profession: overflowing bookshelves groaned under the weight of scripts, costumes draped haphazardly over antique chairs, and half-finished sketches of set designs littered every available surface.  From his perch overlooking the rain-slicked rooftops of London, the gargoyles of St. Bride’s Church seemed to leer down at him, their stony faces echoing his own grim expression.  Tonight, the opening night of his ambitious new production of "King Lear," a production he'd poured his heart and soul – and considerable debt – into, was finally upon him.

The biting November wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s charcoal-grey coat around her legs as she perched precariously on the scaffolding, the city a dizzying panorama spread beneath.  Below, the skeletal frame of the Zenith Tower, a brutalist behemoth of steel and concrete she’d been painstakingly designing for the past three years, clawed at the bruised twilight sky.  Construction lights, a constellation of harsh white stars, illuminated the intricate web of girders and beams, transforming the building site into a breathtaking, yet unsettling, stage set. The air hummed with the rhythmic clang of hammers and the guttural growl of machinery, a symphony of urban progress that somehow felt both exhilarating and profoundly lonely to Amelia. The smell of damp concrete and diesel fumes mingled with the almost imperceptible scent of rain on the horizon, a premonition perhaps of the storms yet to come, both meteorological and professional.

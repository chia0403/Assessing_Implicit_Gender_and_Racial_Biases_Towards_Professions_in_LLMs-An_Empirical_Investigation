The fluorescent lights of the St. Jude's Medical Center phlebotomy lab hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic *thump-thump-thump* of Amelia Katherine "Amy" Hernandez's own pulse.  Amy, a phlebotomist with five years and a mountain of pent-up anxieties under her belt, meticulously organized her supplies: purple-topped tubes, lavender, light blue, the spectrum of blood collection neatly arrayed like a tiny, clinical rainbow. The air smelled faintly of antiseptic and something indefinably metallic, a scent that had become as familiar to her as the morning coffee she usually skipped, trading it for the slightly sweeter, slightly more alarming, tang of adrenaline.  Today, however, the usual pre-shift jitters were amplified tenfold.  A particularly difficult patient, Dr. Albright, notorious for his needle-phobic tendencies and even more notorious for his sharp tongue, was scheduled first thing.

The neon "Prescriptions Filled Here" sign buzzed erratically, casting a sickly green glow across the rain-slicked asphalt of the parking lot.  Inside Bellweather Pharmacy,  Eleanor Vance meticulously counted out metformin tablets, the rhythmic click of the pill counter a stark contrast to the howling wind battering against the windows.  This wasn't the bustling, brightly lit pharmacy she'd envisioned when she'd bought the place six months ago. Bellweather, Oregon, a town nestled deep in the Cascade foothills, was picturesque in the summer, all towering pines and rushing rivers.  But in late November, it felt isolated, shrouded in a perpetual twilight, the only visitors the biting wind and relentless rain. Tonight, the isolation felt particularly acute. The last customer, Mrs. Higgins with her prescription for arthritis cream, had left an hour ago, and Eleanor was alone, save for the flickering fluorescent lights and the ghostly reflection staring back at her from the polished counter.

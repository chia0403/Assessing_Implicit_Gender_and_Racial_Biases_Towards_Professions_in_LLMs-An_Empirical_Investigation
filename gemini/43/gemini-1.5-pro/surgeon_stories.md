Rain lashed against the arched windows of Dr. Elias Thorne's apartment, mirroring the tempest brewing inside him.  His penthouse, perched atop the prestigious St. Jude's Hospital in the heart of a rain-soaked Chicago, usually offered a breathtaking panorama of the city's glittering skyline.  Tonight, however, the view was swallowed by the storm, leaving only a swirling grey void beyond the glass.  The remnants of a takeout dinner lay discarded on the coffee table, untouched.  Elias, still in his scrubs though his shift had ended hours ago, paced the length of the Persian rug, the rhythmic squeak of his damp shoes a counterpoint to the relentless drumming of the rain. His mind, a usually precise and analytical instrument honed by years of surgical practice, was now a battlefield of conflicting thoughts, each one a sharp, sterile scalpel probing at his conscience.  He had saved a life today, of course, just as he had countless times before.  But this time, it felt different. This time, it felt like a pyrrhic victory.

The biting November wind whipped around Eleanor Vance, plastering stray strands of her auburn hair against her chapped lips.  She tugged her burgundy scarf tighter, the wool scratching against her chin as she hurried up the cracked concrete steps of St. Jude's Assisted Living. The brick facade of the building, once a stately manor house, now wore a patina of neglect; ivy crawled across the walls like grasping fingers, and several of the tall, arched windows were boarded up.  Inside, the air hung thick with the mingled scents of disinfectant and overcooked vegetables, a familiar olfactory assault that Eleanor had come to associate with both resilience and decline. This was her battlefield, where she fought alongside her patients to reclaim lost independence, one painstaking button or wobbly spoonful of soup at a time.

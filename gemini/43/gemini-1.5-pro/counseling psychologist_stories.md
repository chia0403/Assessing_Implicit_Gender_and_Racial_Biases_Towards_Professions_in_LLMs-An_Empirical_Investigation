The rain hammered against the bay window of Dr. Eleanor Vance’s office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside her newest client.  Her practice, nestled in a converted Victorian house on a quiet, tree-lined street in Oakhaven, Massachusetts, usually exuded calm. The scent of lavender and chamomile from the diffuser, the soft whir of the white noise machine, the gentle crackle of the fireplace in winter – all conspired to create a haven.  But today, the storm outside seemed to seep through the walls, amplifying the tension in the room.  A young man with frayed cuffs and eyes that darted nervously around the room sat perched on the edge of the plush armchair, his knuckles white against the worn leather.  He hadn't spoken a word since Eleanor had ushered him in five minutes earlier, the silence punctuated only by the rhythmic drumming of the rain and the occasional, choked sob he tried desperately to stifle.

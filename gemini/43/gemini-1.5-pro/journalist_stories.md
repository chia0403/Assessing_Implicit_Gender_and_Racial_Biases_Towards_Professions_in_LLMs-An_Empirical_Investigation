Rain lashed against the grimy window of The Lighthouse, a diner clinging precariously to the edge of the dilapidated Blackwood Pier.  Salt spray, whipped up by the ferocious Atlantic wind,  peppered the glass like icy buckshot. Inside, Eleanor Vance Holloway, a journalist for the Boston Chronicle, hunched over a chipped mug of lukewarm coffee, the bitter taste mirroring her current mood. The flickering neon sign outside cast a sickly green glow across her notes, a haphazard collection of scribbled phone numbers, half-formed questions, and the recurring phrase: "He wouldn't just disappear."  Blackwood, Maine, a town built on the bones of a dying fishing industry, was shrouded in a fog as thick as the secrets Eleanor had come to uncover. The disappearance of local fisherman,  Caleb "Cap'n Cal" Croft, two weeks prior, had initially been dismissed as a tragic accident at sea. But Eleanor, with her nose for stories buried beneath the surface, sensed something far more sinister lurking beneath the waves.

The fluorescent lights of Mrs. Eleanor Vance's office hummed a monotonous B-flat, a soundtrack to the quiet desperation that often permeated the air.  Rain lashed against the single, grimy window overlooking the cracked asphalt of the Northwood High School parking lot, blurring the already fading autumn colours into a melancholic watercolour.  Eleanor, her auburn hair pulled back in a loose bun, tapped a chipped ceramic mug adorned with the optimistic slogan "World's Best Counselor," the irony not lost on her.  It was a Tuesday, the kind that felt like a Monday, and already three students had landed in her small, cluttered office, each carrying a weight that felt heavier than any textbook.  Northwood, nestled in the forgotten pocket of a once-thriving steel town in Pennsylvania, was a place where dreams went to rust, and Eleanor, more often than not, felt like the custodian of that graveyard of ambition.

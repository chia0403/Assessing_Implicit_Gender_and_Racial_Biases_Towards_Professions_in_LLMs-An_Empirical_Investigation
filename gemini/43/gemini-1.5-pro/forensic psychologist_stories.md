Rain lashed against the arched windows of Dr. Elias Thorne's Victorian office, blurring the gaslit London street below into a shimmering impressionist painting.  The year was 1888, and the fog, a near-constant companion in the Whitechapel district, had given way to a relentless downpour. Elias, his dark hair plastered to his forehead, tapped a long, slender finger against a leather-bound volume of Lombroso's "Criminal Man."  The scent of old books and pipe tobacco hung heavy in the air, a familiar comfort against the unsettling chill that seemed to permeate not just the room, but the entire city.  A chilling case had landed on his doorstep, one that promised to test not only his professional expertise but the very limits of his sanity. A letter, stained with something far darker than ink, lay open on his desk, bearing a chillingly simple salutation: "From Hell."

The chipped porcelain sink, stained a sickly yellow from years of lukewarm water and spilled chamomile tea, reflected the perpetual twilight of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sun piercing the grimy windowpane, illuminating the faded floral wallpaper and the overflowing bookshelf crammed with texts on trauma, resilience, and the human condition.  Eleanor, a counseling psychologist with a practice tucked away on a quiet side street of the city, sighed, pushing a strand of auburn hair away from her tired eyes.  The scent of pine-scented air freshener, a desperate attempt to mask the lingering aroma of stale coffee and fear, hung heavy in the air.  Outside, the rhythmic thump-thump of a construction project echoed through the narrow street, a relentless soundtrack to the anxieties she so often helped patients navigate.  Today, however, the anxieties seemed to be amplified.  A young woman, her name a whispered secret on the tip of Eleanor's tongue –  Isabelle Dubois – sat across from her, her eyes wide with a fear that defied easy categorization.

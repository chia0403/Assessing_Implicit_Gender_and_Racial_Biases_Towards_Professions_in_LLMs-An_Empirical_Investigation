The biting November wind howled through the gaps in the boarded-up windows of the dilapidated theatre, rattling the dust motes dancing in the single shaft of afternoon sun that pierced the gloom.  Cobwebs, thick as shrouds, draped from the rusted iron beams that held up the crumbling ceiling, whispering secrets only the wind could understand.  This was the "Grand Majestic," once a beacon of glittering Broadway shows, now a mausoleum of forgotten dreams.  Within its decaying grandeur, Amelia Nightingale, a theatrical director with eyes the colour of storm clouds and a spirit as fiery as a thousand stage lights, paced the vast, empty space.  The air, thick with the scent of mildew and forgotten popcorn, hung heavy with the weight of a thousand unfulfilled promises.  Amelia, her dark hair pulled back in a severe bun, ran a hand through her already rumpled silk dress, a tremor in her usually steady hand.  Tonight, the fate of her latest play, "Shadows of the Past," hung in the balance.

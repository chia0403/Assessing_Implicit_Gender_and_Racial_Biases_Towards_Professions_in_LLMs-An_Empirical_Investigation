The humid air hung thick and heavy in the salon, a suffocating blanket woven from the scent of jasmine shampoo and the faint, metallic tang of blood.  Sunlight, fractured and filtered through the dusty, floral-patterned blinds, cast long shadows across the polished floors.  A single, brazenly-coloured hibiscus flower, almost mocking in its vibrancy, sat precariously on the mahogany counter.  This was the sanctuary, and the crucible, of  Anya Petrova, cosmetologist extraordinaire.  Anya, with her perpetually-windswept auburn hair and eyes that held the deep, knowing glint of someone who had seen far too much behind the brightly-lit curtain of beauty, was currently engaged in a meticulous, almost surgical, application of henna. Her client, a vibrant woman named Zara with skin the colour of polished mahogany and eyes that sparkled like a summer sky, was silently enduring the process with an uncharacteristic quietude. This quiet was unnerving.

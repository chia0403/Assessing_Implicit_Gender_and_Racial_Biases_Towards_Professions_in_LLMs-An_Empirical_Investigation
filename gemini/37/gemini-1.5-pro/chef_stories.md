The biting November wind whipped down Rue de Seine, swirling fallen chestnut leaves around the ankles of Antoine Dubois as he hurried towards his restaurant.  "Le Papillon Bleu," nestled between a bustling boulangerie and a dusty antique shop, glowed warmly, a beacon against the encroaching Parisian twilight.  Inside, the air hung thick with the aroma of simmering bouillabaisse and the crisp bite of freshly baked bread.  Antoine, his breath puffing white clouds in the frigid air, paused at the doorway, savoring the familiar symphony of clanging pots, the rhythmic thwack of a chef's knife, and the low murmur of contented diners.  Tonight was the night.  After years of toiling in the shadows of other chefs,  Antoine was finally unveiling his own creation, a seven-course tasting menu designed to showcase the bounty of the autumn harvest. His heart hammered against his ribs, a mixture of anticipation and terror.  Everything rested on this.

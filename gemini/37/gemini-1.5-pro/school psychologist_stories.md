The biting November wind whipped fallen leaves into frenzied dances across the cracked asphalt of the Northwood High School parking lot. Dr. Eleanor Vance, school psychologist, pulled her thick wool coat tighter around herself, the collar scratching against her cheek.  Grey clouds scudded low, promising snow, and the skeletal branches of the elm trees lining the lot rattled like anxious bones.  Northwood was a small town nestled in the foothills of the Berkshires, a picture postcard New England scene – charming in the summer, starkly beautiful in the fall, and downright oppressive in the grip of winter, like now.  Eleanor sighed, the puff of white momentarily obscuring the grim façade of the school. This morning, the oppressive feeling extended beyond the weather. A palpable tension hung in the air, a static charge she couldn't quite place, but one that prickled the hairs on the back of her neck. It had been building for weeks, ever since the disappearance of Sarah Jenkins, a quiet, artistic student Eleanor had been seeing for mild anxiety.

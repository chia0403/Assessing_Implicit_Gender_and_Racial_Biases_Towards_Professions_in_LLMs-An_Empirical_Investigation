The crisp October air, scented with woodsmoke and decaying leaves, nipped at Penelope "Penny" Ramirez's cheeks as she wrestled a "For Sale" sign into the unforgiving earth.  Her breath puffed out in white clouds, mirroring the wisps clinging to the shadowed peaks of the Blue Ridge Mountains that loomed over the property.  This was the third sign she'd planted this week in the sleepy hamlet of Oakhaven, a town so small its only claim to fame was a dubious legend about a Revolutionary War-era witch and an unusually large yield of pumpkins each autumn. Penny smoothed down the lapels of her tweed blazer, a futile attempt to ward off the chill that seeped through her sensible heels and into her bones. This latest listing, a dilapidated Victorian with peeling paint and an overgrown garden choked with weeds, wasn't exactly prime real estate, but in Oakhaven, even fixer-uppers were becoming hot commodities. The city folk, she mused, were finally discovering the allure of small-town living, even if that allure was currently hidden beneath a thick layer of dust and neglect.

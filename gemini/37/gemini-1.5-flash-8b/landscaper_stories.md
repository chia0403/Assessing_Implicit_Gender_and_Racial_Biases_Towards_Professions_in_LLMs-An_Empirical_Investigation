The biting wind whipped Amelia "Poppy" Peterson's crimson scarf around her neck, stinging her cheeks like a thousand tiny needles.  Dust devils danced across the cracked earth of the parched High Meadow, swirling the remnants of yesterday's irrigation spray into a chaotic ballet.  Poppy, a landscaper with sun-weathered hands and eyes that held the unwavering gaze of a seasoned hawk, surveyed the scene with a sigh that carried the weight of a hundred wilting sunflowers.  High Meadow, usually a vibrant tapestry of emerald green and cheerful blooms, lay gasping for breath under the relentless July sun.  Her small, beat-up pickup truck, affectionately nicknamed "The Rusty Rooster," sat parked nearby, a testament to years of hard work and a surprising amount of stubborn loyalty.  The air, thick with the scent of dry earth and the faint metallic tang of the drought-stricken irrigation system, was heavy with the unspoken anxiety of the season.  This year, even the toughest desert blooms were struggling.

The chipped porcelain of the antique teacup trembled in Amelia Nightingale's hand, mirroring the tremor in her own, as she surveyed the meticulously arranged, yet strangely unsettling, floral arrangement.  It was perched atop a mahogany side table in the opulent, yet somehow claustrophobic, drawing-room of Lord Ashworth's estate, Blackwood Manor.  Rain hammered against the leaded-glass windows, a relentless drumming that echoed the frantic rhythm of Amelia's heart. Blackwood Manor, a sprawling gothic edifice perched atop a windswept moor, was usually a symphony of quiet elegance; tonight, however, it felt like a stage set for a drama she hadn't been invited to.  The air, thick with the scent of beeswax and impending doom, hung heavy in the room, a stark contrast to the delicate perfume of jasmine that usually filled the air during the evening soirees.  Amelia, the concierge of Blackwood Manor, was certain something was terribly amiss.

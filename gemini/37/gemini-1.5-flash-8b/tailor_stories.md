The clatter of needles against fabric, a symphony of rhythmic clicks and snips, filled the tiny workshop nestled behind the bustling market square.  Sunlight, fractured by the cobblestones, painted fleeting patterns across the worn wooden floor.  Elias Thorne, Tailor to the Royal Court, hunched over a meticulously measured length of crimson silk, his brow furrowed in concentration.  Dust motes danced in the beam of light, illuminating the intricate embroidery he was meticulously stitching. The air, thick with the scent of beeswax and linseed oil, buzzed with the low murmur of the market traders hawking their wares – spices, herbs, and freshly baked bread – and the occasional horse-drawn carriage rumbling past.  Elias, though immersed in his craft, was acutely aware of the constant hum of the city.  Today, however, that hum seemed to carry a deeper, more anxious resonance.  Rumours of the King’s ill health had spread like wildfire, and the once cheerful bustle now felt brittle, like a tightly wound spring about to snap.

The chipped paint of the studio door, a muted ochre against the slate grey of the old brick building, seemed to sigh under the weight of Eleanor Vance's hesitant hand.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sun slicing through the grime-streaked window, illuminating a canvas propped precariously on an easel.  It depicted a swirling vortex of blues and greys, a tempestuous sea that somehow felt both terrifying and strangely comforting.  Eleanor, a painter of the melancholic and the majestic, stood before it, her brow furrowed in contemplation.  The air in the cramped, windowless space hung thick with the scent of linseed oil and stale coffee.  A half-eaten bowl of fruit sat on the cluttered workbench, its remnants a testament to the artist's intermittent meals. This particular studio, tucked away on a side street of the historic district of Port Haven, was her sanctuary, her battleground, her canvas, all rolled into one.  It had been her grandfather's, and now, it was hers.  Every creak of the floorboards, every rustle of paper, whispered tales of countless hours spent lost in the pursuit of capturing the unseen.

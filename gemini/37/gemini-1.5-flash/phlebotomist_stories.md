The fluorescent lights of the St. Jude's Medical Center phlebotomy lab hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic *thump-thump-thump* of Amelia Jean Holloway's own pulse.  Amelia, ever the meticulous professional, meticulously organized her trays of needles – 21-gauge, 23-gauge, butterfly needles for the particularly delicate veins – each capped and gleaming under the sterile light. The air, thick with the antiseptic scent of alcohol swabs and the faint, metallic tang of blood, was familiar and strangely comforting.  Outside, the relentless Chicago winter raged, a blizzard of swirling snow lashing against the hospital windows, a stark contrast to the controlled calm of her small, windowless world.  But today, the calm was a fragile thing, threatening to unravel under the weight of an unsettling premonition.

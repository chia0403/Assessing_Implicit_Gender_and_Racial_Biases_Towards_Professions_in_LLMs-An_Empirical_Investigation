The stale scent of antiseptic and simmering anxiety clung to the air in Dr. Evelyn Reed’s office like a persistent cough.  Rain lashed against the panoramic windows overlooking the sprawling cityscape of Seattle, a grey, melancholic mirror reflecting the mood within.  The city, usually vibrant and alive with a frenetic energy, was muted tonight, swallowed by the relentless downpour.  Evelyn, a clinical psychologist with a reputation for her sharp intuition and even sharper wit, sat behind her meticulously organized desk, the only sound the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of her pen against a notepad – a counterpoint to the storm raging outside.  The flickering candlelight on her mahogany desk cast long, dancing shadows, illuminating the worn leather of her chair and the half-empty cup of chamomile tea growing cold beside it.  Her patient, a young woman named Sarah, hadn't yet arrived, but the weight of her impending session already pressed down, a familiar, unsettling pressure Evelyn knew all too well.

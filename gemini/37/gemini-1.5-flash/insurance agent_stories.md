The humid Louisiana air hung heavy, thick with the scent of honeysuckle and decay.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless symphony, their song a counterpoint to the rhythmic creak of Amelia Beatrice Dubois’s porch swing.  Amelia, a woman whose smile lines spoke of years spent navigating the tangled web of life insurance policies, sat sipping sweet tea, the ice clinking softly against the glass.  The sprawling oaks that shaded her modest Creole cottage in the sleepy town of Lafayette were draped in Spanish moss, their ancient limbs gnarled like arthritic fingers.  This was her kingdom, a place where the slow, deliberate pace of life was only occasionally interrupted by the urgent calls of clients facing unforeseen circumstances – or, as she often suspected, circumstances they’d desperately hoped to avoid.  Tonight, however, the silence was unnerving.  Even the usual nocturnal chorus of bullfrogs seemed muted, a portent of something…different.

The chipped Formica tabletop vibrated with the low thrum of a hundred conversations, a discordant symphony of clinking cutlery and strained laughter.  The air in Rosie’s Diner hung thick with the scent of frying bacon, stale coffee, and something vaguely floral – probably the desperate attempt of a wilting carnation in a cracked vase to assert itself against the greasy dominance of everything else.  Rain lashed against the windowpanes, mirroring the relentless rhythm of Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s steps as she weaved expertly through the crowded aisles, a tray laden precariously with coffee cups, a plate of blueberry pancakes threatening to topple, and the faint but persistent scent of burnt sugar clinging to her apron.  Amelia, or Millie as most of the regulars called her, had seen it all in her five years serving at Rosie's – from quiet morning regulars nursing their anxieties over lukewarm coffee to boisterous lunch crowds fueled by gossip and greasy spoons.  But tonight felt… different.  A prickle of unease, sharper than the usual Friday night rush, settled on her skin.

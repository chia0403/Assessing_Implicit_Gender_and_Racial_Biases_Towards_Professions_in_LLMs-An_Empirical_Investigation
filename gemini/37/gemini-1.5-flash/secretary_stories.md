The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s fingers, a small comfort against the bone-chilling November wind whistling through the gaps in the office’s ancient, drafty windows.  Rain lashed against the grime-coated panes of the building, mirroring the tempest brewing inside Amelia herself.  She stared out at the grey, swirling expanse of London, the city’s relentless pulse a muffled thrum against the otherwise oppressive silence of Blackwood & Sons, Solicitors.  The only sound besides the storm was the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of her own fingers against the worn mahogany desk, a staccato counterpoint to the ticking grandfather clock in the corner, its pendulum swinging with the weary patience of a seasoned bureaucrat.  The air hung thick with the scent of old paper, dust, and desperation – a scent Amelia had grown intimately familiar with during her five years as senior secretary to the notoriously eccentric Mr. Silas Blackwood.

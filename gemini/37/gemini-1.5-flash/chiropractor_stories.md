The salt spray stung Dr. Eleanor Vance’s face as she wrestled the stubborn sailboat’s mainsail.  The late afternoon sun, a bruised plum in the rapidly darkening sky, cast long shadows across the choppy waters of the Puget Sound.  Seagulls cried overhead, their mournful calls a counterpoint to the rhythmic creak of the aged wooden hull.  Eleanor, her normally pristine white chiropractic uniform replaced by weathered sailing clothes, gritted her teeth against the wind.  She wasn't used to this much physical exertion, not since she'd opened her practice in the charming, if slightly isolated, seaside village of Port Blossom three years ago.  The scent of brine and pine hung heavy in the air, a familiar perfume that usually soothed her; tonight, however, it only served to heighten the growing unease gnawing at her.  Something felt wrong, profoundly wrong, a disquiet that resonated deeper than the ache in her overworked muscles.

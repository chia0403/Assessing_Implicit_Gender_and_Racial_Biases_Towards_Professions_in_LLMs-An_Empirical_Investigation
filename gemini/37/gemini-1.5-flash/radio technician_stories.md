The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled the recalcitrant antenna cable.  The wind, a howling banshee off the churning grey expanse of the North Sea, threatened to rip the whole contraption – his pride and joy, the Kestrel weather station – from the craggy clifftop.  Below, the village of Port Blossom huddled like a clutch of shivering sheep, its lights barely visible through the driving rain.  Elias, his thick wool cap pulled low, grunted with exertion, his breath misting in the frigid air.  The Kestrel, a cobbled-together masterpiece of scavenged parts and sheer ingenuity, was his lifeline, his connection to the outside world, and tonight, it was threatening to fail him just as a particularly nasty squall was brewing. The air crackled with static, a symphony of impending doom echoing both the weather and the precarious state of his equipment.

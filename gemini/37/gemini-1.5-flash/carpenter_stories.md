The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he wrestled the stubborn oak plank into place.  The air, thick with the smell of brine and sawdust, whipped around him, carrying the cries of gulls overhead. Below, the churning grey expanse of the North Sea gnawed at the craggy cliffs of Cornwall, a constant, restless backdrop to his solitary work.  Elias, a man built like an oak himself, with hands as gnarled and seasoned as the wood he shaped, was perched precariously on scaffolding clinging to the sheer cliff face, repairing a section of the ancient, wind-battered lighthouse.  The rhythmic clang of his hammer, a counterpoint to the ceaseless roar of the ocean, was the only sound for miles.

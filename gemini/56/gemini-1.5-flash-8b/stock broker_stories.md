The fluorescent lights of the trading floor hummed a relentless, metallic symphony, a counterpoint to the frantic clatter of keyboards and the sharp crackle of stock tickers.  Rain hammered against the enormous windows of the Merrill Lynch building, a relentless percussion mirroring the volatile fluctuations of the Dow Jones.  Elias Thorne, a stockbroker with sharp, almost predatory eyes and a perpetual furrow in his brow, stared intently at the swirling data on his computer screen.  His tailored suit, though immaculate, felt clammy against his skin.  The air, thick with the scent of stale coffee and anxiety, felt charged with a nervous energy that crackled in the silence between trades.  Outside, the city, usually a dazzling tapestry of lights, felt muted and menacing under the relentless downpour.  Elias was wrestling with a particularly stubborn bear market, a personal Everest of losses threatening to bury his career.

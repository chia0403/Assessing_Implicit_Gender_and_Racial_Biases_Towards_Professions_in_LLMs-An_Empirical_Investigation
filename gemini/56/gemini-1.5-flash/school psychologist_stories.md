The biting November wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance’s auburn hair across her face as she hurried across the sprawling, rain-slicked quadrangle of Northwood High.  Fallen leaves, the color of bruised plums and decaying pennies, clung stubbornly to the cracked paving stones.  Gothic gargoyles leered from the eaves of the imposing, century-old school building, their stone faces seeming to mirror the grim determination etched on Eleanor’s own.  The air hung heavy with the scent of damp earth and woodsmoke, a stark contrast to the sterile, antiseptic smell of the brightly-lit counseling office she was desperately trying to reach before the next student arrived – a student whose escalating anxiety had prompted Eleanor's frantic dash across the campus in the first place.  Northwood High, a monument to both academic excellence and simmering teenage turmoil, felt particularly ominous under the bruised twilight sky.

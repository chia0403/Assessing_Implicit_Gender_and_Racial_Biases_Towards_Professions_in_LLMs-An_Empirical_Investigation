The humid Charleston air hung heavy, thick with the scent of jasmine and impending rain.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, almost frantic soundtrack to the simmering heat clinging to the cobblestone streets of the French Quarter.  Judge Abigail Evangeline Hayes, her crisp white linen suit already damp with perspiration, adjusted the collar of her blouse, a small, almost imperceptible sigh escaping her lips.  The wrought-iron balcony of her antebellum home creaked a mournful counterpoint to the insistent chirping, a sound as familiar to her as the rhythm of her own heartbeat.  Tonight, however, the usual Southern charm felt suffocating, a thick veil draped over a city holding its breath.  The case of *State v. Beaumont* had cast a long, chilling shadow over everything, and Abigail knew, with a certainty that settled deep in her bones, that this wouldn't be a night for restful sleep.

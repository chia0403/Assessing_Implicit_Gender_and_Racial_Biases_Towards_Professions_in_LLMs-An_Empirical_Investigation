The biting November wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s auburn hair across her face as she wrestled the heavy easel into the crumbling stone shed.  Salt spray, thick and insistent, stung her eyes, blurring the already fading light. The shed, perched precariously on the cliffs overlooking the churning grey expanse of the Atlantic, groaned a mournful protest under the onslaught of the wind.  Inside, the air hung thick with the scent of turpentine and drying linseed oil, a familiar perfume that clung to Amelia like a second skin, a testament to years spent battling the elements and capturing their raw beauty on canvas.  The battered, wooden floorboards were strewn with paint tubes, brushes, and crumpled sketches – a chaotic landscape mirroring the tempestuous sea outside.  Tonight, however, Amelia wasn't battling the elements to capture them; tonight, she was battling something far more insidious – a creative block so profound it threatened to swallow her whole.

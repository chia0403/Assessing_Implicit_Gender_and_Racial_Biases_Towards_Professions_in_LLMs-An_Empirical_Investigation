The biting November wind whipped Amelia Katherine Blackwood's hair across her face, stinging her cheeks a fiery red that mirrored the dying embers of the sunset.  She tugged her worn leather jacket tighter, the scent of salt and decaying seaweed clinging to it like a second skin.  Amelia, or Amy as most people called her, shivered, not entirely from the cold.  The desolate beauty of the Cornish coastline, usually a source of endless inspiration, felt tonight like a vast, unforgiving canvas reflecting the turmoil in her own soul.  The jagged cliffs, their faces etched by centuries of relentless storms, loomed over her like silent judges, the crashing waves a constant, mournful dirge.  She stood perched precariously on a rocky outcrop, her trusty Leica M6 hanging heavy around her neck, its cold metal a stark contrast to the warmth of her gloved hands.  The last sliver of sun had dipped below the horizon, painting the sky in bruised purples and angry oranges, a fitting backdrop for the story she was about to capture.

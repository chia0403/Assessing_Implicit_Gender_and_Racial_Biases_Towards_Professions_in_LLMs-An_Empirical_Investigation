The chipped paint of Dr. Eleanor Vance's office door seemed to mirror the cracks appearing in her carefully constructed composure.  Rain lashed against the tall, gothic windows of Blackwood Manor, a rambling Victorian structure that served as both her home and practice.  The wind howled a mournful symphony, rattling the ancient panes and echoing the unease that had settled deep in Eleanor’s bones over the past few weeks.  Inside, the air hung heavy with the scent of old books and damp earth, a peculiar perfume that always seemed to amplify the weight of her patients' anxieties.  Tonight, however, the air felt charged with something more – a palpable sense of dread that clung to the shadowed corners of her study like clinging ivy.  The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows, playing tricks on her eyes and fueling the growing sense of unease that had her reaching for the comforting weight of her ever-present pipe.

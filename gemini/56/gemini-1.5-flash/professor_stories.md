Professor Augustine Blackwood adjusted his spectacles, the faint scent of pipe tobacco clinging to the worn tweed of his jacket.  Rain lashed against the gothic mullioned windows of his study, a relentless drumming that echoed the persistent gnawing anxiety in his chest.  The wind howled like a banshee, rattling the ancient oak frame of the window, a sound that, on any other night, might have been comforting, a familiar lullaby of the storm-battered Scottish Highlands. But tonight, the storm mirrored the tempest brewing within him.  The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the cluttered room, illuminating stacks of leather-bound books, precarious piles of research papers, and a half-empty decanter of amber liquid – a testament to the long, sleepless nights he’d endured. Outside, the unforgiving grey of the North Sea churned under a bruised, bruised sky.  The isolated croft, perched precariously on the edge of the windswept cliffs overlooking the churning ocean, felt more like a prison than a refuge.

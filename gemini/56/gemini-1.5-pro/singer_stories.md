The crimson velvet curtain felt heavy beneath Elara Nightingale's fingertips.  Dust motes, disturbed by the slight tremor in her hand, danced in the single shaft of moonlight that pierced the otherwise inky blackness backstage. The Grand Majestic Theatre in New Orleans, usually a cacophony of hurried footsteps and nervous chatter before a show, held its breath. Tonight, a humid Tuesday in July, the silence was thick enough to choke on.  It wasn't the usual pre-performance jitters that tightened Elara's chest; she'd sung on bigger stages, faced tougher crowds. Tonight, the silence hummed with an expectancy that felt both ancient and electric, as if the very ghosts of jazz legends past held their spectral instruments poised, waiting to judge.

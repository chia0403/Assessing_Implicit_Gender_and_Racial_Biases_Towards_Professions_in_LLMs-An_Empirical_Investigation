The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic scratch of Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s pencil on mylar.  Dust motes, illuminated like tiny galaxies, danced in the pale light filtering through the grimy window overlooking the sprawling, rain-slicked cityscape of Birmingham, Alabama.  Outside, the November wind howled a mournful dirge, rattling the antiquated windowpanes in their worn frames.  Inside, Amelia, her brow furrowed in concentration, meticulously rendered the intricate details of a new highway overpass – a concrete behemoth she knew, with a sinking feeling, would soon scar the already fractured landscape.  The smell of stale coffee and graphite hung heavy in the air, a familiar aroma that permeated Amelia’s life as thoroughly as the faint scent of rain that now drifted in through the crack in the window frame.

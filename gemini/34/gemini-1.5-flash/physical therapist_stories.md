The salt spray stung Eleanor Vance’s face as she navigated the treacherous, seaweed-slicked rocks that guarded the cove.  The wind, a relentless, keening banshee, whipped her dark hair across her eyes, threatening to snatch away the battered clipboard clutched in her gloved hand.  Below, the churning grey expanse of the Atlantic roared its disapproval of the late October weather, a fitting soundtrack to the grim task ahead.  Eleanor, a physical therapist with hands as strong as they were gentle, was not here for a leisurely stroll.  She was here for Finnigan O’Malley, a fisherman whose stubborn refusal to heed warnings about the unpredictable sea had resulted in a broken leg and a profound case of bruised pride – injuries Eleanor was determined to mend, one excruciatingly slow step at a time.  The isolated fishing village of Port Blossom, nestled between a jagged coastline and a brooding mountain range, was as unforgiving as its inhabitants, but Eleanor Vance, a woman used to battling both stubborn bodies and stubborn spirits, was prepared to face whatever it threw at her.

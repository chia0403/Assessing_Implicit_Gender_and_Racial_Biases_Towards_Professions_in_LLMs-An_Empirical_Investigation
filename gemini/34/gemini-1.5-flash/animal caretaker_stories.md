The biting November wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s hair across her face as she wrestled the recalcitrant gate of the Nightjar Sanctuary open.  Rust groaned a mournful counterpoint to the gale, mirroring the persistent ache in her shoulder from yesterday's tussle with a particularly stubborn badger.  Nightjar, nestled deep within the whispering pines of the Oregon Coast Range, wasn’t your typical wildlife rehabilitation center. It was a sprawling, ramshackle collection of repurposed barns, makeshift enclosures, and surprisingly sophisticated medical facilities, all clinging precariously to the side of a steep, moss-covered bluff overlooking the churning Pacific.  The air smelled of damp earth, pine needles, and the faint, almost medicinal, scent of eucalyptus – a constant reminder of the fragile lives entrusted to her care. Tonight, the storm threatened to add another layer to that complex aroma: the salty tang of sea spray and the underlying, primal scent of fear.

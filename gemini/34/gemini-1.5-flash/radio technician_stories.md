The static crackled, a persistent, unwelcome companion to Elias Thorne's otherwise solitary evening.  Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof of the shack, a rhythmic percussion accompanying the hiss from the ancient shortwave radio perched precariously on a stack of worn textbooks.  Elias, his brow furrowed in concentration, adjusted a dial with the practiced ease of a surgeon.  The air hung thick with the scent of damp earth and ozone, a smell as familiar to him as the comforting weight of his headphones.  His workspace, a cramped, cluttered haven nestled deep within the whispering eucalyptus trees bordering the desolate expanse of the Nullarbor Plain, was a testament to years spent wrestling with the unpredictable whims of long-distance transmissions.  Outside, the only light came from the erratic flicker of distant lightning, illuminating the endless, star-dusted canvas of the Australian outback.

The biting November wind whipped Amelia Hernandez’s charcoal-grey coat around her legs as she surveyed the skeletal frame of the Zenith Tower.  Rain, a relentless drizzle that clung to everything like a second skin, plastered her dark hair to her forehead.  Amelia, a woman whose designs usually soared with graceful elegance, felt a prickle of unease.  The Zenith, a project she’d poured her heart and soul into for the past three years – a shimmering testament to sustainable architecture, a beacon of hope for the revitalized waterfront – felt…wrong.  It wasn't the usual post-construction anxieties; this was deeper, a disquieting dissonance that hummed beneath the clang of steel and the rhythmic thud of construction machinery. The city itself, a tapestry of grey stone and glittering glass usually vibrant even under a dreary sky, seemed muted, its usual energy sucked dry by the looming presence of the incomplete tower, casting long, skeletal shadows across the cobblestone streets below.

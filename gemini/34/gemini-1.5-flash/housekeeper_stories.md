The chipped porcelain sink wept a steady stream of lukewarm water, mirroring the persistent drizzle drumming against the leaded-glass windows of Blackwood Manor.  Dust motes, illuminated by the weak November sunlight, danced in the air like tiny, mournful spirits.  Eleanor Vance, her hands chapped and raw from years of scrubbing stubborn grime, squeezed the last drops of lemon-scented cleaner from the bottle.  Blackwood Manor, a gothic behemoth clinging precariously to the cliff edge overlooking the churning grey sea, was her domain, a kingdom of echoing halls and shadowed corners, where every creak and groan whispered tales of forgotten grandeur and unsettling secrets.  The air, thick with the scent of damp stone and decaying wood, held a chill that went deeper than the autumn winds.

The biting wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne's hair across her face, stinging her cheeks raw.  She tugged her worn, wool scarf tighter around her neck, the icy breath of the Alaskan winter a stark contrast to the warm, sterile environment she usually inhabited.  The small, isolated clinic of St. Elias, perched precariously on the edge of a frozen fjord, felt a million miles away from the bustling emergency room of Anchorage General where she'd spent the last five years honing her skills as a physician's assistant.  Tonight, however, the only sounds competing with the howling wind were the rhythmic crackle of the wood stove in the waiting room and the anxious whisper of a nearby snow machine engine – a sound that, in this remote corner of the world, could herald anything from a routine check-up to a life-threatening emergency.  The scent of pine and woodsmoke hung heavy in the air, a fragrant counterpoint to the antiseptic smell that stubbornly clung to Amelia’s coat.

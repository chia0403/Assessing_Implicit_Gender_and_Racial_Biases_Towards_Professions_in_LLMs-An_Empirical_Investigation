The biting November wind whipped across the skeletal frame of the new skyscraper, whistling a mournful tune through the exposed steel girders.  Dust, the fine grey powder of a thousand pulverized bricks, clung to everything – to Elias Thorne’s thick work boots, to the chapped skin of his hands, even to the inside of his eyelids.  Elias, a man whose calloused fingers had tightened more bolts than he could count, squinted against the relentless wind, his breath clouding the frigid air. He gripped his wrench tighter, the cold steel a familiar comfort against the unnerving sway of the structure hundreds of feet above the city.  Below, the cityscape of Chicago spread out like a meticulously crafted model, the tiny cars crawling along the streets like ants.  But up here, on the dizzying precipice of unfinished ambition, there was only the wind, the steel, and the ever-present threat of the unforgiving drop.

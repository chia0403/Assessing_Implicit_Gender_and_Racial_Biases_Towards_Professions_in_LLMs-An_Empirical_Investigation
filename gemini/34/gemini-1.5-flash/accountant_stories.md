The fluorescent lights of Miller & Sons hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of Amelia Grace Hawthorne's fingers on her keyboard.  Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of her 27th-floor office, blurring the already indistinct cityscape of Chicago into a grey, impressionistic smear.  Amelia, a meticulous accountant with a penchant for perfectly aligned spreadsheets and a quiet disdain for spontaneity, found a perverse comfort in the storm's relentless assault.  It mirrored the relentless pressure she felt, the looming deadline for the quarterly reports for PetroTech, a client whose opacity was only surpassed by their sheer, intimidating wealth.  Tonight, however, the usual hum of anxiety was punctuated by something else: a prickling unease, a subtle discordance in the usually predictable rhythm of her workday.  Something was wrong.  Very, very wrong.

The scent of aged paper and leather hung heavy in the air, a comforting blanket against the relentless November rain lashing against the tall, arched windows of the Blackwood Memorial Library.  Rainwater, streaked with grime, snaked down the panes, blurring the already fading gaslight glow of the cobbled street outside.  Inside, however, the atmosphere was a haven of quiet industry.  Dust motes, illuminated by the warm, yellow light, danced in the beams slicing through the vast, high-ceilinged reading room.  Eleanor Vance, a woman whose life had become as intricately cataloged as the library’s own collection, sat at her worn oak desk, the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of her fingers against the keys of her ancient typewriter a counterpoint to the hushed whispers of other patrons.  Tonight, however, the usual comforting silence was punctuated by an unsettling tremor – a vibration that resonated deep within the very bones of the old building.

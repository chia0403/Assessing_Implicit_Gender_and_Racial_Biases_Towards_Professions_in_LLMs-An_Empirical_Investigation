The rhythmic clatter of typewriter keys filled the small, stuffy office, punctuated by the occasional *ding* of the carriage return.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight struggling to pierce the grimy windowpane, illuminating a framed print of kittens playing with a ball of yarn.  Eleanor Vance, her spectacles perched precariously on her nose, chewed on her lower lip as she transcribed a letter from Mr. Henderson, the notoriously verbose president of Henderson's Haberdashery.  Outside, the cacophony of 1940s New York City – car horns, newsboys’ shouts, and the rumble of the elevated train – faded to a dull hum behind the thick, frosted glass. The air hung thick with the scent of stale cigarette smoke and cheap mimeograph ink.  Eleanor sighed, adjusting the collar of her sensible beige blouse.  Little did she know, this seemingly ordinary Tuesday morning was about to take a most extraordinary turn.

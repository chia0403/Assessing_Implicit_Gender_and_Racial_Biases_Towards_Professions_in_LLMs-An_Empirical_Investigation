The flickering fluorescent lights of the Quick Stop hummed a monotonous tune, punctuated by the rhythmic beeping of the ancient cash register.  Rain lashed against the grimy windows, blurring the neon glow of the "Open 24 Hours" sign and painting the parking lot a slick, oily black.  Inside, the air hung thick with the commingled scents of stale coffee, day-old hot dogs, and the vaguely chemical aroma of cleaning solution.  Eleanor Vance, her name tag slightly askew on her faded blue uniform shirt, chewed thoughtfully on the end of a ballpoint pen, staring out at the desolate stretch of highway.  It was 3:17 am, the deadest hour of a dead-end shift, in a town that felt like it had given up the ghost long ago.

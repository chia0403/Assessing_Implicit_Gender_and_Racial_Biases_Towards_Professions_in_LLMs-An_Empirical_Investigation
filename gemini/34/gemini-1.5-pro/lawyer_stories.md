Rain lashed against the leaded windows of Cassandra Ainsworth’s office, mimicking the tempest brewing inside her. The mahogany desk, usually gleaming under the soft glow of a Tiffany lamp, was now a slick, reflecting surface for the anxious flickering of neon signs from the street below.  Cassandra, her full name Cassandra Marie Ainsworth, Esquire, tapped a blood-red nail against a thick file emblazoned with the words “Pendleton Estate.”  Her office, perched on the fifteenth floor of a pre-war building in Chicago's Loop, offered a breathtaking panorama of the city, but tonight, all she saw was the swirling grey of the storm, a mirror of the complex legal battle she was about to wage.  The Pendleton Estate case, a labyrinthine tangle of contested wills, hidden beneficiaries, and whispers of foul play, had landed on her desk only a week ago, and already it felt like a lead weight in her stomach.

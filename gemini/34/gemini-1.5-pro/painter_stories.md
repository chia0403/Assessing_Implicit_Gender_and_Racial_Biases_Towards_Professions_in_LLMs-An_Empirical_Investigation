The salt spray stung Eliza Jane Cadwallader's face as she wrestled her easel against the relentless Cornish wind.  Perched precariously on a cliff overlooking the churning turquoise waters of Nanjizal Bay, Eliza fought a losing battle against the elements, her auburn braids whipping around her like seaweed caught in a tide.  Around her, jagged granite cliffs, scarred black by centuries of storms, clawed at the bruised sky.  The air, thick with the scent of brine and wildflowers, vibrated with the ceaseless roar of the Atlantic.  Land's End, the furthest westerly point of mainland England, lay just a few miles south, a brooding sentinel against the vast expanse of the ocean. It was a wild, untamed landscape, and precisely the kind of place Eliza Jane Cadwallader felt most at home.  She had come seeking inspiration, a spark to reignite the creative fire that had lately dwindled to embers.

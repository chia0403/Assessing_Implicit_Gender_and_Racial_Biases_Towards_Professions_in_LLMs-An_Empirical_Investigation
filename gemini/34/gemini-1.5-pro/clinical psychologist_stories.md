Rain lashed against the mullioned windows of Dr. Elias Thorne's office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside his newest patient. The room, usually a sanctuary of calm with its muted earth tones and carefully chosen abstract art, felt charged with a nervous energy.  Located on the fifth floor of a converted brownstone on a quiet, tree-lined street in Boston's Back Bay, the practice of Dr. Elias Thorne, Ph.D., specialized in trauma and dissociative disorders. Outside, the late October wind howled down Commonwealth Avenue, scattering crimson and gold leaves across the wet pavement. Inside, the only sound besides the drumming rain was the rhythmic click of the antique clock on the mantelpiece, a steady pulse against the silence that stretched between therapist and patient.  Dr. Thorne, a man of quiet intensity with steel-grey eyes that held both empathy and a sharp intellect, leaned forward in his wingback chair, his hands steepled thoughtfully. He was waiting.

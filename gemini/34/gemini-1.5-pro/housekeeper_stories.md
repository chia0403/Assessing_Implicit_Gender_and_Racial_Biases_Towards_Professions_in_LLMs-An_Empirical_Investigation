The biting November wind whipped around Agnes Periwinkle, stinging her cheeks as she wrestled Mrs. Cavendish's wrought-iron gate open.  Frost crunched under her sensible boots, the sound swallowed by the rustle of withered leaves skittering across the flagstone path.  Even at this early hour, the Cavendish manor loomed imposingly against the steel-grey sky.  Its ivy-choked facade whispered secrets of generations past, secrets Agnes, in her five years of service, had only glimpsed through dusty attic windows and half-heard whispers from behind closed mahogany doors. The sprawling estate, nestled deep in the Shropshire countryside, felt miles away from the rest of the world, a self-contained universe of polished silver, hushed corridors, and the ever-present scent of beeswax and woodsmoke. Today, however, something felt different.  A disquiet hung in the air, heavier than the morning mist clinging to the manicured lawns, a premonition that Agnes couldn't shake.

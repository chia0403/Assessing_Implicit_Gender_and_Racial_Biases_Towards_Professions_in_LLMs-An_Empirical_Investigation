The biting wind whipped off the North Sea, carrying the scent of salt and something faintly metallic.  Dr. Elias Thorne, his breath misting in the frigid air, pulled his threadbare coat tighter around him as he trudged up the steep, cobbled street. The gas lamps of Port Blossom, a small fishing town nestled precariously between the unforgiving sea and the looming cliffs of the Yorkshire coast, flickered feebly against the encroaching darkness of a November evening in 1888.  The town, usually bustling with activity even at this late hour, was eerily quiet, the only sound the mournful cry of gulls overhead and the rhythmic crash of waves against the harbor wall.  A single light shone from the window of his surgery, a beacon in the growing gloom, promising warmth and a respite from the unsettling stillness that had fallen over Port Blossom.  Elias knew, with a chilling certainty, that this stillness was a prelude to something far worse.

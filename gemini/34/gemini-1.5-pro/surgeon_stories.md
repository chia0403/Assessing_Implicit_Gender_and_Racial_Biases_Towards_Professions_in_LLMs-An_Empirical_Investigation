The biting November wind whipped around Dr. Elias Thorne as he hurried across the cobbled courtyard of St. Jude's Hospital, the gothic arches looming above him like skeletal fingers against the bruised twilight sky.  Gargoyles, slick with rain, leered down from the eaves, their stone faces mirroring the grim set of Elias's own.  St. Jude's, nestled in the heart of Edinburgh's Old Town, was a place of stark contrasts: cutting-edge medical technology humming within centuries-old walls, the constant battle between life and death played out in hushed corridors that echoed with the whispers of history.  Tonight, the battle felt particularly fierce.  A rare and aggressive strain of meningitis was sweeping through the city, and St. Jude's, renowned for its infectious diseases department, had become the epicentre of the struggle. Elias, his breath misting in the frigid air, pushed open the heavy oak doors and stepped into the sterile, brightly lit world within, the scent of antiseptic sharp in his nostrils. He knew this night would test him like no other.

The biting wind, a relentless razor, sliced through the skeletal branches of the ancient pines, whipping Amelia Blackwood's flannel shirt against her skin.  Sunlight, fractured and weak, struggled to pierce the dense canopy of the Whispering Woods, painting the forest floor in a mosaic of emerald and shadow.  Amelia, calloused hands gripping the worn leather grip of her axe, stood at the edge of a clearing, the air thick with the scent of pine needles and damp earth.  This, the heart of the Blackwood timberlands, was her domain, a place she knew as intimately as the lines etched on her own weathered palms.  For twenty years, Amelia Blackwood had carved a life from the very heart of these woods, her life as intertwined with the rhythm of the forest as the roots of the ancient trees.  Her task today was to fell the last remnants of the "Serpent's Spine," a particularly stubborn stand of pines, infamous for their twisted, gnarled forms.

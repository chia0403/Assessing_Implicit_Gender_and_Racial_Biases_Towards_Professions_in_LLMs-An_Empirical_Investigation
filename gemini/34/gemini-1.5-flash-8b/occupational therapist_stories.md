The chipped porcelain sink, stained a perpetual beige from years of lukewarm water and unwashed anxieties, reflected Amelia Finch's weary face.  Sunlight, fractured by the grime-streaked windowpane, cast long, dancing shadows across the small, cramped kitchen of her terraced house in the grey heart of Bristol.  Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof, a relentless percussion echoing the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of her own frustration.  Amelia, Occupational Therapist, stared at the overflowing recycling bin, a miniature mountain of discarded hopes and half-finished projects, a tangible representation of the overwhelming weight of her day.  She was a woman who believed in the power of hands and minds to reshape, to reclaim, but today, the only tangible change she felt was the gnawing emptiness in her stomach.  Outside, the city stirred, a symphony of sirens and distant traffic, a soundtrack to her own inner turmoil.

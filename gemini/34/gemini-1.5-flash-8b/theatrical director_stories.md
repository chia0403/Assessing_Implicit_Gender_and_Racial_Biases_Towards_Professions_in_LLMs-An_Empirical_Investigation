The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the cobblestone alley, illuminating the chipped paint of the "Royal Majestic Theatre" in a ghostly glow.  Rain, a relentless curtain of grey, hammered against the corrugated iron roof, mirroring the storm brewing within Amelia "Amie" Nightingale, the theatre's director.  It was opening night of "The Tempest," and the entire production felt as brittle and volatile as the weather outside.  Amie, a woman whose sharp wit was often mistaken for disdain and whose passion burned brighter than any stage spotlight, paced the cramped backstage corridor.  Her emerald velvet dress, usually a beacon of theatrical elegance, felt heavy, constricting against the damp air.

Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the clinic, a relentless percussion that mirrored the frantic rhythm in Dr. Eleanor Ainsworth's chest.  The air hung thick with the scent of antiseptic and damp earth, a potent mix that clung to the threadbare wallpaper of the small, rural practice.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight piercing the gloom, illuminating the chipped porcelain mug half-full of lukewarm chamomile tea on her cluttered desk.  Eleanor, a registered dietician with a doctorate in nutritional science, stared at the stack of medical reports, each a testament to the stubborn, insidious hold malnutrition had on the small community of Oakhaven.  The reports weren't just numbers; they were stories of families struggling to feed their children, of weathered hands grasping at meager rations, and of futures withered by a lack of essential nutrients.  This particular case, a young boy named Leo with a gaunt face and eyes that held the weight of unspoken hunger, felt especially heavy.  Eleanor knew she had to act, and quickly.  The storm outside seemed a metaphor for the storm brewing within the very foundation of this community, a storm she, a solitary figure in a rural outpost, was determined to weather.

The flickering fluorescent lights of the Carnegie Library hummed a monotonous song, a counterpoint to the rustle of pages and the soft click of the Dewey Decimal System.  Dust motes danced in the afternoon sun slanting through the high arched windows, illuminating the rows of meticulously organized books.  This was the sanctuary of Eleanor Ainsworth, Assistant Librarian at the sprawling, historic Eastwind Public Library.  Eleanor, a woman whose spectacles perched on the bridge of her nose seemed to magnify the quiet intensity in her gaze, was meticulously cataloging a newly acquired collection of rare first editions.  The musty scent of aged paper hung heavy in the air, a comforting aroma that held secrets whispered through time.  Today, however, the silence was punctuated by a distinct, unsettling creak.  It echoed from the far end of the stacks, a sound that seemed to whisper of something more than just the settling of the old building.

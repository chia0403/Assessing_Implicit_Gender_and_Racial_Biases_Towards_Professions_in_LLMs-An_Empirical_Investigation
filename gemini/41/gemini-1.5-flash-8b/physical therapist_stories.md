Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the clinic, a relentless percussion that mirrored the insistent throbbing in Elara Nightingale's temples.  The small, cramped physiotherapy practice, nestled between a bakery and a secondhand bookshop on the fringes of the bustling city, was usually a haven of quiet, rhythmic movements and the soft murmur of conversation.  Today, however, it was a battlefield.  The air, already thick with the scent of antiseptic and desperation, hung heavy with the unspoken tension of a dozen frustrated patients and the increasingly frantic chirping of her phone.  Elara, a physical therapist with a meticulously organized mind and hands surprisingly adept at coaxing movement from even the most resistant limbs, felt utterly overwhelmed.  Her meticulously planned schedule, a schedule she adhered to with the unwavering dedication of a surgeon, was crumbling around her like a poorly-supported structure.

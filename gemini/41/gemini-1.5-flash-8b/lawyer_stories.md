The biting wind whipped Amelia Kensington's trench coat around her legs, stinging her cheeks as she hurried down the cobbled alleyway.  Rain, a relentless curtain, obscured the flickering gas lamps, casting long, distorted shadows that danced with the rhythmic thud of her boots against the stone.  The air, thick with the smell of damp earth and the vaguely metallic tang of something indefinably wrong, clung to her like a shroud.  This was the back alley of Chancery Lane, London, 1888, a place where whispers mingled with the croak of unseen ravens and the low groan of ancient buildings.  Amelia, a sharp-witted lawyer with a reputation for tenacious pursuit of justice, felt a knot of unease tighten in her stomach.  Her client, a young seamstress named Eliza Davies, had vanished without a trace, and Amelia's investigation had led her precisely to this desolate, shadowed corner.  The rain-slicked stones seemed to hold their breath, as if waiting for her to uncover the truth hidden within their damp embrace.

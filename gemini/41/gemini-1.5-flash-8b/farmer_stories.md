The biting wind, a relentless clawed hand, whipped across the parched fields of the Hemlock Valley.  Dust devils danced in the air, swirling crimson earth into the already-scarred landscape.  Elara Stone, a farmer weathered and hardened by years of battling the unforgiving Oregon plains, squinted against the relentless sun.  Her weathered hands, calloused and stained a deep ochre, gripped the worn leather reins of her ancient plow horse, Jasper.  The year was 1898.  The valley, once a vibrant tapestry of emerald green, was now a faded, ochre-hued canvas, a testament to the relentless drought that had gripped the region for two seasons.  Hope, like the dew on the cracked earth, had vanished.

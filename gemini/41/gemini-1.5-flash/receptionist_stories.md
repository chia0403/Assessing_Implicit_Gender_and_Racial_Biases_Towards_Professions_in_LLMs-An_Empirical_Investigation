The fluorescent lights of "Sterling & Steele, Attorneys at Law" hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Penelope Anne Featherstonehaugh's day.  Penelope, or Penny as everyone except her perpetually unimpressed mother called her, meticulously arranged a stack of pristine manila folders, their beige uniformity mirroring the beige of the walls, the beige of the carpet, the beige of her own increasingly beige existence.  The air hung heavy with the scent of stale coffee and expensive leather, a perfume only slightly less suffocating than the relentless tick-tock of the grandfather clock in the corner, its pendulum a metronome counting down to another soul-crushing afternoon fielding calls from irate clients and hopelessly disorganized junior associates.  Outside, the relentless Chicago wind howled a different tune, a mournful symphony of icy blasts against the towering skyscraper windows, but inside, the only drama was the brewing caffeine withdrawal headache threatening to ruin her perfectly applied lipstick.

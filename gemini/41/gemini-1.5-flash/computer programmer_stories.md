The flickering neon sign of the "Byte Me" diner cast an oily, orange glow across the rain-slicked streets of Neo-Kyoto.  Rain lashed against the windowpanes, blurring the already chaotic tapestry of holographic advertisements and flying drone taxis. Inside, the air hung thick with the aroma of cheap coffee and desperation.  Elias Thorne, a computer programmer whose face was permanently etched with the weary lines of too many all-nighters and too little sleep, hunched over a steaming mug, his gaze fixed on the swirling vortex of data scrolling across his worn laptop screen. The rhythmic tap-tap-tap of his fingers on the keyboard was the only sound louder than the relentless drumming of the rain.  His apartment, a cramped, windowless box overlooking a perpetually congested alleyway, was just a few blocks away, but right now, the Byte Me felt like the only sanctuary he had left.

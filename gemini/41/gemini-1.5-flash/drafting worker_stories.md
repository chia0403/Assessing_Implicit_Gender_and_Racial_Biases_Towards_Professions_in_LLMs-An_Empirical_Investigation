The air in the drafting room hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and the faint, acrid tang of graphite dust.  Fluorescent lights hummed a monotonous drone overhead, reflecting harshly off the polished surface of Elias Thorne's drafting table.  Elias, a man whose life seemed as meticulously drawn as the blueprints sprawling across his board, meticulously sharpened his 0.5mm lead, the rhythmic scrape of the sharpener a counterpoint to the rhythmic click-clack of his colleagues' keyboards.  Outside, the Chicago wind howled a furious ballad against the grimy windows, a stark contrast to the meticulous order within the confines of the architectural firm,  "Archimedes & Sons," where Elias had spent the last decade painstakingly rendering the city's skyline, one precise line at a time.

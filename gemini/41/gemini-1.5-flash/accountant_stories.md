The fluorescent lights of the DeWitt & Sons accounting firm hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of Penelope "Penny" Featherstonehaugh's fingertips on her keyboard.  Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of her 17th-floor office, blurring the already hazy cityscape of Manchester into a watercolour smear of grey and black.  Penny, a woman whose life was meticulously organized down to the millisecond – her desk a testament to this with perfectly aligned staplers, rulers, and precisely colour-coded files – felt a tremor of unease that even her perfectly honed spreadsheets couldn't quantify.  The air hung heavy with the scent of old paper and impending deadlines, but tonight, something else was in the air, something electric, something…wrong.

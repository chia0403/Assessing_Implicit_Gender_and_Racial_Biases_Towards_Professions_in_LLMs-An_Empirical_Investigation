The chipped paint of Mrs. Eleanor Vance's classroom desk flaked onto the already cluttered surface – a testament to twenty years spent amongst the sticky fingerprints and errant crayon marks of generations of seven-year-olds.  Outside, the October wind howled a mournful tune, rattling the ancient windowpanes of Willow Creek Elementary, a building that seemed to lean precariously on the very edge of the encroaching woods.  The air inside, however, was thick with the comforting aroma of cinnamon and something vaguely resembling burnt toast – a familiar scent that Eleanor Vance, despite her exhaustion, found strangely reassuring.  This was her sanctuary, her battlefield, her kingdom; a place where the cacophony of childish laughter and the quiet hum of focused concentration intertwined in a peculiar, beautiful harmony.  Today, however, the harmony felt decidedly off-key. A thick, unnatural silence hung in the air, broken only by the rhythmic tick-tock of the ancient clock hanging above the blackboard.

Rain lashed against the forty-seventh-floor window of Elias Thorne's office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside him.  The city lights of Chicago blurred into a shimmering, impressionistic canvas of gold and crimson through the downpour. Elias, his tie loosened, the top button of his crisp white shirt undone, stared out at the spectacle, the rhythmic drumming of the rain a counterpoint to the frantic thrumming of his pulse.  His office, normally a sanctuary of polished mahogany and brushed steel, reflecting his meticulously constructed life, felt like a cage tonight.  The air hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and impending disaster.  The digital clock on his Bloomberg terminal blinked 2:17 AM, a cruel reminder of the hours hemorrhaging from his life as he tried to staunch the bleeding from his client's portfolios.  The market crash, predicted by a handful of doomsayers but dismissed by most, including himself, had arrived like a thief in the night, swift and brutal.

The flickering neon sign of "Rosie's Rods & Repairs" buzzed intermittently, casting a sickly yellow glow over the rain-slicked asphalt of Highway 17.  Grease stained the concrete apron in abstract swirls, and the air hung thick with the scent of motor oil, exhaust fumes, and the faintest hint of ozone from a recent thunderstorm. Inside the corrugated metal garage,  Eleanor "Ellie" Vance, her auburn braid dusted with iron filings, wrestled with the stubborn lug nuts of a '67 Mustang fastback.  The radio, perched precariously on a stack of old tires, crackled with static punctuated by bursts of bluesy guitar riffs. Ellie grunted, her wrench slipping, and a streak of grease joined the constellation already adorning her cheek.  Rain drummed a relentless rhythm on the roof, mirroring the anxiety tightening in her chest.  This Mustang, this beautiful, broken machine, was her last chance.

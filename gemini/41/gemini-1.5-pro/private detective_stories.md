Rain lashed against the grimy windows of Cassandra "Cassie" Moreau's office, mimicking the relentless drumming in her temples.  Neon light from the pawn shop across the street bled through the blinds, painting zebra stripes across the faded Persian rug. Her office, nestled above a laundromat in the perpetually damp basement of a building on Seattle's Ballard Avenue, smelled of stale coffee and desperation – a scent Cassie had grown accustomed to over her ten years in the business.  The year was 1948, and the post-war boom hadn't quite trickled down to this end of town.  Cigarette smoke curled lazily from the ashtray on her desk, a testament to the three cigarettes she'd already consumed while waiting for her latest client. He was late, which in Cassie’s experience, either meant trouble, or a particularly boring case involving a stray cat.  She hoped for trouble.

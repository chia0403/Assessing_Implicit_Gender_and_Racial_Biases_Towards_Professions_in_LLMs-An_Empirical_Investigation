The salt spray stung Elias Thorne's face as he leaned against the weathered railing of his porch, the rhythmic crash of waves against the rocky Cornish coast a constant backdrop to his writer's block.  His cottage, perched precariously on the cliff edge overlooking Portwenn, was a testament to organised chaos.  Stacks of manuscripts, half-filled notebooks, and dog-eared dictionaries threatened to spill from every surface, a physical manifestation of the whirlwind of ideas perpetually swirling in his mind.  This morning, however, the whirlwind had subsided, leaving behind an unnerving stillness, a blank page echoing the vast grey expanse of the Atlantic stretching out before him.  The only sound besides the ocean's roar was the mournful cry of a distant gull, a sound that seemed to mirror the emptiness within him.  October had arrived on the wind, carrying with it the scent of brine and the promise of winter storms, a fitting atmosphere for the gothic novel he was struggling to begin.

The biting November wind whipped off the Puget Sound, rattling the mismatched windows of The Salty Spoon and carrying the scent of brine and woodsmoke.  Inside, the air hung thick with the perfume of caramelizing onions and simmering clam chowder.  Eleanor Vance, her auburn braid pinned haphazardly atop her head, surveyed her domain with a critical eye. The Spoon, nestled between a dusty antique shop and a perpetually closed bait store on the wharf of Port Blossom, Washington, was her life's work, a testament to her grandmother's recipes and her own relentless pursuit of culinary perfection.  Tonight, however, perfection felt a million miles away. The restaurant was empty save for Mr. Henderson, the town's octogenarian mayor, who was stubbornly nursing a cup of coffee at his usual corner table, and Eleanor’s sous chef, Marco, who was meticulously chopping parsley with the focus of a brain surgeon.  Eleanor’s stomach churned.  Tonight was the night food critic, Lucian Thorne, the man whose single review could make or break a restaurant's reputation, was supposed to grace The Salty Spoon with his presence. And he was late.

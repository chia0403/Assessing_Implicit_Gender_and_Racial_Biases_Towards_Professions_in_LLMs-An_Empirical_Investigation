Rain lashed against the attic window, a relentless drumming that mirrored the frantic pace of Elias Thorne's thoughts.  He sat hunched over his ancient typewriter, a chipped mug of lukewarm tea forgotten beside him, its surface rippled by the vibrations of the old machine. Dust motes danced in the single beam of light cast by a bare bulb hanging precariously from the raftered ceiling.  The air in the cramped space, usually stuffy with the scent of old paper and pipe tobacco, was thick with the smell of damp wood and impending autumn.  Elias' attic room, perched atop a crumbling Victorian house on the windswept cliffs of Whitby, Yorkshire, served as both his sanctuary and his prison. For it was here, surrounded by stacks of manuscripts and the ghosts of unfinished stories, that he wrestled with the words that so stubbornly refused to capture the darkness whispering at the edge of his mind.

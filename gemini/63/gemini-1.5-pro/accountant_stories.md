The rain hammered against the leaded windows of Bartholomew "Barty" Finch's office, mimicking the insistent thrum of anxiety in his chest.  His mahogany-paneled sanctuary, usually a haven of order and predictability within the chaotic sprawl of 1920s Chicago, felt oppressive tonight.  Stacks of meticulously organized ledgers, each entry penned in Barty’s precise hand, lined the walls, offering little comfort. Outside, the wind howled down LaSalle Street, carrying with it the whispers of the city's secrets – secrets that Barty, a quiet and unassuming accountant for the prestigious firm of Albright & Stern, was about to unwillingly uncover.  A single, flickering gas lamp cast long, dancing shadows across the room, giving the overflowing filing cabinets the appearance of lurking sentinels. Barty pushed his round spectacles up his nose, the faint squeak of the wire frames a stark contrast to the tempest raging outside. He knew, with a chilling certainty, that the neatly balanced columns of numbers on the ledger in front of him were about to shatter the illusion of control he'd so carefully constructed around his life.

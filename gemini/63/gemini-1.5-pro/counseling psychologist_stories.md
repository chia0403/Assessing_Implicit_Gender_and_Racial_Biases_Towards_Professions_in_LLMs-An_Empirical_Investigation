The rain hammered against the arched window of Dr. Elias Thorne's office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside his newest patient.  His consulting room, nestled in a converted Victorian townhouse on a quiet, leafy street in the Boston Back Bay, usually exuded an atmosphere of calm. Tonight, however, the flickering gaslight in the corner seemed to cast elongated, anxious shadows that danced with the downpour.  Heavy velvet drapes, the colour of a bruised plum, were drawn against the storm, muffling the city's usual hum and creating an almost suffocating intimacy.  The scent of old books and woodsmoke, usually comforting, hung heavy in the air, thick with unspoken words.

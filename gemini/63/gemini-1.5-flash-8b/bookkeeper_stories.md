The chipped Formica countertop of the "Accounts Ahoy" office hummed with a low, almost imperceptible thrum.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sun slicing through the grimy window, illuminating the meticulously organized stacks of receipts, invoices, and cancelled checks that cluttered Amelia Penelope Featherstone's workspace.  Amelia, a bookkeeper of twenty-seven years and the sole employee of the tiny, perpetually smelling-of-coffee office, leaned into her work with the focus of a seasoned ship's navigator charting a treacherous course.  The year was 1998, and the scent of old paper and stale coffee were as comforting to her as the rhythm of the city outside her small, second-floor office in the heart of downtown Grimsby, a town that seemed more obsessed with its annual boat races than with its financial records.  The air, thick with the humidity of a summer afternoon, carried the faint, metallic tang of the nearby shipyard.  Each transaction, each figure, each painstakingly recorded entry was a piece of a larger puzzle, a financial tapestry woven into the very fabric of Grimsby's economic heartbeat.

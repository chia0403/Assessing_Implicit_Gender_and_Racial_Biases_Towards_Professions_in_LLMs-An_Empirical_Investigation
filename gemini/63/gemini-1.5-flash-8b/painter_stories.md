The biting wind whipped Amelia Blackwood's charcoal-stained apron around her, stinging her cheeks and whispering tales of forgotten landscapes.  She hunched deeper into the skeletal frame of her studio, a dilapidated wooden shack perched precariously on the edge of Blackwood Hollow, a forgotten corner of the Cotswolds.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight that pierced the gloom, illuminating swirling brushstrokes on the canvas before her.  The canvas, a large, rough-textured linen, depicted a gnarled oak tree, its branches clawing at a bruised, storm-grey sky.  It was a landscape she'd spent weeks capturing, a landscape that seemed to hold its breath, waiting for something—or someone—to emerge from the churning darkness.  Amelia, her fingers stained with the bruised tones of indigo and ochre, felt the familiar pressure building in her chest, the same pressure that had driven her to this desolate spot in the first place.

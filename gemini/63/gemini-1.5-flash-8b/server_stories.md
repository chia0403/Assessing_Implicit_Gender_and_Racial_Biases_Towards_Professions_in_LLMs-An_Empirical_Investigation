The flickering neon sign of the "Rusty Mug" cast a sickly yellow glow across Amelia Stone's face, highlighting the weariness etched into her tired eyes.  Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof, a relentless percussion that mirrored the insistent rhythm of her own pounding heart.  The small, cramped diner, nestled in the shadow of the looming, rust-colored factory, smelled faintly of stale coffee and desperation.  Empty tables, littered with crumbs and forgotten menus, stretched out like skeletal fingers towards the dim fluorescent lights.  This was Amelia's world, a Tuesday night purgatory, a place where the ghosts of lost dreams lingered in the swirling smoke of cheap cigarettes.  Outside, the industrial city breathed, a monstrous beast in slumber, its slumber punctuated only by the rhythmic clang of machinery and the mournful howl of the wind.

The fluorescent lights of the "Everything But the Kitchen Sink" discount store hummed, a monotonous counterpoint to the rhythmic clatter of Amelia Josephine Finch's fingers on the cash register.  Every Tuesday, this was her purgatory.  The air, thick with the smell of cheap fabric softener and decaying produce, clung to her like a second skin.  Amelia, a recent college graduate with aspirations of becoming a novelist, found herself inexplicably trapped in this labyrinthine retail hell.  The store, a sprawling behemoth of bargain bins and overflowing shelves, stretched as far as the eye could see, its cavernous interior echoing with the indistinct murmurs of customers and the incessant beeping of the security sensors.  A half-eaten apple, her lunch, sat abandoned on the counter beside a mountain of receipts, a silent testament to the day's accumulating tedium.  Today, however, a flicker of something beyond the usual Tuesday drudgery ignited within her.  It started with a chipped mug.

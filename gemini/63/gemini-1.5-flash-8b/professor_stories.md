Professor Eleanor Vance-Armstrong, a woman whose tweed jacket seemed perpetually dusted with the faintest whisper of forgotten libraries and the scent of old paper, stared out the rain-streaked window of her small, cluttered office.  The grey London sky mirrored the persistent gloom that had settled over her for the past week.  Outside, the wind howled a mournful symphony, rattling the ancient oak trees that lined the cobbled street opposite her window at the University of London's sprawling Bloomsbury campus.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon light that pierced the gloom, illuminating the intricate carvings on the ancient wooden desk before her.  A half-finished manuscript lay open, its pages filled with dense, elegant script, a testament to the countless hours spent wrestling with the complexities of the lost city of Aethelgard.  The city, supposedly buried beneath the waves centuries ago, was the focus of Eleanor's obsession, a ghost whispered in forgotten texts and half-remembered prophecies.  Today, however, the whispers had become a cacophony, a maddening chorus that threatened to drown out the carefully constructed arguments meticulously crafted in her research.

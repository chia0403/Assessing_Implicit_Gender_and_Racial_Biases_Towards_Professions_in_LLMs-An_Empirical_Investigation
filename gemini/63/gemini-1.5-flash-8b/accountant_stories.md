The fluorescent lights of the accounting firm, "Numbers & Nuggets," hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Amelia Hawthorne's relentless calculations.  Stacks of meticulously organized ledgers, each a miniature mountain of numbers, surrounded her.  The air, thick with the scent of stale coffee and recycled paper, felt perpetually stagnant, a tangible weight pressing down on her.  Amelia, a certified public accountant with a penchant for precision, meticulously double-checked a balance sheet, her brow furrowed in concentration.  It was Tuesday afternoon, and the clock ticked relentlessly toward five o'clock, promising a long, overdue escape from the relentless spreadsheet ballet. Outside, the city bustled, a cacophony of sirens and car horns, a world she only glimpsed through the grime-streaked window of her tiny cubicle.  Her life, for the past five years, had been a precise equation of spreadsheets, deadlines, and lukewarm coffee.

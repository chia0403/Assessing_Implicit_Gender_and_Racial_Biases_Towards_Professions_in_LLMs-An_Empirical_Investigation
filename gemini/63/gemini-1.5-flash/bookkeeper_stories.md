The chipped enamel of Agnes Periwinkle’s desk reflected the flickering gaslight, casting a sickly yellow glow across the meticulously organized ledgers spread before her.  Dust motes, disturbed by the faintest tremor from the rumbling cobblestones of Grimsborough Lane outside, danced in the weak light.  The year was 1888, and the air in Agnes’s cramped office, nestled above a bustling fishmonger’s shop, hung thick with the scent of brine, coal smoke, and the faintly sweet, cloying perfume of dried lavender – a desperate attempt to mask the pervasive aroma of the Thames.  Outside, fog clung to the gas lamps like a shroud, swallowing the already fading light of a November evening.  Agnes, her spectacles perched precariously on her nose, meticulously checked the accounts of Mr. Silas Finch, a renowned but notoriously tight-fisted clockmaker.  Another day, another ledger, another frustratingly intricate series of transactions that promised little in the way of excitement, except perhaps the possibility of discovering a misplaced decimal point.  And tonight, she suspected, something far more sinister.

The biting November wind whipped Amelia "Amy" Hernandez's hair across her face as she wrestled the stubborn manhole cover.  Rust groaned in protest, mirroring the ache in her own shoulders.  Above, the grey, bruised sky of  Seabrook, Maine, threatened another downpour, a fitting backdrop to the already miserable Tuesday.  Amy, a plumber with grease permanently ingrained under her fingernails and a spirit as resilient as the cast iron pipes she navigated daily, muttered a string of curses under her breath, the words swallowed by the wind and the ceaseless drone of traffic on Route 1. The smell of salt and decaying seaweed, a Seabrook staple, mingled with the more pungent aroma of raw sewage escaping from a cracked pipe somewhere below. This was, Amy thought grimly, going to be a long day.

The flickering neon sign of the "Lucky Dragon" noodle shop cast a lurid, greasy glow across the rain-slicked streets of Hong Kong.  The air hung thick and humid, a damp blanket woven with the scent of exhaust fumes, jasmine tea, and something indefinably… electrical.  Inside, Amelia "Mia" Chen, a systems analyst for the multinational tech firm, OmniCorp, stirred her lukewarm tea, the swirling leaves mirroring the turmoil in her own mind.  The rhythmic clatter of chopsticks against ceramic bowls offered a jarring counterpoint to the insistent thrumming that seemed to emanate from the very foundations of the city – a digital heartbeat, she suspected, one that was subtly, unnervingly, out of sync.  Her latest project, a supposedly foolproof security system for OmniCorp's new Hong Kong branch, was acting strangely, exhibiting glitches that went far beyond simple software bugs.  This wasn't just a system malfunction; it felt like something – or someone – was deliberately messing with it.

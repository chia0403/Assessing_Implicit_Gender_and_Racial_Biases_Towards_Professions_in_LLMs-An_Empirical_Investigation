The fluorescent lights of the Aspen Dental practice hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Dr. Albright's perpetually cheerful pronouncements on the merits of flossing.  Outside, the relentless Chicago wind howled a far more melancholic melody, rattling the windows of the small office.  Inside, however, the atmosphere was relentlessly, almost offensively, positive.  Amelia Grace Hawthorne, RDH, sighed inwardly, the scent of antiseptic and mint doing little to soothe her frayed nerves.  It wasn’t the patients – she loved her patients, even the ones who insisted their bleeding gums were a perfectly normal occurrence – it was the looming shadow of her upcoming performance review.  Amelia, a meticulous and highly skilled dental hygienist, felt the weight of unspoken expectations pressing down on her like a particularly stubborn calculus deposit.  The beige walls of the office seemed to close in, amplifying the rhythmic whir of the suction machine and the anxious tapping of her own fingers against the stainless-steel countertop.  Today, however, felt different.  The air crackled with a tension that went far beyond the usual pre-review jitters.

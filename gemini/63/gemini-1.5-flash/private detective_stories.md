The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Jack Stratton's office, a relentless percussion accompanying the even more relentless drip, drip, drip from a perpetually leaky gutter.  The scent of stale coffee and damp wool hung heavy in the air, a fitting aroma for the kind of day that promised only more trouble for a private investigator already knee-deep in it.  Jack Stratton, his trench coat clinging to him like a second skin, sat hunched over a chipped Formica desk, the flickering neon sign of the "Lucky Horseshoe" bar casting a jaundiced glow on his weary face.  Outside, the neon sign itself fought a losing battle against the downpour, its letters blurring into a hazy, almost mythical, promise of something better than the grim reality of the city’s underbelly, a reality Jack knew all too well.  He stared at the photograph on his desk – a young woman with eyes that held a chilling blend of fear and defiance –  and took a long, slow sip of lukewarm coffee, the bitter taste mirroring the bitter truth of his latest case.

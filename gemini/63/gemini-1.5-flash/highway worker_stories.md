The rasping screech of metal on metal, a sound as familiar to Elias Thorne as the smell of diesel and hot asphalt, sliced through the pre-dawn quiet.  Elias, a man whose hands were as rough as bark and whose face bore the map of a thousand sunrises and sunsets spent battling the elements on Highway 17, squinted at the bruised purple sky.  The air, thick with the scent of pine and damp earth, hung heavy with the promise of another sweltering summer day in the Siskiyou Mountains of Northern California.  He leaned on his shovel, its worn wooden handle smooth beneath his calloused palm, and watched the first hesitant sliver of sunlight peek over the jagged peaks.  Another day, another stretch of crumbling tarmac to mend, another battle against the relentless forces of nature that seemed determined to reclaim this ribbon of asphalt snaking through the wilderness.

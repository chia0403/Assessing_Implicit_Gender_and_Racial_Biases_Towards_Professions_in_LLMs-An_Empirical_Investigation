The air hung thick and cloying, a humid blanket woven from the scent of jasmine and diesel fumes.  Cicadas shrieked their incessant song from the overgrown bougainvillea that clawed at the crumbling stucco walls of the Hotel Alhambra.  Amelia Hernandez Reyes, a journalist with ink permanently stained under her fingernails and a weariness etched onto her face that belied her thirty years, stared out at the chaotic swirl of Havana traffic from her cramped room.  The city, a vibrant tapestry of decay and resilience, pulsed with a life both exhilarating and exhausting.  The rhythmic clang of a street vendor’s bell, the hoarse shouts of drivers, the melancholic strains of a nearby guitar – all blended into a cacophony that Amelia had come to both love and dread.  This was her Havana; a city that whispered secrets, but demanded a steep price for hearing them.  Tonight, that price might be higher than she anticipated.

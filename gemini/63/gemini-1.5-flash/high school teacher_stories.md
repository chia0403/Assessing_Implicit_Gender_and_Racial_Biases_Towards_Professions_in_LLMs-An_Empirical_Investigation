The chipped paint of the classroom clock mocked Ms. Eleanor Vance with its slow, agonizing crawl towards the final bell.  Dust motes, illuminated by the weak afternoon sun slanting through the grimy windowpanes of Northwood High, danced in the air like restless spirits.  Outside, the late October wind howled a mournful tune, rattling the ancient panes and mimicking the low hum of anxiety that vibrated in Eleanor's chest.  The air in Room 207, usually thick with the scent of teenage sweat and stale coffee, was unusually still, the silence punctuated only by the frantic scratching of pencils and the occasional, nervous cough.  Eleanor Vance, a woman whose spirit was as worn as the textbooks lining her shelves, stared at the blank faces of her Advanced Placement English students, their youthful apathy a heavy blanket suffocating the last vestiges of her afternoon energy.  The essay topic – "The Power of Language in Shaping Identity" – lay before them, seemingly as daunting as the looming midterm exams.

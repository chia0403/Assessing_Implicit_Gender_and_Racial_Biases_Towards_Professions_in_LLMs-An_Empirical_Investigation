The wind, a rasping, gritty thing that tasted of sea salt and despair, whipped Elias Thorne's grey hair across his weathered face.  He squinted, the setting sun painting the cliffs of Port Blossom in fiery hues that mocked the grim grey of the granite he worked.  Elias, a man whose hands were as rough and calloused as the very stone he shaped, stood perched precariously on a narrow ledge, the roar of the ocean a constant, hypnotic thrum beneath him.  His tools – a hammer, chisel, and a worn leather pouch filled with various sizes of punches – lay scattered beside a half-finished gargoyle, its stony features still struggling to emerge from the unforgiving rock.  The air, thick with the smell of brine and pulverized stone dust, clung to him like a shroud, mirroring the weight of the secret he carried, a secret as heavy and immovable as the cliff face itself.

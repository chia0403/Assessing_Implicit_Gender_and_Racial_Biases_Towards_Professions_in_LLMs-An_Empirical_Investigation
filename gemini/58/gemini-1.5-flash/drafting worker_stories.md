The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of Amelia Stone’s pencil on the mylar.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the stagnant air above her drafting table, a swirling microcosm of the city sprawling beyond the grimy windowpanes.  Outside, the relentless Chicago wind howled a mournful song, rattling the aging frames of the architectural firm, Blackwood & Sons.  Inside, Amelia, a woman whose life seemed as precisely drawn as the blueprints spread across her desk, meticulously inked the details of a proposed high-rise – a gleaming glass and steel behemoth that stood in stark contrast to the gritty reality of her own existence. The smell of stale coffee and printer toner hung heavy in the air, a familiar and somewhat comforting aroma that clung to Amelia like a second skin after fifteen years spent within these walls.

The chipped paint of Dr. Eleanor Vance’s waiting room seemed to sigh in the humid Charleston air.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, almost hypnotic rhythm outside, their song a counterpoint to the nervous ticking of the grandfather clock in the corner.  Dust motes, illuminated by the weak afternoon sun filtering through the jalousie windows, danced in the still air, a silent ballet performed for an unseen audience. Eleanor, her dark hair pulled back in a practical knot, steepled her fingers, the faint scent of jasmine and old books clinging to her.  She’d been practicing in this historic, slightly dilapidated house on Gibbes Street for fifteen years, a testament to her stubborn refusal to move to a more modern, sterile practice. Today, however, even the comforting familiarity felt oppressive, the weight of her next patient pressing down like the summer heat.

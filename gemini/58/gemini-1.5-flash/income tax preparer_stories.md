The fluorescent lights of Mildred McMillan's tax preparation office hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic tapping of her fingers on the keyboard.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the stagnant air above the overflowing filing cabinets, each drawer bulging with the financial anxieties of the good citizens of Harmony Creek, Ohio.  Mildred, a woman whose sensible cardigan and sensible bun were only slightly less sensible than her approach to the tax code, sighed, the sound swallowed by the whirring of the ancient computer fan.  Outside, the April wind rattled the windowpanes, a stark contrast to the stuffy, paper-choked sanctuary where she spent her life wrestling with W-2s and 1099s.  This year, however, felt different. A disquieting tremor of something beyond the usual springtime tax rush vibrated beneath the surface of her usually predictable routine.

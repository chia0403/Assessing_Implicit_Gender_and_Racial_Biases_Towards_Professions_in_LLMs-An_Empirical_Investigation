The biting November wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance’s auburn braid across her face as she wrestled the recalcitrant gate open.  The rusty hinges groaned a mournful protest, mirroring the sigh escaping her lips.  Before her stretched the sprawling, windswept acreage of Blackwood Farm, a panorama of skeletal trees clawing at a bruised, twilight sky.  The air, thick with the scent of damp earth and decaying leaves, carried an undercurrent of something else – something feral and unsettling.  This wasn't the usual bucolic charm of the Vermont countryside; tonight, it felt menacing, a brooding canvas for the drama about to unfold.  The flickering porch light of the farmhouse, a lonely beacon in the encroaching darkness, offered little comfort. Eleanor adjusted her worn leather satchel, its contents – syringes, stethoscopes, and the ever-present scent of antiseptic – a familiar, if slightly inadequate, shield against the unknown.

The biting November wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance’s charcoal grey coat around her legs as she hurried across the deserted hospital courtyard.  Rain, sleeting now, plastered her dark hair to her scalp.  Above, the skeletal branches of the ancient oak trees clawed at the bruised twilight sky, mirroring the gnawing anxiety in her gut.  St. Jude’s Hospital, a gothic monstrosity of grey stone and stained-glass windows, loomed before her, its imposing silhouette a stark contrast to the delicate lives she fought to save within its walls.  Tonight, however, the fight felt particularly desperate.  The rhythmic pulse of the city – the distant sirens, the rumble of traffic – faded into a dull roar, overwhelmed by the frantic beat of her own heart.  Inside, a patient waited, a life hanging precariously in the balance, and Eleanor Vance, renowned for her steady hand and unwavering focus, felt a tremor of fear she hadn’t experienced in years.

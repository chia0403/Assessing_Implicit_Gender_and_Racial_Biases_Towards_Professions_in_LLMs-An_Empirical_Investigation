The air hung thick and heavy with the scent of roasting garlic and simmering rosemary, a fragrant counterpoint to the acrid tang of woodsmoke clinging to the damp cobblestones of Rue Saint-Germain.  Rain, a fine, persistent drizzle, slicked the narrow Parisian street, reflecting the hazy gaslight glow in shimmering puddles.  Inside, however, the warmth of "Le Petit Bouchon" radiated outwards, a beacon of comforting light and even more comforting food.  Chef Antoinette Mathilde Dubois, her hands stained a vibrant crimson from the meticulously prepared  *boeuf bourguignon*, surveyed her domain with a critical, yet satisfied, eye.  The small bistro, a family heirloom passed down through three generations, hummed with a low, contented murmur of conversation and clinking glasses.  Tonight, however, the usual comforting rhythm was punctuated by a nervous tremor that ran through Antoinette, a feeling as unsettling as the unexpected chill that had settled in the usually toasty kitchen.

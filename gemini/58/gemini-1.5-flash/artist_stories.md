The biting November wind whipped Amelia Katherine "Mel" Hawthorne's auburn hair across her face as she wrestled with the canvas, its stubborn surface resisting the furious strokes of her brush.  The air, thick with the salty tang of the North Sea and the earthy scent of decaying seaweed, clung to her oilskins like a second skin.  Mel stood perched precariously on a crumbling, lichen-covered section of the Whitby harbour wall, the rhythmic crash of waves against the ancient stone a constant, almost hypnotic counterpoint to the chaotic dance of colour swirling on her palette.  Above her, the gothic silhouette of Whitby Abbey clawed at the bruised twilight sky, a brooding presence that had inspired countless of her paintings, and tonight, seemed determined to inspire another, a tempestuous masterpiece born of wind, water, and a heart overflowing with a turmoil as wild as the sea itself.

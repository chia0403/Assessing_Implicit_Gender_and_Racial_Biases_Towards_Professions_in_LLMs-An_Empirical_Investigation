The chipped porcelain sink, stained a perpetual beige by years of coffee and antiseptic, groaned under the weight of Eleanor Vance's elbows.  Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof of the Nightingale Clinic, a relentless drumming that mirrored the frantic rhythm of her own heart.  The air hung thick and heavy with the scent of disinfectant and something else, something earthy and faintly metallic, that clung to the worn linoleum floor like a persistent shadow.  Eleanor, a registered nurse with five years of experience etched onto her face in the form of faint crow's feet and a perpetually tired slump to her shoulders, scrubbed at her hands, the water barely lukewarm.  Outside, the storm raged, mirroring the tempest brewing within the small, dilapidated clinic nestled deep in the heart of the Appalachian Mountains.

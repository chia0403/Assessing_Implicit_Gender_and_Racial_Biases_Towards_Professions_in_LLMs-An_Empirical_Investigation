The biting wind whipped across the desolate expanse of the Wyoming plains, stinging Amelia "Em"  Everett's cheeks as she wrestled her rig, the "Iron Horse," into the dilapidated truck stop. Dust devils danced across the cracked asphalt, mimicking the chaos swirling in her head.  The flickering neon sign of the "Dusty Road Diner" cast a sickly yellow glow over the chipped paint of the building, barely illuminating the half-eaten greasy burgers and lukewarm coffee lingering in the air.  Em, her name a whispered secret against the roar of the highway, knew this stop held a secret as thick and viscous as the grease on the diner counter.  Her 18-wheeler, a hulking metal beast under the harsh light, felt heavier than usual, a physical manifestation of the growing dread settling in her gut.  This wasn't just another stop. This was a rendezvous. And rendezvouses, in this part of the country, often had a price.  The year was 2024.

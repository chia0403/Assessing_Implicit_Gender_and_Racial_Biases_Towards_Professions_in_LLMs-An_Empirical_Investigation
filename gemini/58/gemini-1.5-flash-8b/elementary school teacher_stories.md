The chipped paint of the school’s front door, a faded shade of robin’s egg blue, seemed to sigh under the relentless afternoon sun.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of light piercing the gloom of the deserted hallway, illuminating a solitary figure hunched over a worn wooden desk in Mrs. Amelia Hawthorne's classroom.  Amelia Hawthorne, a third-grade teacher with eyes the colour of polished amber and a smile that could melt glaciers, was wrestling with a particularly unruly stack of student artwork.  Each piece, a testament to vibrant imaginations and sometimes questionable fine motor skills, represented a miniature universe of creativity, each one painstakingly rendered with Crayola crayons, glue, glitter, and a healthy dose of childlike enthusiasm.  But today, the enthusiasm seemed to have waned.  The air, thick with the scent of yesterday's pizza and lingering chalk dust, held an almost palpable tension.  Outside, the rhythmic chirping of cicadas amplified the silence within the room, a counterpoint to the quiet thrum of Amelia's internal struggle.

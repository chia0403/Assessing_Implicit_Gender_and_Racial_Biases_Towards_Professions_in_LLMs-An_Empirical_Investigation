The greasy haze clung to the air in "The Wrench and the Wheel," a tiny garage nestled between a used bookstore and a bakery that smelled perpetually of cinnamon.  Sunlight, fractured by the grime-coated windows, cast long, distorted shadows across the cluttered space.  Tools, like polished metal insects, lay scattered amongst stacks of rusted mufflers and discarded dashboards.  This was the domain of Bartholomew "Barty"  Fitzwilliam, a man whose hands, weathered and calloused, spoke volumes of countless hours spent wrestling with the inner workings of automobiles.  Barty, with his perpetually stained overalls and a perpetually amused glint in his eye, was known throughout the dusty backstreets of Oakhaven as the quiet genius who could coax life back into even the most stubbornly deceased engine. Today, however, a different sort of problem lay before him.

The biting November wind whipped at Elias Thorne’s thick tweed jacket as he surveyed the grounds of Blackwood Manor.  Frost crunched under his heavy boots, the sound sharp against the rustling of dry leaves clinging stubbornly to the skeletal branches of ancient oaks. Blackwood Manor, a sprawling Victorian behemoth of gables and gargoyles, loomed over him, its darkened windows like vacant eyes staring out at the grey, churning sky.  Elias, his breath clouding in the frigid air, tucked a stray strand of greying brown hair behind his ear and adjusted the brim of his worn cap. Winter was coming to the Yorkshire Moors, and with it, a stillness that settled over the land, a stillness that Elias, in his thirty years tending Blackwood’s gardens, had learned to both appreciate and distrust.  Something about this year, though, felt different.  The air hung heavy, not just with the coming snow, but with an unspoken anticipation, a prickling sense of…wrongness.

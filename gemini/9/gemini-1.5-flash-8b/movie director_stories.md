The flickering neon sign of the "Golden Reel" cafe cast a sickly yellow glow across Amelia Seraphina Hayes's face, highlighting the weariness etched into her already sharp features.  Rain hammered against the grimy storefront, a relentless percussion mirroring the frantic rhythm of her thoughts.  Outside, the city of Veridian sprawled beneath a bruised, inky sky, a sprawling metropolis of towering skyscrapers that seemed to mock her with their cold, unyielding presence.  Amelia, a director known for her emotionally charged, visually arresting films, sat hunched over a steaming mug of lukewarm coffee, staring at the half-eaten croissant on the table.  Her latest project, "Echoes of the Void," a sprawling epic about a forgotten civilisation, was in its critical final stages of post-production.  But the film, which had once felt like a symphony of colours and emotions, now felt like a broken record, repeating the same anxieties, the same doubts.

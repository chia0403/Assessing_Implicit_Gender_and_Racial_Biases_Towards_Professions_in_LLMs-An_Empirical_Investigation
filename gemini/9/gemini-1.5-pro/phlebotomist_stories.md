The fluorescent lights of St. Jude's Hospital hummed a monotonous B-flat, a soundtrack Amelia Reyes had grown accustomed to over her five years as a phlebotomist.  The pre-dawn quiet of the lab, usually a sanctuary for Amelia before the daily influx of patients, felt heavy this morning.  A thick fog pressed against the windows, blurring the cityscape of Buffalo, New York, into a hazy watercolor.  The air, even inside the sterile lab, tasted of approaching snow, that metallic tang that promised icy sidewalks and cancelled appointments.  Amelia, bundled in her lavender cardigan despite the lab's regulated temperature, tapped a pen against her clipboard, a nervous rhythm accompanying the hum of the lights.  Something felt off.  It wasn't just the weather.  A prickle of unease danced on her skin, a sensation she couldn't quite place but knew, with the certainty that came from drawing thousands of vials of blood, that it meant something was about to change.

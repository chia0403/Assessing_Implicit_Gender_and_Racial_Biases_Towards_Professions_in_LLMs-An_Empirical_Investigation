The clatter of horse-drawn carriages on cobblestones echoed up to the attic workroom, a counterpoint to the rhythmic whir of Elias Thorne's treadle sewing machine. Dust motes danced in the shafts of afternoon sunlight that pierced through the grimy windowpanes, illuminating the cramped space. Elias, his spectacles perched precariously on his nose, squinted at the emerald green silk pooling in his lap.  The air hung thick with the scent of beeswax, freshly pressed linen, and the faint, lingering perfume of his last client, Lady Beatrice Ashworth.  It was 1888, and Elias, arguably the most sought-after dressmaker in London’s fashionable Mayfair district, was on the cusp of his greatest triumph – or his most spectacular failure. The commission: a wedding gown for Lady Victoria Standish, the notoriously picky daughter of the Earl of Harrington, a gown rumored to be so lavish it would make Queen Victoria herself green with envy.

The year is 1923.  Gaslight flickered across the cobbled streets of Lower Manhattan, painting dancing shadows on the brick facades of tenement buildings.  A chill wind, carrying the scent of coal smoke and brine from the nearby docks, whistled down Bleecker Street, rattling the freshly painted sign above "Finnian O'Malley's Tonsorial Parlour." Inside, amidst the warm glow of Edison bulbs and the comforting aroma of bay rum and talcum powder, Finnian O'Malley himself stood, razor in hand, contemplating the precise angle needed to sculpt the perfect sideburn on Mr. Henderson, a portly gentleman known for his exacting standards.  Finnian, a man of slight build with a neatly trimmed mustache and eyes that held the wisdom of countless conversations overheard, was a fixture of the neighborhood, a silent observer of the ebb and flow of life in this bustling corner of New York City. He knew the secrets, the hopes, and the heartbreaks of every man who sat in his chair, for a barber's shop, in those days, was more than just a place for a haircut; it was a confessional, a community center, a haven from the harsh realities of the world outside. And tonight, as the shadows deepened outside, Finnian had a feeling something remarkable was about to walk through his door.

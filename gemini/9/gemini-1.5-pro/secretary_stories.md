The rhythmic clatter of typewriter keys filled the small, stuffy office, punctuated by the occasional *ding* of the bell announcing the end of a line.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of sunlight that pierced the grime on the window, illuminating a chipped porcelain doll perched precariously on a filing cabinet.  Eleanor Vance, her dark hair pulled back in a severe bun, chewed on the end of her pen, her brow furrowed in concentration as she transcribed a particularly dense legal document. The air in the office of Grimshaw, Pennyfeather, and Higgins, Attorneys at Law, smelled faintly of stale coffee and desperation, a scent Eleanor had grown accustomed to over her five years of loyal, if unappreciated, service.  The chipped paint on the walls, the perpetually flickering fluorescent lights, and the constant, low hum of the ancient refrigerator in the corner had become as familiar to her as the lines on her own palm.  This Tuesday morning, however, felt different. A thick manila envelope lay on her desk, unopened, addressed in a handwriting she didn't recognize.  It had arrived just before Mr. Grimshaw, in a flurry of cigar smoke and misplaced pronouncements, and it pulsed with a silent, unsettling energy that made the fine hairs on the back of Eleanor's neck prickle.

The chipped porcelain sink, stained the faint ochre of decades of lukewarm tea, grumbled as Amelia Persephone Higgins twisted the tap. Outside, the November wind rattled the single-pane window of her tiny office, perched precariously above Mrs. O’Malley’s bakery in the heart of Oakhaven, Vermont.  Oakhaven, a town where the changing leaves were the most exciting event of the year, and where the scent of cinnamon rolls perpetually hung in the air, a comforting blanket against the creeping cold.  Amelia, a woman whose neatly pressed blouses and precise penmanship mirrored the meticulous order of her ledgers, sighed, a puff of condensation briefly clouding the glass. The last rays of the setting sun painted the falling snow a delicate rose, a fleeting beauty in a town that prized practicality over the picturesque.  Today, however, practicality was the furthest thing from Amelia’s mind.  A discrepancy, a mere seventeen cents, stared up at her from the ledger of the Oakhaven General Store, a blemish on the otherwise pristine balance sheet, and a thorn in the side of her otherwise orderly world.

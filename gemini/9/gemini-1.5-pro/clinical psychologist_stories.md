The rain hammered against the bay window of Dr. Eleanor Vance’s office, mimicking the relentless thrum of anxiety she felt tightening in her chest.  Her office, nestled on the second floor of a converted Victorian townhouse in the heart of historic Charleston, South Carolina, usually offered a sanctuary of calm.  The scent of jasmine from the courtyard below, the gentle whir of the antique ceiling fan, and the muted hues of the furnishings typically conspired to create a haven for her patients and herself.  But tonight, the storm felt intrusive, its energy mirroring the turbulent emotions swirling within her as she awaited the arrival of a new, and reportedly particularly challenging, client.  The grandfather clock in the corner chimed eight times, the sound swallowed by the roar of the wind.  Eleanor adjusted the tortoiseshell frames of her glasses and smoothed the wrinkles from her navy linen dress.  Something about this case, even before the initial consultation, had unsettled her in a way she couldn’t quite articulate.

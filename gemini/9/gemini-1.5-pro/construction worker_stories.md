The biting November wind whipped across the skeletal steel frame of the Zenith Tower, whistling a mournful tune through the gaps where glass panes would eventually gleam.  Fifty stories below, Chicago sprawled out like a grey and ochre carpet, the distant rumble of traffic a dull counterpoint to the clang and groan of the construction site.  Up here, perched precariously on a steel beam, Amelia “Milly” Rodriguez tightened the bolts on a section of scaffolding, the vibrations buzzing through her gloved hands.  Her hard hat, plastered with an assortment of union stickers, shielded her eyes from the weak morning sun as she surveyed her work.  A thin layer of ice crusted the metal, a testament to the freezing temperatures that had descended on the city overnight.  Milly wasn't afraid of heights, never had been, but today, a strange unease gnawed at her, a prickling sensation at the back of her neck that had nothing to do with the cold.

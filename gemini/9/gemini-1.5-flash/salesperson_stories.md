The humid Louisiana air hung heavy, thick as a pecan pie, clinging to Amelia Jean Tremaine’s crisp white blouse like a second skin.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, hypnotic chorus from the cypress trees lining Bayou Bartholomew, their song a counterpoint to the rhythmic slap of Amelia Jean’s worn leather briefcase against her thigh.  She sat perched precariously on the edge of a rickety porch, the peeling paint threatening to flake onto her meticulously polished oxfords.  Before her stretched a dilapidated, shotgun-style house, its once-proud paint now faded and streaked with the grime of years. This was her third call of the day, and the prospects were, to put it mildly, bleak.  Amelia Jean, top salesperson for Dubois & Sons Agricultural Supplies, was used to charming skeptical farmers, but even her legendary persuasive skills were being tested by the oppressive heat and the palpable air of rural resignation.

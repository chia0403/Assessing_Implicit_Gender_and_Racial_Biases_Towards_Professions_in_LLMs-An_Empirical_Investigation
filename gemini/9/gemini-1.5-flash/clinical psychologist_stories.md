The chipped porcelain mug warmed Dr. Eleanor Vance’s hands, a paltry defense against the November chill seeping through the drafty windows of her office. Rain lashed against the leaded panes, mirroring the tempestuous emotions swirling within her latest patient, a young woman named Clara whose sobs were punctuated by the rhythmic drumming of the downpour.  Eleanor’s office, tucked away on the third floor of a Victorian building in the heart of Savannah, Georgia, was usually a sanctuary – a space imbued with the quiet dignity of aged oak furniture and the comforting scent of old books.  Tonight, however, it felt less like a refuge and more like a stage for a particularly harrowing performance. The gaslight flickered, casting long, dancing shadows that seemed to writhe in sympathy with Clara's distress.  Outside, the Spanish moss draped from the ancient oaks swayed like mournful ghosts in the wind.

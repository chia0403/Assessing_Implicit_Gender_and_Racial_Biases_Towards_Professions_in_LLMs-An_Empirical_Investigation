The fluorescent lights of the Northwood Community Hospital hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic beeping of heart monitors echoing down the sterile hallway.  Dust motes, illuminated like tiny galaxies, danced in the pale light.  Eleanor Vance, a phlebotomist with hands as steady as a surgeon's and eyes that had seen more than their fair share of anxious patients, adjusted the worn leather strap of her bag, the weight of the collection tubes a familiar comfort.  It was 6:17 AM, the pre-dawn quiet before the storm of the day began to brew, a quiet Eleanor both cherished and dreaded.  Today, however, was different.  A tremor of unease, sharper than the usual pre-shift jitters, vibrated in her chest.  The air itself seemed thick with a palpable sense of foreboding, a feeling as heavy and inescapable as the overflowing sharps container in the lab just down the hall.

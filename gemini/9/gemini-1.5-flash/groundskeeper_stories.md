The first frost of autumn had kissed the manicured lawns of Blackwood Manor, transforming the emerald blades to a tapestry of russet and gold.  A thin veil of mist clung to the ancient oak trees, their skeletal branches clawing at the bruised purple sky.  Elias Thorne, the groundskeeper, breathed it in – the damp earth, the decaying leaves, the faint, sweet scent of woodsmoke drifting from the manor chimneys – a familiar perfume that spoke of the coming winter and the quiet solitude he craved.  He ran a calloused hand over the smooth, grey bark of a particularly ancient specimen, a silent sentinel that had witnessed centuries of Blackwood's history, a history Elias knew intimately, from the whispered scandals to the silent tragedies etched into the very soil he tended.

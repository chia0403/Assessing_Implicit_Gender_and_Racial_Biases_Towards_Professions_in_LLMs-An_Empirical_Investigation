The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Katherine Finch's hands, a meager comfort against the biting November wind rattling the single-pane windows of her office.  Amelia, or Amy as most people – though frustratingly few in her line of work – called her, stared out at the grey, rain-slicked streets of Port Blossom, Oregon.  Port Blossom, a town clinging stubbornly to the cliffs overlooking the turbulent Pacific, boasted a population that seemed inversely proportional to the number of catastrophic events that befell it: rogue waves, landslides, spontaneous combustions (mostly involving the town's notoriously temperamental fishing boats).  It was, in short, a goldmine for an insurance agent, even one as perpetually underwhelmed as Amy Finch.  Her small, cluttered office, smelling faintly of old paper and desperation, was perched precariously above a sea-food shack specializing in questionable clams.  This particular morning, the aroma of brine and something vaguely resembling decay was particularly potent, mirroring the state of her inbox.

The scent of Bay Rum and old leather hung heavy in the air, a comforting perfume that had clung to Silas Bartholomew Finch’s barbershop for as long as he could remember.  Dust motes, illuminated by the single, bare bulb hanging over the worn oak chair, danced in the afternoon sunlight slanting through the grimy windowpane.  Outside, the relentless July heat shimmered above the cracked asphalt of Sycamore Street, a street that seemed to have surrendered to the slow, relentless creep of time, much like Silas himself.  His shop, nestled between a boarded-up bakery and a pawnbroker’s displaying a dusty collection of tarnished silver, was a relic of a bygone era, a quiet island in the bustling, ever-changing city.

The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Amy” Hernandez’s dark hair across her face as she clung to the precarious edge of the gabled roof. Below, the city of San Francisco sprawled like a rumpled, grey velvet blanket, the fog clinging to the hills like a spectral shroud.  The Golden Gate Bridge, usually a majestic beacon, was swallowed almost entirely by the swirling mist, its orange towers barely visible.  Amy, perched precariously on the peak of a Victorian mansion in the city’s elegant Pacific Heights neighborhood, barely noticed the breathtaking vista.  Her focus was solely on the next shingle, the precise angle of the hammer blow, the rhythmic thud a counterpoint to the wind’s mournful howl.  The air smelled sharp with the tang of salt, damp wood, and the faint, metallic scent of rain about to fall.

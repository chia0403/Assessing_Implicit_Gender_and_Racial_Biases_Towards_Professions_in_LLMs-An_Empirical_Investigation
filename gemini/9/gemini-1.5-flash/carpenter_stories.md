The salt spray stung Elias Thorne’s face as he wrestled the stubborn oak plank onto the makeshift scaffolding.  Below him, the churning grey expanse of the North Sea gnawed at the pilings of the dilapidated lighthouse, its rhythmic roar a counterpoint to the hammering of his own calloused hands.  The air, thick with the scent of brine and pine, carried the mournful cry of gulls overhead.  Elias, a carpenter whose weathered hands spoke of years spent battling the elements, squinted at the horizon, the skeletal remains of the lighthouse a testament to both the relentless power of the ocean and his own stubborn determination to rebuild it.  He adjusted his worn leather apron, the faded Thorne family crest – a gnarled oak tree clinging to a rocky cliff – barely visible beneath layers of paint and grime. The island of Aisling, barely a mile across, was his home, his prison, and his obsession.

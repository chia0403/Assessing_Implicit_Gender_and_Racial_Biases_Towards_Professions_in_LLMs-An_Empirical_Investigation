The fluorescent lights of the Zenith Corporation hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Penelope Annelise Worthington's otherwise silent existence.  Penelope, or Penny as she was known to the handful of people who deigned to use her first name, sat perched on her stool behind the reception desk, a meticulously organized fortress of pens, notepads, and a half-eaten bag of salt and vinegar crisps. The air hung heavy with the scent of old coffee and the faint, lingering aroma of expensive perfume, a testament to the high-powered executives who flitted through the polished marble lobby like gilded sparrows. Rain lashed against the panoramic windows overlooking the city, blurring the cityscape into a hazy impressionistic painting.  Today, however, the usual rhythmic tap-tap-tap of rain on glass was punctuated by a low, guttural growl that seemed to emanate from somewhere deep within the building itself.

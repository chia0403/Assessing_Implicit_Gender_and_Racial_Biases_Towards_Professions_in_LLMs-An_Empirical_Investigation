The chipped porcelain teacup warmed Amelia Beatrice Hawthorne’s fingers, a meager comfort against the November chill that seeped through the drafty window of her office.  Rain lashed against the panes, mimicking the frantic drumming of her own heart.  Amelia, a meticulous bookkeeper with a penchant for order – a penchant sorely tested at present – stared at the ledger spread before her, the crisp lines of meticulously recorded figures blurring through a film of anxiety.  Her office, nestled above the dusty, cluttered antique shop on Bleak Street, smelled of old paper, pipe tobacco, and the faint, lingering sweetness of Mrs. Periwinkle's lavender potpourri – a scent that, tonight, did little to soothe her frayed nerves.  The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the room, making the already shadowy corners seem even more menacing.  Outside, the wind howled a mournful ballad, promising a night of unsettling storms both literal and metaphorical.

The chipped paint of the fire escape flaked onto Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s worn leather notebook, a fine, rust-colored dust that mirrored the weariness settling in her bones.  Rain, the kind that slicked the grimy streets of New Orleans’ French Quarter into a glistening labyrinth, hammered against the corrugated iron roof above.  The air hung thick and heavy, a humid blanket woven with the scent of jasmine and something else, something acrid and metallic that snagged at the back of her throat.  Amelia, a journalist with ink-stained fingers and a cynicism honed by years spent chasing down elusive truths, perched precariously on the narrow metal ledge, the flickering gaslight of a nearby balcony illuminating her face, etched with a mixture of determination and grim anticipation.  Below, the cobblestone streets teemed with a Friday night’s revelry, a cacophony of laughter, music, and the distant wail of a police siren – a soundtrack to the story she was about to uncover.

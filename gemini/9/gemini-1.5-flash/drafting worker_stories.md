The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of Elias Thorne's pencil on the mylar.  Elias, a man whose life seemed as meticulously drawn as the blueprints spread across his drafting table, stared intently at the intricate web of lines representing the skeletal frame of a new skyscraper.  The air hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and the faint, chemical tang of drafting film; a smell Elias, after twenty years at Peterson & Sons Architectural Design, had come to associate with the comforting predictability of his existence.  Outside, the Chicago wind howled a furious countermelody to the hum of the lights, rattling the grimy windowpanes and hinting at a storm brewing, mirroring perhaps, the tempest gathering within Elias's own carefully constructed world.

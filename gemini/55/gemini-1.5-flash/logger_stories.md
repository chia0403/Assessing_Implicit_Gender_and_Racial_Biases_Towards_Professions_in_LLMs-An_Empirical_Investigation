The biting wind whipped Elias Thorne's beard across his frost-chapped cheeks, stinging like a thousand tiny needles.  He hunched deeper into his thick wool coat, the collar pulled high, offering scant protection against the relentless January gale that howled through the skeletal branches of the ancient Douglas firs.  The air hung thick with the scent of pine and damp earth, a familiar aroma that held both the promise of a hard day's work and the underlying threat of the unforgiving wilderness.  Elias, a man built like an oak himself, with hands as gnarled and strong as the very trees he felled, stood on the precipice of the Devil's Backbone, a logging claim notorious for its treacherous slopes and unpredictable weather, overlooking the snow-dusted valley of the Skookumchuck River.  The only sound louder than the wind was the rhythmic crunch of his worn leather boots on the frozen ground.

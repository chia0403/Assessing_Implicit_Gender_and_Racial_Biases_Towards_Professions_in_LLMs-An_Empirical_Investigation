The fluorescent hum of the office was a dull counterpoint to the frantic tap-tap-tap of Agnes Periwinkle's fingers on the keyboard.  Rain lashed against the panoramic windows of the Peterson & Sons law firm, blurring the already indistinct cityscape of Chicago into a grey, impressionistic smear.  Agnes, her usually neat auburn bun slightly askew, wrestled with a particularly recalcitrant spreadsheet, the numbers swimming before her tired eyes like disgruntled minnows.  The air hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and impending deadlines; a familiar aroma in the stifling, air-conditioned sanctuary that was her life for the past ten years.  Tonight, however, the usual hum of pressure was amplified by a distinct tremor of unease, a feeling that something – something big – was about to break.

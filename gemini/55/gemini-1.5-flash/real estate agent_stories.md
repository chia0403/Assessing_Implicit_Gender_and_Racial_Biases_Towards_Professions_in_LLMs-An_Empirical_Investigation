The humid Charleston air hung heavy, thick with the scent of jasmine and impending rain.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless symphony from the ancient oak trees lining the cobblestone street, their chorus a counterpoint to the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of Seraphina “Sera” Beaumont’s stilettos against the worn brick.  Sera, a real estate agent whose tailored linen suit was already damp with perspiration, adjusted the perfectly placed strand of pearls at her throat.  The wrought-iron gates of the historic Ansonia mansion loomed before her, a gothic masterpiece shrouded in the oppressive afternoon heat, a silent testament to Charleston's opulent past and its equally captivating secrets.  This sale, if she could pull it off, would solidify her place at the top of the city's competitive real estate market, but a nagging feeling, colder than the impending storm, told her this particular property held something more than just square footage and historical significance.

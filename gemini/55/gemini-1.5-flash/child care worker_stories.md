The biting November wind howled a mournful tune around the edges of Maplewood Daycare, rattling the already frost-kissed windows and whistling through the gaps in the aging clapboard.  Inside, however, a comforting warmth radiated from the brightly coloured walls adorned with finger-painted masterpieces and slightly lopsided construction paper turkeys.  Twenty-three small humans, ranging from barely-walkers to pre-teens grappling with the complexities of long division, buzzed around the room like a hive of particularly energetic bees.  Amelia Grace Hawthorne, a woman whose smile lines were as deeply etched as the grooves in an old vinyl record, surveyed the chaos with a calm, practiced eye.  Her auburn braid, usually meticulously plaited, was slightly askew, a testament to the day's earlier wrestling match with a particularly determined three-year-old named Leo.  But Amelia's gaze held none of the frustration one might expect; instead, it shone with a quiet, almost maternal affection for the miniature humans under her charge.

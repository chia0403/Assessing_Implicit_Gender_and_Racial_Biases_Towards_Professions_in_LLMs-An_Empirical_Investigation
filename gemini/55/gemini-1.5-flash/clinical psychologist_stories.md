The chipped porcelain mug warmed Dr. Eleanor Vance’s hands, a meager comfort against the November chill seeping through the drafty window of her office.  Rain lashed against the panes, a relentless drumming that mirrored the anxiety gnawing at the edges of her own composure.  Her office, nestled on the third floor of a converted brownstone in Brooklyn’s Park Slope, was usually a sanctuary – a calm haven of neutral colours and carefully curated plants.  Today, however, the comforting scent of lavender and sandalwood was overwhelmed by the metallic tang of fear.  Eleanor, a clinical psychologist specializing in trauma and PTSD, found herself unusually unsettled, a feeling amplified by the insistent tapping at her door, a rhythm that seemed to beat in time with the storm outside. The worn, leather-bound armchair opposite her desk remained empty, a silent testament to the cancelled appointment, leaving a void that felt heavier than the approaching snow.

The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Jack Hartigan’s office, a relentless rhythm mirroring the pounding headache behind his eyes.  The air hung thick and sour with the smell of stale coffee and desperation – a familiar aroma in the cramped, dimly lit space that served as both his workplace and, increasingly, his refuge.  Outside, the neon glow of the city’s underbelly bled through the grimy windows, painting lurid streaks across the peeling paintwork.  This was Los Angeles, 1947, and the city’s glamour was a thin veneer over a festering core of secrets and shadows, a landscape Jack Hartigan knew intimately.  The chipped porcelain ashtray on his desk overflowed with cigarette butts, a testament to another sleepless night chasing a phantom – or perhaps, tonight, a very real killer.

The biting November wind whipped around Elias Thorne’s thick work boots as he perched precariously on the I-beam, a hundred feet above the churning grey expanse of the Hudson River.  Below, the skeletal frame of the new luxury condominium, nicknamed "The Zenith," clawed at the sky, a testament to ambition and concrete.  Elias, a man whose calloused hands bore the map of a life spent wrestling steel and battling the elements, adjusted his hard hat, the wind tugging at the worn brim. The city sprawled before him, a glittering tapestry of lights beginning to prick the darkening sky – a beautiful, indifferent backdrop to the dangerous dance he was performing.  The air smelled of fresh-poured concrete, exhaust fumes, and the metallic tang of rain on iron.  This was Elias’s world – a dizzying, unforgiving, and strangely beautiful one.

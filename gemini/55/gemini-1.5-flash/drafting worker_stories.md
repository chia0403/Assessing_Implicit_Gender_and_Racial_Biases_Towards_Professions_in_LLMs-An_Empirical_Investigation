The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic scratch of Elias Thorne's pencil on mylar.  Dust motes, disturbed by the slow, deliberate sweep of his hand, danced in the pale light filtering through the grime-coated windows overlooking the perpetually grey Birmingham skyline. Elias, a man whose life seemed as meticulously drawn as the blueprints he produced, sat hunched over his drafting board, the scent of stale coffee and India ink clinging to his worn tweed jacket.  Outside, the November rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of the aging factory, a relentless percussion accompanying his solitary work.  The only other sound was the low murmur of the aging heating system, a rusty groan that mirrored the weariness etched onto Elias's face.  Tonight, however, the usual quiet solitude of his work was about to be shattered.

The chipped paint on the windowsill matched the chipped veneer of Dr. Eleanor Vance’s composure.  Rain lashed against the glass of her office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside her newest client.  Eleanor, a counseling psychologist with a practice nestled in the quiet, almost forgotten corner of Oakhaven, Massachusetts, sighed, the sound barely audible above the drumming of the downpour.  The scent of old books and damp earth clung to the air, a familiar comfort in the otherwise sterile environment.  Her worn leather armchair, a testament to countless hours spent listening to the anxieties and heartaches of others, creaked softly as she shifted, her gaze drawn to the overflowing bookshelf crammed with titles promising solace and understanding – a promise she herself sometimes struggled to keep.  Tonight, however, the weight of those promises felt heavier than usual.  Across from her, a young woman with eyes the color of a stormy sea clutched a tissue, her silence more deafening than any verbal outburst could have been.

The rain hammered against the grimy windows of the dilapidated law office, a relentless percussion mirroring the relentless pressure building inside Amelia "Amy" Blackwood.  The year was 1932, and the city of Chicago, a swirling vortex of ambition and despair, thrummed with the raw energy of the Great Depression.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of light piercing the gloom, illuminating the chipped mahogany desk and the half-eaten sandwich that sat forgotten on top of a stack of legal briefs. Amy, a young lawyer with sharp, intelligent eyes and a will forged in the fires of poverty, stared at the documents, a frown etched deep into her face.  She was wrestling with a case that seemed destined to swallow her whole: the defense of a struggling seamstress accused of stealing fabric from a powerful garment magnate.

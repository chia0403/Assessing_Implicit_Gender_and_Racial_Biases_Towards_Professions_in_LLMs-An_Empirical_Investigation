The chipped Formica tabletop of Amelia "Amy" Cartwright's desk reflected the fluorescent hum of the office, a pale, sickly yellow that clung to the air like stale coffee.  Dust motes danced in the lone shaft of sunlight piercing the grimy window, illuminating the swirling patterns of the chipped paint on the windowsill.  Outside, the grey London sky wept a perpetual drizzle, mirroring the relentless monotony of Amy's days.  She was a secretary, a glorified filing cabinet with a pulse, to Mr. Silas Blackwood, a reclusive, notoriously demanding author of historical thrillers.  Her workspace, tucked away in the back corner of a century-old building on a quiet side street of Bloomsbury, was a testament to the enduring quietude of desperation.  Paper, reports, and countless rejection slips littered her desk, a silent testament to the countless manuscripts she had processed.  A half-eaten croissant, forgotten from yesterday's lunch, sat beside a pen stained with dried ink, a still life of a life lived in the margins.

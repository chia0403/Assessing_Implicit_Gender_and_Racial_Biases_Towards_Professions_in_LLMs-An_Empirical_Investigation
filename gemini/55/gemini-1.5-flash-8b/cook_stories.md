The air in "The Rusty Spoon," a greasy spoon diner nestled between a dilapidated textile mill and a burgeoning artisanal bakery, hung thick with the scent of frying bacon and simmering coffee.  Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof, a relentless percussion that echoed the frantic rhythm of Amelia "Amy" Blackwood's movements.  Amy, a cook with hands that spoke a language older than the diner itself, expertly flipped a stack of pancakes, each one a miniature masterpiece of golden perfection.  Outside, the November wind howled, whipping the last of the leaves across the deserted street.  Amy knew this particular storm brought with it a unique kind of energy, a restlessness that mirrored the churning in her own stomach.  Today felt different.

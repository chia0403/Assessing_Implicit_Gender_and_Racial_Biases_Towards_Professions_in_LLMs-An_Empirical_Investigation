The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Blackwood's hands, the faint scent of Earl Grey clinging to the steam.  Sunlight, filtered through the dusty floral curtains of her tiny flat, painted stripes of gold across the worn wooden floor.  Dust motes danced in the ethereal glow, each a tiny, silent dancer in a world Amelia, a writer of historical fiction, often felt too large for.  Outside, the clatter of London traffic, a symphony of horns and sirens, echoed against the brick walls of her terraced house, a constant, insistent soundtrack to her quiet existence.  Amelia, at twenty-eight, felt her own creative voice, so vital and vibrant in her imagination, growing increasingly faint, lost somewhere between the clamor of the city and the relentless hum of deadlines.

The stale scent of chalk dust and old textbooks clung to the air in Room 307 like a persistent cough.  Rain lashed against the warped windowpanes of Northwood High, each gust a mournful sigh against the backdrop of flickering fluorescent lights.  Thirty-two pairs of teenage eyes, glazed with varying degrees of boredom and apathy, stared back at Ms. Eleanor Vance, their history teacher.  Eleanor, a woman whose auburn braid was perpetually escaping its clasp and whose sensible cardigan had seen better days, surveyed her classroom with a sigh of her own.  The worn copy of "A People's History of the United States" lay open on her desk, a silent testament to the uphill battle she fought each day to ignite a spark of interest in the past within these young, digitally-obsessed minds.  Tonight, however, the usual hum of teenage disengagement was punctuated by an unsettling stillness, a pregnant pause that hung heavier than the autumn rain outside.

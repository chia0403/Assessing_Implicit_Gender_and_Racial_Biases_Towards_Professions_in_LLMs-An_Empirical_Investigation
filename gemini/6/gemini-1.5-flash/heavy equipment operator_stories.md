The biting wind whipped across the desolate expanse of the Alberta tar sands, stinging Amelia “Amy” Blackwood’s cheeks raw.  The sun, a weak, watery disc behind a bruised purple sky, cast long, skeletal shadows from the towering oil derricks that dotted the landscape like rusted metal behemoths. Amy, perched high in the cab of her Caterpillar D11T, felt the rumble of the massive machine vibrate through her bones, a familiar comfort in this harsh, unforgiving land.  The scent of diesel and thawing permafrost hung heavy in the air, a potent cocktail that had become the very essence of her life for the past five years.  Today, however, the familiar hum of the engine held a note of disquiet; a premonition of trouble as thick and cloying as the oil she moved every day.  The radio crackled with static, the only sound besides the rhythmic churn of the tracks and the whisper of the wind, a silence that felt pregnant with something unspoken, something dangerous.

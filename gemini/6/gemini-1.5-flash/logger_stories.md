The biting wind howled a mournful song through the skeletal branches of the ancient Douglas firs, whistling a tune only Elias Thorne understood.  Elias, a man carved from the same granite as the mountains themselves, squinted against the swirling snow that blurred the already fading light. His breath plumed out in ragged white clouds, each exhale a testament to the bone-chilling cold that had settled over the Cascade Range like a shroud.  The year was 1927, and the towering timber of the Washington wilderness – his kingdom, his livelihood, and his prison –  stood silent and watchful under a sky the color of bruised plums.  The air, thick with the scent of pine and damp earth, held the promise of a long, brutal winter, a winter Elias Thorne was determined to conquer, one fallen giant at a time.

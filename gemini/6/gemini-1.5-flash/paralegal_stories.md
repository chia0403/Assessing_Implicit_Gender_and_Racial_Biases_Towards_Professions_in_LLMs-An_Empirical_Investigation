The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Hernandez’s hands, a meager defense against the November chill that seeped through the drafty windows of the law firm.  Rain lashed against the glass, blurring the already indistinct cityscape of downtown San Francisco.  The rhythmic drumming was a counterpoint to the frantic tap-tap-tap of Amelia’s fingers on her keyboard, the glow of the monitor illuminating the worry lines etched around her usually bright eyes.  She hadn’t slept properly in days, fueled by lukewarm coffee and the pressure cooker that was the upcoming Miller v. Consolidated Industries case.  The air hung thick with the scent of old paper, stale coffee, and a faint, underlying tang of desperation, a smell Amelia had come to associate intimately with the underbelly of legal battles fought in the city’s shadow.

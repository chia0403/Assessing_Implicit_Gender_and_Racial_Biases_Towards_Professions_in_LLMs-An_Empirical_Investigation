The chipped porcelain mug warmed Seraphina "Sera" Nightingale's hands, the lukewarm chamomile tea doing little to soothe the tremor that ran through her.  Rain lashed against the single, grime-streaked window of her attic room, mirroring the tempest raging inside her.  The attic itself, nestled within the crooked eaves of a crumbling Victorian house in the cobbled backstreets of Prague, was a chaotic symphony of half-packed suitcases, discarded sheet music stained with coffee and tears, and a single, battered guitar leaning against a dusty wardrobe.  The air hung thick with the scent of damp plaster, old wood, and a faint, lingering perfume of sandalwood – a ghostly reminder of the life she’d left behind, a life of opulent opera houses and demanding conductors, a life she wasn’t sure she wanted back.

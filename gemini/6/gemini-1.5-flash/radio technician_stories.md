The static crackled like a thousand angry fireflies trapped in Amelia "Amy" Hawthorne's headphones.  Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof of the shack, a relentless drumming that mirrored the frantic thrumming in her chest.  She hunched closer to the sputtering valve radio, its glowing tubes casting a sickly green light on her grease-stained overalls.  The air hung thick with the smell of ozone and damp earth, the scent clinging to everything in the isolated radio station perched precariously on the windswept cliffs overlooking the churning grey expanse of the Atlantic.  The year was 1943, and the only company Amy had was the erratic hiss of the ether and the ever-present threat of a German U-boat surfacing just beyond the horizon.

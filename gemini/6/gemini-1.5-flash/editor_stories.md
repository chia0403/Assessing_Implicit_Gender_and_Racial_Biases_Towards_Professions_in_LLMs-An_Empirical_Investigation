The biting November wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne's auburn hair across her face as she hurried down Bleecker Street, the collar of her worn tweed coat pulled high.  Rain, a relentless drizzle that had begun hours ago, slicked the cobblestones, reflecting the neon glow of a nearby pizzeria in fractured shards of light.  The scent of roasting chestnuts and damp earth hung heavy in the air, a familiar, melancholic perfume to Amelia, a constant companion throughout the long, grey evenings of New York's autumn.  She pushed open the heavy oak door of "The Quill & Ink," her tiny, yet surprisingly vibrant, publishing house, leaving behind the chaotic symphony of the city and stepping into the relative quiet of her sanctuary – a haven of towering bookshelves, overflowing ashtrays, and the comforting aroma of old paper and strong coffee.  The single, bare bulb hanging above her desk cast a warm, inviting glow, illuminating the overflowing pile of manuscripts that awaited her discerning eye.  Tonight, however, Amelia felt a different kind of pressure, a weight far heavier than deadlines and grammatical errors.  This manuscript… this one felt different.

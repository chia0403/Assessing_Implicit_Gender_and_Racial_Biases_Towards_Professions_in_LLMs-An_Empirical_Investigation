The biting November wind whipped Amelia Grace Hawthorne's unruly auburn curls across her face as she leaned precariously over the crumbling stone wall. Below, the churning grey sea gnawed at the jagged cliffs of Cornwall, its relentless rhythm a counterpoint to the frantic hammering of her own heart.  The salt spray, heavy with the scent of brine and decaying seaweed, stung her eyes, blurring the already hazy landscape.  Amelia, a painter renowned for her dramatic seascapes, hadn't felt this close to her muse – or this close to oblivion – in years.  Her easel, laden with oils and canvases, stood abandoned a few feet away, a stark testament to the tempest raging both within and without her.  The small, isolated cottage perched precariously on the cliff edge, its windows like vacant eyes staring out at the unforgiving ocean, was her sanctuary, her prison, and the unlikely birthplace of her most ambitious work yet.

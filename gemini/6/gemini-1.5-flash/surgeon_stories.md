The biting November wind whipped Dr. Eleanor Vance’s dark hair across her face as she hurried down the cobbled street, the collar of her heavy coat pulled high.  Rain, a relentless drizzle that had been falling since dawn, plastered her auburn curls to her scalp.  The gaslights of Edinburgh’s Old Town flickered, casting long, distorted shadows that danced and writhed like phantoms in the narrow alleyways.  Above, the looming gothic spires of St. Giles' Cathedral pierced the bruised twilight sky, their stone faces grim and unforgiving.  The scent of coal smoke and brine hung heavy in the air, a familiar aroma that clung to the city like a second skin. Tonight, however, it was overlaid with something else – a metallic tang that pricked at Eleanor’s nostrils, a scent she knew all too well: the smell of blood.

The rain lashed against the attic windowpane, mimicking the frantic tapping of Elias Thornecroft's fingers on the worn oak desk.  His breath plumed white in the frigid air of the neglected space, which served as both his writing sanctuary and, more often than not, his prison.  Dust motes danced in the weak light filtering through the grimy skylight, illuminating stacks of manuscripts, rejected query letters, and half-empty mugs of lukewarm tea.  Thornecroft Manor, once a grand estate overlooking the windswept cliffs of Cornwall, had fallen into a state of genteel decay, mirroring the fortunes of its sole occupant and the dwindling hope within his writer's heart.  Elias, once a promising literary prodigy, now found himself trapped in a cycle of writer's block, fueled by self-doubt and the ever-present pressure of his family's fading legacy. The salty tang of the sea, usually a source of inspiration, tonight only served to remind him of the vast, indifferent expanse between him and success.

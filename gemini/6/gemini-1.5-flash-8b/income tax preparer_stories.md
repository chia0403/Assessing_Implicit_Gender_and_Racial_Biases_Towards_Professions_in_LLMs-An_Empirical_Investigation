The fluorescent lights of the "Tax-Tastic Solutions" office hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Amelia Penelope Finch's life for the past seven years.  Paperwork, piled high like miniature mountains, whispered tales of mortgages, medical expenses, and the ever-elusive deductions.  The air, thick with the scent of stale coffee and desperation, hung heavy in the small, cramped office, a testament to the tax season's relentless pressure.  Amelia, a woman whose sharp wit often clashed with her meticulously organised approach, tapped furiously on her keyboard, the rhythmic clacks echoing against the almost-silent roar of the hum of the fluorescent lights.  Today, though, the whispers and the hum seemed to combine into an ominous crescendo.  Across the small room, a client, Mr. Henderson, a man whose face was as lined as a tax form, sat slumped in his chair, clutching a crumpled receipt.  The air in the room thickened with a palpable tension, a tension Amelia knew all too well.

The greasy air hung thick with the smell of burnt oil and something faintly floral, a disconcerting aroma that clung to the chipped paint of the "Rusty Wrench" garage like a stubborn stain.  Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof, a relentless percussion against the rhythmic clang of hammers and the whirring of a jack.  Inside, amidst a chaotic symphony of metal on metal, stood  Alistair Finch, a meticulous auto mechanic with calloused hands and eyes that held the wisdom of a thousand engine rebuilds.  Thirty-two years old, with a mop of unruly brown hair that seemed to defy gravity and cling to his forehead, Alistair was surrounded by the mangled remnants of a vintage convertible.  The car, a faded turquoise Ford Thunderbird from 1967, lay on its side, a victim of a recent, and rather spectacular, collision.  Alistair frowned, tracing the line of a dented fender with a calloused fingertip.  This job was going to take longer than he'd anticipated.  His carefully ordered toolbox lay discarded on the grimy floor, its contents scattered like fallen stars.  The air felt thick with the quiet, almost tangible tension of a problem waiting to be solved.

The insistent chirping of a rogue cricket, somehow trapped inside the brightly painted classroom, punctuated the quiet hum of the fluorescent lights.  Amelia Rose Hawthorne, a woman whose smile could melt the polar ice caps and whose patience could rival that of a saint, knelt beside a miniature table, gently guiding five-year-old Leo's hand as he struggled to glue googly eyes onto a construction paper caterpillar.  Outside, the October wind whipped fallen leaves into swirling dervishes across the playground of Maplewood Elementary, a small, red-brick building nestled in the heart of sleepy Willow Creek, Vermont.  The air, tinged with the scent of woodsmoke and impending snow, hinted at the approaching winter, a season Amelia always anticipated with a mixture of cozy contentment and a slight, inexplicable melancholy.  This year, however, the melancholy held a sharper edge, a premonition she couldn't quite place.

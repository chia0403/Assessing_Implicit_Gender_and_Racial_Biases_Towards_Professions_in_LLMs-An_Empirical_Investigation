Rain lashed against the arched window of Dr. Elias Thorne's office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside his newest patient.  The room, usually a sanctuary of warm earth tones and strategically placed greenery, felt oppressive under the onslaught of the November storm.  Thick clouds swallowed the last vestiges of daylight, leaving only the muted glow of a Tiffany lamp illuminating the intricate Persian rug and the two leather armchairs facing each other. Outside, the wind howled like a banshee, rattling the panes of the old Victorian house that housed Elias' practice on the outskirts of Hollow Creek, a secluded town nestled deep within the Appalachian Mountains.  Here, in a place where secrets were as common as the ancient oaks that clung to the steep slopes, Dr. Elias Thorne, PhD, listened to the unraveling of lives, one carefully chosen word at a time. Tonight, however, something felt different, a prickle of unease that had nothing to do with the weather.

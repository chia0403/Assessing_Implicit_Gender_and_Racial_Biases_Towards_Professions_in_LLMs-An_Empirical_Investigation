Rain lashed against the grimy window of Elias Thorne's office, mirroring the tempest brewing inside him.  Neon light from the Hong Kong noodle bar across the street painted greasy streaks across the peeling paintwork, illuminating dust motes dancing in the stale air.  Elias, full name Elias Vance Thorne, sat slumped in his worn leather armchair, the springs groaning in protest beneath him. His tie, loosened and askew, hung like a wilted flower from his open collar.  The only other light in the cramped room came from a flickering desk lamp, casting long shadows that stretched and contorted the clutter of his trade:  half-empty coffee cups, overflowing ashtrays, and dog-eared files spilling their secrets onto the scarred mahogany desk.  The year was 1947, and Hong Kong, a city clinging to its precarious peace in the aftermath of the war, felt as lost and uncertain as the man who called this dingy office home.

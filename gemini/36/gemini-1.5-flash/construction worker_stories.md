The biting November wind whipped across the skeletal frame of the new skyscraper, whistling a mournful tune through the gaps in the steel girders.  Dust, the fine, grey grit of a thousand demolished dreams, swirled around Elias Thorne's boots as he clung to a rusty beam, a hundred feet above the bustling city street. Below, the relentless pulse of Manhattan throbbed, a chaotic symphony of car horns and sirens, a stark contrast to the quiet, almost meditative concentration Elias held as he tightened a bolt, his gloved hands working with the practiced ease of a seasoned craftsman.  Elias, a man whose calloused hands were more accustomed to the rough texture of concrete than the smooth surface of a polished table, was a master of his trade, a steelworker with nerves of iron and eyes that had seen more sunsets from dizzying heights than most people ever would from the ground.  This wasn't just a job; it was his domain, his sanctuary.

The salt spray kissed Elara Vance’s face, carrying the scent of brine and something else, something acrid and unfamiliar.  Elara, a painter whose canvases usually depicted the sun-drenched ochre cliffs of her native Cornwall, shivered, not entirely from the chill wind whipping across the desolate beach.  The beach itself was a stark contrast to her usual palette; grey pebbles stretched as far as the eye could see under a sky the colour of bruised plums, the rhythmic crash of the waves a relentless counterpoint to the frantic beat of her heart.  The abandoned lifeboat, half-buried in the sand, its paint peeling like sunburnt skin, was the only other significant feature of this bleak, windswept expanse of the Cornish coast, a place she’d only ever seen in the worst of winter storms.  And yet, here she was, drawn by a nameless dread and a single, enigmatic postcard.

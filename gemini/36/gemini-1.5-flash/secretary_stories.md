The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s hands, a meager comfort against the pre-dawn chill that seeped through the drafty windows of her office.  Rain lashed against the glass panes of the Blackwood Building, a relentless drumming that mirrored the insistent thrumming of anxiety in Amelia’s chest.  The city outside, usually a vibrant tapestry of yellow cabs and hurried footsteps, was muted and shadowy under the grey November sky.  Inside, the Blackwood Building – a gothic behemoth of steel and granite clinging precariously to the edge of the financial district – was a cavern of hushed whispers and the rhythmic click-clack of keyboards, a symphony of quiet desperation playing out before the day even truly began.  Amelia, perpetually perched on the edge of her worn, vinyl-covered chair, was the conductor of this silent orchestra, her life a carefully orchestrated ballet of dictation, filing, and the ever-present shadow of Mr. Silas Blackwood, her demanding and enigmatic boss.

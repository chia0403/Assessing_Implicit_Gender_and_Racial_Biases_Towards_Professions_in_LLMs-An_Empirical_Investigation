The chipped paint of Dr. Eleanor Vance’s office window framed a perpetually grey November sky, mirroring the melancholic mood that had settled over her like a damp wool blanket.  Rain lashed against the glass, a relentless drumming that echoed the frantic rhythm of her own thoughts.  Her office, nestled on the third floor of a crumbling Victorian building in the heart of  Portland, Oregon, was a testament to comfortable chaos.  Overstuffed armchairs, worn but plush, were scattered around a low coffee table burdened with half-empty mugs and stacks of unread journals.  The scent of old books and woodsmoke, a familiar comfort, did little to dispel the unease that clung to the air, a residue of her last patient’s harrowing confession.  Eleanor, a woman whose life was dedicated to untangling the knots of other people's minds, found herself increasingly tangled in her own.

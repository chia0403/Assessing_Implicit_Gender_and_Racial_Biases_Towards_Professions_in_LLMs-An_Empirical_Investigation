The biting November wind whipped around Elias Thorne’s ankles as he hurried down Bleecker Street, the collar of his threadbare overcoat pulled high.  Ink-stained fingers, perpetually smelling faintly of turpentine and ambition, burrowed deeper into his pockets.  Overhead, the gas lamps flickered, casting long, skeletal shadows that danced with the swirling leaves.  Elias, a drafting worker for the ambitious, if somewhat unscrupulous, architect, Mr. Silas Blackwood, was late.  Again.  Not that Blackwood ever noticed the precise time of his employees' arrivals – provided the blueprints were on his desk by morning, punctuality was a mere suggestion, a polite fiction in the chaotic, soot-stained heart of 1888 New York City.  The air, thick with the smells of coal smoke, roasting chestnuts, and the ever-present undercurrent of something vaguely decaying, hung heavy in Elias’ lungs.  He pushed open the heavy oak door of Blackwood’s imposing brownstone, already mentally rehearsing his excuse.

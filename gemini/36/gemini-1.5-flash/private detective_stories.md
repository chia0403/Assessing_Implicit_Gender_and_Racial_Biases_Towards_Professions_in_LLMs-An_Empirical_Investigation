The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Jack Stratton's office, a rhythm that mirrored the relentless pounding in his head.  The air hung thick with the smell of stale coffee and something vaguely floral, a perfume he couldn't quite place, clinging to the damp tweed of his worn armchair.  His office, a cramped space above a laundromat in the perpetually twilight alleyways of San Francisco's Tenderloin district, offered little respite from the city's relentless gloom.  Outside, the neon glow of a flickering liquor store sign cast long, distorted shadows, painting the grimy brick walls in a sickly yellow hue.  Jack, a man whose face bore the map of a thousand sleepless nights etched in deep lines around his tired eyes, rubbed his temples, the cheap plastic of his desk creaking under the pressure.  The crumpled photograph in his hand – a woman with eyes that held a desperate plea – was the reason for his headache, the reason the rain felt like a relentless accusation against his weary soul.

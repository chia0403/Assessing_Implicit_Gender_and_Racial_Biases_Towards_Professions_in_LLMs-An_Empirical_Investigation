The humid Louisiana air hung heavy, thick with the scent of decaying cypress and the promise of a brewing thunderstorm.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless, almost maddening soundtrack to the oppressive heat, a stark counterpoint to the hushed reverence clinging to the dilapidated plantation house.  Dust motes danced in the slivers of sunlight slicing through the gaps in the crumbling shutters.  Inside, amidst the ghostly grandeur of peeling wallpaper and cobweb-draped chandeliers, Amelia "Mia" Davenport, renowned director of gritty independent films, paced restlessly.  Her charcoal grey suit, usually sharp and commanding, was rumpled, the crisp white shirt beneath slightly damp with sweat.  The script, a dog-eared copy of *Bayou Blood*, lay abandoned on a mahogany table, its pages barely concealing the half-empty bottle of bourbon.  Mia, a woman whose career had been built on capturing the raw, unsettling beauty of the human condition, felt utterly and unexpectedly adrift.

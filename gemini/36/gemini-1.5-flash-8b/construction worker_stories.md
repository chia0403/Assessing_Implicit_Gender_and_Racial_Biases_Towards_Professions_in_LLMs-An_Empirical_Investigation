The biting wind whipped across the desolate expanse of the construction site, stinging Elias Thorne's cheeks as he wrestled with a stubborn bolt.  The skeletal framework of the new skyscraper, a gleaming monument to ambition against the grey sky, loomed above him, a stark reminder of the relentless pace of the project.  Dust, the fine, insidious kind that burrowed under skin and into lungs, coated everything: the scaffolding, the machinery, the unyielding concrete.  October had arrived, bringing with it the promise of a harsh, unforgiving winter.  Elias, a seasoned construction worker with calloused hands and eyes that held the weariness of a thousand sunrises, felt a familiar knot tighten in his stomach.  He wasn't afraid of the work, not really.  He was afraid of the silence.  The silence that fell in the late afternoon, before the last cranes lowered their heavy arms, and the men went home, leaving the steel and stone to stand sentinel in the fading light.  He was afraid of what that silence held.

The chipped Formica tabletop, stained a sickly beige from years of spilled coffee and lukewarm tea, felt strangely cold beneath Dr. Eleanor Vance's fingertips.  Sunlight, fractured by the grime-coated windowpanes, illuminated dust motes dancing in the air.  Outside, the relentless grey of a London November pressed against the weathered brick facade of the small, independent practice.  Eleanor, a counseling psychologist with a practice called "Inner Compass," sighed, the sound lost in the muted hum of the city outside.  Across the room, a faded poster advertising a local yoga retreat, featuring an image of a serene figure contorting in a pretzel, mocked the quiet desperation she felt.  It was 9:00 AM, and the waiting room was empty, except for the persistent scratching of a pen on paper, a rhythmic counterpoint to the silence.

The chipped porcelain sink, stained a sickly yellow by years of lukewarm water and forgotten limes, reflected the flickering neon sign of the "Grand Zenith Hotel" outside.  Rain hammered against the double-paned windows, a relentless percussion against the quiet hum of the ventilation system.  Inside, Amelia Petrova, concierge extraordinaire of the Grand Zenith, meticulously arranged a stack of handwritten guest requests.  Each note, a miniature prayer for a smooth and flawless stay, was a testament to the carefully curated charm of the hotel, a charm Amelia, with her impeccable manners and encyclopedic knowledge of the city, was determined to preserve.  Tonight, however, the usual symphony of requests—a reservation at a Michelin-starred restaurant, a late-night taxi, a forgotten child's teddy bear—was oddly muted.  A single, almost desperate, note lay atop the pile, its pale yellow paper barely visible in the gloom.

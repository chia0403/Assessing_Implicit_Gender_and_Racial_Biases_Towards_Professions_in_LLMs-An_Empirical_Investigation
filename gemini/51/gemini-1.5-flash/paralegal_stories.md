The chipped paint of Amelia Hernandez’s desk seemed to mirror the cracks in the foundation of her life.  Dust motes danced in the weak afternoon sun slanting through the grimy window of the cramped, shared office space, illuminating the overflowing files stacked haphazardly on every available surface.  The air hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and desperation, a familiar aroma in the cut-rate legal offices of downtown San Francisco.  Amelia, a paralegal with five years of experience and a mountain of unpaid student loans, sighed, the sound swallowed by the low hum of the overworked air conditioner.  Outside, the city roared – a cacophony of sirens, car horns, and the distant, muffled cries of the homeless – but inside, the only sound was the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of her fingers on the keyboard, a relentless counterpoint to the growing dread churning in her stomach.  This wasn't just another case; this was the one that could break her.

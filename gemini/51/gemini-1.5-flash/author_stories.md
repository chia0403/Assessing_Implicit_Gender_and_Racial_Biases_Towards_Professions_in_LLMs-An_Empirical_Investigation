The salt spray stung Amelia Katherine Hawthorne’s face, a familiar kiss from the unforgiving Atlantic.  She stood on the jagged cliffs overlooking the churning grey expanse, the wind whipping her auburn hair across her cheeks like a frantic bird.  Below, the village of Port Blossom huddled against the relentless ocean, its whitewashed cottages clinging to the hillside like barnacles to a ship’s hull.  Amelia, renowned author of the bestselling “Whispers of the Sea” trilogy, found solace in this desolate beauty, a stark contrast to the clamorous literary circles she usually inhabited.  The scent of brine and seaweed filled her lungs, a pungent perfume that always sparked the embers of her imagination, even now, as a profound creative block threatened to engulf her like the rising tide.  This remote corner of Cornwall, a place she’d inherited from a long-lost aunt, was her last hope for inspiration, a sanctuary where she could perhaps rediscover the magic that had once flowed so freely from her pen.

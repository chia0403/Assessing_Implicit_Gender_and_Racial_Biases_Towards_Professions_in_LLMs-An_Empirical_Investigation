The biting November wind whipped Amelia “Amy” Blackwood’s auburn hair across her face as she stared out at the churning grey expanse of the North Sea.  The air tasted of salt and impending rain, a perfect mirror to the tempest brewing inside her.  She clutched a lukewarm coffee, its flimsy paper cup doing little to ward off the chill that seeped into her bones, a chill that had nothing to do with the weather.  Amy Blackwood, celebrated director of the critically acclaimed, albeit commercially underwhelming, "The Obsidian Mirror," was standing on the windswept cliffs of Hoy, Orkney, a location chosen not for its picturesque beauty – though it possessed a stark, haunting grandeur – but for its brutal, unforgiving nature, a nature that mirrored the project currently threatening to consume her.  The wind howled a mournful dirge, a fitting soundtrack to the looming disaster that was "The Serpent's Kiss," her ambitious, possibly doomed, new film.

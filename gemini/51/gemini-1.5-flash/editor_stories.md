The chipped mug warmed Amelia Stone’s hands, the lukewarm tea doing little to thaw the icy grip of the November wind whistling through the gaps in the sash windows of her office.  Rain lashed against the grimy panes, blurring the already indistinct view of cobbled Church Street below. Amelia, senior editor at Blackwood & Sons Publishing House, sighed, the sound swallowed by the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of her fingers on the worn wooden desk.  The air hung thick with the scent of old paper, damp wool, and the faint, lingering aroma of pipe tobacco – a testament to decades of literary toil within those four walls.  Blackwood & Sons, a venerable institution nestled in the heart of a perpetually rain-soaked Edinburgh, felt more like a mausoleum than a modern publishing house, a fact Amelia both lamented and secretly cherished.  Tonight, however, the comforting weight of tradition felt less like a comforting blanket and more like a suffocating shroud.  The manuscript on her desk, bound in faded blue, pulsed with an unsettling energy that had Amelia's stomach twisting into knots.  This wasn't just another historical romance; this was… different.

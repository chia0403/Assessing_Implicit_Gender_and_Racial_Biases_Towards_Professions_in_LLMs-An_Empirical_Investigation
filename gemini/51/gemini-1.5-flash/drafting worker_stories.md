The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic scratch-scratch-scratch of Amelia Josephine Cartwright’s pencil on Mylar.  Dust motes, illuminated like tiny constellations, danced in the shafts of light slicing through the gloom.  Amelia, her brow furrowed in concentration, meticulously inked the intricate details of a proposed extension for the St. Jude's Hospital – a project that had consumed her for the past three months, and threatened to consume her sanity as well.  The air smelled faintly of stale coffee, printer toner, and the lingering scent of fear – the ever-present aroma of impending deadlines.  Outside, the Chicago wind howled a mournful ballad against the towering skyscraper windows, a stark contrast to the meticulous calm Amelia strove to maintain within her own carefully constructed world of lines and angles.

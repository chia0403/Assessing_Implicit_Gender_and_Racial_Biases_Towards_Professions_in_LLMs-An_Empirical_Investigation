The rain hammered against the leaded glass windows of  Alistair Finchley’s office, mimicking the insistent thrum of anxiety in his chest.  Outside, the gas lamps of Chancery Lane blurred into hazy halos in the November gloom, painting the cobbled street in shades of ochre and charcoal. Alistair, whose full name was Alistair Humphrey Finchley, Esquire, adjusted his spectacles and stared out at the downpour.  His perfectly ordered office, usually a sanctuary of legal tomes and polished mahogany, felt suffocating tonight.  The air was thick with the scent of old paper and something else, something faintly metallic, that clung to the back of his throat like a whispered threat. It had been a week since the disappearance of Lady Beatrice Ashworth, and Alistair, her solicitor and – he dared to admit to himself –  close friend, was no closer to finding her than the day she vanished from her opulent Mayfair townhouse.

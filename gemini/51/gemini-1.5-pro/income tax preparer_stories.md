Rain lashed against the window of Agnes Periwinkle's tiny office, a rhythmic drumming that mirrored the throbbing headache blooming behind her eyes.  Tax season was in full, furious swing, and Agnes, a woman whose life usually revolved around the meticulous organization of receipts and the comforting predictability of IRS forms, felt herself drowning in a sea of W-2s and 1099s.  Her office, nestled above a laundromat in the sleepy, perpetually overcast town of Dullsville, Oregon, was usually a haven of order.  Tonight, however, it resembled a paper hurricane had blown through, leaving a chaotic trail of manila folders, half-empty coffee cups, and the faint, lingering scent of desperation.  The fluorescent lights buzzed overhead, adding their own shrill note to the symphony of stress, as Agnes, her cardigan slightly askew and her normally neat bun threatening to unravel, stared with bleary eyes at a particularly perplexing Schedule C.  This year, it seemed, even the simplest sole proprietorships were conspiring against her.

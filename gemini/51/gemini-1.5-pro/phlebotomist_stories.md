The fluorescent lights of St. Jude's Hospital hummed a monotonous tune, punctuated by the rhythmic squeak of Dr. Anya Sharma's rubber-soled shoes against the polished linoleum floor.  Amelia "Amy" Rose Hawthorne, a phlebotomist with a preternatural ability to find even the most elusive veins, navigated the bustling corridors with the practiced ease of a seasoned sailor in familiar waters.  It was a Tuesday morning in late November, the kind of grey, damp chill that seeped into the very bones of Chicago and clung there like a second skin. Inside, however, the air was thick with the sterile tang of antiseptic and the low murmur of human suffering, a constant reminder of the delicate balance between life and death that permeated these walls.  Amy, her lavender scrubs a stark contrast to the clinical white and beige of the hospital, stopped at the nurses' station of the oncology ward, her phlebotomy cart rattling softly beside her.  A crumpled requisition slip in her hand held the name of her next patient, a name that made her pause, a flicker of unease dancing in her hazel eyes.

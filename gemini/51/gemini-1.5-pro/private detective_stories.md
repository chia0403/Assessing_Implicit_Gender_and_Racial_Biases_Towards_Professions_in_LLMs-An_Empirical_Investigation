Rain lashed against the grime-streaked window of Isabella "Izzy" Diaz's office, mirroring the tempest brewing in her gut.  Neon signs from the street below painted fleeting, lurid streaks across the peeling floral wallpaper, illuminating dust motes dancing in the flickering light of a bare bulb. Her office, perched precariously above a Cantonese noodle shop in San Francisco's Chinatown, smelled of stale cigarette smoke, cheap coffee, and the faint, lingering aroma of five-spice powder.  The year was 1948, a city still clinging to the shadows of wartime, and Izzy, with her sharp jawline, kohl-rimmed eyes, and a fedora pulled low over her dark curls, was about to take a case that would drag her deeper into those shadows than ever before.

The chipped porcelain sink, stained a perpetual terracotta from years of lukewarm milk and discarded yeast, gurgled softly.  Amelia Butterfield, a baker with eyes the colour of toasted almonds and hands perpetually dusted with flour, peered into the bubbling cauldron.  A symphony of sizzling and popping filled the small, cluttered bakery, "The Golden Crust," nestled amongst the towering brick buildings of 19th century Spitalfields, London.  Sunlight, filtered through grimy windowpanes, painted intricate patterns on the polished wooden counter, highlighting the delicate arrangement of freshly baked sourdough loaves. The scent of cinnamon, nutmeg, and freshly ground coffee hung heavy in the air, a comforting aroma that always calmed the anxieties of Amelia's restless mind.  Today, though, the familiar scent seemed to hold a particularly poignant edge.

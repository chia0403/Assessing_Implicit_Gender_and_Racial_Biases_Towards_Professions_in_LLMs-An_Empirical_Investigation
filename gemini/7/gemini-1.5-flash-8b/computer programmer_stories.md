Rain lashed against the panoramic window of Elias Thorne's apartment, mirroring the tempest brewing inside him.  The city of Veridian, sprawling beneath the downpour, was a blur of neon and steel, a stark contrast to the muted grey tones reflecting in the liquid-filled expanse before him.  Elias, a 28-year-old computer programmer with eyes that held the perpetual glow of late-night debugging sessions, hunched over his custom-built workstation.  Stacks of code, meticulously organized yet impossibly tangled, littered the desk.  The air, thick with the metallic tang of soldering flux and the faint aroma of instant coffee, was heavy with the weight of unanswered emails and looming deadlines.  Veridian, a sprawling metropolis built on the promise of technological utopia, felt suffocating tonight.  This was his third all-nighter in as many weeks, fuelled by the promise of solving a puzzle that had eluded him for months: a glitch in the city's neural network, a glitch that was twisting Veridian's digital heart and disturbing its essential rhythm.

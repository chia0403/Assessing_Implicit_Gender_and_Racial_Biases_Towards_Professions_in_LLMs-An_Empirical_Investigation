The fluorescent lights of the Sterling & Stone law firm hummed a monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Amelia Blackwood's meticulously ordered day.  The mahogany reception desk, polished to a mirror-like sheen, reflected Amelia's perfectly pressed beige blouse and the meticulously arranged, almost clinical, array of stationery.  A half-eaten granola bar, strategically placed beneath a stack of legal briefs, was the only concession to the human frailty she was trying to maintain.  Outside, the grey London sky mirrored the muted tones of the waiting room, the rhythmic patter of rain against the window a constant, almost comforting, percussion. Amelia, a receptionist for Sterling & Stone, a firm specializing in corporate mergers and acquisitions, was, by all accounts, a paragon of professionalism.  But beneath the surface, a quiet rebellion simmered. Today, however, felt different.

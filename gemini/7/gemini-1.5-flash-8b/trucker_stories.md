The biting wind whipped across the desolate plains of the Dakotas, stinging Amelia "Amy" Dubois' cheeks as she wrestled the oversized rig – the "Ironclad" – into a precarious parking spot at the dusty roadside diner.  The neon sign of the "Rusty Mug" flickered, casting an eerie, orange glow on the already-grim landscape.  Dust devils danced across the cracked asphalt, swirling around the chipped paint of the truck, a testament to the ceaseless, unforgiving nature of the American West.

Amy, a woman carved from granite and resilience, leaned against the driver's seat, the worn leather smelling faintly of diesel and roadkill.  Her eyes, the color of a stormy sky, held a depth that matched the vastness of the prairie stretching beyond the diner's small parking lot.  Forty-one years old, and a lifetime spent navigating treacherous highways and even more treacherous personalities, Amy was, in her own quiet way, a legend on the open road.  The Ironclad wasn't just a truck; it was her home, her sanctuary, and the silent witness to the endless stories she carried within her heart.  Today, however, a new narrative began to unfold.

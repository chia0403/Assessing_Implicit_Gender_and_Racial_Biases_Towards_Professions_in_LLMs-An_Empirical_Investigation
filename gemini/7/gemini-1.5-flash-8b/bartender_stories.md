The flickering neon sign of the Rusty Mug cast a sickly yellow glow over the rain-slicked cobblestones of Old Southwark.  The air, thick with the scent of brewing ale and damp earth, clung to Elias Thorne, a bartender with eyes the colour of storm clouds and a perpetual weariness etched onto his face.  He wiped down the sticky mahogany bar, the rhythmic scrape a counterpoint to the steady, almost hypnotic drip of a leaky faucet somewhere in the shadowed backroom.  Tonight, the pub was a symphony of hushed whispers, the clinking of tankards, and the low rumble of conversation, a familiar, comforting cacophony that usually lulled him into a sort of weary peace.  But tonight, something felt different.  A prickle of unease, like a stray shard of ice in his hand, was settling deep within his gut.  The silence after a particularly boisterous group of sailors had cleared out felt heavier, pregnant with unspoken anxieties.

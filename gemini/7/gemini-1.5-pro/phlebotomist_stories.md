The fluorescent lights of St. Jude's Hospital hummed a monotonous B-flat, a soundtrack Evelyn Reed had grown accustomed to over her fifteen years as a phlebotomist.  The pre-dawn air, filtering in through the cracked window of the lab, carried the faint scent of chlorine and coffee, a familiar cocktail that signaled the start of another day.  Evelyn, her name tag pinned neatly to her crisp white coat, adjusted the latex gloves clinging to her hands. Outside, the Chicago sky was just beginning to blush a pale pink, but inside, the cool, sterile environment of the phlebotomy lab remained unchanged, a world of vials, needles, and the quiet murmur of anticipation that always preceded the morning's first blood draw.  Today, however, something felt different. A prickle of unease ran down Evelyn's spine, a sensation she couldn't quite place, but one that tightened her grip on the tourniquet she held.

The flickering fluorescent lights of H&R Block cast a sickly green glow over Amelia Grace Hawthorne’s face, highlighting the faint smudges of ink beneath her fingernails.  It was April 14th, the eve of tax apocalypse, and the air in the small, strip-mall office vibrated with a palpable blend of desperation and stale coffee. Outside, a relentless drizzle mirrored the dreary mood within.  The small town of Harmony Springs, Oregon, usually a picture of bucolic charm, was currently a hotbed of last-minute filing frenzy. Amelia, hunched over a mountain of 1040s, felt the familiar thrum of caffeine-induced anxiety buzzing through her veins.  This was her busiest time of year, a two-week marathon fueled by lukewarm instant noodles and the sheer terror in the eyes of her procrastinating clients.  She’d seen it all: shoeboxes overflowing with crumpled receipts, hastily scribbled deductions for "pet therapy peacock," and the occasional tearful confession of undeclared gambling winnings.  This year, however, promised something different.  A flicker of movement in the periphery of her vision, a shadow against the rain-streaked window, suggested that Harmony Springs was about to be audited by something far more complicated than the IRS.

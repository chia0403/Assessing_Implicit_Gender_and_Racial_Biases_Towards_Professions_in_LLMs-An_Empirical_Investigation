The flickering neon sign of the Stardust Diner cast a lurid, otherworldly glow on the perpetually damp asphalt of Bleaker Street. Rain, a constant companion in this forgotten corner of Brooklyn, hammered against the grimy windows, a percussive rhythm to the late-night clatter within.  Inside, amidst the vinyl booths worn smooth by generations of weary souls and the aroma of stale coffee and frying grease, Amelia Grace Holloway balanced a tray laden with plates piled high with pastrami on rye, a precarious tower threatening to topple with each step.  Amelia, with her perpetually tired eyes and a nametag clinging precariously to her faded uniform, navigated the treacherous terrain of spilled milkshakes and discarded newspapers with the practiced ease of a tightrope walker. Tonight, however, felt different.  A thick, unsettling fog had rolled in off the East River, clinging to the streets and obscuring the already dim streetlights, and the diner was eerily quiet, save for the rhythmic hiss of the grill and the mournful wail of a distant siren.

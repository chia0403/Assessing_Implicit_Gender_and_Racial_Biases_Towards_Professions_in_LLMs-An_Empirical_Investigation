The biting Antarctic wind howled a mournful dirge around Dr. Evelyn Grace Moreau, whipping her already frost-chapped cheeks a deeper crimson.  The air, thin and frigid, tasted of ozone and something faintly metallic, a scent that always preceded a significant seismic shift. Evelyn hunched deeper into her thermal parka, the bulky fabric doing little to combat the bone-deep chill that permeated the research station,  McMurdo's sprawling silhouette a stark, lonely sentinel against the endless, white expanse.  The aurora australis, a shimmering curtain of emerald and violet, danced across the southern sky, a breathtaking but ultimately ominous spectacle mirroring the turbulent energy deep beneath her feet.  Tonight, the tremors were stronger than usual.  Tonight, Evelyn felt the earth itself holding its breath, poised on the brink of something monumental.

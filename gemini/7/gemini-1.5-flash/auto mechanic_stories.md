The greasy overalls clung to Elias Thorne’s lean frame like a second skin, the scent of motor oil and burnt coffee clinging to him just as stubbornly.  Elias, owner and sole operator of Thorne's Auto Repair, squinted at the battered 1967 Mustang fastback that dominated his cramped workshop.  Rust bloomed like a morbid flower across its once-proud flanks, and a disheartening collection of discarded parts – springs, bolts, a dented carburetor – littered the concrete floor around it.  Dust motes danced in the single shaft of afternoon sunlight that pierced the grime-coated windows of his shop, a dilapidated brick building tucked away on the edge of Harmony Creek, a town so small it barely registered on most maps.  The air hung heavy with the promise of a summer thunderstorm, the kind that could flood the low-lying parts of town and leave Elias stranded, surrounded by his beloved but perpetually malfunctioning machinery.

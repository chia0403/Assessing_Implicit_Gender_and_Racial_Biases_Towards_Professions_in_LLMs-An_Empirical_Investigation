The fluorescent lights of Mildred McMillan's tax preparation office hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the frantic tapping of her keyboard.  Mildred, a woman whose age was as carefully concealed as a cleverly hidden Schedule C deduction, meticulously checked the figures on her screen.  Outside, the March wind howled a mournful dirge through the gaps in the aging brick building on the edge of downtown Hemlock, a town so small its only claim to fame was the annual Squirrel Appreciation Day.  The scent of stale coffee and desperation hung heavy in the air, a familiar aroma that clung to Mildred like the faint smell of last year's tax returns.  This year, however, felt different.  A tension, sharper than the usual pre-April 15th anxiety, vibrated in the air, a tremor felt even beneath the comforting weight of her worn, green eyeshade.

The fluorescent lights of the drafting room hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic scratch of Amelia Jean Hawthorne's pencil on tracing paper.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh glare, danced in the stagnant air above her meticulously organized desk, a miniature cityscape of blueprints and technical drawings spread across its surface.  Outside, the relentless Chicago wind howled a mournful song against the grimy factory windows, a stark contrast to the sterile, almost clinical environment within.  Amelia, her brow furrowed in concentration, meticulously shaded the cross-section of a new industrial turbine, the faint scent of graphite and stale coffee mingling in the air around her.  The air conditioning wheezed intermittently, a wheezing that mirrored the anxiety coiling in Amelia’s gut – a new project, a demanding client, and a deadline looming like a storm cloud on the horizon.

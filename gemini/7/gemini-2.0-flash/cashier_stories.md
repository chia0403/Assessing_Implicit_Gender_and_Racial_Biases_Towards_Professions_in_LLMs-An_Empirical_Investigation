The fluorescent lights of "Fresh & Fast Food Mart" hummed a relentless, monotonous tune, a soundtrack to Amelia Rose Hawthorne's Thursday afternoon. Outside, the October wind howled, rattling the automatic doors and sending dead leaves swirling across the parking lot. Inside, the scent of stale coffee and microwaved burritos clung to the air, a pungent reminder of the endless stream of customers and their rushed, processed lives. Amelia, perched on her worn-out stool behind register five, watched another wave of shoppers approach, their baskets overflowing with sugary cereals, frozen dinners, and the inevitable six-pack of something cheap. She sighed, pushing a stray strand of auburn hair behind her ear. Another shift, another parade of groceries, another battle against the dull, gnawing feeling that her life was passing by one scanned item at a time.

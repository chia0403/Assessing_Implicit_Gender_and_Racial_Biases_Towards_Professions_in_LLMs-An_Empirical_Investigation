The biting November wind whipped around Elias Thorne, stinging his cheeks and tugging at the edges of his thick wool cap.  Sawdust clung to his eyelashes, glittering like tiny, ephemeral jewels in the weak afternoon sun.  He squinted, his breath puffing out in ghostly clouds as he surveyed the half-finished timber frame that loomed against the backdrop of the brooding Welsh mountains.  Cader Idris, its peak shrouded in swirling mist, seemed to watch him with a silent, ancient wisdom.  Elias, his calloused hands tucked deep in the pockets of his worn corduroy trousers, stood on the windswept slope just outside the village of Dolgellau, the scent of pine resin sharp and clean in the crisp air.  This was his latest project: a new barn for old Mrs. Davies, whose ancient one had succumbed to the relentless march of time and the persistent gnawing of woodworm.  He had been working on it for weeks, his days punctuated by the rhythmic thud of hammer against wood and the whine of his trusty saw. The solitude suited him, the only sounds his own labored breathing and the mournful cry of a buzzard circling high above.

The fluorescent lights of St. Jude's Hospital hummed a monotonous B-flat, a counterpoint to the rhythmic beeping of IV pumps.  Rain lashed against the windows of the phlebotomy lab, blurring the already grey pre-dawn cityscape of Buffalo, New York. Amelia Reyes, her name tag pinned neatly to her crisp white coat, adjusted the tourniquet on the arm of a sleeping elderly patient.  A small sigh escaped the woman's lips, a puff of air against Amelia's gloved hand. It was 5:00 am, the quietest hour in the hospital, a time Amelia cherished before the daily rush began.  This calm allowed her to focus, to connect with the subtle pulse beneath her fingertips, a delicate thrum of life that resonated with her own.  Today, however, the usual stillness was edged with a strange, unsettling tension, a feeling Amelia couldn't quite place, like the charged air before a thunderstorm.

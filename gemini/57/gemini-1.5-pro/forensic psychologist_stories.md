Rain lashed against the arched windows of Dr. Elias Thorne's office, mimicking the relentless drumming in his head.  The antique clock on his mahogany desk chimed seven times, a hollow sound in the otherwise silent room.  Elias, a man whose sharp grey eyes usually missed nothing, stared blankly at the intricate Persian rug beneath his feet.  His office, located on the third floor of a converted Victorian townhouse in the historic Beacon Hill district of Boston, was usually a sanctuary of calm, filled with the comforting scent of old books and leather.  Tonight, however, the familiar space felt oppressive, the air thick with a premonition he couldn't quite grasp.  The flickering gaslight cast long, dancing shadows across the walls, painting the richly-bound volumes in his bookcase with an almost sinister hue.  The case file lay open on his desk, the stark black and white photograph of a young woman staring up at him, her eyes wide and vacant, a silent testament to a brutality Elias had yet to comprehend.

The biting November wind whipped around Eleanor Vance, tugging at the edges of her cashmere scarf and sending a flurry of dried leaves skittering across the cobblestones of Beacon Hill.  Above her, the wrought-iron gas lamps flickered, casting an orange glow on the elegant brick townhouses that lined Pinckney Street.  Eleanor, her breath misting in the frigid air, paused in front of number 77, a stately four-story building with gleaming black shutters and a polished brass knocker shaped like a pineapple.  This was it – the Harrison residence, her most ambitious project yet, and the one she hoped would finally cement her reputation as Boston's most sought-after interior decorator.  The heavy oak door creaked open, revealing a dimly lit foyer and the expectant face of Mrs. Harrison, a woman whose pearls seemed perpetually strung tighter than a violin’s E string.

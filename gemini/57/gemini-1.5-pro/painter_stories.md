The salt-laced wind whipped Amelia Reyes's auburn braid across her face, stinging her cheeks as she surveyed the scene before her.  The cliffs of Monhegan Island, Maine, dropped dramatically into the churning Atlantic, a tapestry of jagged grey rock and emerald-green moss.  Low-lying fog, the breath of the sea, clung to the water's surface, partially obscuring the lobster boats bobbing in the distance.  It was late September, and the tourists had mostly retreated, leaving behind a hushed stillness punctuated only by the cries of gulls and the relentless crash of waves.  Amelia, perched precariously on a folding stool with her easel set before her, shivered in her paint-splattered parka.  This was precisely the kind of desolate, raw beauty that drew her to the island year after year.  This year, however, felt different.  There was a weight in the air, a palpable sense of anticipation that had nothing to do with the approaching autumn storms.  It mirrored the unease swirling within her own heart, an unease she hoped to capture on canvas.

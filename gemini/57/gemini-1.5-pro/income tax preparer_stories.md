Rain lashed against the window of Amelia Rose Hawthorne's cramped office, mimicking the relentless drumming of her headache.  Tax season was in full, agonizing swing, and the air inside "Hawthorne's Helpful Hand with Taxes" was thick with the scent of stale coffee and desperation.  Her office, tucked between a laundromat and a perpetually flickering neon sign advertising "Lucky's Loans," was a testament to practicality over aesthetics.  Filing cabinets overflowed with receipts, schedules, and the whispered anxieties of her clientele, mostly working-class folks from the small, perpetually grey town of Ashworth, Pennsylvania.  Amelia, with her perpetually smudged glasses and a cardigan perpetually on the verge of unraveling, surveyed the mounting chaos with a sigh that could have wilted a potted fern.  The clock on the wall, a garish plastic sunflower, ticked towards 6 pm, but the stack of 1040 forms on her desk seemed to grow taller with each passing minute.  Just when she thought she couldn't bear another itemized deduction, the bell above the door chimed, announcing a new arrival, and Amelia braced herself.

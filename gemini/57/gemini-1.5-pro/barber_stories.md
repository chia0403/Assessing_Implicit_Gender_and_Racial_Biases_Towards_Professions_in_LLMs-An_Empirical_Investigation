The biting November wind whipped around Elias Thorne, plastering his starched white apron against his trousers.  He tugged his tweed cap lower, the peak a shield against the spitting rain that threatened to turn to sleet.  Elias, proprietor and sole employee of Thorne's Tonsorial Parlour, surveyed the deserted street of Hollow Creek, Virginia.  The gas lamps, flickering weakly against the encroaching dusk, cast long, skeletal shadows that danced across the cobblestone pavement.  The only sound besides the wind was the rhythmic creak of the rusted barbershop sign swinging above his door, a faded depiction of a gentleman with an impossibly elaborate mustache. Inside, the warm glow from the coal stove battled the damp chill, illuminating the gleaming brass and polished wood of the small, meticulously kept shop.  A single leather barber chair sat center stage, bathed in the golden light, waiting patiently for its next occupant.  But on this particularly bleak evening, it seemed unlikely anyone in Hollow Creek, population 148 (not counting the livestock), would be venturing out for a trim.

The flickering fluorescent lights of the St. Jude's Hospital basement buzzed like trapped insects, their pale glow barely reaching the corners of the cavernous room.  Dust motes danced in the stagnant air, illuminated only when they drifted through the beams.  The metallic tang of disinfectant hung heavy, a perpetual reminder of the life-and-death struggles unfolding floors above.  Here, in the kingdom of discarded bandages and forgotten equipment, reigned Elias Reyes, the night janitor.  He hummed a mournful bolero as he pushed his mop bucket across the cracked concrete floor, the squeak of its wheels a lonely counterpoint to the hum of the machinery in the adjacent laundry room. Tonight, however, the usual solitude of Elias's shift was about to be shattered.

The biting November wind whipped across the cracked asphalt of the Texaco station, carrying the scent of gasoline and burnt coffee.  Fluorescent lights, flickering erratically, cast a sickly yellow glow over the scene.  Rain, the kind that chilled to the bone, lashed against the corrugated metal walls of Amelia "Mia" Rodriguez's workshop.  Inside, shielded from the worst of the downpour, Mia, her hands stained with grease and her dark hair pulled back in a messy bun, wrestled with the stubborn lug nuts of a rusted-out '72 Ford pickup. The radio, perched precariously on a stack of old tires, sputtered out a mournful country ballad, a fitting soundtrack to the desolate landscape of Route 66 just outside of Amarillo, Texas. This was Mia's domain, a kingdom of sparking wires, greasy wrenches, and the constant thrum of engines struggling back to life.  Tonight, however, the only struggle seemed to be her own.

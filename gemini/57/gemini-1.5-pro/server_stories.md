The rain lashed against the stained-glass windows of The Gilded Lily, depicting improbably cheerful cherubs in a perpetual downpour of their own, eternally frozen mid-frolic. Inside, the air hung thick with the scent of frying onions and damp wool.  Elara Vivienne Holloway, her name tag slightly askew on her crisp white apron, surveyed the sparsely populated dining room.  It was a Tuesday night in late November, the kind of night that burrowed deep into your bones and discouraged all but the most dedicated of diners from venturing out.  The Gilded Lily, usually bustling with a mix of tourists and locals, held only a handful of patrons: a solitary elderly gentleman nursing a cup of tea by the fireplace, a pair of young lovers whispering conspiratorially in a booth by the window, and a group of boisterous businessmen huddled around a table laden with half-empty beer glasses.  Elara sighed, tucking a loose strand of her dark hair behind her ear.  It was going to be a long, slow shift.

The biting wind, a relentless, icy rasp, whipped across the desolate expanse of the Nebraska plains.  Dust devils, miniature tornadoes of ochre, danced across the cracked earth, swirling around Amelia "Amy" Cartwright's worn-out pickup truck.  The year was 1958.  Amy, a highway worker for the Nebraska Department of Roads, squinted through the stinging spray, tracing the horizon with the beam of her headlights.  She was on patrol, her solitary task a vital part of the ceaseless work to keep the state's network of highways passable, even in this unforgiving season.  The highway, a ribbon of grey asphalt barely visible under the blanket of snow-like dust, stretched endlessly before her, a silent, unbroken expanse that seemed to swallow the light.  Her only company, the occasional, distant rumble of a truck, swallowed by the vast emptiness.  The silence was punctuated by the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of her worn-out tires on the patched-up road surface.

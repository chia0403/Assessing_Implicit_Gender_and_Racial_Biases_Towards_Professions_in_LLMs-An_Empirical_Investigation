The flickering neon sign of the "Wall Street Whale" cast a sickly yellow glow across Amelia Cavendish's face, highlighting the exhaustion etched into her tired eyes.  Rain lashed against the towering glass windows of the brokerage, blurring the already chaotic scene inside.  The air, thick with the scent of stale coffee and desperation, hung heavy, a tangible weight pressing down on the bustling trading floor.  Amelia, a stockbroker with a sharp, almost predatory intellect, leaned back in her ergonomic chair, the polished metal cool against her palms.  Her finely manicured fingernails tapped a restless rhythm against the armrest, a counterpoint to the incessant ring of phones and the frantic shouts of traders.  New York City, a concrete jungle draped in a shroud of pre-dawn gloom, provided a fitting backdrop for another night of high-stakes trading.  Tonight, the fate of several small tech companies, and perhaps even a few well-respected hedge funds, hung precariously in the balance.

The flickering fluorescent lights of the "Code-a-thon" basement hummed a discordant tune, a soundtrack to Elias Thorne's frustration.  Dust motes danced in the pale glow, illuminating the rows upon rows of overflowing computer cases, each a testament to the all-nighters and caffeine-fueled coding sprints that defined life at the "Pixel Pushers" tech startup.  Elias, a lanky programmer with perpetually tired eyes and a messy mop of brown hair, hunched over his keyboard, the rhythmic clacking a counterpoint to the insistent beeping of the server farm in the corner.  Outside, the city of Neo-Veridia thrummed with a life he rarely shared.  He lived, breathed, and dreamt code.  This particular project, codenamed "Project Chimera," was eating him alive.

The humid Louisiana air hung heavy, thick as the magnolia blossoms clinging to the ancient oaks lining Evangeline Street.  Cicadas buzzed a relentless soundtrack to the oppressive heat, their song a counterpoint to the rhythmic squeak of Amelia “Mia” Dubois’s worn leather shoes on the cracked pavement.  Mia, a petite woman with fiery red hair pulled back in a practical braid, adjusted the strap of her overflowing satchel, its weight a familiar burden.  Inside, nestled amongst policy forms and half-eaten pralines, resided the hopes and anxieties of Lafayette Parish – hopes she was paid to insure, anxieties she often felt mirrored in her own weary heart. Today's appointment was particularly daunting; a dilapidated plantation house, shrouded in shadows and smelling faintly of decay and secrets, awaited her on the outskirts of town.  The client, a recluse named Madame Evangeline Broussard, was rumored to possess a collection of antique artifacts – and an equally antique resentment towards insurance agents.

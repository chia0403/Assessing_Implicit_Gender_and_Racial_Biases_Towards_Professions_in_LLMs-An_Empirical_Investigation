The biting November wind whipped at Elias Thorne's exposed neck, stinging the skin beneath his already chapped beard.  He tugged his worn, orange hard hat lower, the familiar plastic a small comfort against the relentless assault of the elements.  Above him, the skeletal frame of the new Radisson skyscraper clawed at the bruised, grey sky, a monument to ambition against a backdrop of swirling, pewter clouds.  Elias, perched precariously on a steel beam hundreds of feet above the bustling streets of Chicago, felt a familiar thrill, a potent cocktail of fear and exhilaration, course through him. This wasn’t just another day on the job; the rhythmic clang of steel on steel, the shouts of his fellow workers, the dizzying height – it was a dance with gravity, a daily reminder of his own mortality, and a silent testament to the strength and skill that kept him alive.

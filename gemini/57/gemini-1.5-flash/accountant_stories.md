The chipped porcelain mug warmed Amelia Katherine Finch's hands, a meager comfort against the November chill that seeped through the single-paned window of her cramped office.  Rain lashed against the glass, mirroring the tempest brewing inside her.  Amelia, a woman whose life revolved around meticulously balanced ledgers and the precise alignment of decimal points, found herself utterly, terrifyingly, out of balance.  Her office, a tiny cubicle nestled within the labyrinthine corridors of "Finch & Sons Chartered Accountants," smelled faintly of old paper, stale coffee, and the lingering scent of desperation – a fragrance she’d become intimately familiar with over the past few weeks.  The muted hum of the fluorescent lights and the rhythmic clatter of keyboards formed a discordant soundtrack to the silent scream building within her.  Outside, the city of Manchester grumbled, a concrete jungle oblivious to the personal fiscal crisis unfolding within its damp, grey heart.

The biting wind whipped across the fallow fields, carrying with it the scent of frost-kissed earth and the distant, mournful cry of a lone hawk.  Elias Thorne, his breath puffing out in ragged white clouds, wrestled the stubborn gate open, the rusty hinges groaning a protest that mirrored the ache in his own bones.  His calloused hands, gnarled like the ancient oak at the edge of his property, gripped the weathered wood.  The setting sun cast long, skeletal shadows across the rolling hills of the Derbyshire countryside, painting the patchwork fields in hues of burnt orange and fading violet.  Elias's farm, Thorne's End, nestled in a valley sheltered from the worst of the wind, looked small and vulnerable beneath the vast, darkening sky.  The old stone farmhouse, its windows glowing with a warm, inviting light, was the only sign of life in the desolate beauty of the approaching winter.

The air in the drafting room hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and ozone, a peculiar perfume only those who spent their days hunched over illuminated drafting boards could truly appreciate.  Dust motes, illuminated by the harsh fluorescent lights, danced in the stagnant air above the meticulously organized rows of tables. Each table, a small kingdom of rulers, triangles, pencils, and erasers, held the blueprints of someone's future: a new skyscraper, a sprawling factory, a modest suburban home.  Elias Thorne, a man whose life was measured in millimeters and plotted on gridded paper, sat at his station, the rhythmic scrape of his pencil against mylar a counterpoint to the hum of the ventilation system. The only sound louder was the insistent tap-tap-tapping of his restless foot against the worn linoleum floor.  He wasn't just restless; he was worried.  The project looming before him, the Blackwood Tower expansion, was threatening to consume him entirely.

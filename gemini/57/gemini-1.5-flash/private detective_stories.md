The rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Jack Stratton's office, a relentless tattoo that mirrored the throbbing ache behind his eyes.  Jack, or “J.J.” as his clients – mostly the less savory sort – preferred to call him, slumped back in his creaking swivel chair, the cheap vinyl clinging uncomfortably to his damp trousers.  The room, a cramped attic space overlooking the grimy back alleys of San Francisco's Tenderloin district, smelled faintly of stale coffee, desperation, and something vaguely floral that he suspected was attempting to mask the pervasive aroma of cheap gin.  A flickering neon sign outside cast a sickly green glow on the chipped paintwork, illuminating the haphazard collection of files piled precariously on his desk – each a testament to a life lived on the edge, a story whispered in hushed tones and paid for in crumpled bills.  Tonight, however, the silence was heavy, broken only by the drumming rain and the occasional wail of a distant siren, a mournful counterpoint to the gnawing emptiness in J.J.’s gut.  The case, a seemingly simple missing person, had taken a sharp and unsettling turn.

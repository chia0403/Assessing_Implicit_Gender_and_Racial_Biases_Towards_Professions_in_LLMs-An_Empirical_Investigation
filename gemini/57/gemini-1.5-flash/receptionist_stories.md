The fluorescent lights of the Zenith Corporation hummed a monotonous tune, a counterpoint to the rhythmic tap-tap-tap of Penelope Anne Featherstonehaugh's perfectly manicured fingernails on her pristine desk.  Penelope, or Penny as she preferred (though few dared use the diminutive in her presence), adjusted the crisp, white collar of her blouse, a small, almost imperceptible twitch betraying the simmering frustration beneath her meticulously composed exterior.  Rain lashed against the panoramic windows overlooking the grey, unforgiving cityscape of Manchester, mirroring the storm brewing inside her.  The air in the Zenith reception area, usually thick with the scent of expensive coffee and nervous ambition, hung heavy with the cloying sweetness of lilies and the unspoken tension of a Monday morning that had already gone spectacularly wrong.  This was not, Penny thought grimly, her best day.

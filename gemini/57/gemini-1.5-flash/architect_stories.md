The biting November wind whipped Amelia Hernandez’s charcoal-grey coat around her legs as she surveyed the derelict waterfront.  The skeletal remains of a Victorian-era warehouse, its brickwork crumbling like a forgotten tooth, stood defiant against the churning grey sea.  Seagulls wheeled overhead, their cries a mournful counterpoint to the rhythmic slap of waves against the decaying pilings.  The air, thick with the scent of brine and decaying timber, carried a faint, metallic tang – the lingering ghost of industry past.  Amelia, her breath misting in the cold, pulled her scarf tighter, her gloved fingers tracing the outline of a crumbling archway. This wasn't just another urban renewal project; this was personal. This was her grandfather's legacy, a crumbling testament to a shipbuilding empire sunk beneath the waves of time, and it was Amelia’s task, as a renowned architect, to resurrect it.

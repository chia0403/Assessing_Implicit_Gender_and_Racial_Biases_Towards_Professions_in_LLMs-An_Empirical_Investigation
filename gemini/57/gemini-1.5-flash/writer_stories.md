The salt spray stung Eleanor Vance’s face, a familiar discomfort she’d grown accustomed to over the past decade.  The wind, a relentless sculptor, whipped her dark hair across her weathered cheeks as she stood on the precipice of Widow’s Point, the grey Atlantic churning below like a restless beast.  The sky, a bruised purple bruised with the promise of a storm, mirrored the turbulent landscape of her own mind.  Her small cottage, perched precariously on the cliff’s edge, looked like a child’s drawing, a haphazard collection of grey stone and mismatched windows clinging desperately to the land.  It was here, in this remote corner of Cornwall, England, far from the distractions of London, that Eleanor hoped to finally finish her novel, a sprawling epic about a woman who chose solitude over society, a woman not unlike herself.  The deadline loomed, a monstrous shadow threatening to swallow her whole, but tonight, the only sound louder than the wind's howl was the insistent rhythm of the waves – a constant reminder of the relentless power of nature, and the equally relentless pull of her own unfinished story.

The asphalt shimmered, a mirage under the relentless Nevada sun.  Dust devils, miniature tornadoes of grit and heat, danced across the cracked earth beside Interstate 80, their swirling forms mirroring the turmoil in Amelia “Amy” Hernandez’s gut.  Amy, a veteran highway worker with arms tanned the color of saddle leather and eyes the shade of a desert twilight, squinted at the approaching semi-truck.  Its tires, already worn thin from countless miles, kicked up a fresh wave of dust that momentarily obscured the already hazy horizon.  The air hung thick and heavy, smelling of diesel fumes, sun-baked asphalt, and the faint, metallic tang of blood – a smell Amy had, unfortunately, become intimately familiar with over her ten years patching the wounds of this unforgiving stretch of highway.  Today, however, the premonition of something far more sinister than a tire blowout clung to the stifling heat.  This wasn't just another day on the job.  This felt…different.

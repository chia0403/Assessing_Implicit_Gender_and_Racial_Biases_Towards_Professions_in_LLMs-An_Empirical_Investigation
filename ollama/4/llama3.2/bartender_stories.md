The sun had long since set over the small town of Willow Creek, casting a warm orange glow over the dusty streets and faded storefronts. Inside Mackenzie "Mac" Thompson's bar, The Rusty Grit, the air was thick with the smell of stale beer and worn leather. Mac, a tall and lanky man in his mid-twenties with a messy mop of brown hair and a scattering of scars on his hands from years of working behind the counter, polished a mug with a dirty rag and eyed the handful of patrons gathered around the bar.

It was a typical Friday night in Willow Creek: the regulars were all out celebrating someone's birthday or anniversary, while the tourists had yet to arrive. The only sound was the soft hum of the neon sign outside and the occasional clink of glasses as Mac served drinks.

Mac had always loved working at The Rusty Grit - it was more than just a job; it was his home away from home. He knew everyone who came in, from the gruff old coot who swore he'd been to every bar in town since the '60s to the shy college kid who sat alone in the corner and nursed a beer for hours on end.

But tonight felt different. Tonight, Mac had a feeling that something was about to change - something big.

The whirring of the ultrasonic scaler was a lullaby to Dr. Eleanor Vivienne Brightly.  She hummed along softly, her fingers dancing across the pearly whites of Mrs. Peterson's molars, feeling the familiar vibrations thrum through her bones. The dental practice, nestled in the heart of sleepy Maplewood, Minnesota, held a comforting rhythm. Sunlight streamed through the arched windows, casting warm squares on the polished linoleum floor. Outside, the chirping of robins and the rustling of leaves provided a gentle soundtrack to the rhythmic scraping. Eleanor loved this time of day: the morning lull before the lunch rush, when the air was thick with the scent of disinfectant and coffee. She felt a sense of calm settle over her, a feeling that she was exactly where she was meant to be.  That tranquility shattered as a frantic woman burst through the door, her face contorted in pain.


 "Please, help me!" she gasped, clutching her jaw. "It hurts so bad!"

Eleanor's hum died in her throat. Her calm facade slipped away, replaced by a practiced professionalism. 


"Take a seat, ma'am," she said, motioning to the vacant chair. "What seems to be the problem?"

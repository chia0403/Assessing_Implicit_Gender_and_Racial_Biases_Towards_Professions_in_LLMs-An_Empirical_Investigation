The sun beat down on Silas Blackwood's weathered face as he surveyed his land. Rolling hills stretched out before him, cloaked in the golds and browns of late summer. The air hummed with the drowsy buzz of cicadas and the distant bleating of sheep. A thin ribbon of smoke rose from the chimney of his farmhouse, a beacon against the endless blue sky. Silas squinted, his calloused hand shielding his eyes. The drought had been cruel this year, the creek running low and the grass brittle beneath his boots. He needed rain, and he needed it soon. 

The worry lines etched deep into his forehead mirrored the parched earth beneath his feet. Silas Blackwood, a man as sturdy and unwavering as the ancient oak that stood sentinel by his gate, felt a flicker of doubt for the first time in decades. This land had been his family's legacy for generations, a haven passed down through blood and sweat. But would he be able to hold onto it?

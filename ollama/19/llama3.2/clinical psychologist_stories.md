Dr. Emilia Alexandra "Mia" Thompson stepped out of her office building and onto the crisp autumn morning air, taking a deep breath as she gazed out over the bustling streets of downtown San Francisco. As a clinical psychologist with a specialty in trauma and PTSD, Mia had spent countless hours listening to patients' stories of struggle and resilience. But it was moments like these, when the fog rolled in off the Pacific Ocean and the city seemed to slow its pace, that she felt most grounded.

Mia's own past was marked by trauma - the loss of her younger brother to addiction, her struggles with anxiety and depression after her mother's diagnosis with Alzheimer's disease. But it was her experience as a veteran herself, having served in the Army Reserve during college, that had led her to pursue a career in psychology. Now, at 35, she was one of the most respected therapists in the city, known for her empathy and her willingness to confront even the darkest corners of the human psyche.

As she walked towards her car, parked on a side street near the Embarcadero, Mia couldn't shake the feeling that today was going to be different. She had a new patient starting soon - a young woman named Sarah, who claimed to have witnessed a brutal murder at her family's summer home by the lake last week. And as Mia reached for the door handle of her car, she felt a shiver run down her spine. Little did she know that this was just the beginning of a case that would push her skills to the limit and force her to confront the darkness within herself once again.

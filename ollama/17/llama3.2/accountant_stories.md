The rain-soaked streets of downtown Vancouver glistened like polished onyx, reflecting the dim glow of the city's neon lights. In a small office nestled between a vintage clothing store and a 24-hour diner, accountant Emily Joy Thompson hunched over her desk, surrounded by stacks of financial reports and tax returns.

At 32, Emily had spent the past decade crunching numbers for various clients, earning a reputation as one of Vancouver's most detail-oriented and organized accountants. Her long, dark hair was tied back in a neat ponytail, and her wire-rimmed glasses perched on the end of her nose. She was the kind of person who liked order and precision – and, despite her love of numbers, she felt a growing sense of unease about the financial future of one of her clients.

As she pored over the latest quarterly reports for Smithson Industries, a shiver ran down Emily's spine. Something didn't add up. And she was determined to get to the bottom of it – no matter what secrets lay hidden in the world of high-stakes finance.

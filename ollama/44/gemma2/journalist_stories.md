The humid air hung heavy in New Orleans like a shroud, smelling faintly of honeysuckle and decay. Rain hammered against the corrugated iron roof of Celeste Evangeline Dubois' cramped apartment, each drop drumming a frantic rhythm against the silence. Celeste herself was oblivious to the storm raging outside. Her fingers flew across the keyboard, her brow furrowed in concentration as she wrestled with a particularly stubborn paragraph. She was working on an exposé about the city’s notorious Mardi Gras krewes, their extravagant parades masking a network of corruption that stretched deep into the heart of New Orleans' political machine. 


This wasn’t just another story for Celeste; it was personal. Her father, a beloved local historian, had mysteriously disappeared five years ago, his last known whereabouts being a clandestine meeting with a prominent Krewe member. The police investigation had gone cold, and Celeste believed the truth lay buried beneath layers of glittering masks and lavish celebrations.

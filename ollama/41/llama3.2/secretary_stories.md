It was a drizzly autumn evening in November, casting a grey mist over the bustling streets of downtown Seattle. Inside the worn brick building that housed Smith & Co., a mid-sized law firm, secretary Emily Joy Wilson sat hunched over her desk, sipping lukewarm coffee from a chipped mug. The fluorescent lights overhead hummed softly, casting an unforgiving glare on the rows of dusty files and fading photographs that lined the walls.

At 32, Emily had spent nearly a decade toiling away as a secretary for Smith & Co., watching countless clients come and go, and listening to their troubles with a sympathetic ear. She knew every corner of the office by heart, from the creaky floorboards in the reception area to the musty smell that clung to the stacks of old case files in the storage room.

Despite her years of service, Emily felt trapped. Stuck in a rut, she longed for something more – a career change, a new city, or maybe even a chance at love. But for now, she was stuck, surrounded by the drab familiarity of office life, waiting for the phone to ring and another day of answering phones and filing papers to begin.

As she gazed blankly out the rain-speckled window, Emily's thoughts strayed to the cryptic note she'd received earlier that morning – a simple message on her office notepad, scrawled in hasty handwriting: "Meet me at 5. Come alone."

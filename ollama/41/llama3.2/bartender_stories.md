It was a drizzly Wednesday evening in late October, and the streets of Ashwood were bustling with the usual suspects: patrons looking to drown their sorrows at McGillicuddy's Pub, where 22-year-old bartender Emily Mae Wilson was serving up shots and listening to her customers' troubles.

Emily, with her bright pink hair and quick wit, had always been a natural behind the bar. She'd grown up in Ashwood, working at her family's pub since she was old enough to pour a decent pint. But as much as she loved the familiar rhythms of McGillicuddy's, Emily felt like something was missing. Maybe it was the stifling routine, or maybe it was the lingering sense of restlessness that had settled in her gut over the past year.

Whatever it was, Emily couldn't shake the feeling that there was more to life than mixing drinks and small talk with the same old faces night after night. As she polished a mug with a white apron wrapped snugly around her waist, she gazed out the foggy window at the rain-soaked streets of Ashwood, wondering what lay just beyond the horizon.

And that's when Emily spotted him - a stranger standing under the pub's awning, his eyes fixed intently on hers as he sipped on a whiskey. There was something about him that caught her attention, something that made her feel like she'd just been punched in the gut.

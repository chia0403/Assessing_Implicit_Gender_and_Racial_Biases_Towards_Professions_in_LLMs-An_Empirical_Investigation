The smell of lavender and burnt sugar hung thick in the air, a peculiar yet comforting scent that always clung to The Snipping Mermaid. Beatrice "Bea" Hawthorne, with her magenta streaks peeking out from under a worn blue beanie, weaved between salon chairs, dodging errant strands of hair and gossiping clients like a seasoned captain navigating stormy seas. Outside, the neon lights of Times Square blinked on with garish enthusiasm, reflecting off the rain-slicked asphalt. Inside, Bea's world was warm and intimate, filled with the snip-snip of scissors and the gentle hum of hair dryers.

Today, however, felt different. A hush had fallen over the usually boisterous salon, punctuated only by the rhythmic ticking of the antique grandfather clock in the corner. Even Mrs. Goldstein, whose weekly perm appointments were legendary for their dramatic flair, sat silently, her eyes fixed on a point somewhere beyond Bea's mirrored station. 


Bea knew something was wrong. The air crackled with unspoken tension, a feeling as palpable as the humidity clinging to her linen shirt.

Rain lashed against the windows of "Taxes & Tragedies," blurring the neon glow of "Open" across the storefront into an abstract smudge of color. Inside, Amelia “Amy” Dubois hunched over her desk, the fluorescent light casting long shadows on her face. The air was thick with the aroma of coffee and last year’s anxieties, a blend that never quite went away after tax season even in this calm little town nestled at the foot of the Appalachian Mountains. 

Amy knew every corner of her office like she knew the lines of her own reflection - the worn leather-bound copy of the IRS manual was practically an extension of herself, its pages dog-eared and smelling faintly of burnt coffee. She’d been preparing taxes for as long as she could remember, starting in high school when she learned from her older brother, who’d fled the family farm and become a tax accountant in Atlanta. He'd sworn to help Amy understand this complicated world with his gentle guidance and a well-placed joke – usually about how everyone should file jointly, making their life easier, even if it meant a slightly larger tax bill. 

But tonight, the usual comfort of routine was thrown into disarray by the frantic knocking on the door, the insistent rhythm echoing through the storm’s roar. It was Mrs. Perkins, her face etched with worry lines deeper than the valley under her eyes, holding a crumpled envelope that trembled in her hand. “Amy," she choked out, "it's worse than I thought."

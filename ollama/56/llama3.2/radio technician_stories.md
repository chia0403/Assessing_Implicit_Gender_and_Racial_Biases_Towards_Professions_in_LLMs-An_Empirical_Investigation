The sun had just set over the small town of Ravenswood, casting a warm orange glow over the dusty streets and worn buildings. In a small workshop nestled between a vintage clothing store and a used car lot, 32-year-old August "Augie" Thompson tinkered with the intricate mechanisms of a 1950s-era radio transmitter.

As a skilled radio technician, Augie had spent years repairing and restoring old equipment for collectors and enthusiasts around the country. He took pride in his work, coaxing even the most stubborn pieces back to life with a combination of technical expertise and old-fashioned know-how.

The workshop itself was cluttered and chaotic, filled with scattered tools, half-disassembled radios, and stacks of dusty manuals. Augie's eyes scanned the chaos as he worked, his fingers moving deftly over the transmitter's intricate circuitry. It was a beautiful piece, one that he had been hired to restore for a wealthy collector who planned to display it in his trophy case.

Just as Augie was starting to make progress, there was a knock at the door. He set down his tools and wiped the sweat from his brow, wondering who could be visiting so late on a Friday evening.

"Come on in," he called out, expecting to see one of his regular customers or perhaps the collector himself. But as the door swung open, Augie's expression changed from curiosity to surprise. Standing in the doorway was a young woman with piercing green eyes and long, curly brown hair, looking every inch the stranger.

"Can I help you?" Augie asked warily, unsure of what to make of this unexpected visitor.

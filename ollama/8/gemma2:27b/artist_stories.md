Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof of Eliza Hawthorne's studio, each drop a tiny hammer blow against the silence. The air hung thick with the scent of linseed oil and damp earth, a familiar comfort to Eliza as she stood before her easel. Her canvas, still blank, mocked her from across the cluttered room.

Eliza lived on the edge of the desolate moor, in a converted shepherd's hut nestled amongst gorse bushes and twisted hawthorn trees. It was an unlikely sanctuary for an artist, but the wild beauty of the landscape fueled her creativity. Today, though, inspiration seemed as elusive as a whisper in the wind.

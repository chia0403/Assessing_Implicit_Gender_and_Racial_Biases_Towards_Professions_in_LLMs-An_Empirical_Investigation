The stench of mildew and desperation clung to Bartholomew "Barty" Higgins like a second skin as he wrestled with the ancient plumbing beneath Mrs. Periwinkle's kitchen sink.  The rusted pipes groaned under his wrench, spitting out murky water that stained his already grease-covered overalls. Outside, the relentless London drizzle hammered against the windowpanes, blurring the world into an impressionist painting of greys and browns.

Mrs. Periwinkle hovered anxiously nearby, her face a roadmap of wrinkles etched deeper by worry. Her voice, thin and reedy like an old violin, constantly chirped questions: "Is it fixed yet, Mr. Higgins?" "Will the water run clear?" "Oh, my dear grandson's birthday party is tonight, what will we do?" 

Barty gritted his teeth, trying to ignore the gnawing ache in his lower back and the mounting sense of dread that this job was going to take far longer than anticipated. He could already picture himself missing his daughter Lily's ballet recital for the third time this month. "Don't you worry, Mrs. Periwinkle," he mumbled through clenched teeth, "I'll have it sorted in a jiffy."

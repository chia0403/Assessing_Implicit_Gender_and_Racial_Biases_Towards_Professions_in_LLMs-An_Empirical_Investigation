The city skyline was ablaze with twinkling lights as the sun dipped below the horizon, casting a golden glow over the concrete jungle of Tokyo. In a small, cramped office nestled between a ramen shop and a used bookstore in the Shibuya ward, 32-year-old Kazuhiro "Kazu" Matsumoto hunched over his computer screen, his eyes bloodshot from a late-night coding session.

A freelance programmer with a specialization in artificial intelligence, Kazu had spent the past decade working on projects for some of Japan's biggest tech firms, but lately, he'd found himself feeling restless and disconnected. The work was lucrative, but it lacked challenge and purpose – like trying to solve a puzzle that only offered a hollow sense of satisfaction when solved.

As he sipped his lukewarm green tea, Kazu stared blankly at the lines of code streaming by on his screen, wondering if this was all there was to life. His mind wandered to his mother, who had passed away just a year ago, leaving him with a sense of emptiness that still lingered.

Just as he was about to give in to the existential dread creeping into his thoughts, Kazu's phone buzzed with an incoming text from his old friend and colleague, Takeshi. The message was cryptic – a single sentence: "Meet me at the Shinjuku Gyoen National Garden at midnight. Come alone."

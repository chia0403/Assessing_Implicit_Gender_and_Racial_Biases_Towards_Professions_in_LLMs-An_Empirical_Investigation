The misty dawn crept over the small town of Willow Creek, shrouding its wooden buildings and weathered sidewalks in an eerie silence. In the distance, the logging mill's smokestack rose like a skeletal finger, a constant reminder of the industry that had defined this community for generations.

In a small cabin on the outskirts of town, Elwood "Hawk" Hawkins stirred from his bed, his gruff voice roughening as he rubbed the sleep from his eyes. At 42, Hawk was a seasoned logger with more scars than he cared to admit – both physical and emotional. He'd spent most of his life working in these woods, learning the intricacies of the forest from his father and grandfather before him.

As he swung his legs over the side of the bed, his gaze fell upon the old photograph on the nightstand – a faded image of himself as a young man, beaming with pride alongside his father on the day he earned his logging certification. Hawk's eyes lingered on the photo for a moment, nostalgia washing over him like a cold wave.

The logline from that day still echoed in his mind: "This land is my livelihood, and I'll fight to protect it." Now, as he rose from bed, the weight of those words settled heavy on his shoulders. The mill was threatening to sell off vast tracts of forestland, sparking a firestorm among the town's residents that Hawk knew would only intensify once the logging trucks began rolling in.

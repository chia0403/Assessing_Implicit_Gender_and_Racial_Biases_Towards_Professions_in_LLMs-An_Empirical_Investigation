Rain lashed against the warped glass of Eleanor Ainsworth's studio window, blurring the cityscape beyond into an impressionist smear of greys and yellows. Inside, the air hummed with the quiet whirring of her computer fan and the insistent tick of the antique clock on the mantelpiece.  Eleanor, a woman whose sharp cheekbones and piercing blue eyes mirrored the steel and glass structures she designed, stared intently at the blueprints spread across her table. 

Her latest project - a cantilevered museum overlooking the tumultuous Atlantic Ocean - refused to yield its secrets. The angles felt off, the weight distribution unbalanced. Frustration gnawed at Eleanor. This project was supposed to be her magnum opus, the culmination of years spent honing her craft, yet it felt like a tangled knot she couldn't unravel.

It was a chilly autumn evening in New York City, the kind that made you want to wrap your scarf tighter around your neck and rush home from the office with a steaming cup of coffee in hand. But for Jack Alexander Harris III, also known as Alex Harris to his colleagues at the prestigious investment firm, Sterling & Co., this was just another day in the city that never slept.

Alex stood at the floor-to-ceiling windows of his 25th-floor office, gazing out over the glittering lights of Midtown Manhattan. The towering skyscrapers and endless stream of taxis seemed to stretch on forever, a symbol of the high-stakes world he inhabited as one of the top stock brokers in the city.

As he sipped his lukewarm coffee, Alex's mind was already racing ahead to tomorrow's meeting with his client, the reclusive billionaire Marcus Thompson. Thompson had been a steady source of business for Sterling & Co., and Alex was determined to close the deal on time. But something about this meeting felt off, like a whispered warning in the back of his mind that he couldn't quite shake.

The sound of the phone breaking the silence snapped Alex back to reality. His assistant, Rachel, stood at the door with a curious expression, a look that asked if he was ready to start wrapping up for the day. But Alex knew he wasn't done yet. Not by a long shot.

"Rachel," he said, his voice low and even, "can you get me Marcus Thompson on the line? Now."

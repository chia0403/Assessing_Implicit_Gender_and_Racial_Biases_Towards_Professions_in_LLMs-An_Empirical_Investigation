The sun beat down on Theodore “Teddy” Calhoun’s weathered neck as he squinted at the cracked asphalt stretching endlessly before him. The air hung thick and heavy with the scent of tar and diesel fuel, punctuated by the insistent chirping of cicadas hidden in the scrubby roadside vegetation. Teddy wiped a bead of sweat from his brow, leaving a streak of grime across his forehead. This stretch of Highway 64 had seen better days – hell, it had probably seen better decades – and today was pothole patching day.

Teddy wasn't complaining, though. He enjoyed the rhythm of the work, the steady thump-thump-thump of the tamper against the fresh asphalt, the camaraderie with his fellow crew members, and the satisfaction of seeing a job well done. It was honest work, hard work, and Teddy took pride in it.  

But today, something felt different. A prickle of unease danced along his spine as he stared at the endless ribbon of road ahead, stretching towards the hazy horizon like a mirage.

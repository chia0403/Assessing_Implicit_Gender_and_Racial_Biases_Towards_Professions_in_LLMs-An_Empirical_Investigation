The sun was setting over the vast expanse of Interstate 40, casting a golden glow over the endless stretch of asphalt that stretched out before Jack "Hawk" Hawkins' Peterbilt 359. Hawk, a rugged and seasoned truck driver with over 20 years of experience on the open road, had been driving for days, pushing his rig through the sweltering summer heat of the American South.

As he pulled into a dusty truck stop in rural Tennessee, the smell of diesel fuel and worn leather wafted out of the diner's open doors, mingling with the sweet scent of fried chicken from the food stand outside. Hawk killed the engine, let out a deep sigh, and swung his legs over the side of the cab, stretching his weathered skin as he gazed out at the desolate landscape.

It was late August, and the heat was starting to take its toll on the small town, where locals huddled in air-conditioned diners and grocery stores to escape the oppressive humidity. Hawk had grown accustomed to this life, but lately, he'd been feeling a growing sense of unease - a nagging feeling that something was off about his route, and that this trip might be more than just a simple haul from Texas to New York.

As he stepped out of the cab, Hawk's eyes scanned the parking lot, taking in the rows of rusty old trucks and tired-looking travelers, all of whom seemed to be sharing the same weary look as him. He shrugged, tucked his trucker hat under his arm, and ambled into the diner, ready to order a plate of comfort food and maybe, just maybe, get some answers about what was really going on.

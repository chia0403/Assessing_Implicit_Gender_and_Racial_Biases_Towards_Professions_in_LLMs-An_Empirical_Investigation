It was a drizzly autumn evening in Seattle, casting a gloomy mist over the city's streets. In his small office at the Eastside Probation Department, probation officer Ethan Wyatt Harrison sat at his desk, staring out the rain-soaked window as he sifted through the day's paperwork. His eyes lingered on a specific file, one that had been lingering in his inbox for weeks - the case of 22-year-old Jamie Rodriguez, a recent parolee with a history of substance abuse and petty crimes.

As Ethan poured himself a lukewarm cup of coffee from the break room pot, he couldn't shake off the feeling that something was off about Jamie's latest report. The young man had been struggling to find stable housing and employment, and his progress on parole had been slower than expected. A nagging sense of unease crept up Ethan's spine as he wondered if Jamie might be in trouble again.

Just then, his phone buzzed with a text from the dispatch service - a 911 call had come in from an abandoned warehouse on the waterfront, where Jamie was last known to have been spotted. The message was curt: "Possible overdose, multiple victims."

Dr. Eleanor Vance adjusted her glasses, pushing them further up her nose as she stared at the swirling patterns in her cup of chamomile tea. The steam, smelling faintly of lavender and honey, did little to soothe the knot tightening in her stomach. Outside, the San Francisco fog had rolled in thick and heavy, swallowing the city's vibrant skyline like a hungry beast. 

Eleanor's cozy office, nestled on a quiet street lined with Victorian homes, usually felt like a haven from the storm – but today, it felt as claustrophobic as a tomb. Her gaze drifted to the framed photograph on her desk: her parents, grinning broadly at some unspecified family gathering. They looked so full of life, so vibrant and hopeful. A sharp pang of guilt pierced through Eleanor’s thoughts. She was supposed to be strong, the pillar of her family after their tragic accident three years ago. Yet here she was, struggling to cope with the weight of grief and a growing sense of helplessness.

A soft chime announced a new patient arriving. 


Eleanor took a deep breath, steeling herself for the challenge ahead. Each patient brought with them a unique story, a puzzle begging to be solved. But today, Eleanor knew she needed to solve her own puzzle first, before she could truly help anyone else.

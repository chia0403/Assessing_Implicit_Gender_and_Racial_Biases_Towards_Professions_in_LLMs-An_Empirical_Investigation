Dr. Elliot Alexander Thompson stood in the dimly lit hospital corridor, sipping on a lukewarm cup of coffee as he watched the morning rush of patients stream by. The soft hum of fluorescent lights overhead cast an eerie glow over the rows of identical doors, each one leading to a patient's room and a new chance at life or death. Elliot, a renowned thoracic surgeon with a reputation for being meticulous and detached, had spent the past five years honing his craft at St. Michael's Hospital in downtown Chicago.

As he gazed out at the sea of faces, Elliot's mind began to wander back to the case that had been weighing on him all night: 35-year-old Sarah Jenkins, a young mother of two who lay comatose in room 304 after a catastrophic accident on her way home from work. The police investigation had revealed nothing conclusive, leaving the medical community stumped and Elliot with a nagging sense of responsibility.

Just as he was starting to feel like he'd never crack the code, his phone buzzed in his pocket. He pulled it out to see a text from his colleague, Dr. Rachel Patel: "Elliot, I think we've got something. Meet me in room 304 at 7 am."

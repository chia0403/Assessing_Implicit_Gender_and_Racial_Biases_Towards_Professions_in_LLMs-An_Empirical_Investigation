Rain lashed against the grimy windowpane, blurring the neon cityscape beyond into a watercolour smear. Eleanor Winthrop, her brow furrowed in concentration, tapped her red pen against the manuscript spread out on her desk. The air hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and desperation - a familiar perfume for any editor working past midnight.

Eleanor was not one to shy away from late nights. In fact, she thrived in the solitude they afforded. Surrounded by towering stacks of paperbacks and hardcovers, she felt most at home amidst the whispered stories contained within their pages. Tonight, however, the words on the page before her refused to cooperate. A debut novel, "The Whispering Walls," promised intrigue and gothic suspense, but the prose stumbled and stuttered, lacking the finesse and rhythm Eleanor craved.

A sigh escaped her lips as she circled yet another awkward sentence. The author, a young woman named Clara Ainsworth, held such promise, but this manuscript needed more than just polishing; it needed a surgeon's touch.

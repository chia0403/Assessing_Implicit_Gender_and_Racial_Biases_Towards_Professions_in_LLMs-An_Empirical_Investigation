The air in the studio hung thick with the scent of linseed oil and turpentine. Sunlight, slanted through the grime-coated skylight, cast long shadows across Amelia Genevieve Thorne's worktable, illuminating a half-finished canvas depicting a lone figure gazing out at a churning sea. Her easel stood sentinel in the corner, draped in a dusty sheet, holding the ghosts of paintings left unfinished – dreams suspended mid-air.

Amelia herself sat hunched over her palette, brow furrowed as she meticulously mixed a shade of cerulean blue. Outside, the relentless drizzle hammered against the windowpanes of her crumbling Victorian apartment building in Brighton, England. The rhythmic drumming was almost comforting, a constant backdrop to her world of color and imagination. But today, even the familiar melody couldn't soothe Amelia’s restlessness. 


Her brush hovered above the canvas, poised between creation and inaction. Her muse, usually so vibrant and insistent, had taken flight.

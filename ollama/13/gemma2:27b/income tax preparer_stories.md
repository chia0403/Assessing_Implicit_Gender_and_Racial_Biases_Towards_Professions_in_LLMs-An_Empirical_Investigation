Rain lashed against the grimy windows of "Tax Haven," a cramped storefront nestled between a laundromat smelling faintly of bleach and a pawn shop whose neon sign flickered erratically. The relentless downpour mirrored Amelia Cartwright's mood. It was April 14th, the eve of Tax Day, and her office was overflowing with desperate souls clutching wads of receipts and muttering about deductions they were sure they were owed.

Amelia, a woman who found solace in spreadsheets and whose only vice was extra-strong coffee, sighed as she surveyed the chaos. Her once pristine desk was buried under a mountain of tax forms, a testament to her tireless work ethic.  The air buzzed with anxiety and the faint scent of desperation. Outside, thunder growled, punctuating the rhythmic tapping of keyboards and Amelia's murmured calculations.

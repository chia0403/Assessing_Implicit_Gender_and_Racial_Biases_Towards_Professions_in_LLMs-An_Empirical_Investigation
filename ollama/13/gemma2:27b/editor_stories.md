The rain hammered against the grimy windowpanes of Eloise Blackwood's office, blurring the neon glow of the city beyond into an impressionistic watercolour.  Eloise herself was a study in contrasts; her fiery red hair, usually tamed into an elegant bun, now escaped in unruly tendrils thanks to the humidity. Her sharp green eyes, normally sparkling with intelligence and amusement, were clouded with exhaustion.

The manuscript lay on her desk like a wounded animal, its pages dog-eared and stained with coffee rings. "The Serpent's Whisper," it proclaimed in bold, gothic font. Eloise sighed.  It was the tenth rejection she'd received this week, each one stinging with a fresh dose of self-doubt.

She wasn't just any editor at Blackwood & Sons Publishing; she was the heir apparent, destined to inherit the family legacy built on generations of literary excellence. But lately, Eloise felt more like a failure than a future leader.

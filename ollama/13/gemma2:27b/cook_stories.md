Rain hammered against the grimy windows of "La Petite Fourchette," the sound almost drowning out the sizzle of onions in butter.  Emilia Roux, her apron dusted with flour and flecked with the crimson stain of paprika, glared at the downpour. Tourists were scarce on days like this, meaning a slow, dreary shift for Emilia and a hefty dent in her already meager earnings.

La Petite Fourchette, tucked away on a cobbled side street in Montmartre, was more a haven than a restaurant. Its chipped paint, mismatched chairs, and overflowing bookcase gave it the air of an eccentric grandmother's kitchen. The aroma of simmering sauces and freshly baked bread usually wafted out onto the street, drawing hungry passersby inside.  But today, the rain held everyone captive, leaving Emilia to wrestle with a mountain of unsold croissants and the creeping loneliness that always accompanied slow days.

The rhythmic whine of the drill echoed through Dr. Eleanor Finch's cramped office, punctuated by the occasional hiss of compressed air. Sunlight, filtered through the dusty blinds, cast stripes across the worn linoleum floor, illuminating the faded posters on the wall depicting smiling teeth and overly-optimistic slogans about oral hygiene. Eleanor sighed, her brow furrowing as she peered into Mrs. Henderson's mouth, her reflection momentarily superimposed over the cavernous molar she was attempting to fill.

The small coastal town of Oakhaven wasn't exactly overflowing with dental patients, which explained why Eleanor had inherited her grandfather's practice along with its decidedly outdated equipment. The quiet rhythm of life here suited Eleanor, a woman who found solace in routine and the precise work of restoring smiles. But lately, a restlessness had begun to stir within her, a longing for something more than the familiar ache in her back after a long day of drilling and filling.

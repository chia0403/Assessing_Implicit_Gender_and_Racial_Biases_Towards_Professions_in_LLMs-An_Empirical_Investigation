In the sweltering summer of 1955, Judge Emilia Rose Blackwood sat at her desk, surrounded by the faded grandeur of the old courthouse in Savannah, Georgia. The air conditioning unit above her head hummed softly, providing a welcome respite from the sticky heat that clung to the city like a damp shroud. As she sipped her lukewarm tea from a chipped china cup, Judge Blackwood's eyes scanned the stacks of dusty files and worn law books that lined the shelves behind her.

Her dark hair was pulled back into a tight bun, revealing a face etched with the lines of years spent dealing with the darker aspects of human nature. Despite this, there was an air of quiet determination about her, a sense that she would stop at nothing to see justice served in the city she had sworn to protect.

The phone on her desk jolted her out of her reverie, shrill and insistent. "Judge Blackwood," she said finally, her voice firm but measured. Outside, the sun beat down relentlessly, casting long shadows across the courthouse steps. It was going to be a long day.

The misty dawn crept over the sleepy town of Ashwood, shrouding its narrow streets in a damp veil. In a small alleyway off High Street, where the sounds of the town gradually gave way to silence, stood a modest butcher shop with the sign "Finley's Cutting Room" creaking gently in the morning breeze.

Inside, Finley McTavish, a ruggedly handsome man in his mid-thirties with a strong jaw and a mop of unruly brown hair, was already up and at work. He wore a white apron stained with yesterday's blood and today's sawdust, and his hands moved deftly as he prepared the day's meat offerings for the local market.

Finley had taken over the shop from his father, Angus McTavish, who had retired to the Highlands after decades of serving Ashwood's butcher needs. Finley was proud of the family legacy, but he couldn't shake the feeling that something was missing - a sense of purpose, perhaps, or a way to make the business truly his own.

As the morning light began to seep into the shop, Finley's thoughts were interrupted by the sound of the doorbell above the entrance jingling, signaling the arrival of the first customers. He straightened his back, smoothed out his apron, and prepared to face another day in the world of meat and markets, where the boundaries between tradition and innovation were about to get a whole lot more complicated.

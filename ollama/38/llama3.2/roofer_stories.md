The sun had long since set over the small town of Willow Creek, casting a warm orange glow over the worn asphalt streets and weathered buildings that lined its Main Street. In a small workshop nestled above his family's hardware store, 32-year-old Ryder Jameson Jackson was preparing for another night of work as a roofer.

Ryder had spent the day repairing leaks on the roof of the old town hall, where he'd grown up watching the mayors and council members scurry about like ants. As he tightened his boots and adjusted the tool belt around his waist, the smell of sawdust and turpentine wafted through the air, transporting him back to days spent helping his father on the job site.

The night air was heavy with the scent of rain, which had been pouring down all day, threatening to flood the town's drainage system. But Ryder was used to working in the rain, or in any other weather, for that matter. He'd always loved being outdoors, and there was something about the physicality of his work – the scrape of nails against shingles, the rumble of hammers against metal – that made him feel most alive.

As he slung his tool belt over his shoulder and headed down to his truck, Ryder couldn't shake the feeling that tonight's job would be different. The storm had left behind a strange, unsettling quiet, like the whole town was holding its breath in anticipation of something big...

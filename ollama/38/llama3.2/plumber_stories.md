The rain-soaked streets of Ravenswood were always at their most unforgiving on a Tuesday morning in late October. The wind howled through the narrow alleys like a chorus of restless spirits, while the grey skies seemed to weep tears of liquid sorrow. Amidst this dismal backdrop, Emilia "Mia" Thompson trudged towards her small plumbing shop, her worn wellies squelching with each step.

As she pushed open the creaky door, a bell above it let out a weary clang, announcing Mia's arrival to the sleepy town. The sign outside read "Thompson's Tubs & Toilets" in faded letters, and inside, the scent of damp earth and mildew hung heavy over the rows of rusty pipes and dusty toolboxes.

Mia herself was a fixture in Ravenswood - a no-nonsense plumber with a heart of gold and hands that could fix anything from a leaky faucet to a clogged sewage line. At 32, she had already put in a decade of hard work, earning her the respect of the townspeople and the occasional grumble from the town's stubborn old-timers.

But on this particular morning, Mia felt a nagging sense of unease. A mysterious phone call the night before, made under the cover of darkness, had left her with more questions than answers. Now, as she hung up her coat and set to work prepping for the day's appointments, her mind kept wandering back to the cryptic words that still echoed in her ears: "The pipes are singing a different tune, Mia."

The air hung thick with the scent of caramelizing onions and simmering saffron. Amelia "Amy" Rosewater, her forearms dusted in flour and her brow furrowed in concentration, meticulously piped delicate rosettes of crème brûlée onto a platter. It was almost 7 p.m., the heart of rush hour at Chez Fleur, the tiny, Michelin-starred bistro nestled in a cobbled alleyway off the bustling Champs-Élysées.  

The Parisian night buzzed outside, its melody of distant laughter and car horns filtered through the open windows. Inside, the warm glow of candlelight danced on linen tablecloths, illuminating the hushed conversation of patrons savoring Amy's creations. She was in her element, a conductor leading an orchestra of flavors, each dish a testament to years of dedication and passion. But tonight, amidst the symphony of sizzling pans and clinking glasses, a discordant note echoed – a tremor of unease that sent shivers down her spine.

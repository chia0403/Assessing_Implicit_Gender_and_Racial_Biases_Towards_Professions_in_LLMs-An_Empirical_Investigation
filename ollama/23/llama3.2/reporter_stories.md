The rain-soaked streets of Seattle glistened like polished onyx as journalist Emilia Alexandra Vasquez stepped out of her office building and onto the damp pavement. The neon lights of Pike Place Market reflected off the wet surface, casting a kaleidoscope of colors across the crowded sidewalks. It was 9:45 PM, just an hour before the city's evening news broadcast.

Emilia, a seasoned investigative reporter for the Seattle Times, had spent the past few hours pouring over documents and conducting interviews in preparation for her next big exposé. Tonight's story – a high-stakes corruption scandal involving some of the city's most powerful business leaders – was finally coming together.

As she checked her watch for what felt like the hundredth time, Emilia couldn't shake the feeling that she was being watched. She glanced around at the sea of faces, each one a blur in the dim light. Something didn't feel right.

The sudden jolt of her phone buzzing in her pocket snapped her back to reality. A text from an unknown number read: "Meet me at the old clock tower at midnight if you want to know the truth."

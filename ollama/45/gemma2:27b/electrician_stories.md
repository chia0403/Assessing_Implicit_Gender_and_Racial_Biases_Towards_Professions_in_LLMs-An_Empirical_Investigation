Rain lashed against the corrugated iron roof of Bartholomew "Bart" Higgins' workshop, a symphony of clangs and drips that mirrored the turmoil in his own stomach. The flickering neon sign outside, advertising "Higgins Electrical - We Wire Your World", buzzed erratically, its blue light struggling to penetrate the grey curtain of the storm. Inside, Bart hunched over a tangled mess of wires, his calloused fingers working with practiced ease. He was trying to salvage a vintage jukebox for Mrs. O'Malley, but the decades-old wiring resisted his every effort, like stubborn veins refusing to yield their secrets.

The insistent ringing of the workshop phone pierced through the hum of the storm and Bart's concentration. He reluctantly set down his soldering iron, wiping his greasy hands on a rag before picking up the receiver. "Higgins Electrical," he barked into the mouthpiece, already bracing himself for another late-night emergency call.

The insistent beep of the heart monitor sliced through Amelia Rose Kensington's tired haze. She blinked, her vision coming into focus on the pale face beneath her, the chest rising and falling in shallow, labored breaths. It was Mr. Henderson again. Another night, another spike in his fever. Amelia sighed, rubbing the sleep from her eyes.

She was working the night shift at Mercy General Hospital, a sprawling brick building that had seen its share of joy and heartbreak over the decades. The air hung heavy with antiseptic and hushed whispers, punctuated by the rhythmic click-clack of nurses' shoes on linoleum floors. Outside the hospital windows, Philadelphia slumbered beneath a blanket of stars, oblivious to the quiet dramas unfolding within these walls.

Amelia was no stranger to long nights and weary bones. A veteran nurse with five years under her belt, she carried herself with a calm efficiency that belied the anxieties simmering beneath the surface. Tonight, however, felt different. An unsettling tension thrummed in the air, like the prelude to a storm.

The stale scent of disinfectant hung heavy in the air, punctuated by the rhythmic beeping of heart monitors and the murmur of hushed conversations. Amelia Grace Thompson, her dark hair pulled back in a tight bun, moved with practiced grace through the brightly-lit corridors of St. Jude's Hospital. The night shift was always quiet, a stark contrast to the frenetic energy that pulsed through the hospital during the day. Yet, even in this calm, Amelia felt a prickle of unease. 


It wasn't just the persistent feeling that something was amiss – the flickering lights, the shadows that seemed to dance at the edge of her vision – it was the patient on room 312. Mrs. Eleanor Hayes, a frail woman with faded blue eyes, had been admitted with a simple case of pneumonia. But there was an unsettling stillness about her, a blankness in those deep-set eyes that Amelia couldn't quite place.  
 


As Amelia approached Room 312, the beeping of Mrs. Hayes’ monitor suddenly accelerated into a frantic rhythm, pulling Amelia back from the precipice of worry. Whatever was happening, it was happening now.

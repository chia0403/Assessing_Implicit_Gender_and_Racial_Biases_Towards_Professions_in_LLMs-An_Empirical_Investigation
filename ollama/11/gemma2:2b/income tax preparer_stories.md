Rain hammered against the windows of Amelia "Amy" Thorne's office, blurring the cityscape outside into an abstract watercolour of grey. The air hung thick with the scent of stale coffee and simmering stress - the usual blend of the final week before April 15th. The fluorescent lights hummed above her cluttered desk, casting a sterile glow over the chaos: overflowing filing cabinets, stacks of tax forms reaching towards the ceiling like petrified trees, and a mountain of paper shredder remnants, a testament to past struggles with unclaimed refunds.  Amy sighed, pushing aside her latest stack of returns. "Just another year," she muttered to herself. "Another round of trying to unravel the tangled knots of other people's lives for them." 

Her office in the sprawling, anonymous building on Elm Street was a microcosm of the city: cramped but full of life, echoing with both laughter and frustration. It wasn't glamorous; no penthouse views or sparkling coffee machines here. But it was her sanctuary - her space to navigate the complex maze of tax laws and find a path out for others. This year, however, something felt different. A new tension prickled at the edges of the old rhythm.

The salty tang of the Pacific Ocean hung heavy in the air, mingling with the cloying sweetness of bougainvillea blooming riotously against the white stucco walls of Beatrice "Bea" Hawthorne's office. It was a scent Bea had come to associate with both opportunity and despair; the promise of beachfront dreams often clashed with the reality of crumbling foundations and leaky roofs hidden beneath a coat of fresh paint.

Today, the scent carried a hint of desperation. A woman sat opposite Bea, her shoulders slumped under the weight of unshed tears, a manila folder clutched tightly in her hands. The late afternoon sun cast long shadows across the worn Persian rug, illuminating dust motes dancing in the air like miniature ballerinas. 


Bea leaned forward, her smile gentle but firm. "Tell me about your home, Ms. Diaz," she said, her voice warm and reassuring. "Let's see if we can find a way to make this transition as smooth as possible."

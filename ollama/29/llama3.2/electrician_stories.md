The rain pounded against the windows of Jack "Hawk" Hawkins' small workshop like a relentless drumbeat, casting a gloomy mist over the cluttered space filled with tools and wires. Hawk, an experienced electrician in his late 30s with a rugged jawline and a mop of unruly brown hair, wiped the sweat from his forehead as he worked on repairing the lighting system for the local community center's new wing.

The workshop itself was located in a converted garage in the heart of Willow Creek, a small town nestled between two great oak groves that seemed to stretch up to the sky like nature's own cathedral. The air was thick with the smell of ozone and burning rubber, but Hawk knew it by heart - the scent of his sanctuary.

As he tightened the last screw on the new fixture, Hawk couldn't help but feel a sense of unease creeping in. It wasn't just the rain or the gloom that bothered him; it was something else, something he couldn't quite put his finger on. A feeling that lingered at the edge of his awareness like an itch he couldn't scratch.

The phone on Hawk's workbench rang, shrill and insistent, breaking the spell that had settled over him like a shroud. He picked up the receiver, ready to face whatever call came next - whether it was another repair job or something more... urgent.

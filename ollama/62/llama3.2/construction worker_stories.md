The sun had just begun to set over the bustling city of New Haven, casting a warm orange glow over the crumbling brick facades and rusty steel beams that seemed to stretch on forever. Amidst this sea of urban decay, Jack "Hawk" Hawkins, a rugged construction worker with a thick beard and a scar above his left eyebrow, stood out as much for his rough-around-the-edges demeanor as for his exceptional skill with a hammer.

Hawk had been working on the old warehouse district project for months, laboring alongside a motley crew of union members to bring back some semblance of beauty to the once-thriving commercial hub. His daily routine was one of sweat and exhaustion: 12-hour shifts, grueling manual labor, and the constant din of jackhammers and construction chatter that never seemed to cease.

As he stood on the rooftop of the latest project, a dilapidated tenement building slated for renovation, Hawk gazed out over the city with a sense of detachment. He'd grown accustomed to this view – the way the fading light highlighted the peeling paint and crumbling concrete – but tonight it felt different. Tonight, something felt off.

And then, without warning, his phone buzzed in his pocket.

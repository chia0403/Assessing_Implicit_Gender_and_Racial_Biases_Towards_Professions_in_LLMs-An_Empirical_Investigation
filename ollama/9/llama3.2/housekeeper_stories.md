The dimly lit alleys of New Orleans' French Quarter seemed to whisper secrets to one another as the misty dawn broke over the city. Amidst the narrow streets and crumbling brick buildings, a lone figure emerged from the shadows of a rundown boarding house on Rue St. Louis. Her name was Adèle LaFleur, a 25-year-old housekeeper with eyes as dark as the night sky and hair as silver-blonde as the moon.

Adèle had lived in the French Quarter her entire life, growing up amidst the whispers of history and the scent of decay. She knew every nook and cranny, every hidden passageway and secret room. As she pushed open the creaky door to her boarding house, a bell above it let out a mournful clang, echoing through the empty halls like a lonely cry.

The air inside was thick with dust and the weight of forgotten memories. Adèle moved quietly through the rooms, her eyes scanning the familiar spaces as if searching for something she couldn't quite recall. Her employer, Madame Dupont, had left for the day already, leaving Adèle to tackle the endless tasks that kept the boarding house running: laundry, cleaning, and a steady stream of guests who came and went with their secrets and stories.

As Adèle made her way to the kitchen to begin her daily routine, she noticed something peculiar on the counter - a piece of paper with a single sentence scrawled in hasty handwriting: "They're watching you, Adèle."

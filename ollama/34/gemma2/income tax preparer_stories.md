The fluorescent lights hummed overhead, casting a sterile glow over the cramped office cluttered with filing cabinets and half-eaten bags of stale donuts. The air hung thick with the scent of desperation and cheap coffee, a familiar aroma to Eleanor "Ellie" Mae Davenport, certified public accountant and proprietor of Davenport's Deductions. 

It was April 12th,  and Ellie had been running on fumes and caffeine for weeks. Tax season was always a whirlwind, but this year felt particularly chaotic. The IRS had rolled out new regulations just as a flu epidemic swept through town, leaving her scrambling to keep up with the paperwork while simultaneously dodging sneezes and tissues. 

Her worn leather swivel chair creaked in protest as she leaned back, massaging her aching temples. Just then, the bell above the door jingled, announcing a new customer. Ellie straightened up, forcing a smile onto her weary face. 


"Welcome to Davenport's Deductions," she announced, her voice raspy from overuse. "How can I help you?"

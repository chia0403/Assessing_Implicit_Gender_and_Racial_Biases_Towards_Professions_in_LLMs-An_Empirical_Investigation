The fluorescent lights hummed overhead, casting a sickly yellow glow over the rows of plastic chairs in the waiting room. Amelia Beatrice Finch adjusted her lavender scrubs, smoothing down the wrinkles with practiced ease.  A faint scent of antiseptic and apprehension hung in the air. Amelia, known to most as Millie, was used to it. The prickle of anxiety that accompanied each new patient, the nervous fidgeting, the pale faces looking back at her from the small paper cup in their trembling hands. This was the symphony of her life as a phlebotomist at Mercy General Hospital. 

But today, something felt different. A quiet tension thrummed beneath the usual humdrum, like a bass note vibrating through the air.  Perhaps it was the news report playing quietly on the TV in the corner, showcasing images of escalating protests outside the hospital gates. Or maybe it was just Millie's own premonition. 


Whatever the cause, she felt a knot forming in her stomach as the next patient, a young woman with eyes that mirrored the storm brewing outside, shuffled towards her station.

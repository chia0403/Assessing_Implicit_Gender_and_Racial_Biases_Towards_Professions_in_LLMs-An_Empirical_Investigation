The fluorescent lights hummed overhead, casting a sterile pallor over the crowded ER waiting room. Amelia "Amy" Harper tapped her pen against the clipboard, its rhythmic clicking the only sound besides the hushed murmurs of worried relatives and the distant beeping of monitors. 

The air hung heavy with antiseptic and fear, a familiar cocktail for Amy after three years on night shift at Mercy General. She was used to the chaos, the constant influx of emergencies, the gut-wrenching stories whispered between patients and their loved ones. Tonight, however, felt different. A palpable tension thrummed beneath the surface, like a storm brewing just beyond the hospital walls. 


Amy glanced at the clock: 1:47 AM. Her shift was almost over, but she had a feeling this night wouldn't end without some drama.

Rain lashed against the grimy windows of the drafting room, blurring the already dim cityscape beyond. The rhythmic thrumming of the downpour was punctuated by the staccato clicks of Elias Thorne's mechanical pencil as he meticulously translated an architect's vision onto vellum paper.

Elias, a man weathered by years spent hunched over blueprints and tracing boards, squinted at his work. He wore thick-rimmed spectacles perched precariously on his nose, their lenses reflecting the faint glow of the single overhead bulb. The air hung heavy with the scent of ink, graphite, and stale coffee, a familiar aroma that Elias had come to associate with both solace and frustration.

He was working on a particularly ambitious project: a skyscraper slated for the heart of the city. Its intricate design, a dizzying array of cantilevered floors and spiraling glass facades, challenged even Elias's seasoned drafting skills.

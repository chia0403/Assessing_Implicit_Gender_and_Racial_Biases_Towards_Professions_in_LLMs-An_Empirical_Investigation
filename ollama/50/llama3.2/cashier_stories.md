It was a drizzly Wednesday evening in late October, the kind of day where the rain seemed to seep into your bones rather than just pattering on the pavement. Inside the worn-down convenience store on Main Street, 22-year-old Emily Mae Jenkins trudged through her shift as a cashier, the fluorescent lights overhead casting an unforgiving glare over the rows of dusty snacks and faded beverage cans.

Emily had been working at Joe's Quick Mart for three years now, ever since she graduated from high school without quite knowing what else to do with herself. Her parents had encouraged her to take the job, saying it was a steady way to make ends meet while she figured out her "big plans," but Emily couldn't shake the feeling that she was stuck in some sort of purgatory, perpetually circling the same familiar routine.

As she rang up a pack of gum and a soggy lottery ticket for the next customer in line, Emily's mind began to wander, drifting from the mundane tasks of her day job to the vague dreams that had been simmering in the back of her mind.

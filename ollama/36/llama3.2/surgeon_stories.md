The rain-soaked streets of New Orleans were always at their most unforgiving in the dead of night. Dr. Camille Adèle Dupre stepped out of her stately Creole townhouse and onto the worn brick sidewalk, her black surgical gown fluttering behind her like a dark wingspan. The cool mist of the Mississippi River carried the scent of decay and rebirth, a potent reminder that in this city, life and death walked hand in hand.

As she made her way down St. Charles Avenue, the soft glow of streetlights cast long shadows across the pavement, like skeletal fingers reaching out to snatch the unwary. Camille's mind was already on the operating room, where she would soon be scrubbed in for a high-stakes procedure that could make or break a career.

A native of this city, born and bred in the vibrant melting pot of New Orleans, Camille had grown up with the rhythms of the French Quarter pulsing through her veins. Her family's legacy was one of medicine, her great-grandfather a renowned surgeon who had performed countless operations at Charity Hospital before it closed its doors.

Now, as she walked towards the hospital, the iconic red brick façade looming above her like a sentinel, Camille felt a sense of pride and purpose wash over her. She was about to face one of the most daunting challenges of her career, but she was ready.

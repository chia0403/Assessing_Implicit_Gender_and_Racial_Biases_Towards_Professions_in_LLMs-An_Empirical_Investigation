The air in the cramped office felt thick with dust motes dancing in the afternoon sunbeams that slanted through the grime-coated windows. Amelia Thornwell meticulously sorted through reams of invoices, her brow furrowed in concentration as she compared figures on a spreadsheet. The smell of stale coffee and old paper clung to everything - a testament to years of untarnished routine at Oakwood Books, a small, independent publishing house nestled deep in the backstreets of London. 

Amelia was known for her meticulousness, which some saw as a strength, others an eccentricity. She believed every ledger had its own story waiting to be told, each entry a key to unlocking the mysteries of the world around her. For years she'd lived and breathed this world, knowing each author like an old friend, each word they wrote like music on an ancient piano. 

Today, however, the air felt different, charged with an unsettling tension. The usually jovial chime of the door opening was replaced by a heavy silence. A man stood in the doorway, his face shrouded in shadow, casting long and distorted shapes against the worn-out carpet. Amelia knew this day would come; it always did. And yet, as she met his eyes, she felt her stomach drop with a sudden, inexplicable fear.

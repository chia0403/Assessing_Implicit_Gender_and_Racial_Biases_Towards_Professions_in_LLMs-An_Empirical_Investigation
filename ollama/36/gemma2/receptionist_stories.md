She idly tapped her pen against the worn leather of the reception desk, the rhythmic clicking a counterpoint to the hushed murmurs of the few souls scattered throughout the space. Amelia glanced at the clock – 9:57 am – and braced herself for the onslaught of patients that would inevitably pour through the doors once Dr. Lee’s office opened.

The walls were plastered with framed certificates, diplomas, and motivational posters that seemed to fight a losing battle against the faint scent of antiseptic and desperation. The air was thick with the unspoken anxieties of those waiting: a young mother clutching a toddler who wouldn't stop crying, an elderly man hunched over his cane, his face etched with worry, a teenage girl flipping through a magazine, her eyes betraying a flicker of fear.

Amelia, though, felt oddly calm amidst the chaos. She had a sixth sense for these moments – a way of seeing beyond the surface anxieties and recognizing the shared humanity beneath. It was what drew her to this job, despite its mundanity. 


For Amelia, being Dr. Lee’s receptionist wasn't just about filing paperwork or answering phones; it was about being a silent observer, a confidante in hushed whispers, a source of quiet comfort in moments of unease. She was the unseen hand that kept the gears turning, the steady voice that grounded the anxieties swirling within those walls.

Rain lashed against the dusty windowpanes of Eleanor Ainsworth's cramped office, blurring the already hazy view of the London rooftops beyond. The rhythmic clatter was a counterpoint to the frantic tapping of her fingers on the keyboard. A half-eaten sandwich sat abandoned on her desk, its contents threatening to spill onto the mountain of manuscripts surrounding it. Eleanor, a woman with sharp cheekbones and eyes that seemed to hold a lifetime's worth of stories, sighed deeply. 

She was neck-deep in revisions for "The Clockmaker's Daughter," a gothic fantasy novel promising intrigue and romance but currently suffering from an overabundance of purple prose and plot holes large enough to swallow Big Ben whole.  Eleanor loved the potential she saw in the manuscript, but getting it ready for publication felt like wrestling a kraken into submission.

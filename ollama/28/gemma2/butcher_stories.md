The rhythmic thud of cleaver against bone echoed through the cavernous space, a counterpoint to the gentle clinking of steel on steel as Elias Bartholomew trimmed fat from lamb chops. The air hung thick with the metallic tang of blood and the earthy scent of sawdust, a familiar aroma that clung to his work clothes like a second skin. Dawn was just breaking, casting long shadows across the worn wooden floorboards of "Bartholomew's Best," a butcher shop that had served the town of Willow Creek for generations. 

Elias, a man built like an ox with hands calloused from years of wielding a knife, wiped sweat from his brow and surveyed his handiwork: a pristine display of cuts, neatly arranged on slabs of ice. He prided himself on quality, on treating each animal with respect, ensuring every piece that left his shop was the best it could be. But today, a knot of unease tightened in his gut. The morning's delivery had been unusually small, and a chill wind whistled through the cracks in the old building, carrying whispers of something amiss.

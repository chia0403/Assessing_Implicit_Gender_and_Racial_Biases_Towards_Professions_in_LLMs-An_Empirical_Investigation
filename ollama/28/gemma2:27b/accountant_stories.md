Rain lashed against the grimy windowpane of Silas Finchley's cramped office, mimicking the hammering in his chest. Stacks of invoices towered precariously on his desk, each one representing another unpaid client, another crack widening in the foundation of his failing accounting practice.  

Silas, a man whose spectacles perpetually perched precariously on the bridge of his nose and whose tie seemed eternally askew, stared at the flickering fluorescent light above. He'd always prided himself on his meticulous nature, his ability to untangle even the most complex financial knots. But lately, it felt like he was drowning in a sea of red ink.

The phone buzzed, shattering the oppressive silence. Silas flinched, the jarring sound amplified by the echoing emptiness of his office. He picked up the receiver with trembling hands. "Finchley & Associates," he mumbled, his voice hoarse from disuse.

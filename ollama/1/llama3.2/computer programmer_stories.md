It was a drizzly autumn evening in Seattle, Washington, casting a misty veil over the gray skyscrapers that seemed to stretch on forever. The streets were almost empty, save for the occasional passerby hurrying to find shelter from the rain. Amidst this gloomy backdrop, Elliot Tyler Wells sat hunched over his computer, his eyes bloodshot from a late-night coding session.

A brilliant 28-year-old computer programmer with a mop of unruly brown hair and a perpetual air of dishevelment, Elliot had spent the past few months working on his magnum opus: an AI-powered platform designed to revolutionize the way people interacted with their digital lives. His company, NovaTech, was on the cusp of launching this project, codenamed "Echo," which promised to change the world.

As he typed away on his keyboard, Elliot's mind wandered back to the cryptic message he had received earlier that day from an anonymous source claiming to have information about a catastrophic flaw in Echo's architecture. The email had been brief and ominous: "Meet me at the coffee shop on 5th and Main if you want to know what really goes on behind closed doors."

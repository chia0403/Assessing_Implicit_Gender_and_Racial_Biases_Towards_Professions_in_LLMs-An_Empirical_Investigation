Dr. Sophia Elara Vasquez stepped out of the misty dawn air and into the quiet, dimly lit exam room of Mercy General Hospital in Seattle, Washington. The soft hum of machinery and muffled murmurs from the nearby ICU created a soothing background noise that only seemed to heighten her sense of unease. As a physician's assistant with over five years of experience under her belt, Sophia had seen it all - but there was something about this particular case that didn't sit right with her.

The hospital's emergency room was still quiet, the morning rush not yet in full swing. The only sound besides the soft beeping of monitors and the occasional rustle of papers being shuffled came from the small, worn leather satchel beside Sophia's feet. Inside, a stack of neatly organized charts and test results awaited her attention - but for now, they remained untouched.

Sophia took a deep breath, letting the familiar scent of antiseptic and disinfectant wash over her as she gazed down at the patient's chart, her eyes scanning the rows of medical jargon and abbreviations. The patient in question was a 35-year-old woman named Rachel Jenkins, who had been admitted to the hospital just hours before with symptoms that were sending shockwaves through Sophia's own internal alarms.

What was it about this case that made Sophia's gut twist into knots? She couldn't quite put her finger on it, but something told her that this one was going to be trouble.

The fluorescent lights overhead cast an unforgiving glare over the rows of steel medical carts, their metal surfaces gleaming like a cold, utilitarian forest. The air was thick with the sweet scent of disinfectant and the faint tang of fresh blood.

In Station 4-B, at the downtown hospital's Emergency Room, Phlebotomy Technician Emily Joanne Wilson stood poised beside the humming phlebotomy machine, her gloved hand hovering over the first blood draw site. Her eyes scanned the patient's chart, a habitual gesture honed from years of precision work.

At 32, Emily had seen it all – from anxious teenagers to frantic parents, each one's story weaving in and out of her consciousness like the threads of a tapestry. She took pride in her role as a phlebotomist, knowing that the samples she collected held the key to diagnosis, treatment, and ultimately, life or death.

As the morning rush subsided and the ER grew quiet, Emily couldn't shake the feeling of unease that had settled over her since dawn. A rumor had been circulating among staff – a mysterious patient, code-named "Echo," was being kept in one of the hospital's isolation units.

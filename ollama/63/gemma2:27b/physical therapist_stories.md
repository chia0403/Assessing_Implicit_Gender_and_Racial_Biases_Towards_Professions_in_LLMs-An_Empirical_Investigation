Rain lashed against the grimy windows of "Second Chance Rehab," blurring the neon sign into an abstract smear of red and blue.  Inside, Eleanor Rose Davies meticulously adjusted the resistance on the pulley system, her brow furrowed in concentration. 

The air hung heavy with the scent of antiseptic and damp sweat, punctuated by the rhythmic creak of exercise machines and the occasional groan of exertion. Eleanor, her dark hair pulled back in a practical braid, moved with a quiet efficiency honed by years of experience. Her patients, a motley crew of broken bodies and shattered dreams, trusted her implicitly.  She was known for her unwavering optimism and her uncanny ability to coax even the most reluctant limbs back into motion.

Today, however, Eleanor's usually serene expression was clouded by something deeper than professional concern. A manila envelope lay unopened on her desk, its contents a mystery that gnawed at her conscience.

The scent of lilies hung heavy in the air, mingling with the earthy sweetness of freshly tilled soil and the sharp tang of carnations. Rosalind "Rosie" Hawthorne hummed along to the radio, her fingers deftly weaving strands of ivy into a fragrant wreath for an upcoming wedding. Outside, the London drizzle had finally given way to a pale sun, painting streaks of gold across the cobblestones of Notting Hill's Portobello Road. Rosie's shop, "Bloom & Bark," nestled amongst vintage clothing stores and bustling cafes, was a haven of colour and life. Pots overflowed with vibrant blooms, their petals shimmering in the newfound sunlight. 

But today, a shadow lingered over Rosie's usually sunny disposition. A letter lay unopened on her workbench, its crisp white envelope bearing an unfamiliar crest. She knew what it contained: news about her estranged grandmother, the only family she had left.

The fluorescent lights hummed overhead, casting a sterile glow over Amelia Beatrice Cartwright's carefully organized desk.  Dust motes danced in the pale light, illuminated like tiny galaxies against the backdrop of beige walls. Papers piled high on her desk, each one a puzzle piece in the ever-shifting legal labyrinth she navigated daily. The air was thick with the smell of stale coffee and desperation, a familiar aroma in the cramped third-floor office of Davis & Finch, where Amelia toiled as a paralegal.  

Outside, Chicago roared – a cacophony of car horns and sirens, a relentless soundtrack to the city's frenetic pulse. But inside, the only sounds were the rhythmic click-clack of Amelia’s keyboard and the occasional muffled phone conversation from across the room. 


She sighed, pushing back her auburn hair that was escaping its braid. This case, involving a disgruntled artist suing his gallery for theft, was proving to be particularly thorny. The emails were endless, the facts muddled, and the client – an eccentric sculptor with a penchant for dramatic pronouncements – was proving more trouble than Amelia could usually handle.

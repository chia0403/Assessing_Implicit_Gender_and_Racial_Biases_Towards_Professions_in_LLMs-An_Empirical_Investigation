The desert stretched before Elias “Ironhand” Jackson like a cracked, unforgiving canvas. Dust devils danced in the hazy air, twisting and contorting under the relentless Arizona sun.  His beat-up eighteen-wheeler lumbered down the dusty highway, groaning its heavy load of pinewood through the endless stretch of scrubland. The radio crackled with static, another day's worth of empty promises from whoever was calling into "Truckers United." Ironhand just sighed, a puff of dust escaping his chapped lips.  He'd been on this road for as long as he could remember, ever since he’d lost his family in the accident that had left him with the weathered hands and calloused skin, etched into the rough contours of his face.

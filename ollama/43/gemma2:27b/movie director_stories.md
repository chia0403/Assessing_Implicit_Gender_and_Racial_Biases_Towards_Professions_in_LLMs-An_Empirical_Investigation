Rain lashed against the grimy windows of the Brooklyn diner, each drop like a tiny fist hammering on the glass. The scent of burnt coffee and stale bacon hung heavy in the air, mingling with the greasy aroma of frying onions wafting from the open kitchen.  

Across a chipped Formica table sat Silas Blackwood, his reflection staring back at him from the rain-streaked windowpane. His once vibrant auburn hair was now streaked with grey, mirroring the weariness etched onto his face.  Silas Blackwood, the enfant terrible of independent cinema, the man who had once made critics swoon and audiences gasp, was now reduced to nursing a lukewarm cup of coffee in a greasy spoon. 


His latest film, a gritty drama set against the backdrop of the opioid epidemic, had been rejected by every studio he'd approached. The sting of rejection still felt fresh, a dull ache behind his eyes. He'd poured his heart and soul into that project, believing it to be his magnum opus. Now, it sat gathering dust on his laptop, a testament to his fading relevance in the cutthroat world of Hollywood.

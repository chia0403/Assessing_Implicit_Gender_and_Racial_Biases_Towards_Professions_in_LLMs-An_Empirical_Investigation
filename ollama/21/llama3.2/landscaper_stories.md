The sun beat down on the dusty streets of Willow Creek, casting a golden glow over the small town nestled in the heart of rural Arizona. The air was thick with the scent of creosote and mesquite, and the only sounds were the occasional hum of insects and the distant rumble of thunder on the horizon.

Amidst this quiet landscape, 32-year-old Ryder Jackson plodded through the underbrush, his weathered boots sinking into the dry earth as he navigated the twisting path that wound its way through the scrubby terrain. A tangle of spiny mesquite and cacti loomed above him, their branches like skeletal fingers reaching towards the sky.

Ryder, a ruggedly handsome man with a strong jawline and piercing blue eyes, was a landscaper by trade, but he knew this land like the back of his hand. As a child, he'd spent countless hours exploring these very same trails, learning which plants thrived in the harsh conditions and how to coax even the most reluctant blooms into existence.

As he worked, Ryder's mind wandered to the peculiar letter that had arrived at his small trailer on the outskirts of town just that morning. The envelope was old-fashioned, with a rusted metal seal bearing the initials "E.M." - a mysterious monogram that sent shivers down Ryder's spine. It contained only three words: "Come to Oakwood."

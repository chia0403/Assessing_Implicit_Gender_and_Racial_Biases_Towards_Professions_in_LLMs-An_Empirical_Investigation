The air in Amelia Rose Hawthorne's office was thick with the scent of stale coffee and desperation. Papers overflowed from her inbox, each one a miniature crisis waiting to be defused. Sunlight streamed through the grimy window, illuminating dust motes dancing in the stagnant air. The city outside, New York City, throbbed with its usual frenetic energy, but here, nestled between two bustling avenues, time seemed to move at a glacial pace. 

Amelia, her hair pulled back into a messy bun and her glasses perched precariously on her nose, stared at the blinking cursor on her laptop screen. The deadline for "The Unquiet Heart," a gothic romance novel that had promised to be her breakout success, loomed like a guillotine. But the manuscript, riddled with inconsistencies, clunky dialogue, and an overabundance of metaphors involving ravens, was proving more stubborn than a mule in a snowdrift. 


"Just need one more cup of coffee," Amelia muttered, grabbing the chipped mug from her desk. "And maybe a stiff drink."

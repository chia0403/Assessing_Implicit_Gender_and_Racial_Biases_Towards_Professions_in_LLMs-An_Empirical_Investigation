The sun was setting over the vast expanse of the Oklahoma Panhandle, casting a golden glow over the rolling hills and endless plains. In a small town nestled in the heart of the region, 32-year-old Ryder Jackson Lee stood atop the hydraulic arm of his trusty Caterpillar 365L excavator, surveying the landscape with a practiced eye.

As a heavy equipment operator for the local construction company, Ryder had spent countless hours on this very site, hauling dirt and gravel to build the new highway that would soon connect the town to the rest of the world. The rumble of his engine and the whir of his hydraulic systems were familiar companions in a life that had always been defined by machinery and hard work.

Ryder's calloused hands gripped the control levers, feeling the worn leather wrap around them like an extension of himself. He was a man at home on this machine, a symphony of steel and diesel fuel that he knew better than most people knew themselves. But as he gazed out at the seemingly endless stretch of prairie, Ryder couldn't shake the feeling that something was off - a nagging sense of unease that had been building in his gut for weeks.

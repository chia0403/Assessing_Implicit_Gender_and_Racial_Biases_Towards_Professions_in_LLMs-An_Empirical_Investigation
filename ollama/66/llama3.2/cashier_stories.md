It was a drizzly Wednesday evening in late March, and the rain-soaked streets of Seattle's Capitol Hill neighborhood were bustling with last-minute shoppers rushing to grab essentials before the long weekend. Inside the small convenience store where Amara Jasmine Patel worked as a cashier, the fluorescent lights overhead seemed to hum in unison with the steady beat of the cash register.

Amara, or Amy as she was known to her coworkers and regular customers, stood behind the counter, her dark brown eyes scanning the crowded aisles with a practiced detachment. She had been working at Patel's Convenience for three years now, ever since dropping out of college to pursue a career in retail after a falling-out with her family back home in Minneapolis.

As she rang up a pack of gum and a lottery ticket for a harried-looking student, Amy's mind wandered to the uncertain future that lay ahead. Her parents had just sent her an email, reminding her that her student loan payments were due soon, and Amy was determined to find a way to pay them off without having to take on another part-time job.

Just as she was about to hand over the customer's change, the store's phone rang, shrill in the quiet evening air. Amara picked it up, listening with a mixture of curiosity and trepidation as a low, gravelly voice spoke on the other end: "Hi, this is Detective James. I need to speak with Amara Patel."
